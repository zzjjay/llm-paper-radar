# MLSys 2026 — LLM Inference Deployment Optimization Trend Report

*Analysis of the 57 accepted papers (of ~135 total accepts) whose primary contribution is optimizing how LLMs are served. Read from the abstracts; claims grounded in the specific mechanisms each paper reports. Two caveats stated up front and relied on throughout:*

- *All speedup/reduction figures are the authors' own **self-reported bests** — almost always "up to X", often on a favorable config or microbenchmark. Treat them as ceilings, not expected values. Where a number comes from production traces rather than a microbenchmark, that's noted.*
- *This is one systems venue, and the 57 were auto-selected (see [How this set was selected](#how-this-set-was-selected)). Conclusions about "what the field values" are really "what MLSys 2026 accepted" — the venue's selection function is a confound for any field-level claim, and where a trend is likely a venue artifact rather than a real shift, that's flagged.*

## The two forces

Two largely independent shifts sit underneath almost every paper. They are **co-equal** — neither subsumes the other, and roughly half the set traces to each.

**Force 1 — the workload changed shape.** Reasoning models emit thousands of chain-of-thought tokens, so within a request the cost center moved from reading the prompt (prefill, compute-bound) toward writing the answer (decode, memory-bound). And agents, RAG, and multi-turn chat made requests stop being independent — they now share large spans of context (system prompts, retrieved documents, conversation history, agent templates). Serving systems built for short, independent, single-turn chat are being retrofitted for long, interdependent traffic.

**Force 2 — the hardware changed.** New NVIDIA generations rebalanced the on-chip bottleneck (Blackwell doubled tensor-core throughput but not the shared-memory bandwidth / exponential units around it), interconnect bandwidth kept falling behind compute, and heterogeneity — AMD, GH200 superchips, mixed fleets, client GPUs, phones — became something you design for. This force drives the single largest bucket (kernels/compilers, 11 papers) and the multi-GPU work, none of which is about the KV cache.

**Where they meet: the KV cache — the largest single object, but not a majority.** Around 15–20 of the 57 papers touch the KV cache directly. Force 1 makes it grow without bound (long decode) and makes it reusable across requests (shared context); one specific hardware fact — memory bandwidth being scarcer than compute — is what makes *reading* it the decode bottleneck. So the KV cache links Force 1 to that one hardware trend, and it is the most-contested single object in the set — but it is not "most of the papers," and it does not touch Force 2's kernel/compiler/heterogeneity half at all. Any one-sentence "it's all about the KV cache" framing would be false; the honest summary is two forces, with the KV cache as the busiest intersection of one of them.

**A constraint shaping the whole set:** model weights are frozen (you can't retrain a released model) and the deployment target is an existing engine (vLLM / SGLang / TensorRT-LLM). So nearly every technique is **training-free, drop-in, algorithm-system co-designed** — "plugs into vLLM," "no retraining," "negligible overhead." Note this is partly the venue talking (see caveat above): a pure-algorithm method would more likely be submitted elsewhere.

## How this set was selected

The 57 were filtered from ~135 MLSys 2026 accepts by an LLM classifier (Claude Sonnet, `pipeline/venue_filter.py` against `prompts/inference_relevance.md`), which hard-gates papers whose *primary* contribution isn't LLM-inference-deployment — training-only methods, model releases, pure ASIC/FPGA designs, eval-only studies, and non-LLM applications. Two biases to keep in mind when reading any distribution claim below:

1. **Automated, rubric-driven judgment.** Borderline cases were kept, so a handful of the 57 are not pure text-LLM inference — e.g. db-SP (diffusion-transformer visual generation), TriInfer (multimodal), and the diffusion-LM papers (CDLM, TiDAR, SpecDiff-2) are a different modeling paradigm folded in because they target inference efficiency. The "LLM" in the title is loose at the edges.
2. **The venue pre-filters before we do.** MLSys selects for systems/co-design work regardless of our classifier, so the population is skewed toward systems papers before analysis begins.

## 1. Decode went memory-bound, and the KV cache is the battleground

The recurring physical fact, stated almost verbatim across papers: during decode each new token re-reads the entire, growing KV cache, so long generation is IO-bound, not compute-bound ([MAC-Attention](https://openreview.net/forum?id=b6HBRCejb7), [SpecGen](https://openreview.net/forum?id=yeqrwcWjPu)). (Autoregressive decode was already memory-bound at small batch before reasoning models — the workload shift *amplified* this into the dominant regime, it didn't create it.) The responses split into four escalating stances:

- **Shrink it.** [Kitty](https://openreview.net/forum?id=r3mQiuYKIN) pushes KV to 2-bit — where accuracy normally breaks — by keeping a small fraction of sensitivity-ranked *Key channels* at higher precision, self-reporting ~8× KV-memory cut and 2.1–4.1× throughput. Quantization survived here only as a KV-specific tool.
- **Skip it.** Exploit that cached tokens don't matter equally: [FlexiCache](https://openreview.net/forum?id=GgX6dPJx9M) finds *some attention heads are temporally stable* and offloads their cold pages to host memory (self-reported −70% GPU footprint); [OPKV](https://openreview.net/forum?id=EB5bgzv4qA) makes offload-and-recall sparsity a clean plugin over paged KV; [BLASST](https://openreview.net/forum?id=6INSBXTQ4x) skips whole attention blocks under one calibrated softmax threshold.
- **Reuse computation over it.** [MAC-Attention](https://openreview.net/forum?id=b6HBRCejb7) reuses prior attention results for semantically similar recent queries (match–amend–complete), making per-token cost constant on a cache hit — a different move from compression: keep everything, recompute less. (Microbenchmark-level attention-phase numbers; end-to-end gain is smaller.)
- **Treat it as durable, distributed state.** The most conceptually notable shift — KV has graduated from "a buffer" to state that needs placement, transfer, replication, and fault tolerance: [GhostServe](https://openreview.net/forum?id=xKjYiUgeOK) erasure-codes the KV cache so a GPU failure doesn't force full recomputation; [RaidServe](https://openreview.net/forum?id=5pl9fdbEkq) does cyclic KV placement + proactive backup for resilient tensor-parallel serving; [fabric-lib](https://openreview.net/forum?id=SjVa05wEiY) (Perplexity, in production) builds portable RDMA transport because KV-cache transfer for disaggregation outgrew simple collectives.

## 2. The workload changed: long reasoning outputs and shared context

**Reasoning models made outputs long,** amplifying the decode/KV bottleneck. [SkipKV](https://openreview.net/forum?id=0EsV9SIm8p) finds token-level KV eviction actively fails on chain-of-thought (unstable scoring, and it makes the model generate *more* to re-derive evicted content), so it evicts at sentence granularity and steers toward conciseness. [SpecGen](https://openreview.net/forum?id=yeqrwcWjPu) is framed around reasoning models shifting the bottleneck to memory. [Locality-Aware Beam Scheduling](https://openreview.net/forum?id=dTo8jAXm9K) targets test-time-compute beam search, where concurrent decode paths make KV, not weights, the bottleneck on consumer GPUs.

**Agents, RAG, and multi-turn made context shared,** so the win is detecting and reusing overlap instead of recomputing prefill. [ContextPilot](https://openreview.net/forum?id=RnKvDy1jv2) indexes overlapping context blocks across users/turns; [FlashAgents](https://openreview.net/forum?id=m14PPUfgEc) streams tokens between agents and dedupes shared instruction templates via a radix-tree prefix cache; [BatchLLM](https://openreview.net/forum?id=IuVHde07l6) makes *global* prefix sharing explicit for offline batch jobs (self-reported up to 10.8× over vLLM/SGLang on microbenchmarks + one industry workload) because LRU eviction drops about-to-be-reused prefixes; [Stream2LLM](https://openreview.net/forum?id=FuRo7Ur5Ib) and [TeleRAG](https://openreview.net/forum?id=YsOyCpMUYD) hide RAG retrieval latency behind compute. The sharpest framing is [Span Queries](https://openreview.net/forum?id=qcGGSXpFcM): chat, RAG, inference-time scaling, and agentic workloads are all special cases of one structure (an expression tree of inference calls with commutativity constraints), so cache locality can be optimized generically — self-reported 10–20× TTFT in 492 lines of vLLM changes.

## 3. Prefill and decode want different machines — disaggregation matures past the hype

Prefill (compute-bound, whole prompt at once) and decode (memory-bound, one token at a time) have opposite resource profiles, so splitting them onto separate instances (PD disaggregation) is now the assumed baseline others build on. This year's contributions are refinements and reality-checks — a sign of a maturing subfield:

- [Beyond the Buzz](https://openreview.net/forum?id=NqC5tcBsa0) is the sobering measurement study (hundreds of thousands of design points, on NVIDIA's Dynamo): disaggregation only wins for prefill-heavy traffic and larger models, and only with dynamic rate matching and elastic scaling. The message: it isn't free.
- [PLA-Serve](https://openreview.net/forum?id=dzjCkSEDyG) notes prefill itself isn't uniform (short vs long prompts bottleneck differently) and disaggregates *within* prefill by length.
- [TriInfer](https://openreview.net/forum?id=nNovi8fvGN) extends two phases to three (encode–prefill–decode) for multimodal, giving the vision encoder its own pool (self-reported 3.7× throughput). Note this is a multimodal, not text-LLM, system.

## 4. MoE broke the dense-serving playbook

MoE is now mainstream for frontier models, and a cluster is cleaning up the fallout: optimizations designed for dense models backfire on MoE. [Demystifying the MoE Serving Tax](https://openreview.net/forum?id=lELxqcgrsN) quantifies it — MoE serves 2–3× worse than a FLOP-equivalent dense model — and finds the counterintuitive result that expert load imbalance *hurts prefill but can help decode* (fewer experts activated). The fixes hit different layers: [Layered Prefill](https://openreview.net/forum?id=yyDbI3HXco) shows standard chunked prefill inflates MoE memory traffic ~39% via redundant expert-weight reloads and re-schedules by *layer groups* instead of token chunks; [CRAFT](https://openreview.net/forum?id=zdRvzU9ZCe) does cost-aware expert replication and shows current schemes *over-replicate*; [FarSkip-Collective](https://openreview.net/forum?id=ruOpvLzsGV) goes furthest — it modifies the architecture (skip connections, recovered by self-distillation to within 1% accuracy on models up to 109B) to overlap the all-to-all expert communication that blocks compute (self-reported 32.6% TTFT on DeepSeek-V3). FarSkip is the one place this cluster relaxes the frozen-weights constraint — it retrains, which raises the adoption bar.

## 5. Speculative decoding: attack the same bottleneck from the compute side

If decode is memory-bound, the GPU's compute sits idle — so spend it guessing tokens ahead and verifying in parallel (the compute-side complement to §1). The draft-quality-vs-draft-cost tension drives the designs: [PRISM](https://openreview.net/forum?id=cvU2HuuxEf) decouples draft-model capacity from draft cost by refactoring parameters across predictive steps; [SpecGen](https://openreview.net/forum?id=yeqrwcWjPu) avoids a separate drafter via self-speculation with a sparse-attention "draft." Notably, **diffusion is entering as the non-autoregressive drafter**: [SpecDiff-2](https://openreview.net/forum?id=o42VU86ZsV) calibrates a discrete-diffusion drafter to an AR verifier, [TiDAR](https://openreview.net/forum?id=onfxEjoE4L) fuses diffusion drafting and AR sampling in one forward pass, and standalone [CDLM](https://openreview.net/forum?id=eB8yjR6alL) makes diffusion LMs practical via consistency distillation + KV-cache compatibility. The essential paper here is the reality-check: [Speculative Decoding: Performance or Illusion?](https://openreview.net/forum?id=fzkqtezFEi) benchmarks SD variants in *production* vLLM and finds large gaps between measured and theoretical speedup at realistic batch sizes — the single most important caveat against taking the other four papers' peak numbers at face value.

## 6. Hardware moved, and the kernel/compiler layer scrambled to keep up (the largest cluster)

This is the biggest group in the set (kernels/compilers 11 + multi-GPU 5 = 16 of 57), and it exists because two hardware pressures multiplied the surface a kernel author must cover.

**Pressure A — new generations rebalanced the on-chip bottleneck.** [FlashAttention-4](https://openreview.net/forum?id=mN5RtvuYl3) is the clearest case: Blackwell's asymmetric scaling (tensor cores doubled, shared-memory bandwidth and exponential units didn't) breaks the assumptions the Hopper-era kernel was built on, so FA-4 redesigns the pipeline around fully-asynchronous MMA and software-emulates the exponential to cut non-matmul work. [TokenWeave](https://openreview.net/forum?id=rh2Ylffkq6) (Microsoft, open-sourced) exploits Hopper/Blackwell NVSHARP/Multimem for a fused AllReduce–RMSNorm in only 2–8 SMs; [SuperInfer](https://openreview.net/forum?id=RuslSHdIHa) co-designs scheduling for GH200's tightly-coupled GPU-CPU NVLink-C2C; [ScaleSearch](https://openreview.net/forum?id=innqECyZPK) exists only because GPUs added native NVFP4/microscaling-BFP support and the default max-magnitude scale wastes accuracy.

**Pressure B — heterogeneity went mainstream.** [HipKittens](https://openreview.net/forum?id=xxSSrndQrI) makes AMD a real target (tile primitives rivaling hand-tuned assembly on CDNA3/4, and *productionalized in AMD's AITER library* — one of the few genuinely-shipped artifacts here); [ParallelKittens](https://openreview.net/forum?id=Cv5e5uRXFb) names the structural cause outright — interconnect bandwidth improving slower than compute — and packages multi-GPU overlap into 8 reusable primitives; [db-SP](https://openreview.net/forum?id=XgKteNxNe0) shows interconnect-bound sequence parallelism needs sparsity-aware load balancing once attention is block-sparse (demonstrated on diffusion transformers; the imbalance analysis generalizes, but note it's not a text-LLM result).

**The shared response: raise the kernel-authoring abstraction** so you don't hand-rewrite per (chip × attention-variant × dynamic-shape) — the combinatorial space these two pressures blew open. This is the through-line that makes the cluster coherent rather than a pile of one-off kernels. [Wave](https://openreview.net/forum?id=gcXV1E8HRH) and [Flashlight](https://openreview.net/forum?id=lboOMA8XWr) are Python/compiler-native kernel DSLs; [Event Tensor](https://openreview.net/forum?id=PJqFhAbUHa) is a compiler abstraction for *dynamic* megakernels (data-dependent shapes that static megakernels can't handle); [DynaFlow](https://openreview.net/forum?id=i0yqC9954S) decouples model definition from execution schedule for intra-device operator overlap. The most forward-looking is [FlashInfer-Bench](https://openreview.net/forum?id=IyryZno8Hh): now that LLMs can *write* GPU kernels, it builds the standardized generate→benchmark→hot-swap-into-vLLM/SGLang loop to make that safe and continuous — the plausible next stage of "who writes the kernel." If there is a second story in this set as big as the KV cache, it is this: kernel authoring is being turned from artisanal per-chip work into a compiled, portable, increasingly auto-generated layer.

## 7. The objective function broadened: energy, edge, cost, reliability

The field is visibly widening past "tokens/sec on an H100."

- **Energy as a first-class objective** (a small but distinct cross-cutting thread, not a one-off): [BEAM](https://openreview.net/forum?id=BfNBXM8CCT) co-optimizes GPU frequency + batch shape to spend post-SLO latency slack on energy (self-reported up to −51% GPU energy); [CORE](https://openreview.net/forum?id=PSyHQ8kVUT) coordinates CPU/GPU/memory frequencies for phone LLMs; [Layered Prefill](https://openreview.net/forum?id=yyDbI3HXco) reports −22% per-token energy as a side benefit.
- **The edge/client as a real target:** [IntAttention](https://openreview.net/forum?id=CPCRITwAaP) removes the FP-softmax detour that dominates INT8 attention on Arm CPUs (fully-integer pipeline); [Efficient VRAM-Constrained xLM Inference](https://openreview.net/forum?id=VKqQYg6JPb) does CPU-GPU pipelined sharding for NVIDIA's client SDK; [Locality-Aware Beam Scheduling](https://openreview.net/forum?id=dTo8jAXm9K) targets consumer GPUs.
- **Cost, operability, reliability:** [BOute](https://openreview.net/forum?id=ZVQb92umqX) co-optimizes query routing + heterogeneous-GPU deployment via Bayesian optimization; [OptiKIT](https://openreview.net/forum?id=om4H7AI2hc) democratizes optimization for non-expert enterprise teams; [MorphServe](https://openreview.net/forum?id=PDu13oOl4G) treats quantization as a *runtime knob*, hot-swapping layers to quantized versions under load and reusing freed memory for KV; [HELIOS](https://openreview.net/forum?id=CV52m9NJFK) treats depth as the runtime knob (multiple early-exit models, load only likely-used layers); [FaaScale](https://openreview.net/forum?id=jgL8LuOVyT) tackles serverless cold-scale; [AIRS](https://openreview.net/forum?id=g1RWik4Gy1) is Google's production LLM-rating pipeline under a fixed TPU budget; [Breaking the Ice](https://openreview.net/forum?id=eoEobeKTNZ) profiles vLLM cold-start latency (CPU-bound, predictable).

Two more genuinely orthogonal papers: [Attribution-based Sparse Activation](https://openreview.net/forum?id=gJFigZeb5D) (skip low-attribution FFN neurons per input, training-free — the same "skip work that doesn't matter" instinct as the KV-sparsity papers, applied to the MLP), and [Shannonic](https://openreview.net/forum?id=NhMxI0GbB8) (near-Shannon-limit *lossless* tensor compression for bandwidth-bound edge-cloud transfer).

## Maturity: what's shipping vs what's a prototype

Independent of topic, the papers fall on a readiness spectrum — the axis most useful for deciding what to adopt vs. what to watch. The abstracts support three tiers:

- **Deployed at scale (named production systems).** [Optimizing Deployment Configurations](https://openreview.net/forum?id=gEbKQeIdxB) (Meta, Llama at ~1B MAU) and [Semantic Job Search](https://openreview.net/forum?id=re82zZczHj) (LinkedIn, millions QPS) are experience reports, not prototypes; [fabric-lib](https://openreview.net/forum?id=SjVa05wEiY) runs three production systems at Perplexity; [HipKittens](https://openreview.net/forum?id=xxSSrndQrI) is productionalized in AMD's AITER; [AIRS](https://openreview.net/forum?id=g1RWik4Gy1) is a live Google pipeline; [OptiKIT](https://openreview.net/forum?id=om4H7AI2hc) reports in-production throughput gains. These are the safest to trust and hardest to generalize (their numbers are workload-specific).
- **Industrial-grade, integrated with real tooling** (open-sourced, evaluated on real hardware/traces, plausibly deployable soon): [FlashAttention-4](https://openreview.net/forum?id=mN5RtvuYl3), [TokenWeave](https://openreview.net/forum?id=rh2Ylffkq6) (Microsoft), [BatchLLM](https://openreview.net/forum?id=IuVHde07l6) (Microsoft), [Beyond the Buzz](https://openreview.net/forum?id=NqC5tcBsa0) / [FlashInfer-Bench](https://openreview.net/forum?id=IyryZno8Hh) (built on NVIDIA Dynamo / SGLang+vLLM), the client-inference work targeting NVIDIA's SDK.
- **Research prototypes** (the majority): open code in many cases, but validated on benchmarks/traces rather than deployment — most of the KV, spec-decoding, and MoE algorithm papers. Their "up to X" figures are the least safe to extrapolate; the [Performance or Illusion?](https://openreview.net/forum?id=fzkqtezFEi) result (measured ≪ theoretical at production batch sizes) is the standing warning for this tier.

The reader's takeaway: the KV-cache and spec-decoding clusters are where the *ideas* are densest, but the *production-proven* work concentrates in scheduling/deployment-config, kernels, and communication — the boring-but-shipped end.

## What shifted — and how confident we can be

The bullets below mix two kinds of claim. Those about **this year's set** are grounded in the 57 abstracts. Those about **change over time** are not — this report has no prior-year MLSys data in hand, so year-over-year statements are informed conjecture from general knowledge, labeled **[conjecture]**. Both are useful; conflating them is not.

- **The KV cache is the largest single object of study this year** (~15–20 of 57). *Grounded in this set.*
- **Weight quantization is nearly absent as a standalone thesis** — within these 57, only mixed-precision weight quant ([MixLLM](https://openreview.net/forum?id=VBbMRQ4VOc): bits allocated by output-feature importance, Llama-3.1-70B MMLU-Pro loss 1.92→0.99 at +10% bits), KV-quant ([Kitty](https://openreview.net/forum?id=r3mQiuYKIN)), and format scale-search ([ScaleSearch](https://openreview.net/forum?id=innqECyZPK)) survive, and quant mostly appears as a *runtime component* ([MorphServe](https://openreview.net/forum?id=PDu13oOl4G)). *Grounded in this set — but see the caveat:* this is **very likely a venue-routing artifact**, not a field decline. A pure weight-quant algorithm targets NeurIPS/ICML/ICLR, not a systems venue; its near-absence at MLSys says where such work is submitted, not that it stopped. **[conjecture]** that the field overall has moved on.
- **"Systems co-design over pure algorithms" is table stakes here** — nearly every paper ships an engine integration. *Grounded in this set*, but again **largely a venue selection effect** (MLSys has always selected this way), so it is weak evidence of a field-wide *shift*. **[conjecture]** as a trend.
- **Reality-check / measurement papers are a visible presence** — [Beyond the Buzz](https://openreview.net/forum?id=NqC5tcBsa0), [Performance or Illusion?](https://openreview.net/forum?id=fzkqtezFEi), [Demystifying the MoE Tax](https://openreview.net/forum?id=lELxqcgrsN) all audit hyped techniques at production scale. *Grounded in this set that they exist;* **[conjecture]** that they're more common than in prior years.
- **Reasoning + agentic workloads are the driver behind the KV/prefix/long-decode work.** The papers themselves cite reasoning-model CoT length and agent/RAG context sharing as motivation (*grounded*). That this workload "barely existed two years ago" and will dominate next year is **[conjecture]**.
