* The River of Problems

A 7-billion-parameter model, squeezed down to INT4 and tucked inside your laptop — it runs now, but it has "died." You want to teach it something new and find you simply cannot reach in: every parameter is an integer between 0 and 15, and the gradients that backpropagation produces land nowhere on that grid.

Everyone on this river is chasing the same thing: *how do you train a model that cannot be backpropagated through?* Early on the motivation was saving memory; later it was avoiding GPU clusters; by 2026 the problem had been pushed to its logical extreme — *the model itself is discrete, and there are simply no "gradients" to use*. Each generation swapped in a new flavor of "gradient-free optimization," yet each generation was tripped up by fresh trouble: too slow, too noisy, too memory-hungry, or stubbornly unable to learn on the reasoning tasks that matter most.

QES is the latest stone in this river. To understand where it comes from, we must trace back to the paper OpenAI published nine years ago — "replace backpropagation with evolution."

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | OpenAI ES (Salimans et al.): Training Neural Nets Without Computing a Single Gradient

*** What problem it saw

In 2017, training robots to play games meant reinforcement learning — but RL was painful: it required gradient computation, rewards were sparse and noisy, and synchronizing parameters across hundreds of GPUs was a mess. The OpenAI team had had enough: could they skip gradients entirely?

*** Its solution

Treat a neural network's millions of parameters as a single vector. Scatter a ring of Gaussian-noise perturbations around it (say, 1,000 of them), run a full game episode for each perturbation to collect a reward, then do something almost laughably simple: *"whichever direction got the higher reward is the good direction — take a step that way."* No backprop, no chain rule — just "perturb, score, weighted average."

This is really the "evolution strategies" idea that is over 100 years old; nobody had dared apply it to neural networks before. OpenAI showed that *as long as you can parallelize enough perturbations*, the method holds its own against policy-gradient algorithms.

*** Why it worked (and what pit it dug)

It works because spreading perturbations widely averages out variance. The cost is *you must scatter thousands of perturbations*, each requiring a full forward pass. Tolerable for RL tasks, but — looking back seven years later — that overhead does not quite fit LLMs.

There was also a silent assumption: *parameters are continuous floats*, so perturbations can be arbitrarily small. Once a model is compressed to INT4, that assumption collapses entirely. But that is a story for later.

** 2024 | MeZO (Malladi et al.): Making ES Work for LLM Fine-tuning

*** What problem it saw

By 2024 the situation had inverted: everyone wanted to fine-tune billion-parameter models like LLaMA and Llama-2. Standard backpropagation demands *3–12× the parameter count* in memory — because activations, optimizer states, and Adam's two momentum buffers all need to be stored. Ordinary researchers simply could not afford it.

The "no-gradient" idea from OpenAI ES was tempting. But there was an engineering snag: storing even one perturbation vector for a 10B-parameter model was enormous; store 100 of them and memory explodes.

*** Its solution

MeZO pulled one clever trick — *random seeds*. Instead of storing the perturbation vector, it stores only the random seed that generates it. Whenever the perturbation is needed, regenerate it from the same seed.

Memory consumption collapses to *exactly the same as inference*. A consumer GPU can fine-tune a 30B model.

*** Why it worked (and what pit it dug)

Seed replay — remember that name; QES will borrow and extend it.

But MeZO left two pits: (1) at its core it is ZO-SGD (using only one or two perturbations per gradient estimate), so variance is high and *it learns slowly on complex reasoning tasks*; (2) it still assumes continuous float parameters.

** 2025 | Qiu et al.: True ES, Not ZO-SGD — and It Scales to LLMs

*** What problem it saw

Qiu et al. looked at MeZO and said: what you have is ZO-SGD, not real ES. True ES uses a *population* — hundreds of perturbations per gradient estimate, giving far lower variance. MeZO had regressed to "one or two perturbations at a time" to save memory, which is why it struggled with reasoning.

*** Its solution

Bring back the large-population mechanism from classic OpenAI ES, but use MeZO's seed-replay trick to keep memory in check. Result: *on LLMs, ES genuinely matches — or beats — RL methods (including GRPO)*, especially in reward-sparse settings like mathematical reasoning.

*** Why it worked (and what pit it dug)

It moved "ES is suitable for LLM reasoning" from conjecture to fact. But it was still playing in FP16 parameter space. Once a model is quantized to INT4, its perturbation mechanism breaks just the same — because *the perturbation magnitude is smaller than one quantization grid step*, so adding it changes nothing.

** 2025 | QuZO (Zhou et al.): First to Move ZO into Quantized Space

*** What problem it saw

After PTQ (GPTQ/AWQ) compresses a model to INT4, the model becomes a "specimen" — usable but immutable. To train it further, your options were: QAT (requires STE to approximate gradients, unstable) or QLoRA (hang a FP16 adapter on frozen quantized weights, sidestepping true quantized-weight training). Neither path had actually succeeded at "directly modifying quantized weights."

QuZO's reasoning was direct: MeZO skips gradient computation entirely — so port it to quantized space and *bypass STE and backpropagation*.

*** Its solution

Introduce *stochastic rounding* — after adding a perturbation, apply probabilistic rounding so an INT4 weight can "with some probability" flip to its neighboring value. This gives ZO at least a foothold to move on the discrete grid.

It worked on supervised fine-tuning (SFT) tasks.

*** Why it worked (and what pit it dug)

It established the principle that "quantized space + zeroth-order optimization" can work. But on *reasoning tasks* (math problems, Countdown), it barely learned at all. On Qwen2.5-1.5B INT4, accuracy climbed only from 3.5% to 5.25% — essentially unchanged.

QES later diagnosed the cause, calling it the *stagnation problem*: the reward signal is sparse; the estimated gradient multiplied by the learning rate has a *magnitude smaller than one INT4 grid interval (the quantization precision)*. Every step rounds to zero. The model does not move.

** 2026 | QES (This Paper): Accumulating Vanished Gradients with a Delta-Sigma Idea

*** What problem it saw

QuZO's bottleneck is physical: *your step is smaller than the grid — you cannot walk*. This is not a flaw in the optimization algorithm; discretization is eating the signal.

QES's authors found the answer in an unexpected place: *1960s signal processing*. Specifically, Delta-Sigma modulation in audio DACs — the same problem of "restoring a continuous signal from a coarse discrete output" that engineers solved sixty years ago.

*** Its solution

Two things:

1. *Accumulated Error Feedback*. Each step computes a desired "high-precision update" =u_t=, but the only update actually applied is the rounded discrete step =ΔW_t=. The leftover fractional part =e_t = u_t - ΔW_t= is *not discarded — it is saved and added to the next step*. Small signals accumulate over dozens of steps until they are finally large enough to cross a quantization grid boundary and trigger a discrete update — just as Delta-Sigma modulation restores a continuous signal from 1-bit output "along the time dimension." This shares its mathematical structure with 1-bit SGD and error-feedback SGD in distributed training, and is applied here for the first time to the problem of optimization in quantized space.

2. *Stateless Seed Replay*. But (1) has an engineering problem — =e_t= is a FP16 high-precision vector; storing it costs more memory than the quantized model itself, erasing the entire benefit of quantization. QES upgrades MeZO's seed-replay idea: *store only the random seeds and rewards from the most recent K steps* (a few kilobytes), then "recompute" the error state on demand. Memory complexity drops from O(d) to O(K), where d is on the order of 10^9 and K only needs to be 50.

*** Why it worked (and what pits remain)

The numbers speak clearly: on Qwen2.5-1.5B INT4, base 3.50%, QuZO 5.25%, QES *18.00%*. On the 3B model, QES 31.85% vs QuZO 14.25% — a direct doubling.

The deeper insight is in Section 5's *temporal equivalence* proof: the gap between QES's quantized weights =W_T= and what full-precision FP16 gradient descent would produce at step T (=Θ_T=) is always bounded by *half a quantization grid step*. In other words, *quantization is only a surface layer; the optimization trajectory inside is mathematically equivalent to full-precision training*.

Remaining pits: (1) K and the decay rate γ still require manual tuning; (2) only validated on INT4/INT8/W8A8 — FP4 and binary quantization are next; (3) each update requires K reconstruction passes, so wall-clock time is slow and there is engineering headroom to be reclaimed.

* Frontier Extensions

As of February 2026, QES is the latest advance on this line. From the paper's Future Work section, three directions seem most likely:

1. *More aggressive quantization*. INT4 is today's sweet spot, but binary (1-bit) and FP4 are the next frontier for squeezing models to their limit. The Delta-Sigma idea in QES may actually work *better* on sparser quantization grids — because 1-bit DACs in signal processing use exactly this trick.

2. *Adaptive K and γ*. Currently tuned by hand based on convergence status — an obvious knob waiting to be automated.

3. *A paradigm flip in scale*. The paper's final sentence is ambitious: *on the same hardware, where you once trained an N-parameter FP16 model, you could instead train a 4N-parameter INT4 model* — trading precision for parameter count. If this path proves out, the barrier to "training large models" drops by another order of magnitude.

* Insights

On the surface this line is "iterative refinement of optimization algorithms." What is actually happening underneath is *a role reversal between precision and parameter count*.

For thirty years the unstated premise of training neural networks was: *precision (FP32/FP16) is a given, and parameter count is the resource to be saved*. So gradient descent, Adam, and everything built on top rests on the assumption that "parameters are continuous floats."

The line that QES closes out by 2026 has flipped that premise: *precision is also a resource to be saved; quantization is the default; and "being able to train a quantized model" has become basic infrastructure*. Once quantized training works, the ceiling on "how large a model you can train on given hardware" gets redrawn — precision can be exchanged for parameter count, starting at 4×.

A subtler insight: *Delta-Sigma modulation + 1-bit SGD + accumulated error feedback* — these three things are actually the same thing, appearing in 1962 (signal processing), 2014 (distributed training), and 2026 (quantized optimization). Each generation borrowed the same hammer from a neighbor's toolbox to drive its own nail. The river's suggestion is: *when you are stuck on "signal too small, discretization eating it," the first instinct should be to reach for an old signal-processing textbook*.

* Implications

Concrete take-aways for research and problem-finding:

1. *Classify the bottleneck first, then look for cross-domain references*. QuZO does not fail outright — it gets stuck at stagnation. And "small signal eaten by discretization" is a problem with sixty years of research behind it. The next time your method stalls at a specific point, first ask "what does this bottleneck look like in the abstract, and which other field has seen it?" then go read how that field solved it. The reason Delta-Sigma can solve an optimization problem is precisely this rule.

2. *Hold one engineering invariant firm and let everything else flex*. MeZO's invariant was "memory = inference cost," and every trick orbited it. QES inherited that invariant; accumulated error feedback superficially violates it (one extra FP16 vector), so seed replay was invented to restore it. *Having a non-negotiable engineering target actually forces genuine algorithmic innovation*.

3. *Read papers backwards — it's far faster than reading forwards*. This river spans nine years from QES back to OpenAI ES. Reading only QES, you get lost in "Delta-Sigma"; reading only OpenAI ES, you think it has nothing to do with LLMs. But tracing backwards from QES, each paper naturally answers the open question left by the one before — *the evolution of the problem chain is the true skeleton of the field; individual papers are just flesh on the bones*.
