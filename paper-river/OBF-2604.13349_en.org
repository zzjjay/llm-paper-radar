* Opening in One Line

What exactly should multi-agent systems transmit to each other? The answer has evolved from "pass some text" to "pass the entire head state" to "pass only the most useful slice of the head state." OBF, the paper under review, stands at the far end of this trajectory and makes a counterintuitive claim: transmitting less may actually transmit more.

* Stop 1 ~ The Starting Point: The Most Natural Way for Multi-Agents to Communicate — Plain Language

How was the idea of wiring multiple large models into one system first approached? The most straightforward answer: let them talk to each other.

*CAMEL* (Li et al., 2023b) and *MetaGPT* (Hong et al., 2024) are the emblematic works here. Each agent is assigned a role — say, one plays product manager and another plays engineer — and they converse in natural language. Through turn-taking, complex tasks are broken into collaborative subtasks. The approach has been validated repeatedly: for coding, math, and mutual review, multi-agent collaboration frequently outperforms a single agent.

But there is a hidden trap: *translating what is in your head into words is itself lossy*.

Consider this. An agent is thinking: "This problem could be A or B, but I lean toward A because of X — though X is not very reliable." When it compresses that thought into a message, it writes: "I believe the answer is A." All the hesitation, the alternatives, the varying strengths of evidence — gone. The next agent receives a definite sentence, not a cloud of uncertain inner reasoning.

This is the critique levelled at this line of work by later researchers: natural-language communication forces the model's internal state through a discrete-token bottleneck, *turning something fuzzy into something definite*.

* Stop 2 ~ Why Not Pass Internal States Directly: The Embedding and Latent Camps

If speaking in words is lossy, then stop speaking in words.

A wave of 2024–2025 work shifted toward "passing more primitive things":

- *CIPHER* (Pham et al., 2024): agents exchange not tokens but "embeddings of intended outputs." The idea is to keep "what I mean to say" in vector space and avoid the round-trip to text.
- *COCONUT* (Hao et al., 2025): reasoning happens entirely in latent space; even intermediate steps are never decoded.
- *StateDelta* (Tang et al., 2025): language output is supplemented with changes in hidden state.

The shared intuition of this wave: *don't let information pass through the narrow gate of tokens*. Passing through the gate flattens it; bypassing the gate preserves it.

But there is one further step this line of work can take. Embeddings and hidden states are still "semi-finished" internal representations. The most complete record of a model's "chain of thought" is the KV cache — the key-value pairs stored by the attention mechanism, covering every layer and every token. That is the full physical substrate of what the model has truly "remembered."

* Stop 3 ~ The Extreme Version: Shipping the Entire KV Cache

*LatentMAS* (Zou et al., 2025) pushes this logic all the way: after agent A finishes, it hands its entire KV cache directly to agent B. B does not start by reading A's output; it picks up where A's "brain state" left off.

This is the highest-fidelity communication yet: every layer, every token, every attention trace from A — B receives all of it. There is no "spoken aloud and flattened" step at all.

But the cost surfaces immediately: *KV caches are massive*. For a moderately long prompt on a mid-sized model, the KV cache can run into hundreds of megabytes or even gigabytes. Stack multiple agents, multiple turns, and long prompts together, and transmission and storage costs explode. If LatentMAS is ever to be practical, the problem of oversized KV must be solved.

At this point a new question moves to center stage: *how do we compress the KV transmitted between agents?*

* Stop 4 ~ A Parallel Track: Every Trick the Single-Agent World Has for Shrinking KV

As it happens, "KV is too large" is a problem the single-agent long-context community has been fighting for years, and it has already accumulated a toolbox.

First wave: *selective retention*.

- *StreamingLLM* (Xiao et al., 2024): keep the first few attention-sink tokens plus a recent window; discard everything in between.
- *H2O* (Zhang et al., 2023): score tokens by their cumulative historical attention weight; keep the heavy hitters, drop the rest.
- *Scissorhands* (Liu et al., 2023b): similar idea, evicting by hit frequency.

The shared assumption of this wave: *only a small fraction of tokens are repeatedly attended to; dropping the rest has little impact on generation quality*.

But dropping is dropping — the information in evicted tokens is simply gone. So a second wave emerged to compensate for evictions:

- *RazorAttention* (Tang et al., 2024): summarize the evicted context into a few "compensation tokens" and reinsert them.
- *CaM* (Zhang et al., 2024) and *WeightedKV* (Yuan et al., 2025): rather than discarding the dropped KV, merge it into the retained KV.

The intuition of this wave: hard eviction is too brutal; at least let the evicted tokens leave a shadow behind.

It sounds as if this toolbox could be plugged straight into LatentMAS. But the OBF authors point out: *you cannot do that directly*.

* Stop 5 ~ Where the Two Tracks Merge: What OBF Sees That Others Missed

The key insight of the OBF paper is essentially a pointed question:

#+begin_quote
H2O, StreamingLLM, and similar KV-compression methods were designed for "the same model continuing to generate."
Their criterion for "which token matters" is its contribution to *that model's own* next attention step.
But in LatentMAS, the retained KV will be consumed by *a different agent*.
What is useful to oneself is not necessarily useful to someone else.
#+end_quote

In other words, every existing KV-compression method is optimizing against the wrong target.

This observation leads to OBF's two moves:

** 5.1 Reframing the Problem: From "Compression" to "Communication"

OBF recasts the task as a communication problem: *within a fixed transmission budget, retain whatever KV state is most useful to the downstream agent* — whether those KV entries remain useful to the sender is irrelevant.

This reframing is itself a contribution: before LatentMAS, KV compression was never studied as a communication problem.

** 5.2 The Concrete Technique: Orthogonal Backfill (OBF)

OBF addresses the information loss caused by hard eviction. Intuitively:

1. Select a set of "retained KV" and a set of "to-be-evicted KV," as H2O does.
2. Project the "to-be-evicted KV" onto the directions that the "retained KV" cannot express — i.e., their *orthogonal residuals*.
3. Compute a low-rank approximation of this residual, extracting the most significant directions.
4. Treat these directions as a correction term and add them back into the values of the "retained KV."

By analogy: you are packing for a trip and your suitcase cannot hold everything. The conventional approach is to leave behind the less important items. OBF's approach is: extract from the discarded items the parts that the kept items cannot substitute for, and stitch those parts onto the kept items so they travel together.

*Why orthogonal residuals*: because the retained KV already spans certain information directions. The portion of the evicted KV that overlaps with the retained KV need not be carried separately. What truly needs to be preserved is what the retained KV cannot represent at all — information that lives in the orthogonal subspace.

** 5.3 Experiments

Nine benchmarks covering mathematical reasoning, code, and knowledge QA.

- Communication cost reduced by 79.8% to 89.4%.
- Performance matches or exceeds full-KV relay.
- OBF achieves the best results on 7 of the 9 benchmarks.

The fact that OBF *wins* on 7 benchmarks matters more than merely "matching" full KV. It means transmitting less not only avoids degradation — it actually improves outcomes.

* Stop 6 ~ What Is Really Happening Beneath This Trajectory

Reading these five stops together, what looks on the surface like "evolution of compression techniques" is actually something else happening at a deeper level:

*The fundamental understanding of what "communication" means shifts step by step — from "transmitting things" to "making what arrives useful to the receiver."*

| Stage | Essence of Communication | Evaluation Criterion |
|---|---|---|
| CAMEL / MetaGPT | "What did I say?" | Informational completeness |
| LatentMAS | "I hand over my entire state" | Fidelity |
| OBF | "What you receive is useful for your next step" | Downstream usability |

The most interesting reversal is the one OBF delivers: *more information does not equal better communication*. This resonates deeply with human experience — a good teacher does not dump everything they know onto a student, but selects what the student can absorb right now.

* Stop 7 ~ Where the Line Might Go Next

Projecting one or two steps further along this trajectory:

1. *Training-aware compression*. OBF is training-free. The natural next step is to let the downstream agent learn during training which upstream KV signals it "likes," allowing compression and receiver to be co-optimized.
2. *Task-adaptive budgets*. OBF uses the same compression ratio across all nine benchmarks, but the KV dependency of a math problem differs greatly from that of a retrieval problem. Future work will likely produce schemes that dynamically adjust the retention budget per task or per query.
3. *Accumulated loss across multi-hop agent chains*. The paper validates only single-hop relay. If agents form a chain A → B → C → D, with compression at every hop, does error accumulate? This remains an untouched question.
4. *Formalizing "useful to the receiver"*. The orthogonal-residual approach is an engineering intuition. What the theoretically optimal compression looks like — one that maximizes downstream utility — is still an open question. The answer may lie in the direction of Information Bottleneck theory.
