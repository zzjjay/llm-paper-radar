* The River of the Problem

Can a highly capable AI model shed half its weight without getting dumber?

That sounds like a diet-pill ad, but for Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models it is a genuine engineering question. MoE's design philosophy is "keep a large crowd of experts; wake up only a few for each job"—so the total parameter count is enormous (eating VRAM), yet each inference pass touches only a small fraction (saving compute). The problem: housing that crowd is too expensive, and deployment memory runs out. Hence the idea: can we prune the useless experts away?

The difficulty lies in a question nobody can cleanly answer: *where exactly does a model's capability live?* Is it spread evenly across every expert, concentrated in a handful of them, or hiding at some finer granularity? Without knowing the answer you cannot know what to cut. Cut the wrong thing and the model suddenly "forgets"—a model that could solve math problems can no longer handle elementary word problems after pruning.

This river flows from "how do we decide whether a single neuron matters" thirty years ago all the way to "how do we subtract from a crowd of experts" today. Each generation answers a different version of the same question: *at what scale does importance actually live?*

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1992 | OBS: When pruning neurons, ask "does it hurt to remove?" not "how big is it?"

*** What problem did it see?

The most naive pruning idea is: small weights are unimportant, remove them. This is called magnitude pruning. Hassibi and Stork found it frequently goes wrong—a tiny weight might sit at a critical junction in the network, and deleting it collapses everything. Magnitude simply does not equal importance.

*** Its solution

Switch the criterion: don't ask "how large is this weight?" ask "how much does removing it raise the loss?" That increase can be computed from the second-order derivative of the loss (the Hessian matrix). Intuitively, the Hessian describes how "steep" the loss surface is near that weight—a flat surface means the weight can be changed freely and is safe to delete; a steep surface means it is untouchable.

*** Why it works (and what new pit it digs)

For the first time, "importance" was redefined from a parameter's own property (its magnitude) to its effect on the output (how much it hurts to remove). That idea has lived for thirty years.

But the limitation is obvious: the Hessian is an N×N matrix where N is the number of parameters. Today's models have tens of billions of parameters; this matrix cannot be computed, let alone inverted. Successors have therefore always sought cheap approximations—the most common being to approximate the Hessian with the squared gradient (empirical Fisher information). Remember that word "Fisher": it will become the protagonist of the target paper thirty years later.

** 2022 | Switch Transformer: Split one big brain into a crowd of experts

*** What problem did it see?

This paper is not strictly on the pruning thread, but it built the thing that everyone later tries to prune, so it must come first. The bottleneck at the time was: making a model smarter means making it bigger, but bigger means running all parameters on every forward pass, which is computationally unsustainable.

*** Its solution

Replace the single shared feed-forward layer (FFN) in a Transformer with a collection of FFNs called "experts," plus a "router" that sends each token to exactly one expert (top-1 routing). The result: total parameter count can increase by tens of times (huge capacity), but each token only passes through one expert (compute unchanged).

*** Why it works (and what new pit it digs)

It decoupled "parameter count" from "compute"—that is MoE's core magic. But it also quietly planted a lasting cognitive bias: from this point on, "expert" became the self-evident atomic unit of MoE. Every subsequent attempt to compress MoE started with the reflex "let's delete some experts." That seemingly natural assumption—*capability is organized by expert*—is exactly what the target paper will overturn.

** 2024 | SEER-MoE: Whoever gets called the least, gets cut

*** What problem did it see?

There are too many experts, and VRAM cannot hold the whole model. Since each token uses only one expert, some experts must be idle—cut them and nothing changes. But how do you identify the idle ones?

*** Its solution

Count. Run a calibration dataset and record how many times each expert is activated by the router. Low-frequency experts are declared redundant and deleted entirely. A small regularized fine-tuning pass recovers any lost accuracy.

*** Why it works (and what new pit it digs)

Simple and effective at saving VRAM. But it carries two hidden hazards that later work will expose one by one. First, "called rarely" does not mean "unimportant"—some experts are rare-disease specialists: seldom summoned, but critical when needed; pruning by frequency kills such specialists. Second, and more fundamentally: it assumes that deletion must operate at the whole-expert granularity.

** 2024 | STUN: Cluster first, delete by cluster, then fine-prune inside survivors

*** What problem did it see?

SEER's "try every combination of experts to delete" approach collapses on large models—n experts produce an exponential search space requiring O(k^n/√n) forward passes. Snowflake Arctic has 128 experts; exhaustive search is hopeless.

*** Its solution

STUN's insight: experts have latent structure. Many experts behave nearly identically (co-activation patterns, similar routing weights)—they form natural clusters. So cluster experts by behavioral similarity and keep one representative per cluster; this collapses the exponential search to O(1). This is the "structured" pruning step. Then apply fine-grained "unstructured" pruning (weight-level) inside the surviving experts. Coarse first, fine second—hence Structured-Then-UNstructured.

*** Why it works (and what new pit it digs)

It is the first method in MoE to combine expert-level (coarse, structured) and weight-level (fine, unstructured) pruning, hinting at something important: *operating only at the expert granularity is insufficient; you need to go finer.* But its fine-grained step is unstructured—it zeros out individual weights while the matrix shape stays the same. Real memory and throughput savings require structurally removing an entire block, not just scattering zeros. Zeroing does not truly accelerate inference.

** 2024 | MoE-Pruner: Go inside each expert and score every weight

*** What problem did it see?

Deleting entire experts is too blunt—even a useless expert contains useful weights; even a useful expert contains dead weight. Granularity should be finer.

*** Its solution

Borrow Wanda's idea (weight magnitude × input activation), then multiply by MoE-specific routing scores, producing a score per weight: S = |W| · ‖X · Gate‖. Weights with the lowest scores are pruned. One-shot, no retraining required.

*** Why it works (and what new pit it digs)

It pushes the battlefield from "expert" to "individual weight," and cleverly folds routing information into the criterion. But it shares the same flaw as STUN's fine-grained step: this is unstructured pruning. Weights are zeroed, matrix shapes are unchanged. The number of parameters actually activated per token does not decrease, so inference is not meaningfully faster or cheaper. Moreover, its criterion (magnitude × activation × routing score) is still a collection of heuristic proxies—it never answers the fundamental question: at what scale does capability actually reside?

** 2026 | Fisher-MoE: Capability lives not in experts but in FFN intermediate dimensions

*** What problem did it see?

The target paper looks back at this entire chain and identifies two things everyone has overlooked.

First, *the wrong scale*. From SEER to STUN, everyone has assumed "expert" is the natural compression unit. But the authors run an experiment: in Qwen1.5-MoE, all routed FFNs together have 1.35 million intermediate dimensions; deleting just 12 of them collapses GSM8K (math) accuracy—while factual-knowledge benchmarks are barely affected. What does this tell us? That "the ability to do math" is not owned by any single expert; it is *spread across all experts yet highly concentrated in a tiny number of FFN intermediate dimensions.* Pruning at the expert granularity is like performing microsurgery with a cleaver—removing one expert inevitably discards those few critical dimensions along with a heap of useless ones. The granularity is simply wrong.

Second, *the criteria are crude proxies.* Activation frequency, routing score, weight magnitude—all three classes of importance measures used by prior work are poor approximations of true parameter importance.

There is also an overlooked practical detail: deleting whole experts only reduces total parameter count (VRAM footprint), but the number of parameters activated per token is unchanged, so inference speed does not fundamentally improve.

*** Its solution

Change the scale. Change the criterion.

Change the scale: instead of deleting experts, prune FFN *intermediate dimensions.* Each expert's FFN consists of gate/up/down projections with a d_ff-dimensional hidden layer in between. Fisher-MoE removes entire rows and columns of that hidden layer (rows of W^gate and W^up, columns of W^down), reducing d_ff by roughly half—*without deleting a single expert.* Every expert becomes leaner. Because entire structural dimensions are removed rather than zeroed, the matrices genuinely shrink, yielding ~45% VRAM reduction and ~21% throughput improvement that zero-masking approaches cannot achieve.

Change the criterion: use empirical Fisher importance—the cheap approximation of OBS's second-order derivative from thirty years ago, computed as the squared gradient of the log-likelihood (derived from a second-order Taylor expansion of the KL divergence). Weight-level scores are then aggregated to the level of each intermediate dimension: for dimension j, combine the gradient contributions from the associated gate, up, and down positions into a single score. Dimensions with the lowest scores are pruned.

The authors are explicit: their contribution is *not* Fisher itself (that is decades old), but the choice of "attributable intermediate dimension" as the compression unit.

*** Why it works (and what new pit it digs)

Back to the core question—where does capability live? This paper's answer: *not at the expert granularity, but at the finer granularity of intermediate dimensions.* Once the scale is right, OBS's thirty-year-old criterion (Fisher / second-order) immediately revives and cleanly outperforms a roster of expert-level heuristics. The whole river has come full circle—back to the wisdom at the source, applied at the correct scale.

New pits dug: the paper offers three dimension-budget allocation rules (per-expert IntDim-E, per-layer IntDim-L, and global IntDim-G), but which is optimal and why remains unsettled. And whether "intermediate dimension" is the terminal scale—whether there exists an even more faithful attribution unit—remains an open question.

* Frontier Extension

Nearly simultaneously, the Cerebras team's REAP (2026, ICLR) pushed this debate from a different angle. At the time, a voice was emerging in the community: instead of *deleting* experts, why not *merge* them (average similar experts into one)? On classification-style tasks this seemed appealing. REAP refuted it theoretically: merging causes "functional subspace collapse"—once experts are merged, the router loses its ability to fine-tune its selection per input, introducing irrecoverable error. Deletion, by contrast, preserves the functional structure of the surviving experts. So on generative tasks (code, math), deletion > merging.

The interesting contrast is this: REAP and Fisher-MoE independently arrived at a shared subtext—*crudely manipulating whole experts, whether by deletion or merging, carries a cost.* But their remedies diverge: REAP stays at the expert granularity and does deletion more carefully (using conditional means weighted by routing scores × activation norms to avoid killing rare specialists); Fisher-MoE escapes the expert granularity entirely and descends to intermediate dimensions. One perfects the original scale; the other switches scales. This is precisely why the line has not converged—everyone agrees the old approach is flawed, but "what is the right scale?" is far from settled.

* Insights

On the surface this river is about "how to compress MoE." Beneath it, what is actually changing is a question of understanding: *by what unit is a model's capability organized?*

- Early work (OBS) asked "is this individual parameter important?"—the criterion was right (second-order), but the scale was too fine and computationally intractable.
- Middle work (SEER/STUN/MoE-Pruner) was led astray by Switch's architecture: since the architecture carves the model into "experts," compression naturally operates by expert. This is an *assumption shaped by the tool*—because the expert scalpel existed, people assumed capability grew along expert boundaries.
- Fisher-MoE's real contribution is not inventing a new criterion (Fisher is from 1992) but *overturning the assumption*—using one counter-intuitive experiment (deleting 12 out of 1.35 million dimensions crashes GSM8K) to prove that the granularity of capability is not the expert but the finer intermediate dimension. Once the scale is right, a thirty-year-old criterion instantly revives.

The deepest lesson of this entire line: *what often blocks a field is not "the criterion is imperfect" but "the unit was chosen wrong."* Everyone was polishing the criterion at the wrong scale (frequency → routing score → magnitude)—searching for keys at the wrong table.

Where does it go next? Almost certainly further pursuit of "what is the correct attribution unit?" Is intermediate dimension the endpoint? Could the right unit be some kind of "capability circuit" shared across experts and layers? And Fisher-MoE's unresolved three-way allocation contest (E/L/G) is, at its core, asking whether dimensional importance is local or global—yet another scale question. The river is still flowing.

* Takeaways

For research practice, this line offers one sharp reminder: *when an entire field is busy improving criteria and methods but nobody questions the "basic unit," the opportunity is usually hiding in that unquestioned assumption.*

- When you see a cluster of papers competing over "whose importance score is more accurate" (SEER vs STUN vs MoE-Pruner), don't rush to join the scoring contest. Step back and ask: is the *object* they are scoring (the expert) even the right thing to score? Fisher-MoE surpassed all of them by changing the object, not the score.
- Old methods are not necessarily obsolete—they may just be applied in the wrong place. OBS's second-order criterion was set aside in 1992 as "computationally infeasible." Fisher-MoE moved it to the correct scale (intermediate dimensions) and it worked immediately. *Rather than inventing a new wheel, fit the old wheel onto the right vehicle.*
- A single counter-intuitive destructive experiment is more convincing than ten positive benchmarks. "Delete 12 dimensions and GSM8K collapses"—that one experiment punctured the assumption that capability lives in experts completely.

The actionable conclusion: next time you face a field where everyone is racing metrics, look first for the *unit assumption that nobody wrote down but everyone silently accepted*, then design the smallest possible destructive experiment to test it. Once the assumption cracks, old criterion + new scale is often all you need for a strong paper.
