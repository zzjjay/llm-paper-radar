* One sentence

AngelSlim's sharpest single thread is a five-step rewrite of "small models can't go too small": from "4-bit nearly lossless" → "3-bit acceptable" → "2-bit hits a wall" → "retrain from scratch" → "industrially viable 2-bit model you can actually afford to train."

* Reading backwards: tracing AngelSlim to its roots

Let me start with AngelSlim's own strongest claim: HY-1.8B-int2 is "the first industrially deployable 2-bit large model." That is what sets it apart from everything else. The rest of the toolkit — FP8, EAGLE-3, sparse attention, vision pruning — is assembling other people's work into one system. Only this 2-bit thread is where AngelSlim says "we went further than our predecessors."

So let's walk this thread backwards.

** Layer 1: Who AngelSlim is critiquing

AngelSlim's HY-1.8B-int2 uses *SEQ (Stretched Elastic Quantization)*, explicitly crediting Liu et al. 2025 — i.e., *ParetoQ*. It did not invent a new quantization function; it adopted SEQ directly from ParetoQ. But it lands one critical punch: conventional ternary methods suffer "catastrophic accuracy drops exceeding 16%" on 1B-scale models, while AngelSlim achieves industrial usability — via initialization from an instruction-tuned model plus 89B tokens of QAT.

It also takes a side-swipe at BitNet: "HY-1.8B-2Bit requires only 10% of the token budget of BitNet-2B." The implication: the train-from-scratch path that BitNet follows burns money most companies cannot afford.

** Layer 2: Who ParetoQ is critiquing

ParetoQ's own posture is "we are the first to rigorously compare 1-bit, 1.58-bit, 2-bit, 3-bit, and 4-bit under a single unified framework." Its target is the field's accumulated confusion — some papers champion 4-bit as optimal, others insist 1.58-bit is the answer, but because every group uses different training recipes the conclusions cannot be compared. ParetoQ says the ledger is unsettled.

After running the experiments, it arrives at a concrete finding: *there is a learning phase transition between 2 and 3 bits*. At 3-bit and above, a quantized model's weight distribution still resembles the original pre-training distribution — the model is recognizably "itself," just at lower precision. Below 2-bit, internal representations "change drastically" — the model must learn an entirely new low-bit vocabulary. Engineering conclusion: 2-bit sits right at the sweet spot — easier to train than 1.58-bit, yet saves nearly half the memory compared to 3-bit.

** Layer 3: Who BitNet b1.58 is critiquing

ParetoQ's comparison set is impossible to complete without BitNet b1.58. BitNet b1.58's own slogan is *"The Era of 1-bit LLMs"* — a LLM trained with ternary {-1, 0, +1} weights that claims to match same-size FP16 LLaMA.

Its target is the PTQ path: take a trained FP16 model, compress it to 4-bit after the fact and it holds, compress further and it collapses. BitNet b1.58's answer is "don't do PTQ — train low-bit from the start." The key insight is the added 0: this is not pure binary but ternary, with 0 acting as a sparse gate.

** Layer 4: Who the original BitNet is critiquing

BitNet b1.58 stands on the shoulders of the original BitNet (2023). The original BitNet is true 1-bit, with only {-1, +1}. Its innovation is *BitLinear* — a layer that can directly replace `nn.Linear`, training binary weights from random initialization. It claims to demonstrate "a scaling law similar to full-precision Transformers."

Its target is then-mainstream 8-bit quantization: 8-bit was treated as a ceiling, anything lower was considered unusable, and BitLinear blew through that ceiling all the way to 1-bit.

** Layer 5: GPTQ — where it all began

Tracing further back, we reach *GPTQ* (Frantar et al., 2022, ICLR'23). GPTQ is the cleanest representative of the PTQ path: use second-order Hessian information to quantize weights to 3–4 bits in a single pass, nearly losslessly. A 175B model quantized in 4 GPU-hours, fitting on a single card.

GPTQ honestly states: "3–4 bit nearly lossless; 2-bit and ternary can only deliver reasonable accuracy." That sentence — "reasonable" — is the wall everyone who came after had to find a way around.

That is far enough back. Going further leads to older CNN binarization work (XNOR-Net, Ternary Weight Networks), which has little direct bearing on LLMs. We stop at GPTQ.

* Reading forward: how the problem evolved step by step

** Stop 1: GPTQ — taking post-training quantization to its limit

Rewind to 2022. LLMs have grown to 175B parameters and no longer fit on a single A100. Everyone's first instinct is "compress." The most natural approach is *PTQ* (Post-Training Quantization): the model is already trained; don't retrain it, just chop off decimal precision from the weights.

GPTQ pushed this path to its extreme. Its insight: instead of naively rounding 16-bit floats to 4-bit integers, use second-order gradient (Hessian) information — "if I shave a little off this weight, how much does it hurt downstream output?" — and solve layer by layer for the optimal rounding of every weight. Result: a 175B model quantized in 4 GPU-hours, 4-bit precision with almost no accuracy loss.

But GPTQ itself says something important: at *2-bit and ternary*, it can only deliver "reasonable accuracy" — meaning noticeably degraded, just barely usable. That sentence sets the ceiling everyone after GPTQ has to contend with.

The intuition is easy to grasp: you trained a model that learned to express itself with 65,536 distinct floating-point values, and now you tell it to use only 4 values (2-bit). That it doesn't completely collapse is surprising. Post-training recovery has hard limits, because the original model was never optimized with low-bit constraints in mind.

** Stop 2: Original BitNet — if PTQ can't go lower, train low-bit from the start

The original BitNet (2023) follows a very direct logic: PTQ hits a wall because the model was trained without ever thinking about being compressed. So let it be 1-bit from birth.

The implementation is quintessentially engineering: write a layer called *BitLinear* that directly replaces PyTorch's `nn.Linear`. From the first forward pass, weights are forced to {-1, +1} (using a straight-through estimator so gradients can still flow back). The whole network starts from random initialization with weights that are always binary, but the optimizer adapts to this constraint — the network learns to spread information across many binary weights.

Result: where 8-bit quantization was barely keeping models functional, BitNet went all the way to 1-bit and remained competitive, with the scaling law intact — bigger models still improved.

But 1-bit is severe. Every weight can only say "yes" or "no" — it has no way to say "doesn't matter."

** Stop 3: BitNet b1.58 — add a zero so the model can say "doesn't matter"

BitNet b1.58 (2024) sees the original BitNet's problem: pure binary loses too much information. Its fix is elegantly minimal — expand the weight domain from {-1, +1} to {-1, 0, +1}. The added 0 acts as a sparse gate: if a weight is unimportant for a given feature, set it to zero and exclude it from computation.

Why 1.58 bits? Because the information entropy of three values is log₂(3) ≈ 1.58.

The payoff is large. The paper title announces *"The Era of 1-bit LLMs"*, claiming ternary LLMs match same-size FP16 LLaMA. The hardware story is even more compelling — ternary weights reduce matrix multiplication to essentially addition (multiplying by -1, 0, or +1 needs no multiplier), which means specialized chips could be designed around it.

Sounds perfect. But two problems lurk underneath.

First: BitNet b1.58 *trains from scratch*. Using it means abandoning all existing pre-trained models and burning trillions of tokens from random initialization. A research lab can afford that; the vast majority of companies cannot.

Second: is ternary actually optimal? Is 1.58-bit better or worse than 2-bit? At the time, this was an unsettled question — some papers said ternary wins, some said 4-bit wins, but every paper used a different training recipe, different data, and different initialization. No apples-to-apples comparison existed.

** Stop 4: ParetoQ — put every bit-width on the same table

ParetoQ (2025) arrives specifically to settle this debt. Its posture is clear: "Stop talking past each other. I'll use one training recipe, one dataset, and run 1-bit, 1.58-bit, 2-bit, 3-bit, and 4-bit through the same pipeline. Then we'll see."

The result is a very specific finding: *there is a learning phase transition between 2-bit and 3-bit*. At 3-bit and above, the quantized model's weight distribution remains close to the original pre-training distribution — the model is still recognizably "itself," just at lower precision. Drop below 2-bit and the model's internal representations begin to "change drastically" — it must learn a new internal language suited to low-bit expression.

The practical implication: at 3-bit and above you can use PTQ or light fine-tuning; below 2-bit you must retrain. And *2-bit itself lands just below the phase-transition boundary* — easier to train than 1.58-bit, yet saves nearly half the memory compared to 3-bit. It is the sweet spot on this curve.

ParetoQ also contributes a key tool: *SEQ (Stretched Elastic Quantization)*. Conventional INT2 uses the value set {-2, -1, 0, 1} — asymmetric, with a zero-point offset. SEQ shifts it to {-1.5, -0.5, +0.5, +1.5} — symmetric, no zero-point, a much better fit to the actual weight distribution (Gaussian-Laplacian mixture).

But ParetoQ remains an academic contribution. It tells you 2-bit is the sweet spot; it does not tell you how to train a 2-bit model that can actually be shipped into a production system.

** Stop 5: AngelSlim — actually building the 2-bit model and shipping it

AngelSlim (2026) does exactly that: it turns ParetoQ's conclusions into a product. Its *HY-1.8B-int2* directly reuses ParetoQ's SEQ quantization function, but makes two engineering choices in the training pipeline:

First choice: *do not train from random initialization* — instead, start from an already-trained instruction-tuned 1.8B model and run QAT (Quantization-Aware Training). This is a complete rejection of the BitNet train-from-scratch path. The claim: AngelSlim burns only 10% of the tokens BitNet-2B required.

Second choice: *89B tokens of QAT*. That is not small, but it is two orders of magnitude fewer than the trillions of tokens needed for full pre-training.

AngelSlim argues that conventional ternary methods suffer "catastrophic accuracy drops exceeding 16%" on 1B-scale models — and that this instruction-init + QAT path is precisely what lets it avoid that collapse.

This is where the thread closes. AngelSlim did not forge a new ruler; it took ParetoQ's ruler — "2-bit is the sweet spot" — and turned it into something a production assembly line can actually use.

* What is really happening underneath this thread

On the surface, the bit-width is simply dropping: 16 → 8 → 4 → 3 → 2 → 1.58 → 1. But what is actually happening is that *the philosophy of compression has shifted roots three times*.

*First root shift (GPTQ → original BitNet): from "compress after" to "native low-bit."* The GPTQ line believes "train the model first, then find a way to compress it." BitNet says that cannot work — quantization awareness must be present from the very first training step.

*Second root shift (BitNet → ParetoQ): from "find the lowest possible bit-width" to "find the efficiency inflection point."* The BitNet family chases "how low can we go." ParetoQ flips the frame — the goal is not the lowest bit-width but the bit-width just above the cliff edge where going lower causes collapse. The answer is 2-bit.

*Third root shift (ParetoQ → AngelSlim): from "train from scratch" to "reuse pre-training."* Academic train-from-scratch is a luxury. AngelSlim says: initialize from an instruction-tuned model and run QAT — recover 90% of the budget that the BitNet approach would have burned.

String these three root shifts together and a simple engineering evolution pattern emerges: *first prove it can be done, then prove it can be done cheaply, then prove it can ship as a product*. Academia owns the first two steps; industry owns the third.

Where does the next step go? Two guesses:

1. *1-bit gets picked up by industry again.* If AngelSlim's "instruction-init + QAT" recipe can be extended below ternary, the BitNet path becomes viable — hardware vendors are already waiting for dedicated ternary accelerators.
2. *Mixed precision + adaptive bit-width.* Different layers at different bits — attention at 4-bit, MLP at 2-bit, embedding at 8-bit. ParetoQ's phase-transition finding hints at this direction: the "cliff edge" may sit at a different bit-width for each layer.
