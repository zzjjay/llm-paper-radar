* The Problem River

After compressing a large language model from 16-bit to 4-bit, its ability to solve math problems, write code, and answer domain-specific questions degrades. How do you get that ability back?

Why is this hard? Ten years ago it was simple for CNNs — just fold quantization into the training loop and re-run with the same loss (QAT). But today's LLMs are different: a Llama Nemotron goes through SFT, then RL, then model merging — a pipeline with five or six stages. If you want to "fold quantization into the training loop," which stage do you fold it into? Every stage? The training data may not even be public. Worse, if you take SFT data and fine-tune a post-RL model, the capabilities the RL phase learned are immediately washed away.

So the core question is: *In the post-training era of LLMs, how do you recover the capabilities lost to quantization without re-running the entire training pipeline?*

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Hinton KD: Soft Labels Carry Far More Information Than Hard Ones

*** What problem it saw

The old way of training a classifier used one-hot labels — "this is a cat," everything else is 0. That discards an enormous amount of information: the teacher network actually knows that this cat "looks a bit like a dog but nothing like a car." That subtle probability structure is completely erased by hard labels. The student can only learn a coarse right/wrong signal.

*** Its solution

Have the teacher emit a complete probability distribution (with temperature raised to flatten it), and have the student minimize the KL divergence against that distribution. "Cat 0.7, dog 0.2, wolf 0.08, ..." — those nonzero small probabilities are the real knowledge the teacher has learned about what things resemble.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfall it leaves behind)

Soft labels have lower variance, produce more stable gradients, and carry denser information. Intuitively, the teacher is not telling the student the answer — it is telling the student how to think about the question.

The pitfall left behind: at the time this was purely about model compression — distilling a large model into a smaller one. No one thought to connect it to the "low-precision computation" thread.

** 2018 | QAT (Jacob et al.): Quantization Must Enter the Training Loop, Not Be Patched In Afterward

*** What problem it saw

The dominant approach in the CNN era was PTQ — train first, quantize later. At 8 bits things were fine; at 4 bits everything collapsed. The reason: weights learned in the full-precision world are not faithfully representable after quantization. Quantizing after the fact means information is simply lost.

*** Its solution

Fold "quantize–dequantize" operators into the forward pass so that weights experience quantization error during training. Gradients in the backward pass remain full-precision (straight-through estimator) to keep optimization stable. The model naturally nudges its weights toward "quantization-friendly" configurations.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfall it leaves behind)

QAT turns quantization error into something the model can optimize against, rather than a disturbance forcibly imposed afterward. On CNN + ImageNet it became virtually the standard approach.

The pitfall left behind — one that would detonate a decade later: QAT requires the original training pipeline and original training data. In the CNN era this was no problem (train ResNet once, done), but applied to LLMs every assumption in this requirement breaks down.

** 2018 | Apprentice (Mishra & Marr): Distillation + Quantization, Their First Handshake

*** What problem it saw

Even with QAT alone, low-precision (3–4 bit) CNN accuracy still could not recover. Jacob's approach works well at 8 bits, but struggles further down. The reason: the student model (low precision) must feel its way toward quantization-friendly weights entirely on its own — the search space is very narrow.

*** Its solution

Have a full-precision "teacher CNN" guide the student from the side. The student does not just fit training labels; it also matches the teacher's output distribution. This is the first formal marriage of KD and quantization.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfall it leaves behind)

The teacher effectively draws a "you should walk this path" guideline for the student within the quantized space. The student does not have to feel its way from scratch — it copies the teacher's target directly.

The pitfall left behind: the ratio of distillation loss to task loss is still set by intuition. And the assumption remains that teacher and student share the same training data and the same training procedure.

** 2018 | Quantized Distillation (Polino et al.): Welding Distillation Directly into Quantization Training

*** What problem it saw

Apprentice treats KD as an "auxiliary" addition. Polino is more aggressive: if distillation is so helpful for quantized models, why not make the distillation loss the core objective of training the weight-quantized network rather than a supplement?

*** Its solution

Explicitly write the distillation loss into the optimization objective of the weight-quantized network. At the same time, explore making the quantization points themselves learnable — the gradient signal from distillation shapes not just the weights but also the quantization scheme.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfall it leaves behind)

This further fuses "quantization" and "distillation" from two separate concerns into one.

The pitfall left behind: the entire mechanism still lives in the small-data world of CNNs and supervised learning. The token-level, massive-vocabulary, multi-stage training paradigm of LLMs had not yet been encountered.

** 2023 | LLM-QAT (Liu et al.): No Data Needed — Because the Model Can Generate Its Own

*** What problem it saw

The first wall encountered when porting QAT to LLMs is data: open-source models like GPT/LLaMA give you the weights but not the training data. Without the original data, QAT stalls — using an unrelated dataset to fine-tune causes the model to drift toward that dataset's distribution, destroying its general capabilities.

*** Its solution

Have the full-precision model generate its own data — give it a seed token, let it continue, and you have training samples. Use these self-generated samples to do distillation-style QAT. Data-free, neatly sidestepping the problem that open-source models do not release their training data.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfall it leaves behind)

Data self-generated by the teacher model naturally falls within the distribution the teacher is familiar with; the student learning from it will not be pulled astray by "foreign data." This is the first time the KD + quantization paradigm truly landed on LLMs.

The pitfall left behind: the assumption is still a "single-stage, relatively simple training pipeline." But by 2024–2025 LLMs were running complex multi-stage pipelines of SFT → RL → model merging, and that assumption was about to collapse again.

** 2024 | BitDistiller (Du et al.): Distillation Too Strong Causes Overfitting — It Needs Tuning

*** What problem it saw

Quantizing an LLM to below 4 bits (3-bit, 2-bit), vanilla KL distillation misfires: the student over-relies on the teacher's specific patterns and breaks on out-of-domain samples.

*** Its solution

Asymmetric quantization (compressing the dynamic range) + self-distillation (teacher = the high-precision version of itself) + a mixture of forward and reverse KL to avoid over-matching from a single-direction KL.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfall it leaves behind)

Forward KL pushes the student to "cover" all of the teacher's modes; reverse KL pushes the student to "focus" on the teacher's high-probability modes. Mixing the two is more stable than either alone.

The pitfall left behind: still focused on integer quantization (INT3/INT4); did not touch new hardware formats like FP4; did not consider the new complications introduced by RL post-training.

** 2026 | QAD (This Paper): Discard the Task Loss Entirely

*** What problem it saw

By the NVFP4 generation the problem had changed its face completely.

First, *NVFP4's hardware characteristics render traditional PTQ ineffective*: NVFP4's block size is only 16 (vs. 32 for MXFP4). This fine granularity makes techniques like GPTQ, AWQ, and SmoothQuant — which "flatten outliers" — ineffective: outliers are already locally scaled within the block, so transformation yields no benefit. PTQ on small models directly drops accuracy.

Second, *the post-training pipeline makes QAT impractical*: Models like Llama Nemotron Super and Nemotron Nano V2 go through a multi-stage pipeline of SFT → RL → merge. To do QAT you would need to re-run every stage with a quantized forward pass — engineering-wise, this is not feasible.

Third, *doing QAT with SFT data washes out the capabilities learned by RL*: This is the most damaging finding. Table 3 in the paper makes it brutally clear: running QAT on AceReason with SFT data drops AIME25 from 63.5 (BF16) to 46.1 — worse than doing nothing but PTQ (58.7). The task loss is in essence "an extra SFT stage," and it erases the reasoning capabilities the RL phase had built.

*** Its solution

Discard the task loss entirely. Use *only* KL divergence to align the quantized model's output distribution with the full-precision teacher's.

#+begin_example
  QAT loss = cross_entropy(student_output, label)        <-- discarded
  QAD loss = KL(teacher_distribution || student_distribution)
#+end_example

The teacher is the BF16 model before quantization. The student is the NVFP4 model after quantization. No labels, no task objective — just "match the teacher's distribution," nothing more.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfall it leaves behind)

The key insight is in Table 1 of the paper: QAT achieves cross-entropy as good as BF16, *but its KL divergence is large* (0.311); QAD is the opposite — cross-entropy is slightly worse, *but KL divergence is nearly zero* (0.004).

QAT appears to be fitting the task, but it is actually reshaping the model into "a different entity that can solve the task but is no longer the original model" — the subtle capabilities learned by RL are lost. QAD does not care about the model's performance on data; it only cares whether the model resembles the original. Once the distributions are aligned, all capabilities of the original model (including those from RL) are automatically inherited.

Two unexpected findings on top of that:
- *Cross-domain transfer*: AceReason trained QAD using only code data still recovers math capabilities. Because the teacher's distribution implicitly encodes knowledge across all domains — as long as the student matches the distribution, data from any domain will do.
- *Insensitivity to data quality*: Even using completely random token sequences for QAD does not break the model (it holds at the PTQ baseline). The training data is essentially irrelevant.

Pitfalls left behind:
- When RL training itself needs to be done with quantization (rather than patched afterward), how does QAD's philosophy transfer? The paper itself flags QARL (Quantization-Aware RL) as an open problem.
- KL distillation requires the teacher model to be resident in GPU memory — memory and compute costs are effectively doubled for large models.
- Learning-rate tuning remains sensitive (SFT-heavy models need 1e-6, RL-heavy models need 1e-5), with no automatic prescription.

* Frontier Extensions

This paper is an NVIDIA technical report released in January 2026. As of 2026-05, no papers explicitly critiquing or substantially improving it have appeared publicly. QARL (quantization-aware training inside the RL loop) is an open direction the paper itself points to; industry is experimenting (huang2025qerl, yao2025flashrl focus on throughput and have not addressed accuracy recovery yet).

Foreseeable next steps:
1. *QARL*: Fold quantization awareness into the RL training loop itself rather than patching afterward.
2. *Distribution-aware training data synthesis*: Since QAD is insensitive to data, there should be room to synthesize data "most conducive to aligning the teacher's distribution."
3. *QAD at narrower precisions (FP3/INT3)*: Current QAD is demonstrated on NVFP4; can it survive one more level of compression?

* Insight

Reading through this lineage, what is really happening is not "distillation becoming progressively more valued" — it is that *task loss goes from protagonist to villain*.

In the CNN era, task loss was absolute truth — the model's entire reason for existing was to solve that task, so you trained it with that loss. Distillation was merely a "seasoning," helping the model take fewer wrong turns in the low-precision world.

In the LLM post-training era, *capability* and *task performance* are no longer the same thing. A model that has gone through SFT + RL + merge has its capabilities "cultivated" by that entire pipeline — not defined by any single loss. If you fine-tune it with any specific task loss (even the original SFT loss), you are effectively running one more round of post-training on it. And the essence of post-training is to change the model — change means loss.

So QAD's real contribution is not "it used KL divergence." It is the recognition that *the model is already what it should be; the only job after quantization is to make a faithful copy, not to train it again*. KL divergence is the technical vehicle for this philosophy: it does not care whether the model solves the task; it only cares whether the model resembles the original.

Zooming out: the entire evolutionary thread is really saying one thing — *as model capabilities grow more complex, "training" and "copying" must be separated*. Early models were simple: training was copying (train once, reproduce faithfully). Later models are complex: training can no longer be simply reproduced, and "making a faithful copy of an existing capability" becomes an independent engineering problem. Distillation graduates from an auxiliary technique to the main arena for this new problem.

Where is this most likely to go next? Two directions:

1. *QARL*: Putting quantization awareness inside the RL loop itself. This will be harder than QAD — RL training is already unstable on its own, and quantization makes the reward signal noisier still.
2. *Deeper "distribution alignment" objectives*: QAD currently aligns the next-token distribution. Future work may align attention patterns, KV cache distributions, or even intermediate layer representations. The path of "task-agnostic, align only to the model itself" has further to go.

* Takeaways

What this suggests about how to do research —

*When a long-standing assumption is quietly overturned, an entire research thread opens up for a "track switch."* QAT's implicit assumption is that "task loss points in the right direction for improving the model." LLM post-training quietly overturned this (task loss actually destroys capabilities), but the community took years to notice. QAD did not invent a new algorithm (KL distillation is from 2015); it is *the first work to clearly see that "task loss is harmful in the new environment" and reorganize the solution accordingly*.

The takeaway: you can find research opportunities by examining "what the field takes for granted" — find a premise everyone accepts without proof and ask "does this premise still hold in the new environment?" The LLM era has inherited a large number of "default assumptions" from the CNN era. Every one of them is worth re-examining.

A second takeaway: *the strongest simplification is often removal, not addition*. BitDistiller is "adding things" (mixed KL, asymmetric quantization). QAD is "removing things" (deleting the task loss entirely). The latter is both more radical and more general. When you find that a solution is still not good enough after stacking multiple mechanisms, ask the inverse question: "Can I delete one of the core components?" — that is often the exit.
