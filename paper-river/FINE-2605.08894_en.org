* The River of the Problem

Compress a 7-billion-parameter model down to an average of 1 bit — or even 0.5 bits — per weight, leaving only two or three possible values each. Can the model still speak coherently?

Researchers working on this problem have been fighting the same battle for three years: perplexity (PPL) rises after low-bit quantization, so how do you push it back down? Everyone shares an unspoken premise — *PPL rises because the forward numerical computation is wrong* — and so the methods grow ever more elaborate: error compensation, scaling, rotation, codebooks, ternary weights, binary weights.

FINE walks in and slams the table: hold on — PPL is not the whole story. Even when the forward pass is numerically accurate, the model turns stupid during generation, because its *decision tree has collapsed*. At each prediction step, the number of plausible candidate tokens drops from ten down to three or five; as decoding proceeds the tree keeps narrowing until only a single thin thread remains. This collapse is *orthogonal* to forward-pass accuracy — you cannot fix it by driving ‖WX − Ŵ X‖ to zero.

That is the true undercurrent surging through this river: *the bottleneck in extreme quantization stopped being forward numerics long ago; instead, the model's "smoothness" is quietly collapsing, and nobody has been watching it.*

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022 | GPTQ: Quantization Is a Column-Wise Problem, Not a Weight-Wise One

*** What It Saw

The simplest approach, round-to-nearest (RTN), holds at 8-bit but starts failing at 4-bit. The reason is that weight columns in LLMs are highly correlated — when you flatten column i, the remaining columns could in principle "absorb" the error, but RTN makes an irreversible decision for each weight, eliminating any chance of mutual compensation.

*** Its Solution

GPTQ reformulates quantization as an optimization problem: minimize ‖WX − Ŵ X‖². It then uses second-order activation information (Hessian H = XX^T) to quantize column by column: after quantizing column i, the resulting error is immediately redistributed to the remaining columns via the inverse Hessian. This objective has an approximate closed-form solution, making it fast — a 7B model in a matter of minutes.

*** Why It Works, and What It Buries

GPTQ is nearly lossless at 4-bit and acceptable at 3-bit. But its entire mathematical skeleton — row-by-row updates plus closed-form reconstruction — looks only at the forward pass. "How the backward gradient flows" is not in the objective. At 2-bit, the forward cosine similarity is still 0.97, but the backward cosine drops straight to 0.70 (measured by FINE in §6.1). GPTQ's ceiling is not compute — it's that the question it asks is too narrow.

** 2024 | OmniQuant: Make "Where to Clip" Learnable Too

*** What It Saw

GPTQ is extremely sensitive to the clipping range [min, max] at low bit-widths. A single outlier drags the entire group scale along with it. But GPTQ is closed-form, leaving no room to "learn."

*** Its Solution

Introduce two learnable parameters γ, β into the quantization formula for the clipping bounds:
  h = (γ · max(w) − β · min(w)) / (2^N − 1)
Then use layer-wise distillation (original outputs as teacher) and optimize by gradient descent. The underlying logic is still "compress the forward pass," just done more gently.

*** Why It Works, and What It Buries

OmniQuant is substantially better than GPTQ at 2-bit (PPL 13.76 vs. 39.58 on LLaMA-2-7B). But its objective is still ‖z_θ − z_θ̂‖² — "the student imitates how the teacher computes." The student learns the forward output; the backward gradient is left completely unattended. That hole waits for FINE to fill.

** 2024 | BitNet b1.58 / OneBit: Extreme Compression Requires Training From Scratch

*** What It Saw

The PTQ paradigm simply cannot turn the corner below 2-bit. A model trained in FP16 and then forced into ±1 ternary slots is fundamentally incompatible at the operator level — you have to let the model know from the very beginning that its weights will eventually have only three possible values.

*** Its Solution

BitNet b1.58 uses ternary weights {-1, 0, +1} (averaging 1.58 bits), while OneBit uses sign matrices plus high-precision compensating vectors. Both apply QAT, with the key being RMSNorm/LayerNorm inserted before and after linear layers — stabilizing the forward activation distribution and, as it turns out, incidentally smoothing the backward gradients (though nobody realized this at the time).

*** Why It Works, and What It Buries

QAT allows the model to adapt to low-bit operators from training, bringing performance close to FP16. But the training objective is only LM loss — the model simply needs PPL to converge. When FINE (§5.2) measures the backward gradient layer by layer in a B158 model, it finds the backward gradients in intermediate layers are noticeably larger than in BF16. From the forward perspective everything looks normal, yet those quietly inflated gradients are the root cause of rPPL collapse. LayerNorm happened to rescue half the problem — but nobody realized what it was actually rescuing.

** 2024–25 | BiLLM / ARB-LLM / STBLLM: Pushing to Even Greater Extremes

*** What They Saw

1.58-bit is still not the limit. Can we push further with binary weights — keeping salient weights at higher precision, applying structured sparsity, or iteratively refining?

*** Their Solutions

- BiLLM: salient weights at high precision; all others fully binarized
- ARB-LLM: alternating binary refinement (multiple iterations to approach full-precision behavior)
- STBLLM: structured binarization + sparsity, breaking through the 1-bit barrier

*** Why It Works, and What It Buries

This line of work pushes the compression ratio into the 0.5-bit range. But the yardstick remains PPL and downstream accuracy, and the mindset remains "how do we make the forward pass more precise." What FINE wants to say is: *you are fighting on the wrong front — the real bottleneck stopped being forward numerics long ago.*

** 2026 | FINE: A New Dimension, Not Just a New Algorithm

*** What It Saw

FINE measures the "smoothness" of quantized models using a deceptively simple quantity — the *input gradient* C_avg = E_x ‖∇_x f‖_2. The result surprises everyone:

- 4-bit model's C_avg ≈ BF16
- 3-bit starts to rise
- 2-bit explodes (the distribution plot in Figure 1 makes this immediately clear)

Even more striking is the rPPL experiment in §4. Standard PPL only tests how correct a single decoding path is. FINE instead measures "how bad are the top-k candidate tokens the model offers" — using the FP16 model as a judge, computing the perplexity of the quantized model's 1st, 2nd, ..., 40th candidates. On BF16, rPPL rises slowly; on 2-bit quantized models it collapses sharply — meaning *the quantized model's decision tree shrinks from 10 viable branches down to 5 or fewer*, shedding candidates at every step, compounding as decoding progresses.

This collapse is invisible to PPL because PPL only evaluates the one path you actually took.

*** Its Solution

Add an objective that forces "backward gradients to be preserved too":

  min ‖W X − Ŵ X‖² + α · ‖W^T G − Ŵ^T G‖²
        ^^^^^^^^^^^      ^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^
        forward fitting    backward smoothness

But these two objectives are mathematically orthogonal — forward fitting drives row-wise updates while backward smoothness demands column-wise consistency, making GPTQ's closed-form solution directly incompatible. FINE offers two engineering paths:

- *LGP (PTQ)*: grafted onto OmniQuant, adding the α term to the learnable loss so that γ and β are optimized jointly.
- *LGR (QAT)*: adds α · ‖∇_x⁽¹⁾ f‖² on top of BitNet b1.58's LM loss. Crucially, this must use the *layer-1* gradient, not layer 0 — FINE discovers that the embedding input exhibits a "gradient ridge" phenomenon (equally large whether quantized or not), so the layer-0 gradient carries no signal about quantization damage.

*** Why It Works, and What It Leaves Open

LGP raises average accuracy on LLaMA-2-7B from 46.03 (OmniQuant) to 46.67 — seemingly modest, but FINE itself emphasizes: this is not a SOTA paper; it is *validating a previously unused dimension*. Even without directly optimizing PPL, adding LGR causes PPL to improve as a side effect — smoothness corrects the relative ranking of multiple-choice answers, e.g., flipping "BCDA" to "DBAC" (the figure in §6.3), turning wrong answers into right ones while absolute probabilities barely change.

The genuinely hard open problems: fitting and smoothness are a Pareto trade-off at low bit-widths (the anisotropy experiment in §6.1); the weight solution space that achieves both simultaneously exists but is extremely narrow (§6.2). Nobody knows what a closed-form solution looks like.

* Extensions at the Frontier

As of May 2026, FINE is barely a month old and no published follow-up work builds directly on it. But the open questions it leaves suggest two plausible directions:

- *A joint closed-form solver*: can we find a low-rank approximation algorithm that preserves both forward and backward passes simultaneously? FINE §6.2 proves that under LLMs' low-rank activations and gradients the solution space is nonempty — just narrow. This is a numerical linear algebra problem.
- *Smoothness-aware architecture design*: FINE §6.4 notes that LayerNorm is critical for sub-2-bit models partly because it incidentally smooths backward gradients. This hints that future low-bit-friendly architectures may need to *explicitly design* smoothness operators — not just LN/RMSNorm, but potentially full layers with Lipschitz constraints.

Uncertain: whether this line will evolve into a "smoothness-dedicated optimizer" or be absorbed into the loss functions of existing methods. FINE bets on the former — the entire paper positions itself as "proposing a dimension, not proposing a method."

* Insight

Reading the full arc of this river, the most critical shift is this:

*For three years, the field believed "the essence of low-bit quantization is a numerical precision problem." After FINE, that belief becomes unstable.* What is actually happening is — once you compress weights below 2-bit, the model's *geometric properties* (smoothness, backward gradient structure) collapse earlier than its forward numerics. The latter is explicit and compressible to ε; the former is hidden and coupled to the expressive capacity of the weights themselves.

One layer deeper: all measurement is biased. GPTQ/OmniQuant/BitNet's entire ecosystem optimizes PPL because PPL is easy to measure. But PPL only evaluates the probability along a single path; it is blind to "decision-tree width." FINE introduces rPPL not to invent a new metric, but to *reveal that PPL has been blindfolding the field*. This is a paradigm-level reminder: when an entire community is racing to improve a single number, that number itself may be deceiving them.

A third layer (speculative): FINE hints that extreme quantization may have no "lossless tipping point" at all. At low bit-widths, fitting and smoothness narrow the solution space in orthogonal directions; future algorithms should shift their goal from "find a lossless solution" to "make principled Pareto trade-offs between the two." This resembles the collapse of the "loss → 0" fantasy in late-stage LLM scaling — yet another myth of "perfect compression" being punctured.

* Takeaways

Lessons for how to do research:

1. *Watch the axis nobody else is watching.* GPTQ → OmniQuant → BitNet spent three years competing on the forward pass; FINE was the first to measure the backward gradient — something so simple that everyone assumed it didn't need measuring. Many breakthroughs come not from harder techniques but from newer questions.
2. *The metric itself can lie.* PPL ≠ generation quality; downstream acc ≠ model understanding; AUC ≠ business impact. When a community treats a single metric as ground truth, actively search for its blind spots.
3. *Negative results can be the core contribution.* FINE does not replace SOTA; LGP/LGR are proposed only to demonstrate "adding this term genuinely helps." The paper's real contribution is *falsifying a hidden assumption* — "forward accuracy is everything."
4. *Differential improvement matters more than absolute value.* +0.6% acc would get a SOTA paper savaged by reviewers, but in FINE it holds — because the claim is not "+0.6%," it is "adding this term is always better than not adding it, proving the dimension is real."

Practical landing points:

- If you are doing low-bit quantization engineering: add a ‖W^T G − Ŵ^T G‖² term to your loss function and see whether you get a free accuracy boost.
- If you are evaluating quantized models: alongside PPL, run an rPPL-k test to check whether the decision tree has collapsed.
- If you are designing low-bit-friendly architectures: treat Lipschitz continuity / smoothness as an explicit constraint rather than hoping LayerNorm saves you by accident.
- If you are writing a paper: don't only compare absolute numbers against SOTA — find the *neglected dimension* behind your improvement; that dimension itself is the contribution.
