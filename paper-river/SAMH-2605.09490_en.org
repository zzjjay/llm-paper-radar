* The Problem River

A GPU can only hold so much at once. When a large model "thinks through a
problem"—deriving answers step by step before responding—every step leaves
a note (KV) in GPU memory. The longer the reasoning, the more notes accumulate,
until memory is exhausted. HBM, the most expensive and scarce GPU memory, cannot
sustain more than a handful of concurrent requests.

Can we simply discard unimportant notes? That is exactly what the field has
been doing for three years. The result: fine for ordinary dialogue, catastrophic
for "thinking"—drop half the notes and accuracy plummets from 90% to 0–2.5%. An
intermediate result computed 500 tokens ago may be the critical citation for the
proof that comes next; discarding it is like deleting a line of code and
expecting the program to still run.

The central question of this river: *We want the model to think longer, but
memory cannot keep up. How do we economize without making the model dumber?*

The difficulty is that "thinking" is not like "talking." Speech is linear—old
content can fade gracefully. Reasoning is non-linear—any intermediate product
may be revisited at any moment. "Unimportant right now" is a lie: five hundred
steps later it may be indispensable.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Transformer: Turning Memory into Parallelizable Matrices

*** What Problem It Saw

RNNs process one token at a time—painfully slow—and struggle to retain
long-range dependencies.

*** Its Solution

Every position attends directly to every other position (self-attention),
computing a weighted sum. All positions are processed simultaneously,
saturating the GPU.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

Parallelism makes it fast, and dependencies of any distance are connected
directly. But the price is that every token must store a "key" and a "value"
for future tokens to look up. Generating n tokens requires O(n) KV cache
memory. Short texts are fine; long texts—memory explodes.

This is the starting point of every story that follows.

** 2019–2020 | Sparse Transformer / Longformer: Attention Is Actually Sparse

*** What Problem It Saw

Full attention costs O(n^2) computation and O(n) memory. Plotting the attention
matrix reveals that the vast majority of entries are near zero—the connections
that truly matter are few.

*** Its Solution

Compute only the positions that "should be non-zero": local windows + a small
number of global tokens + sparse patterns. Sparsity is baked in at the design
level.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

Mathematically sound; long texts can now run. But the catch is that sparse
patterns are fixed *at training time*. An already-trained GPT/Llama cannot be
directly sparsified. To save memory this way, you have to retrain from scratch.

The next generation therefore asked: can we leave the model untouched and adapt
on the fly at inference time?

** 2023 | H2O: Heavy-Hitter Oracle — Dynamic Eviction at Inference Time

*** What Problem It Saw

Once an LLM is trained, its architecture cannot be changed. Yet memory still
explodes during generation. A *inference-phase* solution is needed.

*** Its Solution

It uncovered a phenomenon: attention mass follows a long-tail distribution—20%
of tokens capture 80% of attention scores. So only those "heavy hitters" are
kept in memory; the rest are evicted as generation proceeds.

Formally this is cast as a dynamic submodular optimization: with each new token,
recompute who the heavy hitters are and evict the rest.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

Elegant and effective. A few dozen lines of code changes, half the memory saved,
and accuracy barely drops on general text. The KV compression field exploded.

But H2O was validated on *ordinary dialogue*. It silently assumed: *tokens that
are evicted will not be needed again*. That assumption holds in casual
conversation—what was said moments ago quickly becomes irrelevant. But in
reasoning?

** 2024 | StreamingLLM / ScissorHands: H2O's Siblings, Pushing Eviction to the Limit

*** What Problem It Saw

Straightforward eviction in H2O discards the "attention sink" tokens at the
very beginning of a sequence (tokens the model relies on abnormally for
attention normalization), causing long streaming generation to collapse.

*** Its Solution

StreamingLLM: permanently retain the first 4 sink tokens plus a sliding window.
ScissorHands: observes that importance is "persistent"—once judged important, a
token stays important—enabling stable retention of a small critical set.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

Turned inference-time compression from barely usable to genuinely practical—
million-token streaming generation no longer crashes. But every branch of this
lineage shares one action: *evict*. Evicted tokens are truly erased from memory.

At this point, the field considered the problem solved. Then reasoning models arrived.

** 2025 | R-KV: Reasoning Tokens Are Different — Evict More Intelligently

*** What Problem It Saw

Models like OpenAI o1 and DeepSeek-R1 generate thousands of "thinking tokens"
whose importance structure is completely unlike ordinary text—heavily repetitive
in form ("Step 1: …, Step 2: …") yet semantically distinct at every step.
H2O/StreamingLLM treat these repeated phrases as redundant and discard them,
destroying the reasoning chain.

*** Its Solution

Introduce a redundancy penalty: Z = lambda * I - (1 - lambda) * R. Importance I
is measured by attention; redundancy R is measured by cosine similarity between
KV vectors—high similarity lowers the score. Estimated over a window of the
most recent 8 tokens.

Reported result: 10–34% cache budget suffices for lossless reasoning.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

The first KV compression method designed specifically for reasoning; the scoring
scheme is more nuanced.

But three pits remain: (1) it still *evicts*—just more precisely. (2) Looking
only at the last 8 steps produces unstable signals over long chains. (3)
Post-RoPE scoring drifts on long sequences (a flaw later caught by TriAttention).

The deeper pit: everyone in this lineage, from H2O to R-KV, never questioned
the original premise: *if memory is insufficient, eviction is the only option*.

** 2026.05 | SAMH (This Paper): Wait—Why Must We Evict at All?

*** What Problem It Saw

Running R-KV in their own environment at 50% memory budget: accuracy 0–32%.
Cutting the cache in half zeros out GSM8K entirely.

The authors realized something: *the entire field had been asking the wrong
question*. For three years everyone asked "whom should we evict?" But the
fundamental question is "why must we evict at all?" HBM may be scarce, but CPU
DDR is plentiful; PCIe bandwidth is faster than commonly assumed. An evicted
token is not "unimportant"—it is merely "unimportant right now." Five hundred
steps later it may be the linchpin.

*** Its Solution

Structure memory as a *four-tier pyramid*:
- T0 HBM: prompt tokens + attention sinks + recent window + high-importance tokens
- T1 DDR: medium-importance tokens offloaded to CPU, full precision, prefetched
  before each attention computation
- T2 Compressed: 8-bit quantization (experiments find reasoning tokens are
  precision-sensitive; this tier should be used with caution)
- T3 Evicted: lowest-importance tokens, truly discarded

Scoring uses *cumulative* attention scores from the beginning of the sequence
(R-KV looks only at the last 8 steps), because reasoning is non-stationary—a
token may not be revisited until 500 steps later.

Key theorem — "zero approximation error offloading": a token prefetched from
DDR and used in an attention computation produces an identical result to one
that never left HBM. In other words, *moving* a token incurs no precision loss;
*evicting* it does.

This yields a clean conclusion: *accuracy depends only on the eviction ratio,
not on the HBM ratio*. Validated on a 3×3 grid across 7B / 14B / 32B models.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It decouples two things that had always been coupled: "memory budget" and
"reasoning quality." Previously, less HBM = must evict more = model gets dumber.
Now, less HBM means *offload*; only when truly necessary does one *evict*.

The cost is only PCIe transfer overhead—measured at 5–7% latency, in exchange
for a 14B model running at half its HBM footprint while matching the full-cache
baseline (90% vs. 86%).

New pits:
1. The T2 compression tier is largely unusable—reasoning tokens are sensitive
   to quantization; left for future work.
2. Cross-architecture generalization is unverified (only tested on
   DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen variants).
3. Chains that are truly "un-evictable" (e.g., MATH Level-5 proofs of 4K
   tokens) still require 0% eviction, at which point SAMH degenerates to
   pure offloading.

* Frontier Extensions

Parallel and follow-on work, diverging in direction:

- *TriAttention (Mao et al. 2026)*: Catches R-KV's post-RoPE drift problem and
  switches to pre-RoPE triangular-series scoring for stability. Still operates
  within the eviction paradigm.

- *ThinKV (Xu et al. 2025)*: Classifies CoT segments by type and applies
  differentiated compression. Under 5% retention with <4% accuracy loss, but
  everything stays in HBM.

- *Hold Onto That Thought (Weston et al. 2025)*: Benchmarks various KV
  compression methods on the same models; concludes that heavy-hitter approaches
  are strongest—but confirms the fundamental ceiling of eviction.

* Insight

On the surface this lineage is "KV compression getting smarter and smarter."
Underneath, it is the story of *a hidden premise being broken*.

In the three years after H2O, every paper competed on "how to evict more
accurately"—better scoring, finer windows, more stable statistics. The entire
field silently accepted the equation "insufficient memory → must evict." R-KV
already achieved impressive granularity, yet at 50% budget it still collapses—
not because the scoring is imprecise, but because eviction itself has an
absolute ceiling.

SAMH's contribution is not yet another better scoring function (its scoring is
actually the simplest possible: raw cumulative attention). Its contribution is
*refusing the premise*—between "HBM is insufficient" and "data must be deleted,"
there is no logical necessity. A middle tier (DDR + PCIe) existed all along and
was simply overlooked.

This is a classic case of "attacking from an orthogonal dimension": while
everyone optimizes within a two-dimensional plane, stepping back reveals a third
dimension.

More abstractly: when a research line is grinding at the third decimal place,
the real breakthrough is rarely "be a bit cleverer." It is to *return to the
founding implicit assumption and ask "why must this be so?"* The observation
that attention is sparse is real. The heavy-hitter theory is real. But "sparse →
delete" is an unnecessary leap.

Where does the field go next? Three plausible directions:

1. *Make DDR itself obsolete*: as LPDDR/CXL becomes mainstream, offloading
   becomes the default, and the next generation of research questions will
   concern cross-node KV sharing.
2. *Make the T2 compression tier genuinely viable*: SAMH already exposes the
   problem—reasoning tokens are quantization-sensitive. Designing low-precision
   representations specifically for reasoning tokens is an open field.
3. *Predictive prefetching*: the current approach fetches a token when it is
   needed; the next step may be to predict which tokens will be needed next and
   prefetch them in advance—borrowing ideas from CPU cache prefetching.

* Takeaways

This lineage offers three concrete handles on "how to find problems":

1. *Watch for a field's implicit premises* — don't look for problems inside
   papers; look in the assumptions *shared across* papers. Every paper from H2O
   to R-KV accepted eviction. Therefore "why must we evict?" is a gold mine.
   Practically: when you read five papers in the same area, focus on what *none
   of them question*.

2. *Hardware hierarchy is a forgotten dimension* — algorithm researchers see
   only "is memory sufficient?"; systems researchers see HBM / DDR / NVMe /
   network. When an algorithm is stuck optimizing within one tier, crossing tiers
   often opens new space. Practically: in any ML optimization, always ask
   *does this truly have to happen at this tier?*

3. *The simplest method + rejecting a premise beats a complex method +
   accepting it* — SAMH's scoring is the most naïve form of cumulative
   attention, far simpler than R-KV's elaborate formula. Yet because it rejects
   "we must evict," it works far better. Practically: *when your solution is
   grinding at the SOTA boundary, don't add complexity first—question the
   premise first*.
