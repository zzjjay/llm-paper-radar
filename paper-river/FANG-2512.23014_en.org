* The River of Problems

A large model can easily have billions of parameters — but can you cut half of them away and still have it speak coherently?

The question sounds simple. The difficulty lives in three words: "no retraining." If retraining were allowed, the answer is straightforward — prune and then train another round. But retraining a 70B model costs more than most teams can afford. So the real battlefield is: *you can only cut, never patch*.

Then what do you cut? Clip one critical neuron and the entire model collapses. Yet testing every parameter is out of the question — there are simply too many. For over thirty years, generation after generation of researchers have been answering the same question: "*Which parameters can safely be discarded?*" The answers have evolved from Newtonian mechanics (second-order derivatives), through statistics (activation fluctuations), and most recently into functional anatomy (neurons each handling their own domain).

What has truly changed along this trajectory is not the technology — it is the understanding of *"what actually lives inside a model."*

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1989 | OBD: Turning "pruning weights" into a mathematical problem for the first time

*** What problem it saw

Before OBD, neural-network pruning was essentially guesswork — prune the small weights, or delete a few after training and see whether accuracy survived. But "small weight" and "unimportant" are not the same thing: a weight can be tiny yet sit on a pathway that happens to be critical, and removing it causes collapse.

The problem LeCun identified was: *nobody could answer "if I remove this weight, by how much will the loss increase?"*

*** Its solution

Expand the loss function as a Taylor series around the current weights. The first-order term is the gradient, which is approximately zero at convergence, so it can be ignored. What remains is the second-order term — the Hessian matrix. OBD assumes the Hessian is diagonal (weights are mutually independent), so the "deletion cost" of each weight becomes 0.5 × h_ii × w_i² — a remarkably clean formula.

Sort by that score from smallest to largest, prune the bottom k, and in theory the impact on the loss is minimized.

*** Why it worked (and what new trap it set)

For the first time it turned "importance" — a subjective notion — into an objectively computable number. That mathematical instinct has persisted to this day: every method that follows computes some form of "importance score."

But it buried a significant trap: *the diagonal Hessian assumption is far too crude for large models*, and the true Hessian is N×N (N = number of parameters) — for a 70B model that is 70B × 70B, impossible to store let alone invert. OBD worked fine on MNIST; in the LLM era it simply cannot run.

For thirty years researchers kept grappling with one question: *"Do we even need the Hessian? And if so, how can we afford to compute it?"*

** 2023 | SparseGPT: Decomposing a global problem into layer-by-layer subproblems

*** What problem it saw

By 2023, everyone wanted to prune GPT-3- and LLaMA-scale models. The global second-order methods of OBD/OBS were completely intractable — the Hessian complexity exploded. LLM-Pruner (2023) attempted gradient approximations, but with limited success.

The problem Frantar and Alistarh identified was: *nobody could prune a 175B model to 50% sparsity without retraining and still preserve performance.*

*** Its solution

Pivot the perspective entirely: abandon "minimize the global loss," and instead minimize "per-layer reconstruction error."

Concretely, for each layer W, find a sparse W̃ that minimizes ||WX - W̃X||². Here X is the activations produced by a small calibration batch after passing through the preceding layers. This objective cares only about whether the output looks the same, not about the global loss.

The key insight: within the per-layer framework the Hessian shrinks to X·X^T — a small matrix that can be inverted — enabling OBS-style compensation: *after removing one weight, redistribute its "responsibility" to the remaining weights.*

*** Why it worked (and what new trap it set)

The coupling between layers is severed by the assumption that "activations are ground truth": as long as each layer's output closely approximates the original, the model as a whole will not collapse. This made it possible to prune 175B to 50% sparsity in a matter of hours.

But it set a new trap: *the resulting matrix is unstructured sparse* — zeros scattered randomly throughout the weights. There is no dedicated hardware support on GPUs, so inference is no faster than with the dense original.

Academically beautiful; engineeringly useless. The next paper had to fix this.

** 2023 | OBC (Structured): Switching from scattered deletion to whole-row/column removal

*** What problem it saw

SparseGPT's sparse matrices cannot be accelerated on general-purpose GPUs, making the pruning worthless in practice. To actually save memory and improve inference speed, *entire rows or columns must be removed together* — structured pruning.

But structured pruning has a long-standing difficulty: the granularity is coarse. Dropping an entire column of weights causes much larger damage than dropping a handful of scattered individual weights. The problem Kurtić et al. identified was: *how to graft the fine-grained OBS compensation mechanism onto coarse-grained deletion.*

*** Its solution

Recast OBC's objective from "which weight should be removed" to "which *channel* should be removed." The same per-layer reconstruction-error formula, the same second-order compensation — but now instead of correcting a single weight, the remaining columns collectively "make up" the output that the deleted column used to produce.

Mathematically, this generalizes the single-weight OBS formula to the channel dimension, paired with a GPU-friendly implementation that keeps the computation feasible.

*** Why it worked (and what new trap it set)

It addressed the hard engineering requirement of hardware-accelerable pruning, bringing structured pruning back onto the stage. The cost is a slight accuracy drop compared to SparseGPT — a few percentage points — but in return comes genuine inference speedup.

New trap: *OBC still requires inverting a Hessian* (even though it is a small within-layer one), with O(N³) complexity in the number of channels. When the FFN hidden dimension of LLaMA reaches tens of thousands, this is still slow. Moreover, OBC looks only at the weight structure, *not at activation statistics* — the same weight behaves completely differently across different tokens, and OBC averages all of that away.

** 2024 | FLAP: Forget the Hessian — activation fluctuation is enough

*** What problem it saw

An et al. posed a provocative question to the entire OBC/SparseGPT lineage: *do we really need the Hessian?*

They observed something concrete: many channels produce output activations that vary almost not at all across different inputs — near-constant signals. Such channels carry very little information; removing them barely affects downstream behavior. Conversely, channels whose activations fluctuate widely are precisely the ones the model uses to distinguish inputs.

The sharper point: OBC-style compensation requires calibration data *and the inversion of a Hessian matrix*, which can take hours on a 70B model — hostile to industrial deployment.

*** Its solution

Use activation fluctuation directly as the importance metric. Concretely: for each channel, compute the variance of its activations across the calibration set (the "fluctuation"); prune those with the smallest variance.

No Hessian, no compensation, almost zero overhead. A single machine can prune 70B in a few minutes.

*** Why it worked (and what new trap it set)

The intuition that "higher fluctuation means higher importance" is sound: a neuron that always outputs 0.5 versus one that swings between −10 and +10 depending on the input — clearly the latter is doing more complex work.

But FLAP left a fundamental trap: *it relies on the calibration set to estimate "fluctuation."* If your calibration set is drawn from WikiText, the fluctuation estimates reflect WikiText's statistical properties. If the model is actually deployed for code generation, mathematical reasoning, or medical Q&A, those estimates may be entirely off-target: *some neurons are quiet on WikiText but are critical nodes for code — and you just pruned them by mistake.*

FANG is the paper that sees exactly this.

** 2025 | FANG: Neurons each handle their own domain — one yardstick cannot measure all of them

*** What problem it saw

Looking at FLAP/OBC/SparseGPT as a lineage, every method implicitly assumes *"a neuron has a single importance score that applies uniformly to all inputs."* The calibration set is merely a tool for estimating that score.

FANG's diagnosis: this assumption is wrong.

Findings from interpretability research (a series of works in 2023–2024) reveal that *neurons in LLMs are functionally specialized* — some handle code syntax, others scientific terminology, others emotional tone. This is analogous to Broca's area managing language and the hippocampus managing memory in the brain.

If you calibrate only on WikiText (predominantly prose), neurons that process code will appear to be low-activation and receive low importance scores — and get pruned. When the model is later deployed to write code, those pruned neurons turn out to be the critical specialists. *You haven't been pruning; you've been selectively erasing.*

This is the essence of what the paper calls the "calibration set distribution mismatch" problem.

*** Its solution

Three steps:

1. *Group neurons by function.* Use K-means to cluster the token representations of the calibration set into K groups (K=7 in the paper), representing K "context types" — think of them as prose, code, Q&A, lists, terminology, numerics, and dialogue. Then for each neuron, compute its contribution to each token group (using Taylor-expansion sensitivity scores), forming a K × N matrix. Apply the *Linear Assignment Problem (LAP)* algorithm to assign each neuron to one of the K groups — so every group consists of "specialists for that context type."

2. *Prune within each group independently, with group-relevance weighting.* When pruning the "code specialist group," tokens in the calibration set that are semantically closer to code receive higher weight; vice versa for the "prose specialist group." This way, importance estimates for each group *reflect only the context that group is responsible for*, avoiding dilution by irrelevant data.

3. *Retain a shared-neuron group.* Some neurons score highly across multiple groups — they are "generalists" and are never pruned. Additionally, apply *adaptive sparsity allocation*: layers with higher functional complexity are pruned less; simpler layers are pruned more, with complexity measured by the cosine similarity between a layer's input and output representations.

Finally, within-group pruning still uses OBC or FLAP mechanics — FANG does not replace them. What it replaces is *how importance is defined* in the first place.

*** Why it worked (and what new trap it set)

It transforms pruning from *"assign a single score to every neuron collectively"* into *"assign scores within each functional partition."* This amounts to acknowledging that a model is not a homogeneous paste, but an organ with internal anatomy.

Empirically, FANG pushes the downstream accuracy of FLAP and OBC up by 1.5%–8.5% — at 30%/40% sparsity, that magnitude is enough to move zero-shot performance of a pruned 70B model from the 60–65% range to the 68–71% range.

New traps it sets:
- Functional grouping is derived from clustering calibration-set tokens — *if the calibration set never covers a certain context type, the "specialist neurons" for that context will still be misclassified as "universally low-activity."* The problem is pushed one level back, not eliminated.
- K=7 is set manually. The true functional partitions may far exceed 7 and may be hierarchically nested.
- For every new model, the grouping must be redone from scratch — non-trivial transfer cost.

* Frontier Extensions

FANG is the most recent paper on this trajectory (arXiv late December 2025, AAAI 2026). As of this writing, no later paper has specifically advanced "function-aware grouped pruning" a further step.

But several directions worth watching are already building in parallel:
- *Learned grouping*: let the model learn its own functional partitions rather than imposing them via clustering after the fact;
- *Cross-model functional transfer*: can the functional groups identified in LLaMA be carried directly over to Qwen?
- *Sparse activation routing (MoE) as a cognate idea*: MoE training forces neurons into distinct "experts" from the start — equivalent to FANG's functional partitioning done before pruning. These two paths may eventually converge.

* Insights

Flatten the entire trajectory and look at it all at once: what has truly evolved is not the algorithm, but *"what the model is being treated as"*:

| Era   | Model treated as              | Pruning metaphor                        |
|-------+-------------------------------+-----------------------------------------|
| 1989  | A mass of weights             | Trimming redundant connections          |
| 2023  | A stack of matrix layers      | Keep each layer's output unchanged      |
| 2024  | A set of activation channels  | Cut the silent channels                 |
| 2025  | An organ with a division of labor | Don't touch the cells of vital organs |

*The further along the timeline, the more a model looks to researchers like a structured living organism rather than a mathematical object to be optimized.* Behind this shift is the feedback from two years of interpretability research (feature circuits, neuron functional analysis) — interpretability is not merely post-hoc explanation; it is *reshaping the methodology of hands-on operations like pruning, quantization, and distillation.*

Where is the most likely next step? Two directions, in my estimation:
1. *Merging pruning with routing.* FANG groups neurons at prune-time; MoE groups them at train-time. The next step may be "learn the routing while pruning" — the result is not a smaller model, but a model that "activates different subnetworks depending on the context."
2. *Standardizing functional atlases.* FANG's K=7 is empirical. If someone could produce a stable, reproducible "functional atlas" for mainstream LLMs (analogous to a brain atlas), then pruning, fine-tuning, and safety alignment could all perform precise surgery on that map — without rediscovering the anatomy each time.

* Takeaways

Two points for "how to do research":

1. *The sharpest papers often invent no new tools — they change the coordinate system.* FANG invented no new importance metric and did not alter the mathematics of OBC or FLAP. It simply pulled out the implicit assumption of *"which neurons should be scored together"* and recut along a different axis. If you cannot find a breakthrough in a seemingly saturated field, go back and audit the silent assumptions the entire field shares — those are the gold mine.

2. *Cross-subdomain transfer frequently ignites new methods.* The interpretability community only finished working out "neuronal functional specialization" in 2023; by 2025 it was already rewriting pruning. Keep your eye on findings that *have matured in domain A but have not yet been applied in domain B* — that is where the arbitrage opportunity lives.

Grounding these points in practice:
- If you are working on LLM deployment or compression, *stop calibrating everything with a single WikiText corpus.* Whatever your downstream task is, tilt the calibration set in that direction; FANG's codebase is worth forking as a starting point.
- If you are doing interpretability, *actively expose the "functional modules" you discover to the people doing model operations downstream* — quantization, pruning, and fine-tuning are all waiting for your atlas.
- If you are choosing a research direction, look at the triangle of "training — inference — compression": all three are being re-unified under the same "functional partitioning" framework. Whoever writes the paper that brings all three together first will claim a new set of coordinates.
