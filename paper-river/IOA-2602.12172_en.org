* The River of the Problem

How do you teach a small model with a few billion parameters everything a large model with hundreds of billions of parameters knows?

It sounds simple enough — have the large model generate data, hand it to the small model, and call it done. But a decade of work has shown the road keeps bending: from copying probability distributions, to feeding synthetic instructions, to selecting hard examples, to appending chain-of-thought, to maximizing data diversity. Each new trick buys a few more points on the benchmark, yet nobody has been able to say clearly what "teaching" is actually supposed to look like. This paper's bet: drag pedagogy into the picture. Humans have been teaching other humans for thousands of years — surely there is something worth borrowing.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Hinton's Soft Labels: Where Distillation Began

*** What Problem It Saw

At the time, training a neural network meant pairing each image with a hard label: "this is a cat, label 1." But Hinton noticed that a well-trained large model doesn't output 0/1 at inference — it outputs a probability distribution: "90% cat, 8% tiger, 2% dog." Those 8% and 2% carry real information, silently telling the student "cats and tigers look alike; cats and dogs don't." A small model trained on hard labels can never learn this similarity structure.

*** Its Solution

Let the small model directly learn the teacher's probability outputs. Add a temperature parameter T to "soften" the distribution so that weak signals like 8% and 2% become more legible. In essence, the teacher no longer just announces the answer; it also explains why this answer is right and why the others are mostly wrong.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Left)

It upgraded "knowledge" from discrete labels to continuous distributions. But there is a hidden assumption: you must be able to access the teacher's probability output at every step. If the teacher is a closed-source API that only returns text, this approach fails immediately.

** 2020 | TinyBERT / MobileBERT: Copy Not Just the Output but the Whole Reasoning Circuit

*** What Problem It Saw

By the BERT era, models had a dozen or more Transformer layers. Aligning only the final layer's output is like letting the student see only the teacher's finished answer, never the working. All the grammatical, syntactic, and semantic knowledge encoded in the intermediate layers went to waste.

*** Its Solution

Layer-by-layer alignment. Let the student's i-th layer imitate the teacher's j-th layer, matching both the attention matrices and the hidden states. The student doesn't just copy the answer — it copies every intermediate draft along the way.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Left)

This was the pinnacle of white-box distillation — treating the teacher's internal states as a teaching resource. The price: you must be able to open the teacher's skull. Then GPT-3 and GPT-4 arrived, and OpenAI welded it shut.

** 2023 | Alpaca + Self-Instruct: A Pragmatic Response to the Closed-Source Era

*** What Problem It Saw

The strongest teachers — GPT-4, Claude — are APIs. All you get is text in and text out; logits and intermediate layers are gone. Every white-box distillation method accumulated over the previous decade became useless overnight.

*** Its Solution

Stop looking inside the teacher altogether. Self-Instruct has a large model generate a large batch of (instruction, response) pairs; Alpaca uses 52K such synthetic examples to fine-tune LLaMA-7B. The entire paradigm retreats from "align internal representations" to "have the student imitate teacher input–output behavior."

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Left)

Blunt but effective — LLaMA-7B fine-tuned on Alpaca data could convincingly pretend to be a ChatGPT. The pitfall is equally obvious: synthetic data is a uniform stew, variable in quality, unordered in difficulty, and random in coverage. Whatever the student eats depends entirely on what the teacher happened to write that day.

** 2023 | Lion + Distilling Step-by-Step: Seasoning the Stew

*** What Problem It Saw

Self-Instruct samples uniformly, but what the student needs most is not uniformly distributed — it needs the slice it "just barely can't handle." Easy examples waste time; impossibly hard ones cause the student to give up. On top of that, seeing only a final answer gives the student no access to the reasoning path.

*** Its Solution

Lion takes the adversarial route: let the student attempt each question, identify where it fails, then ask the teacher to generate more examples of precisely that type. Distilling Step-by-Step (DSS) takes the chain-of-thought route: have the teacher emit the full reasoning trace, so the student learns not just the answer but the process.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Left)

For the first time, "targeting" and "process orientation" were built into synthetic data generation. But a fundamental issue remained: data is synthesized in one shot, or at most iteratively for a few rounds. Nobody designed it as a *curriculum* — deciding what to teach first, what to teach next, when to raise the difficulty — all of that was still left to intuition.

** 2024 | MetaMath + CounterDistill: Squeezing Every Drop from Each Example

*** What Problem It Saw

Data volume had grown, but diversity was still lacking. A student that sees 100 equation problems all phrased the same way will freeze when the wording changes.

*** Its Solution

MetaMath takes a seed problem and rewrites it from multiple perspectives — forward, backward, with different numbers, in different contexts. CounterDistill adds counterfactual variants — "if this condition changed, how would the answer change?" Both are doing "local perturbation of knowledge," ensuring the student sees the same concept wearing many different faces.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Left)

This step addressed the "breadth" problem. But the root issue remained: no ordering, no diagnosis, no adaptation. In front of the student sits a pile of carefully crafted but randomly stacked workbooks.

** 2025 | POCL + Progressive Distillation: Remembering to Sort Things

*** What Problem It Saw

What is at the core of human education? Order. First grade doesn't start with calculus. Yet every synthetic-data method before this treated data as a static pool — shuffle, feed. Nobody sorted by difficulty or organized by conceptual dependencies.

*** Its Solution

Introduce curriculum learning: rank examples by a "difficulty" metric (e.g., KL divergence between teacher and student output distributions), then train from easy to hard. Progressive Distillation goes further — it uses intermediate training checkpoints of the teacher as progressively harder targets, letting the student close the gap one step at a time.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Left)

A rudimentary "teaching process" finally emerged. But this entire line is white-box — you either need the teacher's probability distributions (POCL needs KL) or the teacher's intermediate training checkpoints (Progressive Distillation). For the most powerful closed-source teachers (OpenAI o1, Gemini 2.5), none of this works.

* 2026 | IOA: Plugging a Full Pedagogical System into Black-Box Distillation

*** What Problem It Saw

By late 2025 the landscape looked like this:
- The black-box synthesis camp (Alpaca → Lion → MetaMath) had data but no pedagogy
- The white-box curriculum camp (POCL → Progressive Distillation) had pedagogy but only worked with open-source teachers
- The gap in the middle — "strongest closed-source teacher + genuine teaching process" — was empty

And even within the white-box curriculum camp, the understanding of "teaching" was still primitive: just sort by difficulty. But thousands of years of human pedagogy offer something more systematic: Bloom's mastery learning (don't move on until prerequisites are mastered) and Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (instruction should be slightly ahead of the student's current level — not too far).

*** Its Solution

Decompose distillation explicitly into three stages, each corresponding to one pedagogical act:

1. *Knowledge Identifier (Diagnosis)*: Run the student model over a batch of problems and observe where its knowledge gaps lie. Direct synthetic data generation to target those gaps specifically. — Corresponds to "diagnosing the learner."
2. *Knowledge Organizer (Curriculum Design)*: Arrange the generated data into a curriculum ordered by prerequisite dependencies and difficulty gradient. The student must master A before advancing to B (Bloom's mastery learning), and the difficulty increment at each step is controlled (Vygotsky's ZPD). — Corresponds to "curriculum design."
3. *Knowledge Adapter (Differentiated Instruction)*: For a given knowledge point, the teacher's explanation may be highly abstract (it is, after all, a large model). The Adapter rewrites those explanations into versions calibrated to the student's current cognitive level, simplifying derivation chains and reducing abstraction. — Corresponds to "teaching to the learner."

Crucially, all three steps operate entirely at the text level — no teacher logits, no intermediate layers, no training checkpoints required. This means the strongest closed-source teachers can be used.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfalls Remain)

It transforms "teaching" from a metaphor into an executable three-stage pipeline. The reported numbers are strong — 94.7% of teacher performance at less than 1/10 the parameter count, MATH +19.2%, HumanEval +22.3%.

But the pitfalls are clear:
- All three stages depend on the teacher model itself to execute (diagnosis, curriculum ordering, rewriting). What if the teacher isn't naturally good at pedagogy?
- "The student's current cognitive level" is a fuzzy concept — how to quantify it, and how to update it dynamically, is handled in fairly heuristic ways in the paper
- Bloom and Vygotsky are theories of human learning; whether they are structurally isomorphic to what happens inside language models has never been proven

* Frontier Extensions

As of February 2026, IOA is the latest paper on this line. Foreseeable next steps:
- *Bidirectional adaptation*: The student model feeds back to the teacher on exactly where it didn't follow, letting the teacher adjust in real time — moving toward genuinely dialogic instruction
- *Meta-curriculum*: Instead of hand-specifying "use Bloom + Vygotsky," let the model learn for itself which pedagogical strategy works for which student
- *Formalization of pedagogy*: Translate these educational-theory concepts into optimizable objective functions rather than a few sentences in a prompt template

* Insight

On the surface, this ten-year trajectory is "the evolution of distillation techniques." Underneath, it is *the definition of "knowledge" being rewritten again and again*.

- In Hinton's view, knowledge = a probability distribution
- In TinyBERT's view, knowledge = intermediate representations
- In Alpaca's view, knowledge = behavioral imitation (input-to-output mapping)
- In Lion / DSS's view, knowledge = hard examples + reasoning processes
- In MetaMath's view, knowledge = multi-perspective coverage of the same concept
- In POCL's view, knowledge = an ordered learning path
- In IOA's view, knowledge = a complete dynamic "teaching–learning" process

Each generation is not merely a technical upgrade; it is a deeper understanding of what "teaching" actually is — from treating it as "compressing the teacher," to "imitating the teacher," to "the teacher actively instructing the student."

The next step is almost certainly to add "student agency" — the student can ask questions, say it doesn't understand, propose counterexamples. When distillation becomes a two-way dialogue rather than a one-way broadcast, this line will no longer be called distillation; it will merge with the stream of agent learning.

* Inspiration

Pedagogy is not a metaphor — it is an engineering resource waiting to be tapped.

Human teaching has over a thousand years of accumulated method: Socratic questioning, Bloom's taxonomy, Vygotsky's ZPD, Thorndike's law of exercise, Gagné's nine events of instruction. The AI community has long used these as slogans; IOA is the first work to seriously translate two of them into executable pipelines. This means you can:

- Continue translating other teaching methods (scaffolding, spaced repetition, deliberate practice) into distillation pipelines — each one is potentially a paper
- Run it in reverse: experimental results from AI distillation can feed back into education research — which theories genuinely work and which are historical baggage can be tested at machine-experiment cost, which is a hundred thousand times cheaper than human experiments

At a deeper level: whenever a field falls into "metric arms-racing," the fastest way to break out is not to add one more technical trick but to borrow a time-tested framework from a neighboring discipline. That is exactly the path IOA takes.
