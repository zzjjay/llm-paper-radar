* The Problem River

A large MoE model contains hundreds of small "expert" networks, only a handful of which fire on any given token. The trouble is: every expert must still sit in GPU memory. Can we throw away half the experts and keep the model essentially intact?

The problem is hard because two obvious paths both look right and both fall short. The first is *pruning*—drop infrequently used experts outright. Clean and simple, but the knowledge those experts carried vanishes with them. The second is *merging*—average similar experts into one, preserving knowledge in principle, yet the fused expert often ends up a muddled compromise that performs worse than plain pruning. For a decade the two camps kept trading wins. REAM argues there was never any need to pick a side: you can make merging look like pruning.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1991 | Jacobs MoE: Division of Labor Among Networks

*** What problem did it see?

Early neural networks were "one network does it all." A single network had to learn to recognize cats, cars, and faces simultaneously, and ended up mediocre at everything. Worse, learning a new task would overwrite old ones.

*** Its solution

Jacobs et al. hit on a natural idea: instead of forcing one network to handle everything, train a pool of small networks (experts) plus a "gate" that decides which expert handles each input. Over time each expert gravitates toward the slice of the input space it handles best.

*** Why it worked (and what new trouble it buried)

Division of labor leading to specialization is intuitively sound. But this version was an academic toy—few parameters, few experts. Nobody imagined that twenty-some years later the idea would be scaled to hundreds of experts and hundreds of billions of parameters, then collide with the brand-new problem of "doesn't fit in GPU memory."

** 2017 | Shazeer Sparsely-Gated MoE: Making MoE Actually Scale

*** What problem did it see?

By the deep-learning era, practitioners had discovered an iron rule: bigger models work better. But bigger models run slower and cost more. Could you have "many parameters but only compute a fraction each step"? The idea—conditional computation—had been around since Bengio 2013, but nobody had made it run efficiently on GPUs; sparse activation and GPU parallelism are naturally at odds.

*** Its solution

Shazeer inserted Jacobs-style MoE layers between LSTM blocks and made two decisive changes: (1) each token activates only the top-k experts (say, 2 or 4), with all others treated as absent for that step; (2) a load-balancing loss forces all experts to be used, preventing a few from monopolizing all tokens.

The result: models could scale to 137 billion parameters while the actual FLOPs per token barely increased—capacity exploded with almost no extra compute.

*** Why it worked (and what new trouble it buried)

Top-k sparsity plus load balancing are the two pillars that made MoE viable. Every subsequent MoE LLM (Switch Transformer, Mixtral, DeepSeek-MoE, Qwen3-MoE, GLM4.5-MoE) is essentially this recipe amplified.

But here lies the seed of the REAM river: activating only a few experts saves compute, yet every expert's weights must still reside in GPU memory. As models grew ever larger (routinely exceeding a hundred billion parameters), deployment cost actually exceeded that of dense models. This is the question that all subsequent "MoE compression" papers spend the next decade trying to answer.

** 2022 | Chen TSEP: First Wave—Just Prune Them

*** What problem did it see?

After Shazeer, people built ever-larger MoEs. The deployment problem crystallized: fitting these models on ordinary GPUs was simply impossible. But when users run downstream tasks—classification, question answering—do they actually need every expert?

Chen et al. ran an experiment: the same pretrained MoE on MNLI, CoLA, and SQuAD showed dramatically different expert activation distributions across tasks. In other words, for any specific task, most experts are essentially decorative.

*** Its solution

So prune them. During downstream fine-tuning, slice the training into E phases (E = number of experts). At the end of each phase, remove a batch of the "least specialized" experts—judged by how many tokens the router sends their way—until only one expert per layer remains and the MoE degenerates into a dense model.

*** Why it worked (and what new trouble it buried)

Experiments showed 99.3% of performance retained at 2× inference speedup, proving that MoEs contain substantial redundancy.

Three pits remain, however: (1) the pruned model is task-specific; switching tasks requires re-pruning; (2) fine-tuning is mandatory, which is computationally expensive; (3) once pruned, the knowledge inside those experts is gone forever. For "general-purpose LLM deployment," this is far too costly. The next generation of methods must answer: can we keep the knowledge instead of discarding it?

** 2024 | MC-SMoE: Merge to Retain Knowledge

*** What problem did it see?

Li et al. identified two hard limitations of the TSEP line: mandatory fine-tuning, and irreversible knowledge loss upon pruning. They proposed the opposite approach: instead of discarding similar experts, *fuse* them into one.

But a pilot experiment revealed something counterintuitive: naively importing standard model-merging techniques (e.g., simple weight averaging) performs poorly on MoEs. Two reasons: (1) redundant experts dilute the signal from critical ones; (2) neurons in different experts are permuted differently, so a hard average is essentially scrambling them.

*** Its solution

M-SMoE does three things: (1) *neuron permutation alignment*—find which neuron in expert A corresponds to which in expert B before merging; (2) identify a "dominant expert" per group from router activation frequencies, and fold the others in with frequency-weighted averaging; (3) notice that merged expert weights naturally have low-rank structure, then apply SVD to compress further.

The full pipeline is MC-SMoE: first Merge, then Compress.

*** Why it worked (and what new trouble it buried)

It delivered "knowledge retention" in practice with up to 80% parameter reduction.

But it depends on router statistics to form groups. The router is trained or calibrated on a particular dataset—change the dataset and the groupings may shift entirely. MC-SMoE is therefore unstable in a task-agnostic setting, which is exactly what HC-SMoE targets next.

** 2025 | HC-SMoE: Ignore the Router, Watch the Output

*** What problem did it see?

MC-SMoE uses router signals to decide which experts are "similar." But the router itself may be contaminated by bias—some experts happen to be selected frequently on the calibration data without actually being functionally close. This causes grouping instability across tasks.

*** Its solution

Chen et al. replaced the grouping criterion: instead of looking at how the router decides, look at whether experts produce *similar outputs* given the same inputs. They then apply *hierarchical clustering*, bottom-up, to progressively merge functionally similar experts. No retraining required.

A second detail: they use "static grouping"—each layer retains exactly r experts—rather than the dynamic grouping of M-SMoE (which might leave 3 experts in one layer and 8 in another), making deployment more predictable.

*** Why it worked (and what new trouble it buried)

Redefining "similar" from "routing-similar" to "functionally-similar" is semantically cleaner. HC-SMoE outperforms prior methods on Qwen and Mixtral.

Yet HC-SMoE pushed the entire merging camp into an awkward position: on multiple-choice (MC) discriminative tasks the merging approach looks good; on generation (GEN) tasks, performance drops badly. This phenomenon became the detonator for the next paper, REAP.

** 2025 | REAP: Merging Has Original Sin—Go Back to Pruning

*** What problem did it see?

Lasby et al. at Cerebras ran a rigorous controlled comparison: the same MoE models, subjected to M-SMoE, HC-SMoE, and other merging methods alongside various pruning methods, evaluated on *generation tasks*. Merging lost systematically and by a non-trivial margin. More importantly, they wanted to understand *why*.

*** Its solution

They proved theoretically that MoE merging carries *irreducible error*—which they call "functional subspace collapse." The intuition: the essence of MoE is that the router can *independently* modulate each expert's contribution for each input; each expert is an independent functional dimension. Once two experts are fused into one, the router can no longer control them independently—that dimension of the functional space collapses permanently. Generation tasks depend on fine-grained token-by-token control, so collapse is immediately visible. Discriminative tasks only need a correct final answer and are far less sensitive.

So just prune properly. REAP assigns each expert a salience score: router weight × output activation norm. This simultaneously captures "how often it is selected" and "how large its effect is when selected." Low scorers are removed; high scorers are kept.

*** Why it worked (and what new trouble it buried)

On Qwen3-Coder-480B, pruning 50% of experts retains 97.6% of coding ability. "Pruning wins" seemed like a settled verdict.

Yet two assumptions in the argument remain attackable: (1) the claim that merging necessarily causes collapse assumes all merging methods share a single gate weight—but that need not be true; (2) REAP does lose to some merging methods on MC tasks, meaning the pruned experts genuinely contained knowledge useful for MC questions. REAM enters through exactly these two cracks.

** 2026 | REAM: Make Merging Look Like Pruning

*** What problem did it see?

Jha et al. spotted a small seam in REAP's argument: REAP wins on generation because when it removes experts it *also removes those experts' router rows*, so the router truly controls only the surviving experts. Traditional merging methods average the router rows too, which is exactly what causes the collapse.

What if there were a merging method that, structurally, looks like pruning—retaining only the router rows of a small number of "center experts"—while still absorbing the knowledge of the merged-away experts? Could it avoid both collapse and knowledge loss?

*** Its solution

REAM has four components, each nudging "merging" toward "pruning":

1. *Upgraded similarity*: Merging requires deciding which experts are similar. REAM combines router-signal similarity (gate logits) with expert-output similarity (output cosine). Both what the router thinks and what the experts actually compute are considered together, giving a more reliable grouping.

2. *Pseudo-pruning grouping*: Traditional merging forces all experts into fixed-size groups. REAM does the opposite—it allows "a few large groups plus many singleton groups." A singleton expert is simply one that wasn't merged, i.e., it is effectively kept as-is. This topology closely resembles what remains after REAP pruning: most experts survive independently; only the genuinely redundant ones are fused together.

3. *Smarter alignment*: Before merging, neuron permutation alignment is needed (MC-SMoE introduced this). REAM computes the neuron-to-neuron correspondence using both activation values and weights jointly, yielding a more accurate assignment.

4. *Layer-by-layer sequential merging*: After each layer is merged, a fresh forward pass is run so that updated statistics are used when merging the next layer. This prevents error accumulation.

Most critically: like REAP, REAM *deletes the router rows* corresponding to the merged-away experts and retains only the center expert's gate weights. This is what sidesteps the "collapse" that REAP identified theoretically.

*** Why it worked (and what new trouble it buried)

Experiments on Qwen3-MoE and GLM4.5 span 14 benchmarks (8 MC + 6 GEN). Results: at 25% compression REAM nearly matches the original model; at 50% compression REAM consistently beats REAP on MC tasks and is competitive on GEN as well.

A more interesting side finding: MC vs. GEN performance depends strongly on the *mixture ratio of calibration data*. More code in the calibration set improves GEN; more general text improves MC. The two live on a Pareto frontier that requires a trade-off. REAM dominates the baseline across most mixture ratios.

The remaining pit: calibration-data sensitivity means "deploy one universally optimal compressed MoE" has no clean solution yet—you need to know what users will primarily do with it. That is the question for the next generation of methods.

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026 (roughly seven weeks after the paper appeared), no obvious follow-up work has directly critiqued or improved on REAM. This is the most recent stop on the river.

Several directions are foreseeable: (1) automating calibration-data sensitivity—letting the model decide what calibration data each layer needs; (2) joint merging + quantization—REAM itself notes these approaches are complementary; (3) dynamic merging—deciding the merging scheme on the fly at inference time based on actual input, rather than performing a single static compression upfront.

* Insight

On the surface this river is a tug-of-war between "prune camp" and "merge camp," but what is really shifting underneath is *the field's understanding of what MoE fundamentally is*.

Phase 1 (TSEP era): MoE is treated as a simple stack of functional modules. Drop the ones that aren't used.

Phase 2 (M-SMoE / HC-SMoE era): Recognition that experts overlap and shouldn't be discarded carelessly. But the router is still treated as an afterthought—an accessory, not the soul of MoE.

Phase 3 (REAP): A window is punched open. The essence of MoE is not "many experts" but " *the router's independent control over each expert*." The moment you merge two experts' router rows together, that independent control is gone and the MoE design itself is broken. This insight is the deepest cut made on this river in 2025.

Phase 4 (REAM): The two things that seemed incompatible—"preserve independent control" and "preserve knowledge"—are decoupled by a small structural trick (pseudo-pruning grouping + router-row deletion). This is a textbook example of "what looked like a forced binary choice turns out to be a false dilemma."

Broader lesson: when a field develops two "opposing camps," the next breakthrough usually isn't one camp winning outright. It's someone identifying that the premise underlying their opposition is flawed. In this river REAP exposed a hidden premise (merging inevitably drags router rows along); REAM then used that premise as a lever to break it.

Likely next step: now that the router's "independent control" has been recognized as the core of MoE, future research may loop back to the router itself—can compression also *optimize* the router rather than treating it as a passive bystander? Every current compression method does the latter.

* Takeaways

*For doing research*: On a question that appears "settled," look for the hidden assumptions inside that settling paper's argument. REAP's proof rests on the premise "merging methods share gate weights." That premise doesn't appear in the conclusion—it hides in the math. Pulling it out and attacking it is exactly how new research space opens up.

*For finding problems*: When a field presents a trade-off (prune vs. merge, MC vs. GEN), resist picking a side immediately. Ask first: "Is this trade-off physically inevitable, or merely an artifact of current methods?" REAM showed it was the latter—the apparent prune-vs.-merge opposition existed only because nobody had tried "merging that looks like pruning."

*One concrete action item*: If you are deploying an MoE model (Qwen3-MoE, GLM4.5, Mixtral, etc.), both REAM and REAP are now open-source (REAM at SamsungSAILMontreal/ream, REAP at CerebrasResearch/reap). At 25% compression both are essentially lossless—worth trying directly. At 50% compression, consider your downstream workload: if it skews toward knowledge QA, REAM has the edge on MC tasks; if it skews toward open-ended generation or coding, REAP holds a slight advantage today, though REAM is close. Either way, calibration-data composition matters far more than you might expect—align it with your actual use case.
