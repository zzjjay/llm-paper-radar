* What this river is about

A very old question: among the billions of weights in a neural network, which numbers actually matter?

For over thirty years this question has been asked repeatedly, each generation squeezing the network in a different way. From LeCun using second-order derivatives in 1990 to identify "connections that can be deleted," to Han pruning entire models by ninety percent in 2015 and still getting them to run, to GPTQ/AWQ/AQLM compressing weights down to 2–3 bits between 2022 and 2024—each answer has been more radical than the last: "turns out even more things are unnecessary."

But all of these methods are doing the same thing: *preserving the numerical values of weights as faithfully as possible*. Even at 4-bit quantization, the goal is "make the quantized value as close to the original as possible." RankClust, a 2026 paper, does the opposite—it says: forget the values, *only relative magnitude (rank) matters*. Pack all the weights in a layer into 32 buckets (K-means), then fill those buckets with any 32 numbers you like, as long as "the bucket that was 5th largest stays 5th largest"—and the model barely loses any accuracy. But scramble that rank, and even if every bucket's mean and variance match perfectly, the model collapses immediately.

The story this river tells is not "compression methods keep getting better," but rather "we are gradually discovering that the things truly carrying information in neural networks are far fewer than we thought—and not where we thought they were."

* Paper 1: [1990 OBD] — The first formal statement that "not all weights matter"

When LeCun wrote Optimal Brain Damage in 1990, neural networks were still small. After training a network, everyone kept all the weights intact—because nobody thought this was a question worth asking.

LeCun posed an uncomfortable question: in a trained network, is every weight actually doing something? The tool he offered was the second derivative of the loss with respect to each weight—high curvature means a perturbation raises the loss a lot, so the weight is important; low curvature means deleting it barely affects the loss.

This work turned an intuition into the first computable metric: *weights are not created equal*.

But OBD left two problems open:
1. Computing second-order derivatives is prohibitively expensive; the full-network Hessian is intractable.
2. It only answers "which weights are unimportant," not "for the important ones, what exactly makes them important?"

The first problem forced later researchers to find cheap approximations; the second problem—that is the thread RankClust picks up thirty years later.

* Paper 2: [2015 Magnitude Pruning] — Replace the expensive metric with a cheap one

Han et al.'s first pruning paper in 2015 ("Learning both Weights and Connections") did something that sounds almost foolish: *forget the second-order derivatives—just look at the absolute value*. Weights with small absolute values are set to zero.

Why does this "dumb" approach work? Because at a well-trained optimum, the first-order gradient is close to zero, and weight magnitude is then approximately linearly correlated with its contribution to the output. LeCun needed the Hessian because he was working on small, not-yet-converged models; Han works on already-trained large models, so the "crude" absolute-value metric turns out to be sufficient.

The real contribution of this paper is not algorithmic—it is that it *proved an order of magnitude*: cut 90% of AlexNet's weights, and accuracy does not drop.

The new question it leaves open: once you have pruned, can the remaining weights be compressed further?

* Paper 3: [2015 Deep Compression] — K-means weight sharing on top of pruning

In the very same year Han's follow-up ("Deep Compression") turned "yes, compress further" into a three-layer stack:
1. *Pruning*—delete unimportant weights.
2. *K-means clustering with weight sharing*—group the remaining weights into K buckets; replace each bucket with its centroid.
3. *Huffman coding*—entropy-code the bucket indices.

Step 2 is precisely the tool RankClust will pick back up eleven years later. Han's reasoning at the time was: "the last few decimal places of weights are just noise anyway; the exact numerical value in each bucket doesn't matter, as long as there are enough buckets." On CNNs this achieved 35× compression.

But the question Han *never asked* is: after K-means, what is the best number to put inside each bucket? He defaulted to the centroid (the natural K-means output), and nobody ever questioned that default.

This is where RankClust's seed is planted.

* Paper 4: [2022 GPTQ] — Revisiting second-order ideas in the LLM era

Seven years pass. Neural networks have grown from 60-million-parameter AlexNets to 175-billion-parameter GPT-3s; models no longer fit in GPU memory. Compression went from "optional optimization" to "mandatory."

Frantar et al.'s GPTQ *revived* the second-order intuition of OBD—but confined it to individual layers to avoid the full-network Hessian explosion. After quantizing each weight, it uses the within-layer Hessian to adjust the remaining unquantized weights, minimizing accumulated error.

GPTQ compressed a 175B model to 4-bit and kept it runnable. But one problem remained unsolved: certain activation channels contain outliers, and quantizing them collapses the model.

* Paper 5: [2022 LLM.int8()] + [2023 AWQ] — Discovering the existence of outliers

Dettmers' LLM.int8() was first to surface this: "Look—some channel activations are outliers (absolute values tens of times larger than other channels); you must keep them in fp16; the rest can be int8."

Lin et al.'s AWQ turned this observation into a method: *don't look at the weight's own magnitude—look at how important the channel that weight lives in is*. Weights in important channels need higher precision; weights in unimportant channels can be aggressively quantized.

These two papers introduced a new insight: *a weight's "importance" is not self-determined—it is determined by the weight's position in the network*. LeCun looked at each weight's own second derivative; Han looked at each weight's own absolute value; AWQ begins to look at a weight's "context."

Yet their goal remains the same: *preserve numerical precision as faithfully as possible*. Outliers get fp16, non-outliers get 4-bit—fundamentally, everyone is still chasing "stay close to the original value."

* Paper 6: [2023 SqueezeLLM / 2024 AQLM / VPTQ] — Non-uniform codebooks

Non-uniform quantization revived the old K-means idea for LLMs—replacing evenly spaced quantization grids with learned codebooks that concentrate more codewords in densely populated weight regions. AQLM pushed this to the extreme, reaching 2-bit quantization.

By the end of 2024, the field had converged on a consensus: *the closer the quantized distribution is to the original weight distribution, the better the outcome*. RTN (round-to-nearest) is worse than GPTQ, GPTQ is worse than AWQ, AWQ is worse than AQLM with a learned codebook. Everything exists to make W^ approximate W as closely as possible.

* Paper 7: [2026 RankClust] — Flipping the table

Aizpurua et al. flip the table. Their experiment is brutally simple:

1. Take a trained Llama 3.1-8B.
2. Apply K-means clustering to each layer (K = 16 to 64, more aggressive than Han's original).
3. *Replace the centroid of each bucket with a completely different number*—but preserve the relative ordering of buckets by magnitude.
4. Check whether the model still works.

The results are beyond what anyone expected:

- Fill buckets with completely random numbers, but preserve rank order → perplexity goes from 9.08 to 9.17, essentially unchanged.
- Match the mean and variance of the original centroids, but scramble rank order → perplexity goes from 9.08 to 20.67, catastrophic collapse.
- Fully random rank permutation → perplexity spikes to 460, total failure.

Even more striking: *flip the sign of an entire layer* (so the largest positive becomes the largest negative and vice versa), but preserve relative ordering—and the model barely loses any accuracy.

What does this mean? For a decade, every quantization method has been chasing numerical precision, but *the thing truly carrying information is not the numerical value—it is the rank*. GPTQ, AWQ, and AQLM work not because they approximate the original values (though that is what they have been striving for), but because they *incidentally preserve rank*. A compression method that preserves only rank and ignores values should work just as well.

Why does rank matter? The paper gives an elegant theoretical explanation: for any linear layer y = Wx, the affine transformation w → aw + b (a > 0) only rescales and shifts the output—it *does not change the ordering among components of y*. The subsequent LayerNorm absorbs the rescaling and shift. So as long as rank is preserved, the next layer's view of "which neuron is stronger" remains identical.

* Paper 8: [2024 Super Weight] — Another pair of eyes from the same era

Worth mentioning alongside RankClust is the contemporaneous work of Yu et al., "Super Weight." They found that LLMs contain a tiny number (single-digit count) of "super weights" such that removing any one of them individually causes the entire model to catastrophically collapse.

The two papers look contradictory—RankClust says "specific values don't matter," Super Weight says "a few specific weights matter enormously." But they both point in the same direction: *information in neural networks is highly structured*. The precise values of the vast majority of weights don't matter; a small number of critical positions cannot be touched. Super Weight sees the "singularities" in that structure; RankClust sees the "ordering relationships" in that structure.

* The deeper spirit of this river

On the surface this is a river of "how compression methods evolved," but *what is really evolving is our understanding of what carries information*.

- OBD in 1990 first acknowledged: weights are unequal.
- Han in 2015 turned "inequality" into an industry-scale method.
- GPTQ/AWQ/AQLM from 2022–2024, building on inequality, further discovered that "importance is contextual"—whether a weight matters depends on which channel it lives in.
- RankClust in 2026 peels back one more layer: *"what matters" is not even the weight itself, but the relationship between weights*.

This is a river that travels from "content" to "relation." Early methods work at the level of numerical values (preserve absolute magnitude, preserve precision, preserve distribution); the latest work operates at the level of topology (preserve ordering, preserve pathways).

This cognitive shift has deeper resonances: in physics, phase transitions care only about universality class (independent of the specific Hamiltonian's details); in information theory, channel capacity cares only about entropy (independent of the specific symbols used). Neural networks may be taking a similar path—specific numbers are a representation; rank is the invariant.

* Where this river might flow next

Several natural implications:

1. *Rank-aware loss during training*—if rank is the true invariant, can we regularize rank directly during training? This would give rise to genuinely novel training regimes.

2. *Purely non-numerical compression*—if rank is the core, it is theoretically possible to encode an LLM as pure "ordering information" (log₂(K!) bits per layer), with compression ratios far beyond anything currently available.

3. *Convergence with Super Weight*—using "a handful of critical singularities + majority of rank-invariant weights" as a unified framework to reorganize all work on compression, robustness, and interpretability.

4. *Attacking rank instead of values*—since rank is so fundamental, should adversarial attacks target rank directly rather than numerical values? This opens a new blind spot in robustness research.
