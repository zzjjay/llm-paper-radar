* The Problem River

Running Llama 3.1 70B with a full 128K context window on an ordinary 64GB MacBook — as of April 2026, *every* inference framework failed to do this.

Not because of compute. Because of *memory*. The model weights quantized to 4-bit take 39 GB; add the 128K KV cache in FP16 (40 GB) and the total is 79 GB — 15 GB over the 64 GB physical limit. You can't buy a bigger Mac: the consumer-tier Apple Silicon ceiling is exactly here.

The central question of this research thread is:

#+begin_quote
*How do you scale the KV-cache memory wall?*
#+end_quote

The difficulty is that several things are knotted together at once:
- The KV cache grows *linearly* with context length — every new token adds another entry. The moment long-context models became popular, this wall appeared right in front of you.
- You can *compress* it, but at inference time you have to *decompress* back to full precision before computing attention — the decompression itself must write a large temporary matrix to GPU memory, burning all the bandwidth on data movement.
- You can compress aggressively (1-bit, polar coordinates), but these "clever" schemes look fine on small models and then collapse catastrophically on 70B / 80-layer depth as errors compound geometrically.
- Different model architectures use different attention *scale factors* (~attn_scale~) — the same quantization method sits quietly on Llama and produces garbage on Gemma.

The entire thread is a repeated balancing act among four goals: compress more / compute faster / stay accurate / remain stable across architectures. Open-TQ-Metal is the first engineering realization that *eats all four*, and along the way it demonstrates that several previously elegant-looking methods simply do not hold up on large models.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Vaswani et al. *Attention Is All You Need*: autoregressive inference requires a KV cache

*** What problem it saw

RNNs process tokens one at a time; at long sequences they become unusably slow. The entire NLP field before 2017 was bottlenecked by this *sequential dependency*.

*** Its solution

Discard RNNs entirely and stack the model entirely from attention mechanisms. During training all tokens are processed in parallel, yielding an enormous speed increase.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

The price of parallelism is that *at inference time* all past tokens' K and V projections must be retained. Every newly generated token must attend to all previous K and V entries. This accumulation is the *KV cache*.

The paper barely mentions it — models were small and contexts short, so the KV cache topped out at a few hundred megabytes and nobody treated it as a problem. This seed would take a decade to grow into an 80 GB memory monster.

** 2018 | Milakov & Gimelshein *Online Softmax*: softmax can be computed in a streaming fashion

*** What problem it saw

Standard softmax must see all scores before normalising (find the max, compute the denominator). This forces the entire score vector to be *materialised* in memory first.

*** Its solution

Scan left to right while maintaining two running statistics: the current maximum seen and the running sum of exponentials. A new value updates both in O(1) — effectively "streaming normalisation."

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

This appears to be an inconsequential numerical trick, but it opens a door: *you no longer need to write all attention scores to memory before running softmax*.

Nobody understood the implication at the time. Five years later FlashAttention picked it up and fundamentally changed how attention is computed.

** 2022-23 | Dao et al. *FlashAttention / FlashDecoding*: fuse attention so scores never leave on-chip cache

*** What problem it saw

Standard attention ~softmax(QK^T / sqrt(d)) V~ must write the ~S×S~ score matrix back to HBM, then read it back for softmax, write back again, read again to multiply V. Bandwidth is entirely consumed *moving one large temporary matrix*.

*** Its solution

Tile Q into small blocks; within each tile, execute the full pipeline ~Q_i K^T -> softmax -> multiply V~ entirely in on-chip cache, maintained by online softmax state — the *score matrix is never written to memory*. FlashDecoding further splits the KV sequence into split-K chunks during decoding so multiple workgroups handle the same query in parallel.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

It targets the right bottleneck: transformers on GPUs are not compute-bound, they are *bandwidth-bound*. Move less data, get immediate speedup.

But it optimises the *compute path*; the *storage format* of the KV cache is untouched. FlashAttention cannot save you from 80 GB of FP16 KV cache for a 70B model.

** 2023 | Kwon et al. *vLLM / PagedAttention*: manage KV cache like an OS manages virtual memory

*** What problem it saw

In server-side inference, KV caches for multiple requests dynamically enter and leave; traditional contiguous allocation wastes 60%+ of GPU memory. An 80 GB GPU might have 50 GB idle but unallocatable.

*** Its solution

Slice the KV cache into fixed-size *pages* indexed by a page table — the same idea as OS virtual memory. This also enables prefix sharing, copy-on-write, and other advanced features.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

An engineering breakthrough that spawned nearly every production inference framework today. But *it solves a management problem, not a capacity problem*. If a single request needs 79 GB (Llama 70B at 128K context), paging cannot help — you need each token's KV to be physically smaller.

** 2024 | Hooper et al. *KVQuant / KIVI / GEAR*: quantize the KV cache to 4-bit

*** What problem it saw

Capacity was finally confronted head-on: FP16 KV cache is simply too large. Hooper et al. even explicitly targeted "10M context" — impossible at FP16.

*** Its solution

Quantize K and V to 4-bit (even 2-bit). KVQuant quantizes K per-channel and V per-token (because K has outliers along the channel dimension); GEAR adds a residual matrix and low-rank approximation to recover accuracy. Outliers are stored separately in FP16.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

Memory footprint drops to one quarter. But *when computing attention* the values must first be dequantized back to FP16/FP32 before ~QK^T~ — this materialises a new temporary ~S×d~ matrix back to GPU memory.

Concretely for Gemma 4: 950 tokens × 50 layers requires 742 MB of temporary matrix. *The memory you saved is paid back through bandwidth* — memory pressure becomes bandwidth pressure.

This is one of Open-TQ-Metal's central targets: *why dequantize at all?*

** 2024 | Zandieh et al. *QJL*: a 1-bit sketch of the KV cache

*** What problem it saw

4-bit is still "conservative," storing 4 bits per value obediently. Could a completely different perspective allow a far more aggressive scheme?

*** Its solution

Project keys onto a random Gaussian matrix and *store only sign bits* — 1 bit per component, just -1 or +1. Queries remain full-precision; attention scores are estimated asymmetrically (sketch × full). Compression ratio easily exceeds 5×.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

Theoretically, random projection followed by sign quantisation preserves relative similarities (cf. SimHash / Hyperplane LSH). Per-layer per-token score correlation ρ ≈ 0.85 looks acceptable.

*But the paper tests only on 8B models (32 layers)*. At 32 layers ~0.85^{32} ≈ 0.006~ — barely usable.

Open-TQ-Metal later ran it on 70B (80 layers): ~0.85^{80} ≈ 2 × 10^{-6}~. The signal is entirely buried in noise. Output degenerates to repeated tokens. This failure mode *only surfaces on large models*.

** 2025 | Han et al. *PolarQuant*: polar-coordinate quantisation — touch only the angle

*** What problem it saw

QJL applies aggressive compression directly in the component dimension. Could a change of coordinate system make the error distribution more tractable?

*** Its solution

Recursively convert KV embedding vectors from Cartesian to polar coordinates (radius + angle), and quantize only the angles. After random preprocessing, angle distributions become highly concentrated — no normalisation needed. Claims 4.2× compression.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

Mathematically elegant: structured angular error ~delta_theta~ is more controllable than random per-component error. On Llama it achieves cosine similarity 0.989 at 5-bit — nearly perfect.

*But they never tested on Gemma*. A philosophical bomb is buried here: angular error enters the attention score as

#+begin_example
    delta_score = alpha * |q| * |k| * (1 - cos(delta_theta))
    # Note the alpha — the attention scale factor
    # Llama: alpha = 1/sqrt(128) = 0.0884 (standard)
    # Gemma 4: alpha = 1.0 (11× larger)
    # Same delta_theta, 11× larger perturbation entering softmax,
    # compounded over 60 layers of exponentiation → garbage output
#+end_example

This is Open-TQ-Metal's second core finding: *the success or failure of a quantization method is determined not by model size but by the architecture's attention scale factor*.

** 2025 | Zandieh et al. *TurboQuant*: synthesis — MSE-optimal quantisation + QJL residual

*** What problem it saw

Each method has its strength: scalar quantisation is robust, QJL compresses aggressively, PolarQuant handles angles gracefully. Can they be unified?

*** Its solution

Main path: MSE-optimal scalar quantisation (random rotation + Lloyd-Max codebook). Residual: 1-bit QJL. Theoretically 3.5 bit/dim with neutral quality; 2.5 bit with only mild degradation.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

Mathematically the most *complete* — error is stratified by contribution before compression. But:

1. *Validated on 8B models only*. Error accumulation from stacking methods has not been tested on large models.
2. *No Apple Silicon / Metal*. The entire research thread assumes a CUDA ecosystem.
3. *No fused kernel*. The dequant-then-attend bandwidth burden is fully inherited.
4. *The alpha problem is ignored*. Gemma 4's ~attn_scale=1.0~ remains a latent threat.

At this point, nearly all "compression cleverness" the field has produced is on the table, but *no one has genuinely combined compression with fused compute, and no one has honestly reproduced results across different architectures*.

** 2026.04 | Vegasena *Open-TQ-Metal*: weaving all threads into one

*** What problem it saw

Looking back along the entire research thread, three gaps are clearly visible:

1. *Dequant-then-attend is self-inflicted bandwidth waste*. KV quantisation saves memory that dequant temporary matrices immediately pay back.
2. *Small-model validation does not imply large-model success*. QJL works at 8B; at 70B the signal vanishes.
3. *Single-architecture validation does not imply cross-architecture success*. PolarQuant is perfect on Llama, produces garbage on Gemma 4 — and no one explained why.

*** Its solution

Four components woven into one complete engineering solution:

1. *Fused int4 SDPA Metal kernel*. The Metal compute shader reads packed int4 KV directly and extracts nibbles *inside GPU registers* via bitwise operations, then runs online softmax in a single pass — *zero intermediate matrices*.

   The key trick is *vectorized nibble extraction*: pre-divide the query by ~{1, 16, 256, 4096}~, then dot-product simultaneously with four masks ~{0x000F, 0x00F0, 0x0F00, 0xF000}~ to extract all 4 nibbles in one operation. Borrowed from MLX's ~qdot~ pattern.

2. *Split-K via MLX Primitives chained through computation graph*. At 128K context, a single-threaded pass takes 480 ms per head — parallelism is essential. But multiple dispatches within a single Metal ~eval_gpu()~ call carry *no ordering guarantee* — a race condition.

   Solution: write the partial accumulation stage and the reduction stage as two separate MLX Primitives, then let the reduce stage take the partial stage's output as its input — *lazy evaluation naturally serialises them*. Elegant.

3. *70B + 80-layer validation*. The first time anyone runs QJL and PolarQuant on a genuinely large model. Both the depth bomb and the architecture bomb detonate simultaneously.

4. *Cross-architecture quantization analysis*. Establishes the new insight that ~attn_scale~ is the critical variable determining whether a quantization method succeeds. The entire prior literature implicitly assumed the relevant axes were *method × model size* — wrong. The axis is *method × attention scale factor*.

*** Why it worked — and what new holes it dug

Four numbers tell the story:
- 48× attention kernel speedup (128K context, Llama 70B)
- 3.2× KV cache compression (40 GB → 12.5 GB)
- Llama 70B @ 128K context fits on a 64 GB Mac for the first time
- Top-1 token *exactly matches* FP16 baseline under greedy decoding

Remaining gaps, stated honestly:
- *Bandwidth wall*: once attention is optimised to the limit, weight loading becomes the new bottleneck (39 GB / 400 GB/s ≈ 98 ms/token is a physical floor). This points to MoE / 2-bit weights / speculative decoding as the next wave.
- *Long-context quality* not systematically benchmarked on RULER / Needle-in-Haystack.
- *Hardware-specific*: algorithm is portable, kernel must be rewritten for CUDA.
- *18% throughput gap vs. mlx-lm* comes from KV cache management (concat vs. pre-alloc), not the attention kernel itself.

* Frontier Extensions

The paper appeared in April 2026; at this point the "extensions" are mostly directions the author flags in the Discussion section:

- *Bandwidth wall, next steps*: compress weights to 2-bit (though the author's experiments show 2-bit weights on Metal are actually 0.67× *slower* — a dedicated kernel is needed); move to MoE (the Gemma 4 MoE variant activating 4B parameters already runs at 59 tok/s, a 4× gain); use speculative decoding (but a 25% acceptance rate is insufficient — smarter draft models are needed).
- *Extension of the cross-architecture analysis*: the author hints that future model designers may need to treat ~attn_scale~ as a first-class variable for quantizability — which in turn feeds back into architecture choices.
- *Ensue distributed memory network*: the author delegated 330 experiments to an 8-agent autonomous loop (orchestrator / profiler / reflector / …). This infrastructure may itself prove a more lasting methodological contribution than any individual paper in the thread.

As of 2026-05 the most recent public paper in this thread is Open-TQ-Metal itself. Follow-on work will most likely split along three directions:
1. Porting fused compressed-domain attention to CUDA for large-scale cloud inference.
2. Designing *quantization-aware architectures* — choosing ~attn_scale~ with quantization robustness as a design constraint.
3. Combining KV cache compression with MoE bandwidth savings — naturally complementary under the bandwidth wall.

* Insights

*What is really happening beneath this entire research thread:*

For the first five years (2022-2025), KV cache compression was a story of *accumulation* — ever more aggressive compression formats, ever cleverer mathematical transforms, ever higher theoretical compression ratios. Each paper compressed harder than the one before.

Open-TQ-Metal is not "harder" in that sense — its compression ratio (3.2×) is actually lower than PolarQuant (4.2×) and far lower than QJL. But it did three things *nobody had done before*:

1. *Fuse* — no longer "compress for storage, decompress to use," but "compressed storage + compressed-domain compute" unified in a single kernel. This is the natural extension of the FlashAttention philosophy (never materialise intermediate matrices).
2. *Validate honestly* — run on genuinely large models and genuinely different architectures. The result: methods that looked beautiful in prior evaluations crumbled. QJL's 0.85 correlation at 32 layers vs. 80 layers is a completely different story; PolarQuant's 0.989 on Llama vs. garbage on Gemma 4.
3. *Find the right axis of variation* — reduce cross-architecture differences to a single number (~attn_scale~). This is not algorithmic progress; it is progress in *understanding*. The field had assumed the relevant evaluation dimensions were "method × model size." Wrong. The axis is "method × attention scale factor."

What is really happening across this thread is an *awakening of evaluation dimensions*. The "cleverness" of algorithms has already saturated; the next wave of progress will come from *who can most honestly expose an algorithm's failure modes*.

A quieter shift is also underway: *the boundary between compute and storage is dissolving*. FlashAttention made the score matrix disappear. Open-TQ-Metal makes the dequant matrix disappear. Next — *what if weights never need to be decompressed before matrix multiplication?* This is exactly the direction that weight-only quantization fused kernels are already pursuing, but the two threads have not yet merged.

*Where the next step most likely leads:*

1. *Quantization-aware architecture design* — treating ~attn_scale~, QK normalisation strategy, and similar choices as design constraints with quantization robustness as an explicit objective.
2. *Bandwidth-first inference stacks* — once attention is pushed to ~10 ms, weight bandwidth is the new bottleneck. MoE + aggressive weight quantisation will dominate the next wave, but at the cost of homogenisation pressure on model architectures.
3. *Validation as a research product* — Open-TQ-Metal's 330 experiments and multi-agent orchestration system may themselves have more long-term impact than any single paper. "Discovering your method's failure mode before you do" could become the central contribution of the next generation of papers.

* Takeaways

*On how to do research:*

- *Works on small models does not mean works on large models.* Depth accumulation is geometric — 0.85 at 32 layers versus 80 layers is a categorically different story. "Validated on 8B" is a *rhetorical move*, not *evidence*.
- *Works on one architecture does not mean works across architectures.* Llama and Gemma look like the same kind of transformer, but a single number — ~attn_scale~ — makes the same quantization method succeed on one and fail catastrophically on the other. Always ask: *which specific architectural parameter is your method sensitive to?*
- *The most valuable papers often do not propose new methods — they honestly expose the limits of old ones.* Open-TQ-Metal's core intellectual contribution — that ~attn_scale~ determines quantization success — may be worth more than its fused kernel.

*On how to find problems:*

- *Systematically scan evaluation dimensions.* When a thread has iterated for years and methods keep getting "cleverer," the evaluation dimensions have usually been conditioned. Pick one dimension *nobody has seriously evaluated* (e.g., cross-architecture ~attn_scale~) and you will very likely find gold.
- *Erase the boundary between storage and compute.* Every time a large temporary matrix is eliminated, there is a bandwidth dividend. The next KV quantisation step, fusing weight decompression, fusing gradient accumulation — all follow the same pattern.

*On what you can do concretely right now:*

- You can now run Llama 70B @ 128K long context on a 64 GB Mac — previously impossible with any framework.
- When choosing a quantization method, check the model's ~attn_scale~ first. Standard ~1/sqrt(d)~ architectures can safely try PolarQuant; non-standard ones (Gemma 4, DeepSeek-V3, some custom architectures) should run a cosine similarity sanity check first.
- When evaluating a new KV compression method, test at minimum across two orders of magnitude of model size and two different ~attn_scale~ values. This is the minimum bar Open-TQ-Metal has set for the field.
- When writing fused kernels, "decode it in registers" is a dramatically stronger pattern than "store it then load it" — a direct extension of the FlashAttention philosophy.
