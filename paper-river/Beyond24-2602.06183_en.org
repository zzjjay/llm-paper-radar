* Target Paper

*To 2:4 Sparsity and Beyond: Neuron-level Activation Function to Accelerate LLM Pre-Training* (arXiv:2602.06183, 2026)

One sentence: the most compute-intensive part of LLM pre-training — the FFN matrix multiplications — is cut in half by using a hardware-accelerable sparse format, delivering a 1.4–1.7× end-to-end speedup with no quality loss.

* What This Problem Line Is Evolving

A very concrete question: *how do we make "training large models" genuinely faster.*

Not inference-faster — training-faster. This is hard. During inference you can prune weights, skip computations, and accept a slight quality drop. But during training, one wrong number corrupts the gradient and ruins the model. So this entire line keeps answering the same question:

*"Can we skip, during training, the computations that are destined to become zero — without hurting the final model's quality?"*

Every paper in this line bites a different corner of that same hard bone.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** Starting Point: A Secret Hidden Inside Dense Training

In 2019, Frankle proposed the *Lottery Ticket Hypothesis*: randomly initialize a large network, train it to completion, then prune — drop 90% of the weights — and the remainder can recover the original performance.

The implication is explosive: *that sparse sub-network was already there from the start.* All the compute we spent training the connections that would later be pruned was pure waste.

But Frankle did not solve the question of whether you could train only that sparse sub-network from the beginning. He merely proved it exists. That open question hung in the air.

** First Hardware Landing: NVIDIA 2:4

In 2021, Mishra et al. at NVIDIA did something concrete on the Ampere architecture: hardware acceleration for *2:4 sparsity* — at most 2 non-zeros in every 4 consecutive weights.

Why 2:4 and not something else? *Hardware.* GPU tensor cores can read 2:4-formatted matrices directly, skip the two zeros, and deliver a theoretical 2× throughput (roughly 1.4–1.5× in practice). It is a format that was forced into existence by hardware constraints.

But this path also introduced a new problem: 2:4 is too rigid. Exactly 50% sparsity, no more. If your model is actually 80% zeros, 2:4 throws away half of the potential speedup.

** Branch One: "Activations Are Sparse at Inference Time"

In 2023, Liu et al. discovered something interesting — they called it *Deja Vu*. Looking at the intermediate activations of an LLM during inference, they found that for any specific input, the outputs of most FFN neurons are close to zero. Moreover, *which* neurons "light up" changes with the input.

The key distinction: all prior sparsity work targeted *weight sparsity* (which parameters are zero). This was the first serious look at *activation sparsity* (which intermediate values are zero). The difference is fundamental — weight sparsity is static, fixed after training; activation sparsity is dynamic, different for every input.

Deja Vu used this for inference acceleration: predict which neurons will activate for a given input, and skip the rest ahead of time. But only at inference time.

** Branch Two: Why Activation Sparsity Exists

That same year, Li et al. documented the *Lazy Neuron* phenomenon — they found that in networks with ReLU activations, more than 90% of FFN intermediate positions are exactly zero. And the larger the model, the higher the activation sparsity.

Li was not building an accelerator; he was doing phenomenology: why are neural networks naturally sparse? This gave Liu's Deja Vu a theoretical foundation — sparsity is not accidental, it is an intrinsic property of this architecture.

But this left another problem: that sparsity *cannot be hardware-accelerated.* The zero positions are essentially random; the hardware has no way to know which ones to skip.

** Confluence: Venom — Relaxing 2:4

At this point the two branches merged: hardware supports 2:4 (Mishra), and activation sparsity can reach 90% phenomenologically (Li). Could one design a format that is both more flexible than 2:4 *and* hardware-accelerable?

*Venom* introduced the v:n:m format: partition the matrix into row blocks, keep only 4 non-zero columns per block, then apply 2:4 within those 4 columns. This enables 80–99% sparsity while still achieving 6–10× hardware acceleration.

Venom is a *format invention* — it does not tell you which positions to zero out, but it offers a promise: "if you can arrange your zeros into this shape, I will accelerate them."

A new question immediately followed: how do you arrange the zeros into Venom's desired shape during training?

** Training Pain Point: Every Mask Change Makes the Loss Jump

If you apply Venom during training, you must recompute "which positions should be zero" at every step. Every time the *pruning mask changes, the loss function has a discontinuous jump* — some weights suddenly switch from "participating in computation" to "not participating."

In 2024, Hu et al. proposed *SSTE (Soft-Thresholding STE)*: replace hard pruning with a soft threshold, making the loss function continuous and training stable.

This is a *training technique* that makes frequent sparsity-pattern changes during training feasible. But it does not answer the question on the Venom side — what *decides* which positions to zero out?

** Progress on the Activation Side: Making Activations Inherently Sparse

Meanwhile, in 2025, Haziza et al. found that replacing the activation function with *Squared ReLU* (ReLU²) naturally pushes FFN activation sparsity past 90%. This is an almost unbelievably simple finding — swap the activation function, get 90% sparsity for free.

But the zero positions *are still random.* Hardware still does not recognize them.

** Target Paper: Actively Arranging Activations into Venom Shape

By 2026, the *Beyond 2:4* team could see the complete puzzle:

- Weight side: 2:4 already accelerates, but is too rigid (Mishra)
- Activation side: naturally 90% sparse (Li, Haziza), but shape is random
- Format: Venom offers a flexible sparse format
- Training: SSTE stabilizes dynamic sparsity during training

Only one piece was missing: *how to make that 90% activation sparsity land exactly in the shape Venom wants.*

Their solution is called *Neuron-Level Experts (NLE)* — essentially applying the MoE (Mixture of Experts) idea at the granularity of individual FFN neurons:

1. *Cluster first*: run a few steps in dense mode at the start of training, then cluster the FFN columns (neurons) by similarity — each cluster becomes an "expert group."
2. *Route next*: each token uses L2 distance to select its nearest expert group and activates only the neurons in that group.
3. *Arrange*: tokens routed to the same group are batched together, so their computation naturally forms a Venom-shaped batch matrix multiplication.

The key distinction: *Deja Vu passively observed sparsity; they actively manufacture it* — and the sparsity they manufacture is the kind the hardware recognizes.

Combined with a dense phase at the end of training to clean up any sparsity artifacts, the result is a 1.4–1.7× end-to-end speedup with no benchmark regression.

* Frontier Extensions

Honest note: the target paper is from February 2026 and is the most recent work in scope; within the paper-river range I found no papers that explicitly critique it. Likely next directions (speculative, not citing):

- *Expert granularity selection*: NLE uses fixed expert sizes — can they be made dynamic?
- *Integration with MoE models themselves*: MoE already has sparse activation; stacking NLE on top may be redundant or conflicting. The paper mentions compatibility but does not explore it deeply.
- *Hopper/Blackwell hardware*: current implementation uses Venom on A100; new hardware with native sparse support (e.g., H100 fp8 + 2:4) may enable further gains.

* Insight

On the surface this line is "iterative improvements in sparsification techniques." But underneath it is *a transfer of ownership over zeros*:

- Early era (LeCun, Frankle): zeros appear *after training* — pruning is post-hoc work.
- Middle era (Mishra, Venom): zeros must conform to *hardware-dictated shapes* — you come to the hardware's terms.
- Later era (Deja Vu, Li): zeros *grow naturally inside the model* — observe and exploit them.
- Present (Beyond 2:4): zeros are *actively arranged into the shape the hardware wants.*

With each leap, *the moment at which zeros are produced* moves earlier in the training process. From "post-hoc pruning" → "format constraint" → "runtime observation" → "proactive shaping during training."

This points to a deeper shift: *we are turning "sparsity" from a post-hoc optimization trick into a first-order design variable of network architecture.* MoE was the earliest symptom; NLE is a finer-grained continuation. The next step is almost certainly to encode the sparsity pattern directly into the training objective — letting the model learn *how to be sparse in the way that best fits the current hardware* at the same time it learns the task.

The boundary between hardware and algorithm is dissolving. Algorithms no longer pretend hardware is undifferentiated compute, and hardware no longer strives to support arbitrary algorithms — they are co-evolving. This line of work is one cross-section of that evolution.
