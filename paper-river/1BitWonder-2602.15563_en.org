* The River of the Problem

A fixed slab of GPU memory — say 8 GB — and you need to fit a language model inside it. You have two knobs: how large the model can be (how many parameters), and how many bits each parameter occupies. The product of the two knobs equals that 8 GB.

Which way do you turn them? Pack in a 4B-parameter 16-bit model, or a 31B-parameter model where every weight costs just 1 bit? Both occupy roughly the same memory, but which one is smarter?

The question sounds like a shopping decision, but it is actually the closing act of the deepest thread in large-model research over the past six years: from "bigger is better" to "size and data must scale together," then to "precision counts too," and finally to "can we simply push precision all the way to 1 bit and spend the savings on making the model bigger?" Each step came from someone noticing that the previous generation's arithmetic had missed a variable.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2020 | Kaplan et al.: Writing "Model Growth" as an Equation for the First Time

*** What Problem Did It See?

Before 2020, scaling up models was mostly intuition. Three years after the Transformer, everyone knew that bigger was better, but nobody could say "if I make it ten times larger, how much will scores improve?" Every decision to spend more compute was a gamble.

*** Its Solution

The OpenAI team systematically trained hundreds of models at different scales and fit the relationship among loss, parameter count N, data volume D, and compute C as a power law:

#+begin_example
  loss ≈ A / N^alpha + B / D^beta + irreducible loss E
#+end_example

The sharper conclusion was: given a fixed compute budget, it pays more to invest in a larger model than to feed it more data.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Left

The formula gave the whole field a betting equation: "I have N GPUs; what loss can I reach?" GPT-3 was built by following that advice — pour money into parameters.

The hole it left: the experiments were conducted at relatively small scale, and when extrapolated to hundreds of billions of parameters, the relative importance of parameters versus data was off. The next paper would dig up that hole.

** 2022 | Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al.): You Think the Model Isn't Big Enough — Actually the Data Isn't

*** What Problem Did It See?

Kaplan's formula said "mainly scale parameters," so GPT-3 was 175B parameters trained on 300B tokens — parameters far outnumber data. The Chinchilla team re-ran the experiments more carefully and across a wider range, and uncovered an embarrassing truth: GPT-3 was severely underfed. Given the same compute, they could train a 70B-parameter model on 1.4T tokens and get stronger performance.

*** Its Solution

The new formula said: parameters and data should scale *together*, at an optimal ratio of roughly 20 tokens per parameter. When compute doubles, both N and D grow by sqrt(2) — not "mainly grow N" as Kaplan suggested.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Left

The intuition is to treat data as nutrition: no matter how big the model, it cannot learn without seeing enough of the world. Chinchilla recalibrated the entire industry's training recipe — the LLaMA family followed it.

The hole it left: the story still has only two knobs — *parameters* and *data*. How many bits does each parameter occupy? Nobody cared; 16 bits was assumed. Only when inference costs soared did people start thinking about the third knob: precision.

** 2023 | BitNet (Wang et al.): Tell the Model During Training That It Only Gets 1 Bit

*** What Problem Did It See?

During inference, fp16 weights must be transferred from memory to compute units — that data movement is the bottleneck. The most direct saving: quantize fp16 to int4 after training (post-training quantization, PTQ). But below 4 bits things start to break, and 1 bit is nearly impossible.

Why does it break? Because during training the model always saw continuous fp16 weights and never "planned" to be flattened. Suddenly rounding weights to ±1 leaves the model completely unprepared.

*** Its Solution

BitNet reverses the logic: simulate quantization during training (QAT, Quantization-Aware Training) — forward pass uses ±1 quantized weights, backward pass updates full-precision gradients. The model learns from step one that "you will end up with only 1 bit" and finds robust solutions on its own.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Left

The intuition: the loss landscape has many flat basins. QAT forces the optimizer to find basins where "jiggling the weights a little doesn't hurt much."

The hole it left: pure ±1 has too little expressiveness; training is unstable and needs normalization tricks to stay alive. BitNet proved "it runs," not "it's optimal." The next paper relaxed this constraint ever so slightly.

** 2024 | BitNet b1.58 (Ma et al.): Add a Zero, Fix Everything

*** What Problem Did It See?

The biggest problem with ±1 is that there is no "do nothing" option — every weight must cast an active vote, with no abstention. This makes it hard for the model to express sparsity and makes training dynamics awkward.

*** Its Solution

Expand the allowable values from {-1, +1} to {-1, 0, +1}. Each weight now carries log₂(3) ≈ 1.58 bits of information. One extra value sounds like a small difference, but for optimization it is qualitative — the model can now "switch off some channels first" and gradually learn.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Left

With the zero added, BitNet b1.58 at the 7B scale nearly matches fp16 baseline performance. The paper proclaimed the arrival of the "1-bit era" and convinced the community that extreme low-bit quantization is genuinely viable.

The hole it left: the "parity" claim rested mainly on loss and perplexity. But low loss does not mean the model can write code or do math. Can downstream generation tasks really hold up at 1.58 bits? That is what Kumar's group set out to answer.

** 2025 | Kumar et al.: Folding Precision into the Chinchilla Formula

*** What Problem Did It See?

The BitNet line said "1-bit is viable," but "viable" is not the same as "efficient." Given a fixed memory budget, is it better to pack in a 1-bit giant or an 8-bit medium model? The Chinchilla formula cannot answer — it has no concept of bits.

*** Its Solution

Kumar extends the Chinchilla formula by one term. Previously, *effective model capacity ∝ N* (parameter count); now, *effective model capacity = N × f(P_w)*, where P_w is the bit-width and f is a saturating function — the marginal gain from more precision diminishes. Fitting this formula to a suite of QAT experiments, their conclusion is: under a fixed compute budget, the optimal precision is around *6 bits*. That saves considerably over 16 bits, but is far from 1 bit.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Left

The reasoning is sound: precision has diminishing returns — going from 0 to 4 bits gains a lot, going from 4 to 16 bits gains little. So a sweet spot must exist.

The hole it left: they implicitly assume quantization uses a *uniform integer grid* (evenly spaced points). If the grid itself is poorly chosen, the "optimal bit count" they found is really just the limit of a bad grid, not of precision in general. 1-Bit Wonder seizes on exactly this point.

** 2025 | ParetoQ (Liu et al.): A Systematic Sweep of the Extreme Low-Bit Regime

*** What Problem Did It See?

BitNet says 1-bit works; Kumar says 6-bit is optimal. Are both right? The former relies on training tricks; the latter on a particular formula. A clean controlled experiment across low bit-widths was missing.

*** Its Solution

Liu's group runs 1, 2, 3, and 4 bits through the same QAT pipeline and plots a Pareto frontier. They also discover a key trick: QAT should not be switched on from step one. Early in training, weights are reshuffling dramatically; forcing quantization at that point leads the model astray. Letting the model grow freely in fp16 for about 1000 steps before enabling QAT works much better — they call this "late-stage QAT."

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Left

The intuition: early training is like shaping wet clay — its hardness is not yet set, and heavy constraints cause collapse. Apply the constraint later, once the model has a rough shape, and it can refine itself within the low-precision limits.

The hole it left: the sweep covers 1–4 bits, but every point uses a linear/integer grid. What if you switch to a nonlinear grid? That dimension had not been explored systematically.

** 2026 | 1-Bit Wonder (Maskey et al.): Change the Grid, Flip the Conclusion

*** What Problem Did It See?

Looking back across the whole line: whether researchers studied 1-bit or 6-bit, *how the grid is constructed* was never touched — everyone defaulted to evenly-spaced points in [-1, 1].

But trained weight distributions are bell-shaped: the vast majority of weights cluster near zero, with sparse tails on either side. Using wide spacing near zero and narrow spacing in the tails wastes resolution exactly where it is needed most.

More damning: if the grid is poorly designed, the "6-bit optimum" measured by prior work may not be the limit of precision at all — it may only be the limit of a bad grid.

*** Its Solution

Replace the quantization grid from "uniform integers" with "nonuniform centroids learned by k-means." For each layer's weights, run a 1D k-means — the grid points follow the actual density of the weight distribution, dense where the weights are dense, sparse where they are sparse. The idea itself is old (Lloyd 1982), but no one had plugged it into an LLM scaling-law analysis seriously.

Then re-fit Kumar's precision-aware scaling law using this k-means quantization scheme. The results:

- At the same bit-width, k-means loss is strictly lower than uniform; the advantage is largest at low bit-widths.
- Re-solving for the optimum: the optimal bit-width *drops from 6 bits to 1 bit*.
- Empirical validation: under a fixed 8 GB memory budget, three models are compared — 4B/16-bit, 12B/4-bit, and 31B/1-bit — and 31B/1-bit wins on most downstream tasks (MMLU, HellaSwag, MBPP, HumanEval, …).

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Left

It exposes the hidden assumption running through the entire line — "the grid is uniformly spaced" — and shows that assumption alone accounts for several bits' worth of performance gap. Once the grid is fixed, the entire calculus of "how large a model / how many bits" changes.

The hole it leaves: k-means grids have no native hardware support and require lookup-table kernels. When batch sizes are large, the memory-bandwidth advantage is consumed by compute overhead. Should a new hardware generation be designed specifically for nonlinear grids? That is the open question for the next wave.

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026, 1-Bit Wonder has been out for three months and the community is still digesting it. Several directions are foreseeable:

- *Native hardware support for nonlinear grids*: k-means quantization currently relies on software lookup-table emulation. If GPUs or accelerators natively support "multiply-accumulate with a 16-entry lookup table," the 1-bit advantage would grow further.
- *Applying this scaling law to MoE / KV cache*: this paper covers only weight quantization in dense models. Do expert weights in MoE architectures and the KV cache during inference enjoy the same nonlinear + low-bit dividend?
- *Compressing training-time precision too*: BitNet, ParetoQ, and this paper all still train in fp16 with a quantized forward pass. If backward gradients were also compressed to 4 bits, total training cost could drop another notch.

* Insights

Reading through this river, a consistent pattern emerges: *every generation's advance came from lifting a hidden assumption that had been silently accepted*.

- Kaplan assumed "data is unlimited" → Chinchilla lifted it.
- Chinchilla assumed "precision is irrelevant" → BitNet/Kumar lifted it.
- Kumar assumed "the grid is uniformly spaced" → 1-Bit Wonder lifted it.

Each time a hidden assumption is lifted, the optimal solution jumps by a large margin. Not because the new method is particularly clever, but because the previous generation had filled in a "free variable" arbitrarily without realizing it could be optimized.

This implies that nearly all current intuitions about LLM scaling rest on a pile of "seemingly unimportant" default choices — optimizer, activation function, attention pattern, tokenizer vocabulary size, batch size… every one of them may hide a "lift it and profit" opportunity.

At a deeper level: what this paper truly flips is not "can 1-bit be used" but *the evaluation criterion*. In the BitNet era, "usable" meant loss matching fp16; here, "optimal" is measured by downstream generation tasks (MMLU, HumanEval, and friends). When evaluation shifts from loss to downstream performance, the preference for larger models is amplified further — because downstream capability depends more steeply on model scale. So "1-bit is optimal" is actually two factors stacked: k-means makes low-bit more robust *and* downstream tasks favor larger models more strongly.

Where will the field go next? My bet is on two directions: (1) bring nonlinear quantization into hardware roadmaps so k-means kernels run as fast as int4; (2) systematically revisit all "long-settled defaults" — optimizer, attention implementation, tokenizer — and ask whether any of them contain a 1-Bit Wonder moment waiting to happen.

* Takeaways

This river offers three concrete research-practice lessons:

1. *Find the knobs nobody has touched.* BitNet, Kumar, and ParetoQ all tuned "how many bits," but no one touched "how the grid is cut." When an entire field crowds onto one dimension, looking one step sideways is where the next paper lives.
2. *List the assumptions first, then question them.* Any scaling or optimality claim rests on a stack of hidden defaults. Cultivate the habit of asking: "They say 6-bit is optimal — optimal under what premises?" That question alone is the seed of a paper.
3. *The evaluation method determines the conclusion.* This paper used downstream tasks instead of loss/PPL and the conclusion flipped. The next time you see "method X matches baseline performance," ask first: matches on which metric? Would a different metric tell a different story?

Applied practically: you can take any "everyone just does it this way" default in your own work — a CI system's timeout setting, a database indexing strategy, the wording of a prompt's system message — and run a "lift the default" experiment. Most of the time you won't find a 1-Bit Wonder, but occasionally you will.
