* The Problem River

A 70B model in full precision requires 140 GB of VRAM. Compressed to 2-bit, that theoretically drops to 17.5 GB — small enough to fit on a consumer GPU. But the price is steep: each weight can only take one of four values. How do you cram a blob of 16-bit reals into four slots and still keep the model's accuracy nearly intact?

The difficulty lies in the constraints: you have only 128 calibration samples and cannot retrain. You must operate layer by layer — finish one, lock it in, move to the next. But here is the catch: by the time you reach layer 30, its inputs are already the outputs of 29 "degraded" preceding layers. Errors from earlier layers pile up like a snowball rolling downhill. You think you are minimizing layer 30's error, but you are actually minimizing error on inputs that have already been contaminated.

This entire research line keeps asking the same question: *Without retraining, how do you prevent errors from compounding as you move layer by layer through the network?*

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1989–1993 | OBD/OBS: Brain Surgery for Neural Networks

*** What Problem Did It See?

Neural networks of that era were small, yet even after training they had too many parameters. Naive magnitude pruning — just cutting small weights — was too crude: a weight with a tiny absolute value might have a large effect on the final output, and vice versa.

*** Its Solution

LeCun's insight: Taylor-expand the loss function. The increase in loss caused by removing weight w_i is governed primarily by the diagonal of the Hessian matrix (OBD), or more precisely by the inverse Hessian (OBS). The Hessian tells you whether a weight is worth keeping and, if you remove it, how the remaining weights should be adjusted to compensate.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Pit It Dug

It transformed pruning from a heuristic into a second-order optimization problem. But: (1) computing the inverse Hessian is expensive; (2) accuracy typically still requires retraining after pruning. Thirty years later, descendants of OBS would return — not to solve pruning, but quantization.

** 2020 | AdaRound: Stop Rounding to the Nearest Grid Point

*** What Problem Did It See?

The PTQ era was just beginning. The most natural way to map 32-bit weights to 8-bit integers is to round to the nearest grid point. Nagel et al. showed: *this is suboptimal*.

Concretely: quantizing a weight from 0.73 to 0.75 versus 0.625 — 0.75 looks closer. But if the context (the interaction of this weight with others) means that 0.625 produces smaller output error, then 0.625 is the right choice.

*** Its Solution

Learn a small variable h_i ∈ [0,1] per weight that decides whether it rounds up or down. The optimization objective is the layer-level output error, with the calibration target set to the full-precision output.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Pit It Dug

It revealed a simple truth: *quantization is a combinatorial optimization problem*, not a weight-by-weight rounding problem. But it requires learning — many gradient-descent steps over 128 calibration samples, which is unacceptably slow for a 70B model. The next generation would eliminate the learning step.

** 2022 | OBC / OBQ: Bring OBS to Quantization

*** What Problem Did It See?

AdaRound needs gradient descent. Can we get a closed-form solution instead? Frantar and Alistarh looked back at OBS from thirty years prior: pruning is fundamentally "set a weight to zero"; quantization is "set a weight to a grid point" — the structure is identical.

*** Its Solution

Directly transplant OBS's inverse-Hessian compensation formula: each time a weight q_i is quantized, the remaining unquantized weights are adjusted using the inverse Hessian to keep the overall output error unchanged. Then pick the next weight and repeat.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Pit It Dug

Entirely closed-form, no learning required. But far too slow — every time one weight is quantized, the entire inverse Hessian must be updated. A 70B model would take a week. The next paper solves the speed problem.

** 2022 | GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2210.17323): Making Closed-Form Quantization 100× Faster

*** What Problem Did It See?

OBC processes row by row and recomputes the inverse Hessian after each update. Can all columns be processed in one shot?

*** Its Solution

Key observation: all rows (output channels) within the same layer share the same Hessian, because the Hessian depends only on the input activations. So factorize the Hessian via Cholesky as H = LL^T, then scan column by column from right to left: quantize one column, apply a batch compensation using the precomputed Cholesky factors, and continue.

A 175B OPT model is quantized in 7 hours. This is the engineering inflection point for PTQ — quantization became cheap enough to be practical.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Pit It Dug

L_std(Q) = ||(W-Q)·X_q||² — this is what GPTQ minimizes. It looks reasonable: keep the quantized layer's output close to the original layer's output.

But wait: *X_q is the activation produced by the first 29 quantized layers.* The full-precision weight W will never see such input at deployment time. In other words, GPTQ is making "full-precision weights applied to contaminated inputs" close to "quantized weights applied to contaminated inputs" — but what you actually want at deployment is "quantized weights applied to contaminated inputs" to be close to "full-precision weights applied to clean inputs."

The objective is wrong. This bug remained hidden for three years.

** 2025 | GPTAQ / QEP: Mismatch-Aware Calibration — Naming the Drift

*** What Problem Did It See?

Li et al. (GPTAQ) and Arai & Ichikawa (QEP) nearly simultaneously discovered that GPTQ's calibration objective is missing a term. The "drift" introduced by quantizing preceding layers, D = W(X_f - X_q), is a real error that GPTQ never tries to compensate — it focuses only on the current layer's own quantization error and ignores the perturbation propagated from upstream.

*** Its Solution

Change the calibration target from Y = W·X_q to Y = W·X_f. That is: "I want my quantized layer, when applied to the contaminated input X_q, to still produce output close to what the full-precision layer would produce on the clean input X_f."

Formally: L_mis(Q) = ||W·X_f - Q·X_q||² = ||(W-Q)·X_q + D||²

The extra term D is the upstream mismatch. The current layer has an opportunity to partially compensate for D by adjusting its quantized values Q.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Pit It Dug

The intuition is exactly right — you are finally aligning to "the output you actually want" rather than to "an imagined, uncontaminated intermediate state."

But a new problem surfaces in practice: *how trustworthy is the D estimated from 128 calibration samples?* If you trust it 100% (α=1, full compensation), you are overfitting to accidental perturbations in those 128 samples; if you trust it not at all (α=0), you degrade back to GPTQ. What value in between should you choose?

GPTAQ used a globally fixed α (e.g., 0.5). But the CoreQ team plotted the data (Figure 2) and found: *the optimal α varies enormously across layers, models, and even projection types.* The deepest layers may need α near 1; middle layers perhaps 0.3; the K, V, and Q projections of attention heads each differ. A single fixed α is necessarily suboptimal.

** 2026 | CoreQ (This Paper): Compute a Closed-Form α for Each Layer

*** What Problem Did It See?

α is the knob controlling how much mismatch to compensate. Tuning it by hand requires a validation search, and must be redone for every layer, every model, and every bit-width — violating the spirit of learning-free PTQ. Can the data itself tell you what α should be?

*** Its Solution

Geometric decomposition. Project the mismatch D onto the subspace that the current layer can actually reach, R = {A·X_q : A ∈ R^{m×n}}:

D = S̃·X_q + η, where S̃·X_q lies in R (compensable) and η is orthogonal to R (uncompensable).

The intuition: your quantized values Q can only affect the output by modifying W → W+ΔW. The set of all outputs reachable by such modifications forms R. The component of D that lies outside R — namely η — is a "hard residual" that no adjustment to Q can remove.

CoreQ's closed-form solution:

α_corr = ||s||² / (||s||² + ||η||²)

where s = S̃·X_q. The meaning: *look at how much of the mismatch's energy this layer can potentially compensate, and how much is irremovable; the compensable fraction becomes your α.*

If most of the mismatch lies in the compensable subspace (||η|| is small), α approaches 1 — compensate aggressively. If most of the mismatch is irremovable noise (||η|| is large), α approaches 0 — compensate little, to avoid overfitting. Entirely data-driven, zero hyperparameters.

Theory (Theorem 3.1) proves: under finite-calibration error, the optimal α* = μ²/(μ²+σ²) — the ratio of the true signal energy to the estimation-error energy. CoreQ uses ||s||²/||η||² as an observable proxy for this ratio.

Once the new calibration objective is set, a discrete rounding problem remains. CoreQ writes it as a triangular least-squares problem (the Hessian Cholesky factorization gives a triangular structure R = L^T), which each row can solve greedily from right to left — equivalent to LDLQ but under the new objective.

*** Why It Worked — and What Challenges Remain

Early greedy rounding errors propagate through the off-diagonal entries of R, corrupting the "conditional center" for all subsequent columns. So CoreQ also provides an upgraded variant, *K-CoreQ*: treat each row's rounding as a depth-n tree search, and retain the K lowest-cost partial paths (beam search). K=4 or K=8 yields significant error reduction; K=1 degenerates to greedy.

Experimentally: on LLaMA-2-70B at 2-bit, CoreQ reduces WikiText-2 perplexity by *48–56%* relative to the strongest baseline.

* Frontier Extensions

CoreQ is a May 2026 paper; as of now it represents the leading edge of the "mismatch-aware PTQ + adaptive compensation" line. Several adjacent directions are worth watching:

- *FOEM (2507.11017, "First-Order Error Matters")*: Emerging in the same period, it approaches quantization compensation from a first-order error perspective. It is a parallel development, not a descendant of CoreQ.
- *Chen et al. 2025 (2507.18553, "GPTQ as Babai's Nearest Plane")*: Reinterprets GPTQ through lattice theory, recasting the entire PTQ problem as a Closest Vector Problem (CVP). If this line continues, it could push CoreQ's beam search toward full lattice decoding algorithms (sphere decoding).
- *SAES-SVD (2026, ICLR)*: Also concerned with error accumulation, but uses SVD-based compression rather than quantization. It shows that "upstream drift compensation" is not a problem unique to quantization.

* Insights

Reading this 30-year evolutionary arc, what stands out is not "algorithms getting cleverer" but *assumptions becoming more honest*.

Each generation's breakthrough is essentially *puncturing one hidden assumption of the previous generation*:

- OBS → AdaRound: Punctured the assumption that you can retrain after pruning. PTQ must be a one-shot operation.
- AdaRound → GPTQ: Punctured the assumption that learning is necessary. The Hessian's closed-form solution is good enough.
- GPTQ → GPTAQ: Punctured the assumption that computing error on full-precision inputs is correct. At deployment, inputs are contaminated.
- GPTAQ → CoreQ: Punctured the assumption that a fixed compensation strength is appropriate. Each layer's trustworthiness is different.

At a deeper level: this line repeatedly demonstrates the lesson that *once a proxy objective is written incorrectly, all downstream optimization is wasted effort.* No matter how elegantly GPTQ optimizes its objective, it cannot produce the right result. CoreQ's real contribution is not "a cleverer algorithm" — it is "a more honest objective function."

Where is this line most likely to go next? Three directions:

1. *Joint cross-layer mismatch optimization.* CoreQ still operates layer by layer, computing each layer's α independently. But the 30 layers' α values interact — what an upstream layer omits compensating, a downstream layer might be able to handle. The joint optimization space is enormous.
2. *Push beam search toward full lattice decoding.* Chen et al. have already reinterpreted GPTQ as Babai's algorithm; the natural next step is sphere decoding.
3. *The boundary between learning-free and finetune-light will blur.* CoreQ has nearly reached the limit of "no learning." Going further means either returning to finetuning (back to QAT) or switching paradigms entirely (e.g., SVD, low-rank approximation).

* Takeaways

This line of work offers a few very concrete lessons for doing research:

1. *Focus on the proxy objective, not the algorithm itself.* GPTQ's algorithmic efficiency was refined for three years, but the real gold was buried in "the objective function is wrong." When everyone is racing to improve the algorithm, step back and ask: "Is what we are minimizing actually what we want?"

2. *Fixed hyperparameters someone else left untouched usually contain an adaptive opportunity.* GPTAQ used a fixed α=1 or 0.5 — that alone is a signal, meaning "nobody has seriously thought about what α should be." CoreQ turned that 0.5 into a closed-form solution, and a paper was born.

3. *The true value of a theoretical analysis is revealing why "the knob should be in the middle."* Theorem 3.1 in CoreQ proposes no new algorithm; it proves one simple thing: the optimal α* = μ²/(μ²+σ²) must be strictly between 0 and 1. But that proof is precisely what transforms "a middle value is better" from intuition into inevitability — and thereby legitimizes ||s||²/||η||² as an observable proxy.

The practical upshot: *the next time you encounter a baseline with a fixed hyperparameter, your first instinct should be to ask whether the optimal value of that hyperparameter can be estimated from the input data itself.* Nine times out of ten it can — and nine times out of ten that estimate beats hand-tuning.
