* One-liner
Reasoning models have become increasingly verbose — models like OpenAI o1 and
DeepSeek-R1 that "think out loud" can burn thousands of tokens on a trivial
question. This river traces how, over the past two years, the field has
progressively learned to make models shut up on their own: from hard token
caps, to difficulty-aware budgets, and finally to CRISP — a scheme where the
model uses a concise version of itself as its own teacher.

* Who is the real protagonist
The real protagonist is not CRISP — it is a question:
*How do you make a thinking model say less without making it dumber?*

CRISP is simply the cleanest answer currently on that timeline.

* Evolutionary narrative: how the problem grew

** Layer 0: Reasoning models learned to think out loud — and to ramble

Late 2024: OpenAI released o1, followed by DeepSeek-R1, Qwen3, Gemini 2.5,
and s1 in quick succession. What these models share: before answering, they
emit a long internal monologue — enumerating cases, second-guessing themselves,
recomputing. It genuinely helps; benchmark scores climb steadily.

But the cost arrived with it. Even trivial questions get the full treatment.
Ask "what is 1+1?" and the model starts with "Let me think — the essence of
addition is……", spending hundreds of tokens before giving the answer. Reasoning
tokens translate directly into inference cost, and that is not acceptable.

Researchers named this *overthinking*. The question was now well-posed:
*Can a model be taught to deliberate deeply only when the problem warrants it,
and stay terse otherwise?*

** Layer 1 (Route A): Hard RL — enforce a token budget

The most intuitive fix: penalize the model during training for exceeding N
tokens.

- *L1* (2025) adds a token-count penalty inside the GRPO training loop.
- *ThinkPrune* (2025) jointly constrains length and accuracy in RL.
- *DLER* (2025) finds a flat cap too blunt and adds curriculum learning —
  loosening the constraint early, tightening it later.
- *Leash* (2025) shapes the penalty as a sigmoid to smooth the gradient.

This route works. But it steps on three landmines:

1. *Cannot escape ground-truth answers* — RL needs an accuracy signal, and
   accuracy requires labels. Useless in unannotated domains.
2. *Can rigidify the model*. Xu et al. (2026) show that prolonged RL causes
   models to become "overconfident" — they stop exploring, and reasoning
   diversity drops sharply.
3. *The cap is static*. Easy and hard questions are treated identically: easy
   questions are under-compressed, hard questions are truncated mid-thought.

The third problem is the most damaging. The next layer addresses it directly.

** Layer 1 (Route B): Stop treating all questions equally — adapt to difficulty

If easy questions should be compressed more and hard ones less, let the
algorithm figure that out by itself.

- *DiPO* (2026) uses the model's own rollout pass rate as a difficulty signal:
  high pass-rate questions get squeezed harder.
- *DIET* (2025) follows the same logic and assigns each question its own token
  budget.
- *LiteCoT / DAP* (2025) take a different angle — distill concise solutions
  directly from a stronger model.

This route is smarter than A, but it introduces two new problems:

1. *Difficulty must be computed*. Estimating difficulty is itself expensive —
   you need multiple rollouts per question just to estimate the pass rate.
2. *LiteCoT and similar methods reintroduce distribution shift*. The student
   learns the teacher's voice, not its own. This recurring problem in the RL
   community: training on another model's reasoning traces often captures
   surface form while losing the underlying reasoning.

At this point a natural question surfaces:
*Can difficulty adaptation emerge implicitly, without being computed explicitly?*

** Layer 1 (Route C): Let the model be its own teacher — self-distillation

Self-distillation is a strand that predates the reasoning-compression problem.
The basic idea: same model, two roles — one generates targets as teacher, the
other learns as student.

- *SDFT* (2026, Shenfeld et al.) already showed that self-distillation SFT
  forgets old skills less readily than standard SFT.
- *OPSD* (2026, Zhao et al.) brings this to reasoning compression: the teacher
  writes demonstrations, the student learns. *But the teacher has access to
  ground-truth answers* — reintroducing the original sin of requiring labels.
- *SDPO* (2026, Hübotter et al.) uses dense feedback for credit assignment,
  also requiring supervision.
- *PACED* (2026) weights by student ability, but ability estimation again
  requires correct/wrong signals.
- *OPCD* (2026, Ye et al.) distills system-prompt behavior — already very close
  to CRISP, but does not address difficulty adaptation.

Self-distillation eliminates distribution shift — teacher and student share the
same origin. But as long as the teacher side needs any one of {answers,
difficulty, reward}, the method is still circling the pits of Routes A and B.

At this point all the cards are on the table. The remaining question is:
*Is there a form of self-distillation that requires zero external signal?*

** Layer 2: CRISP's one key move

CRISP's answer is disarmingly simple:

#+begin_quote
The teacher is the same model with a single "be concise" instruction prepended.
#+end_quote

The concrete procedure:

1. The student model generates a rollout under its normal prompt (a sequence of
   reasoning tokens).
2. The same model, with a "please be concise" instruction added, re-evaluates
   the logits at every position over *the student's own token sequence* — this
   is "what the teacher would think here."
3. A *per-token reverse KL* is used as the loss, pulling the student toward the
   teacher.
4. Periodically, the teacher weights are refreshed to match the current student
   (teacher weights ← current student weights).

Several subtle design choices are embedded here:

- *No ground-truth answers*. The teacher does not know the correct solution —
  only that the response should be concise.
- *No difficulty estimator*. Yet in practice, easy questions are compressed
  more and hard ones less — because for easy questions the "concise me" and the
  "verbose me" diverge strongly, producing a large KL signal and strong
  compression pressure; for hard questions, both versions must reason carefully
  anyway, so their token distributions are already close, the KL is small, and
  the pressure is light. *Difficulty adaptation emerges from the geometry of KL
  divergence; it is not engineered in.*
- *Reverse KL, not forward KL*. Reverse KL is mode-seeking: it concentrates the
  student on the teacher's high-probability paths rather than imitating the
  full long tail. This choice directly governs how decisive the model's outputs
  become.
- *On-policy*. Distillation happens on sequences the student itself generated,
  not sequences the teacher generated. Distribution shift is eliminated
  entirely.

The whole system has no RL, no reward, no external model, no annotation.
Just a "be concise" instruction and a KL loss.

** Results

- Evaluated on Qwen3-8B/14B and R1-Distill-Llama-8B.
- MATH-500: tokens cut 57–59%, accuracy *increases* 9–16 points.
- AIME 2024: on the 14B model, tokens cut 41%, accuracy up 10 points.
- DeepPlanning (multi-step agent planning): tokens cut 42–51%.

The combination of compression *plus* accuracy gain is counterintuitive. CRISP's
explanation: *the removed tokens were actively interfering with good decisions —
second-guessing, redundant enumeration, meandering trial-and-error. Removing
them lets the genuine reasoning surface.*

** Where does this line go next?

CRISP eliminates all external signals, but several dimensions remain open:

1. *The prompt dimension*. The current system uses only "be concise." Replacing
   it with "be rigorous," or "show work for X but skip Y," turns the same KL
   distillation framework into a general-purpose controllable style-shaping
   tool.
2. *Agent scenarios*. The paper already validates on DeepPlanning, but
   judging when to be verbose versus silent in multi-step agent planning is
   far more complex than a math problem. The boundary of applicability is not
   yet clear.
3. *Hybrid with RL*. Fully removing RL is CRISP's selling point, but in some
   tasks ground-truth feedback is free (code either runs or it doesn't). The
   likely near-future recipe is KL distillation as the backbone with sparse RL
   signals as a supplement.

* Core insight (what is really happening across the whole line)

On the surface this looks like "iterative technical improvements to
chain-of-thought compression." At a deeper level, what is really happening is
*the progressive elimination of external signals*:

| Era | Training signal |
|------+----------|
| RL era (L1/DLER) | Ground-truth answers + length reward (two external signals) |
| Difficulty era (DiPO/DIET) | Ground-truth answers + difficulty estimator (still external) |
| Early self-distillation (OPSD/SDPO) | Ground-truth answers + self-distillation (half external) |
| CRISP | One prompt instruction (zero external signal) |

Reading this timeline, a quiet directional current becomes visible in LLM
post-training:
*Make the model use what it already knows to teach itself something new.*

CRISP's significance goes beyond the specific task of compressing CoT. It
demonstrates a paradigm: *different capability dimensions — conciseness,
rigor, style — may each be internalizable into weights via the same posture of
"prompt one version, KL-distill the other."* If that road proves out, the
heavyweight "external reward model" architecture of RLHF could be substantially
slimmed down.

* Most likely next steps

1. *Generalize prompt-conditioned distillation*. Distill not just conciseness
   but any capability dimension that can be described by a prompt.
2. *Sparse RL signals as a safety net*. KL distillation handles style and
   length; sparse RL handles correctness. A combined approach.
3. *Long-horizon agent scenarios*. The real test is multi-step, multi-modal
   agent reasoning. Math problems are just the appetizer.
