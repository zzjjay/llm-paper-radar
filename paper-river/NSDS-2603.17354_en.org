* The Problem River

To compress a 7B or 13B model down to something that fits on a consumer GPU, only two paths exist: either drop every layer below 4-bit and watch the model collapse entirely, or be clever about it — give the "critical" layers a few more bits while giving the "unimportant" ones fewer.

Sounds simple. The difficulty lives in one sentence: *how do you know which layers are critical?*

Over seven years, each generation has used a different "scale" to weigh this question: first the Hessian (second-order gradients), then the distribution of activations, then weight outliers, then weight entropy… Each scale captured one face of the truth and missed another. NSDS is the latest chapter of this river in 2026 — it argues that predecessors were weighing along only one dimension, the numerical one, but weights have a second dimension called structure, and weighing only the numerical side causes a whole class of layers to slip through: the ones that look mild on the surface yet explode once compressed.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2019-2020 | HAWQ / HAWQ-v2: The Hessian as a "Sensitivity Meter"

*** What Problem It Saw

The most naive way to compress a network below 4-bit is to apply the same bit-width everywhere. But in practice some layers are load-bearing walls — disturb them slightly and performance collapses; others are decorative panels — compress them to 2-bit and nothing changes. A uniform policy means shaving down the load-bearing walls too.

At the time, nobody could say which layers were which.

*** Its Solution

HAWQ borrowed an old tool from optimization theory: the Hessian. The Hessian matrix of a function at a point describes how sharply it curves there. Large Hessian = perturbing the parameters slightly sends the loss soaring = this layer is sensitive. Small Hessian = a flat region, the loss barely moves = this layer is not sensitive.

HAWQ-v2 therefore used the Hessian trace to rank all layers and allocated more bits to layers with larger traces.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

The math is elegant. But computing the Hessian requires a backward pass, which requires calibration data. For a 70B model, that step alone is enough to make you cry through the night. "We have sensitivity estimates, but the cost is too high" — that became the next problem for successors to solve.

** 2023 | GPTQ: Forget Mixed Precision — Push Uniform Low-Bit to Its Limit

*** What Problem It Saw

The HAWQ approach was too expensive. Frantar flipped the question: what if instead of complicating things with mixed precision, you could make uniform 4-bit good enough that you no longer needed it?

*** Its Solution

GPTQ quantizes weights one column at a time; after each column is quantized it applies an OBS-style update to adjust the remaining columns so that quantization error does not accumulate. The essence is using a small amount of calibration data to find a smart rounding strategy for uniform low-bit quantization.

AWQ (2024) is a variant of the same idea: before quantization, multiply each weight channel by a scale factor so that important channels (those with large activations) land more accurately on the quantization grid.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

At the 4-bit level, GPTQ/AWQ squeeze "uniform quantization" almost to its theoretical limit. But push further — 3-bit, 2-bit — and the uniform scheme collapses. The load-bearing-wall problem never disappeared; it was only deferred.

Mixed precision is unavoidable.

** 2023 | HQQ: Calibration Data Is Itself Dead Weight — Drop It

*** What Problem It Saw

GPTQ still needs a small calibration set. In production this is painful — pick a bad calibration set and the quantized model performs suspiciously well on that distribution while degrading on everything else. This is *calibration overfitting*.

And for truly large models (70B+), the cost of one calibration-plus-backward pass is itself a barrier to rapid experimentation.

*** Its Solution

HQQ (Half-Quadratic Quantization) drops data entirely. It formulates quantization as a half-quadratic optimization problem with a closed-form solution for each weight tensor. The only input is the weights themselves; no forward pass is required.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

Zero data, zero gradients, extremely fast. But HQQ uses a uniform bit-width per layer — it solved *how to quantize*, not *how many bits each layer should get*. One piece of the mixed-precision puzzle is still missing.

How great would it be to do *mixed precision* with *no calibration data* at all?

** 2024-2025 | KurtBoost / EWQ: Just Look at the Weights — Single-Heuristic Sensitivity

*** What Problem It Saw

The HAWQ route (Hessian) is accurate but costly; the GPTQ/HQQ route is fast but layer-agnostic. The middle ground — "I want to glance at the weights and immediately know which layers matter" — was vacant.

*** Its Solution

Methods like KurtBoost focus on *outliers* in the weights. Their intuition: if a layer's weight distribution has long, heavy tails (high kurtosis), then quantization will stretch the quantization range to accommodate those large values, squashing the many small values into the same bucket — and precision collapses. High kurtosis layer = sensitive layer = allocate more bits.

EWQ (Entropy-Weighted Quantization) is a different scale from the same family: compute the Shannon entropy of each layer's weight distribution. High entropy = high information density = sensitive.

Both are *calibration-free* and run extremely fast.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

They provide a lightweight answer to the load-bearing-wall problem. But NSDS's Figure 1 exposes their blind spot: *some layers have low kurtosis and mild entropy, yet they collapse just the same once compressed.*

The reason is that these layers have no numerical anomaly; the problem is *structural* — their singular value spectra (from SVD) are rich, carrying a key portion of the model's expressive capacity. A scale that only reads numerical values cannot weigh structural importance.

More subtly: these methods treat every weight matrix inside a layer (Q, K, V, O, up, down) identically. But these matrices *do fundamentally different jobs* inside a Transformer: some are "detectors" deciding which tokens to attend to; others are "writers" writing information back into the residual stream. Treating them identically is like evaluating a chef and a waiter against the same KPI.

** 2026 | NSDS: Two Scales at Once, Applied Per Role

*** What Problem It Saw

Continuing from the previous section. NSDS identified two concrete gaps:
1. A single numerical heuristic (kurtosis / entropy) misses layers that are numerically mild but structurally critical.
2. Different modules within a layer (QK / OV / up / down) serve different functions and should not be measured with the same ruler.

*** Its Solution

NSDS cuts along two dimensions simultaneously.

*First cut: divide a layer by "role."* Drawing on mechanistic interpretability, it merges W_Q W_K^T into a "QK Detector" (deciding who to attend to) and W_V W_O into an "OV Writer" (what to write back); the FFN is treated analogously, with W_in as the "Neuron Detector" and W_out as the "Writer."

*Second cut: apply two scales to each role.*
- *Numerical Vulnerability (NV)*: still kurtosis, inheriting from predecessors.
- *Structural Expressiveness (SE)*: perform SVD, examine the singular value spectrum's "total energy" (L1 norm) and "information entropy" (spectral entropy), weighted differently by role — Detectors are scored by the kurtosis of input singular vectors (how sharply this detector concentrates), Writers by the projection strength of output singular vectors onto the vocabulary space (how strongly this writer influences the final output).

*Third cut: how to merge the two scale readings into one score.* Different scales have incompatible numerical ranges; naively averaging lets the larger one dominate. NSDS normalizes each reading to (0, 1) with MAD-Sigmoid (a z-score based on median absolute deviation), then combines them with Soft-OR (1 - Π(1 - p_i)) — so that a red flag on *any* dimension marks the layer as sensitive.

Layers are then ranked by total score; given a bit budget b̄ ∈ [2, 4], the most sensitive group receives 4-bit and the rest receive 2-bit.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It completes the story of "what to look at": everyone before was searching along the single numerical axis, while NSDS adds the structural axis and the role axis. A red flag on either axis preserves that layer's precision.

New pits opened:
- Still heuristic-based, not directly minimizing task loss.
- Validated only on models ≤14B; behavior at 70B is unknown.
- Whether the "Detector / Writer" mechanistic-interpretability narrative holds for MoE, SSM, and hybrid architectures is an open question.

* Frontier Extensions

NSDS appeared as a preprint in March 2026; as of the time of writing (2026-05) publicly traceable follow-up work is still sparse. Along this line, the most likely near-term developments are:
- *Task-aware + calibration-free hybrids*: use a tiny amount of data for "light calibration" layered on top of NSDS's role decomposition.
- *Group-level rather than layer-level allocation*: assign bits to individual attention heads within a layer, carrying the "role decomposition" all the way through.
- *Transfer to KV cache quantization*: NSDS currently only touches weights; quantizing the KV cache during inference is a separate, largely open territory.

These are trends only — no specific papers to cite yet, and that is stated honestly here.

* Insight

Looking back across seven years, what truly happened in this river is not that "sensitivity estimation got more accurate" — it is that *the way we conceptualize a "layer" kept shifting*.

- HAWQ treated a layer as a mathematical object — a point on the loss landscape.
- GPTQ/AWQ treated a layer as a numerical container — the problem to solve is round-off.
- HQQ treated a layer as an independent sub-problem — decouple it and solve in closed form.
- KurtBoost/EWQ treated a layer as a statistical distribution — summarize it with one number.
- NSDS, for the first time, treats a layer as a *mechanical device* — one that contains functionally distinct parts (Detector / Writer), each with its own temperament.

This move looks like "we just added one more SVD metric," but it fundamentally imports the vocabulary of *mechanistic interpretability* into the engineering discipline of quantization. Once a layer is seen as a decomposable mechanical structure, the downstream possibilities multiply: different-role components can be given different algorithms, different bit allocations, different pruning strategies, different distillation targets independently.

*Where the next step most likely leads*: push the "role" granularity from weight modules down to individual attention heads and neuron groups, ultimately arriving at "each mechanistic circuit decides for itself how many bits it deserves."

* Takeaways

Lessons for navigating research directions:

1. *Don't just add tricks along a single axis.* When an entire generation is working tricks on numerical statistics, opening a cut in a perpendicular direction (structure, geometry, mechanism) frequently yields the largest marginal return. The core value NSDS adds is not that the SE metric is especially ingenious — it is that NSDS points out "there was an SE dimension all along."

2. *"Cheapness" as a property pushes research forward on its own.* From HAWQ to HQQ to NSDS, each step eliminates calibration, gradients, or data — and every time the cost drops by an order of magnitude it unlocks new application scenarios and forces new methods into existence. If your method requires calibration, ask yourself: could it not?

3. *Mechanistic interpretability is transitioning from "understanding models" to "engineering tool."* It was originally invented to answer "why does the model behave this way?" NSDS uses it to answer "how should the model be compressed?" When one field's tools start being used as screwdrivers in another field, that is a sign the tools have genuinely matured — and it also means papers that "borrow from mechanistic interpretability" will appear in large numbers, so entering that space now is still early enough.

The bottom line, for practical use: next time you face a "uniform vs. fine-grained" trade-off, ask one question — besides the dimension I am currently measuring, is there a perpendicular dimension that everyone else has been missing?
