* Target Paper

*LBLLM: Lightweight Binarization of Large Language Models via Three-Stage Distillation* (Song et al., 2026)

One sentence: using three-stage distillation (PTQ initialization → weights-only QAT → activation QAT), push a 7B model to W(1+1)A4 — 1-bit weights + 1-bit per-group bitmap + 4-bit activations — with no high-precision bypass channels, no rotation matrices, on a single GPU, 0.016B tokens, in tens of GPU-hours.

It drives a wedge between two camps: the *expensive QAT route* (BitNet, FBI-LLM — training from scratch for thousands of GPU-hours) and the *cheap PTQ route* (BiLLM, ARB-LLM, BWA — fast quantization but severe accuracy drops). LBLLM argues: both are wrong; start with PTQ, finish with lightweight QAT.

* Evolutionary Narrative

The following traces the timeline step by step, answering one question at each stage: what gap did the previous step leave, and how did this step climb out of it.

** Step 0: LLM.int8 (2022) — First Sighting of the Outlier Ghost

The story begins not with how to make models smaller, but with *why they can't be compressed*.

Dettmers (2022) discovered that naively rounding LLM activations (the intermediate values produced by each layer) to int8 destroys accuracy. Yet most channels behave nicely and can be rounded. The culprit is *a small number of channels* — they occasionally spike to very large magnitudes, hundreds or thousands, blowing up the entire quantization range.

His fix was blunt: identify those outlier channels, keep them in FP16; quantize everything else to int8. This is *mixed-precision*.

Two gaps remain: first, mixed-precision is hardware-unfriendly (switching GEMM kernels between different precisions); second, the decision of "which channels" is made offline and is inflexible. But *the concept of outliers is now firmly established — every subsequent method must wrestle with it*.

** Step 1: SmoothQuant (2023) — Instead of Keeping FP16, Tame the Activations

Xiao (2023) had an elegant idea: activations have outliers because certain channels are naturally more active. What if you *borrow* some of that activation energy upfront and lend it to the weights?

Concretely: divide each activation channel by a scalar s, then multiply the corresponding weight column by s. Mathematically equivalent (W·X = (W·s)·(X/s)), but after this transform the activation range is flattened, dramatically reducing quantization error; the trade-off is that weights become slightly harder to quantize.

This step *converts outlier handling from an online assignment to an offline transform*, which is hardware-friendly. But new gaps appear: how do you choose the smoothing coefficients? And the weights have limits too — below W4A4, this trick is no longer sufficient.

** Step 2: BiLLM (2024) — Push Weights All the Way to 1 Bit

Huang (2024) pushed the target to the extreme: 1-bit weights.

His core observation: weights have outliers too. Most weights cluster near zero and behave well, but a tiny fraction are large and disproportionately important — these are called *salient weights*. If you binarize everything uniformly, those critical weights are destroyed and the model collapses.

BiLLM's approach: two tiers. The important minority of weights are kept at higher precision (a bitmap marks which ones are salient); the vast majority are aggressively binarized. This trick made 1-bit quantization viable in LLMs for the first time.

But the gaps follow immediately: you introduce *extra bits to store the bitmap* and *extra high-precision storage for salient weights*. It claims 1 bit but is really "1 bit + a collection of high-precision patches." Compression ratio and inference speed are both dragged down by those patches.

More importantly: BiLLM never touches activations. They remain FP16.

** Step 3: ARB-LLM (2024) — BiLLM's Scale/Offset Wasn't Solved Optimally

Li (2024) didn't change BiLLM's overall architecture; he changed the solving procedure.

BiLLM finds the optimal scale α and offset μ for each weight group using a closed-form formula — essentially max-min, computed in one shot. Li argues: this solution is actually coupled; scale and offset influence each other and should be *optimized alternately and iteratively* to approach the true minimum reconstruction error.

Hence the name *Alternating Refined Binarization*.

This step improves BiLLM's accuracy further, but *the core paradigm is unchanged*: still PTQ, still a bitmap, still high-precision bypass channels, still untouched activations.

** Step 4: BWA (2025) — Activations Finally Touched, but the Bypass Remains

Song (2025) — the same first author as LBLLM — pushes the goal one step further: *binary weights and binary activations*.

He uses clustering to search for activation quantization parameters, avoiding large data and compute dependencies. This is the first time a PTQ method simultaneously tackles weights and activations.

But — the high-precision bypass channels are not removed. He knows that removing them causes an immediate accuracy collapse. So BWA's "binary weight + binary activation" carries an asterisk: *+ some high-precision correction channels*.

This is the state of affairs on the eve of LBLLM: the PTQ route has reached W1A1, but the bypass patches can never be eliminated without collapse.

** Step 5: LBLLM (2026) — Eliminate the Bypass, Recover Accuracy with Lightweight QAT

LBLLM's insight is crisp: *The reason BWA cannot remove the bypass channels is that it insists on pure PTQ*.

PTQ's essence is "no training; find optimal quantization parameters through math alone." Under the extreme compression of W(1+1)A4, the parameters found by pure math always leave irrecoverable errors, and high-precision channels are needed as a safety net.

The *QAT route* (BitNet, FBI-LLM) can train accuracy back — but it's too expensive: training a binarized model from scratch starts at thousands of GPU-hours.

LBLLM takes the middle road:

1. *Stage 1: PTQ Initialization.* Use an existing PTQ binarization method (similar to BiLLM/ARB-LLM) to get a solid starting point.
2. *Stage 2: Weights-only QAT (WAT).* Activations stay in FP16; apply layer-wise distillation only to the binarized weights, bitmaps, and quantization parameters.
3. *Stage 3: Activation-aware Refinement (AAR).* Compress activations to 4 bits; introduce learnable clipping parameters to handle outliers; fine-tune only the quantization parameters.

Why two separate QAT stages? LBLLM finds that if you quantize weights and activations simultaneously from the start, *the straight-through estimator gradients are corrupted by interference between weight errors and activation errors*, making training unstable. Decoupling them — stabilize weights first, then add activations — allows training to converge.

Results: single GPU, 0.016B tokens, tens of hours — a binarized 7B model that surpasses all PTQ baselines, approaches full QAT quality, *with no high-precision bypass channels and no rotation matrices*.

** Branch: Why LBLLM Also Rejects QuaRot/SpinQuant

Not everyone follows the "binarization + salient-weight bypass" path. Another school (Atom, QuaRot, SpinQuant, DuQuant) argues: instead of keeping bypass channels, *make outliers disappear*.

Their approach: insert a rotation matrix (Hadamard or learned) between weights and activations, redistributing outliers across all channels via a linear transform so that every channel becomes "well-behaved" and can be uniformly quantized to low bit-width.

This works quite well at W4A4 — QuaRot is a strong W4A4 baseline today. But LBLLM explicitly identifies its fatal flaw: *rotation schemes collapse when moving toward 2 bits*. Rotation can flatten outliers, but it cannot conjure expressiveness — the bit-width is simply too narrow; no matter how you rotate, the information capacity is insufficient.

On top of that, the rotation matrix itself must be stored and computed, adding another non-trivial overhead.

* Core Thread: What Is Actually Changing Beneath the Surface

On the surface this looks like "bit-width keeps getting smaller": FP16 → INT8 → INT4 → INT2 → INT1. But *what is truly changing underneath is not the bit-width — it is the strategy for handling outliers*.

- *2022 paradigm*: outliers are unquantizable; keep them in FP16. Strategy: *isolation*.
- *2023 paradigm*: outliers can be transformed. Strategy: *migration* (onto weights, onto other channels).
- *2024 paradigm*: weights have outliers too; handle them in tiers. Strategy: *grouping + partial high-precision fallback*.
- *2025 paradigm*: attempt to push everything to the limit; bypass channels ironically become the primary source of accuracy (a telling reversal).
- *2026 paradigm*: *abandon the search for a clever offline formula*; accept that extreme compression requires training. Strategy: *PTQ for a rough initialization + lightweight QAT to finish*.

In other words, the five-year story is one of *researchers gradually giving up on the question "can I find a smart offline formula to handle outliers?" and accepting "I need just a little bit of training."*

LBLLM is the pivotal evidence for this paradigm shift: it shows that you don't need to train from scratch (the QAT way) — you only need a lightweight 0.016B-token fine-tune after PTQ, and in exchange the bypass channels disappear entirely. The ROI is high enough to leave the "pure PTQ camp" with almost nowhere to stand.

* Where Things Go Next

Several directions I consider reasonably probable:

1. *Lightweight QAT will become the default.* LBLLM has demonstrated that lightweight QAT offers far better cost-effectiveness than either pure PTQ or full QAT. Future extreme quantization methods will likely include a lightweight QAT stage by default. The categorical distinction between "PTQ papers" and "QAT papers" may well dissolve.
2. *Activation quantization will become the new front line.* Weight binarization is approaching a ceiling — there is little room left. But ultra-low-bit activations (A4 → A2) have barely been explored. LBLLM solves A4 with learnable clipping; A2 is waiting for the next clever approach.
3. *KV cache quantization.* LBLLM quantizes the KV cache together with activations but without dedicated optimization. In long-context settings the KV cache is already the dominant bottleneck; a wave of extreme quantization methods specifically designed for the KV cache seems near.
4. *Hardware codesign.* Unusual bit-width combinations like W(1+1)A4 require custom CUDA kernels. LBLLM notes that it reuses kernels from BWA/ARB-LLM, but more aggressive bit-width combinations will inevitably drive demand for new hardware instruction support.
