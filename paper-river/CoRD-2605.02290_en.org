* The River of the Problem

Take a large model that can do "long-chain reasoning" and *teach* that skill to a student model ten-plus times smaller. Once the student has learned it, you run it locally, cheaply, and fast — yet its problem-solving level stays close to the teacher's.

This is what the *reasoning distillation* river has been doing for the past decade. On the surface it looks like one variety of "model compression," but at its core it is completely unlike ordinary quantization or pruning — what you are compressing is not parameters, it is the *thinking process*.

Why is it hard? Because "how the teacher thinks" is far more complex than "what the teacher answers." You have to capture the teacher's scratch-paper process — "wait, this approach is wrong, let me try another one," "let me redo that calculation" — all that *back-and-forth* — and feed it to the student to imitate. Once the teacher's scratch pad runs to ten thousand tokens filled with dead ends and backtracks, the question of "which parts are worth copying" becomes an intractable search problem.

Every generation in this river has wrestled with the same underlying difficulty: *what kind of intermediate reasoning process is "good," and how do you find such processes efficiently?*

- 2016: "Just copy the answer; the process doesn't matter."
- 2022: "The process matters — let the model write it out."
- 2024: "Have another model score each step, and prune the bad ones."
- Early 2025: "Search the entire reasoning tree directly."
- Mid 2025: "That won't work — new reasoning is too long to search. Just pick a few complete trajectories."
- 2026, CoRD: "You're all selecting *within a single teacher* — why not let *multiple teachers* deliberate while they reason?"

Each layer solves the concrete pain points left by the previous one. CoRD did not appear from nowhere; it is the natural next bend in this river.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2016 | Sequence-level KD: Learn the Whole Sentence, Not the Logits

*** What Problem It Saw

Classical knowledge distillation (Hinton 2015) is *token-level logit matching*: the teacher outputs a probability distribution over each token, and the student learns that distribution. This is natural for classification — there are only a handful of classes, and having the student mimic the teacher's "soft labels" makes sense.

But applied to *generation tasks* it breaks down: at each token position, the teacher's logit distribution is conditioned on *the teacher's own preceding prefix*, while the student must make decisions conditioned on *its own prefix*. Once the prefixes diverge — and they almost always do — logit matching becomes meaningless.

*** Its Solution

Kim and Rush said: abandon logits. Have the teacher generate a complete output for each input, and train the student on that output via supervised fine-tuning — collapsing distillation from the token level to the *sequence level*.

This looks like a step backward (less information), but in practice it works better — because the student learns *coherent complete sequences* rather than logits corrupted by prefix mismatch.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left)

Intuition: the natural unit of generation is the *sequence*, not the *token*. Forcing alignment at the token level sacrifices sequence-level coherence.

The pit it left: this approach is completely *uninterested in how the teacher arrived at its answer* — you see the teacher's words but not the teacher's draft. For simple generation tasks (translation, summarization) that is fine, but to distill *reasoning ability*, looking only at the teacher's final answer is clearly insufficient. The next layer would bring the "teacher's draft" onto the table.

** 2022 | CoT: Replace "Mental Arithmetic" with "Writing Out the Work"

*** What Problem It Saw

In the GPT-3 era, people noticed a strange phenomenon: ask a large model directly "Xiao Ming has 3 apples, buys 5 more, eats 2 — how many are left?" and it would often be wrong. But add "Let's think step by step" to the prompt and accuracy doubles.

Why? Because a transformer performs a fixed amount of computation at each token position. Asking it to leap to the final answer in one shot means doing all the reasoning *within a single token*, exceeding its "computational budget."

*** Its Solution

Wei et al., *Chain-of-Thought Prompting*: include a few examples in the prompt that show "write out the reasoning process first, then give the answer," and the model imitates this format. This is equivalent to replacing "mental arithmetic" with "writing out the work" — externalizing the reasoning into intermediate tokens and spreading the computation across hundreds of tokens.

The effect was striking: on GSM8K (elementary school math), PaLM 540B accuracy jumped from 18% to 57%.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left)

Intuition: a transformer's computational depth is bounded by the number of layers, but its "width" (token count) is nearly unlimited. CoT *shifts computation from the depth dimension to the width dimension*.

The pit it left: CoT made the reasoning process explicit, but *intermediate steps can be wrong*. Models sometimes write chains like "3+5=9, 9−2=6" — one step is wrong and every subsequent step inherits the error. During distillation, the student faithfully learns "3+5=9." The next layer needed to address "which steps are trustworthy."

** 2024 | PRM: Score Every Step

*** What Problem It Saw

CoT externalized reasoning but provided *no quality gate* — wrong steps and correct steps were mixed together. If you fine-tune the student directly on the teacher's CoT data, the student absorbs the mistakes too.

Worse: on tasks with binary correctness (math problems with a single right answer), a *correct final answer* can come from *incorrect reasoning* (lucky guess), and *correct reasoning* can yield a *wrong final answer* (arithmetic slip on the last step). Looking only at the final answer cannot filter reasoning quality.

*** Its Solution

Lai et al. and subsequent PRM work: train a *Process Reward Model* — a dedicated model that scores every step of the reasoning. Training data comes from human annotation or MCTS rollouts: for each intermediate step, label "this step was correct / incorrect."

With a PRM, distillation becomes: teacher generates multiple reasoning chains → PRM scores each step → select trajectories where every step scores high → feed to the student.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left)

Intuition: the supervision signal is refined from "final answer" down to "every step"; the student learns *locally correct* reasoning, not "luckily correct" reasoning.

Two new pits:

1. *Annotation cost explodes*. PRM training requires massive step-level annotation — 10–100× more expensive than answer-level annotation.
2. *PRM only filters, it doesn't explore*. It can say "this step is bad" but cannot suggest "so what should this step be?" The next layer would bring in search.

** 2025-01 | MCTS-based Reasoning Search: Turning Reasoning into a Chess Game

*** What Problem It Saw

PRM is passive scoring — it evaluates whatever you give it. But if the teacher's initial reasoning has already gone astray, PRM can only reject the whole thing; it cannot *find* a good path.

This is exactly like chess: an evaluation function (PRM) tells you "how good is the current position," but you also need a *search algorithm* to explore "which move to make next."

*** Its Solution

Yao et al. and contemporaneous work imported Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS):

- Reasoning state = tree node
- Each reasoning step = tree edge
- Expand high-scoring subtrees (PRM provides scores)
- Monte Carlo rollouts estimate long-term value
- Back-propagate values; iteratively refine the tree

The high-scoring paths found by search become the distillation data.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left)

Intuition: reasoning is sequential decision-making; sequential decision-making can be handled by tree search. PRM supplies "heuristics," MCTS supplies "systematic search" — the combination beats PRM alone.

The pit it left: MCTS runtime grows exponentially with search depth. For *short* reasoning (tens of steps), it is barely manageable. But the Long-CoT reasoning about to appear in the next layer routinely runs to thousands of steps — exponential explosion.

** 2025-01 | Deepseek-R1: A Paradigm Shift, Not an Improvement — "A Whole New World"

*** What Problem It Saw

Deepseek-R1 did not improve on any previous distillation method. It used RL to train an entirely *new form* of reasoning:

- Reasoning length shot from CoT's hundreds of tokens to *10,000–20,000* tokens
- Full of *Aha moments*: the model itself writes "Wait, let me reconsider..." or "Actually, I think I was wrong about..." — genuine *strategy switches*
- The reasoning process is *dynamic*: follow one path, notice it is wrong, backtrack, try another, backtrack again to double-check...

This Long-CoT capability is extremely powerful at problem-solving, but it also *completely demolished* the previous generation of distillation tools:

1. *PRM died*. In Long-CoT, a step that "temporarily looks bad" may be the *start of a crucial strategy switch* — PRM prunes away the soul.
2. *MCTS died*. Reasoning depth went from tens to thousands of steps; search space grows as e^thousands vs. e^tens — orders of magnitude apart.
3. *Sequence KD survived, but must be redesigned*. In a 10,000-token trajectory, which tokens are the skeleton and which are noise? Old selection methods do not apply.

*** Its Solution

R1's own solution is RL (training directly with rule-based rewards), but that is not this river's focus. The point is: R1 *created a new problem* — "how do you distill Long-CoT reasoning" — and that problem requires new methods.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left)

Intuition: the leap from CoT to Long-CoT is like going from "write a function" to "write an entire engineering project" — the unit changed, the length changed, the internal structure changed; the entire toolchain must be rebuilt.

New pit: *the entire river must regrow from scratch under this new Long-CoT paradigm*. Every subsequent piece of work is answering "how do you distill Long-CoT."

** 2025-04 | S1 / LIMO: Select Rather Than Search

*** What Problem It Saw

PRM and MCTS both failed against Long-CoT. Now what?

Zhang et al. (S1) and Ye et al. (LIMO) made a counterintuitive discovery: *for Long-CoT distillation, data volume is not the key — quality is*. One thousand carefully curated, high-quality R1 reasoning trajectories produce better distillation results than one hundred thousand ordinary ones.

So how do you define and find "high quality"?

*** Its Solution

Take the *curation-based* route:

1. Use one or more teachers (R1, QwQ, Phi4-Reasoning, etc.) to generate complete reasoning trajectories for each problem (potentially dozens of candidates)
2. Use simple heuristics to select the best one: appropriate length, correct final answer, clear reasoning structure
3. Take these 1,000–5,000 curated examples and supervised-fine-tune a small student (e.g., Qwen-32B)

Simple and blunt — and it works. S1 and LIMO students approach the teacher's level on math reasoning.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left)

Intuition: Long-CoT trajectories already carry high information density; each good trajectory is a rich training signal by itself. You do not need massive data; you need *a small amount of uncontaminated essence*.

The pits left (which CoRD addresses):

1. *Blind sampling wastes compute*. To select 1 good trajectory you may generate 100 and discard 99.
2. *Selection is post-hoc*. You can only pick among trajectories the teacher has *already fully generated*; you cannot intervene during generation.
3. *Teachers do not collaborate*. Each teacher independently generates its own trajectory, and you pick one — but *no trajectory is a hybrid in which teacher A writes the first half and teacher B writes the second half*. R1 excels at strategy switching, QwQ at fine-grained calculation, Phi4 at certain domains — their complementarity is wasted.

** 2024-25 | Wang et al. Multi-teacher Fusion: Fusing at the Answer Layer, Not Yet at the Process Layer

*** What Problem It Saw

Wang et al. and the "Mixture of Agents" series of work had already observed: if multiple LLMs each give an answer and you *fuse* them into one, the result is better than any single model.

But this line of work remained stuck at *answer-level* fusion — feeding multiple complete responses to an "aggregator" model that synthesizes a better response.

*** Its Solution

The canonical pattern:

- Have LLM_1, LLM_2, ..., LLM_K independently answer the question
- Feed their answers + the original question to LLM_aggregator
- LLM_aggregator outputs a synthesized answer

It works well, but *the teachers never communicate at any point during reasoning* — each works in isolation and a "chief editor" assembles the pieces at the end.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left)

Intuition: different models have different blind spots and strengths; fusion provides complementarity.

The pit it left: fusion happens at the *very last step*. If teachers could *deliberate while reasoning* — teacher A proposes "start with Descartes' Circle Theorem," teacher B proposes "start by drawing a diagram," and then you pick the more promising branch to continue — you might produce *reasoning paths that no single teacher could arrive at alone*. That is CoRD's entry point.

** 2026-05 | CoRD: Let Teachers Deliberate While Reasoning, Hybridizing Step by Step

*** What Problem It Saw

The CoRD team *superimposed* the two preceding lines:

- *S1/LIMO line*: introduced curation for Long-CoT distillation, but can only *post-hoc select*; teachers do not collaborate.
- *Wang multi-teacher fusion line*: achieved teacher collaboration, but only at the *answer level*, not at the *reasoning process* level.

Align the gaps of both lines, and the natural question surfaces: *Can we apply multi-teacher collaboration at every step of Long-CoT reasoning?*

The specific pain points are sharper still:

1. *Wasted compute*. S1/LIMO have each teacher generate a full trajectory and then select one — but teacher A's first 5 steps may be excellent while its last 10 collapse, and teacher B may be exactly the opposite. Half the good data is thrown away.
2. *No hybridization*. No single teacher can produce a chain that combines "R1's strategy switching + QwQ's meticulous calculation + Phi4's specialized perspective." Yet hybridization is precisely what might yield *trajectories no individual teacher could produce*.
3. *No dynamic exploration*. Once a teacher has finished generating, that is it; there is no way to adjust exploration depth based on "how well the current reasoning is going."

*** Its Solution

CoRD = *Collaborative Reasoning Decoding*, with three core components, each tailored to one of the pain points above:

*1. Prompt-guided step segmentation.* Different teachers reason at completely different granularities — what R1 covers in one paragraph, QwQ covers in three sentences, Phi4 in two paragraphs — yet they may be expressing the same reasoning step. CoRD hard-codes a "<think> ### Step" marker into the prompt, *forcing all teachers to segment their output in a unified format*. Only then is "step 1" comparable across teachers.

*2. Perplexity-based step scoring.* How do you judge whether a step is good? CoRD uses a *meta-prover* (QwQ-32B in the paper) as the evaluator — it estimates "given the reasoning prefix so far and this candidate step, how likely is the correct answer?" In essence, predictive perplexity serves as the quality signal. The scoring is *local*: it considers only the current step's contribution to predicting the answer.

*3. Beam search, step by step.* Greedily choosing the step with the highest local score can pick paths that look good now but collapse later — R1-style "apparently meandering but ultimately breakthrough" strategies would be pruned. CoRD maintains B *parallel* reasoning paths (beam width). At each step, K teachers each propose one candidate; the B highest-scoring candidates are retained.

The whole process is like *several mathematicians collaborating over a shared draft*: at every step, each proposes what to write next; the best proposal is written down, but *several parallel lines of thought are kept alive* so the group never gets irreversibly stuck.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pits It Left)

Intuition: S1/LIMO is "let each teacher independently complete the whole assignment, then pick the best paper." CoRD is "let all teachers collaborate around one desk, negotiating each step as they go." The latter naturally produces *hybrid* trajectories; the former is structurally incapable of it.

Empirically, CoRD outperforms S1, LIMO-v1/v2, and multi-teacher curation/integration baselines on five reasoning benchmarks; student models approach or even exceed teacher performance. Crucially, CoRD also generalizes well on *out-of-distribution* and *open-ended* tasks — indicating it is not overfitting to specific datasets.

New pits left:

1. *Meta-prover is a single point of failure*. The entire scoring system depends on one evaluation model (QwQ-32B). If the meta-prover is itself biased, the distillation data inherits that bias. The paper experiments with multiple meta-provers, but cannot escape the fundamental problem: "the evaluator is the bottleneck of evaluation."
2. *Beam search compute cost*. Maintaining B paths × K teacher candidates = B×K generations per step. Although the paper says "no significant efficiency overhead," the cost is clearly higher than plain teacher sampling.
3. *Step granularity is still locked by the prompt*. The "### Step" marker unifies segmentation, but the question of *what granularity is optimal* is sidestepped — different problems may call for different granularities.
4. *Validated only on math/reasoning tasks*. For code generation, agentic tasks (action-observation loops), and other scenarios with more complex reasoning structure, it is unknown whether CoRD still applies.

* Frontier Extensions

CoRD appeared on May 4, 2026 (ACL 2026 Findings). No published papers have yet appeared that explicitly critique or build directly on CoRD — the most recent waypoint on this river is CoRD itself.

Several directions can be anticipated, based on hints in the paper's limitations section and broader field trends:

1. *Replace the evaluator with cheaper signals*. Perplexity-based scoring currently requires a 32B meta-prover at every step. The next move may be a *learned step value function* — a lightweight small model trained to predict step quality — eliminating the need to run a 32B model for each step.
2. *Extend CoRD to agentic reasoning*. Long-CoT is *single-turn multi-step reasoning*; agentic tasks are *multi-turn multi-step reasoning + external feedback*. CoRD's "step-wise multi-teacher collaboration" framework could in principle extend to agents — at each turn and each step, multiple agents propose the next action; the best is executed — but this requires integrating environment feedback into the perplexity scoring.
3. *Self-CoRD: single-teacher self-collaboration*. If even "multiple teachers" is too costly, could *multiple samples from the same teacher* serve as "virtual multiple teachers"? That would enable collaborative decoding without a heterogeneous model pool.
4. *Coupling with RLHF / DPO*. CoRD currently generates high-quality trajectories used only for SFT. The natural next step is to feed these trajectories into RL (using final-answer correctness as the reward) for further refinement.

* Insights

*The fundamental evolutionary axis of this river is that the minimum unit of distillation keeps shrinking, while the moment of teacher collaboration keeps moving earlier.*

- 2016 (KD): distillation unit = *complete answer*; teacher collaboration = *zero*.
- 2022 (CoT): distillation unit = *reasoning sequence*; teacher collaboration = *zero*.
- 2024 (PRM): distillation unit = *step* (filtering begins); teacher collaboration = *zero*.
- 2025 (S1/LIMO): unit back to *trajectory*, but now *multiple teachers generate + individual selection* begins — the weakest possible form of collaboration.
- 2025 (Wang fusion): collaboration appears, but only at the *final answer*.
- 2026 (CoRD): unit = *single step*; collaboration = *every step*.

*Both axes converge in the same direction simultaneously — finer granularity + earlier collaboration.* CoRD is the product of their intersection. If both axes advance one more step, what would that look like? Probably *token-level multi-teacher collaboration* — but transformers have no mechanism to "see what the other is thinking" at the token level, so the step level may be the physical limit.

*Second observation: a paradigm-shift paper (like R1) truly "resets the river" rather than improving on it.*

R1 did not improve any predecessor's work — it defined a *new input* (Long-CoT), instantly rendering the entire previous generation of tools designed for CoT (PRM, MCTS) obsolete. Within a year, the entire reasoning distillation community was forced to start over: first came S1/LIMO's "brute-force curation," then CoRD's "step-wise collaboration."

This kind of "reset paper" appears periodically in every field (attention vs. RNNs, diffusion models vs. GANs, R1 vs. CoT). Their signature: *the paper does not solve the old problem — it introduces a new scenario that renders the old problem irrelevant.*

*Third observation: "lateral acquisition" — grafting the results of one parallel line onto another — is a recurring pattern in current LLM research.*

- The *PRM/MCTS* line, originally built for traditional RL, was transplanted into reasoning distillation.
- The *Mixture of Agents (Wang)* line, originally aimed at producing better responses, was transplanted by CoRD into reasoning distillation.
- *Beam search*, a decades-old tool from the NMT era, was repurposed by CoRD for a "step-level vocabulary."

CoRD's true insight is *not inventing something new* but *recognizing that three existing lines could be twisted into one rope*. This kind of "combinatorial innovation" is more common than "first-principles breakthroughs" in mature fields.

*Where is the next move most likely?*

If "finer granularity + earlier collaboration" is the main axis, the answer is not to keep shrinking granularity (already at the physical step limit), but to *make evaluation cheaper* and *extend collaboration to more complex reasoning scenarios*:

1. *Shrink or eliminate the meta-prover* — replace the 32B model with cheaper signals (self-consistency, teacher agreement).
2. *Generalize to agentic reasoning* — at each agent step, have multiple teacher agents collaboratively propose the next action and tool call.
3. *Couple with RL* — use CoRD-generated data for SFT, then apply RL for further refinement.

* Implications

*For "finding problems"*: the intersection of two parallel lines is more valuable than the extension of either one.

CoRD is neither "S1's next generation" (still within the curation paradigm) nor "Wang's next generation" (still within the answer-fusion paradigm). It drilled a well *at the crossing of the two*. When you see two "hot but mutually unaware" research lines in a field, their intersection is often the next breakthrough.

*For "doing research"*: when a paradigm-shift paper (like R1) first appears, all the old methods in the river temporarily fail. That is the "opportunity window.*

In the months after R1 appeared, the community was still trying to understand the new phenomenon using old frameworks. Whoever first recognized "the old tools no longer work — we need to redesign" could claim the next wave of publication slots. S1/LIMO produced the first-generation response within three months of R1; CoRD produced a more refined response a year later. This is the characteristic rhythm of *a new paradigm spawning new methods*.

*For "reading papers"*: never read a paper in isolation — always ask "who is it criticizing, and who will criticize it?"

CoRD is hard to understand in isolation — why segment into steps? Why use perplexity instead of PRM? Why beam search? Place it back in this ten-year river, and every design decision maps precisely onto a specific wound left by a previous generation. The paper's design choices are not free-floating "creativity" — they are *forced into existence by the failures of their predecessors*.

*What can you actually use?*

- If you are doing *reasoning model distillation*: CoRD is directly usable — an open-source implementation is available (DISL-Lab/CoRD). If you have multiple open-source LRMs (QwQ, Phi-Reasoning, R1-distilled series), you can use CoRD to generate higher-quality SFT data.
- If you are working on *multi-model collaboration* in other contexts: CoRD's "step-wise multi-teacher decoding" framework can be borrowed wherever your task can be decomposed into steps — code generation (one step per function), agentic workflows (one step per action).
- If you are *choosing a research direction*: watch for fields that have just been disrupted by a paradigm-shift paper — there is always a short window of "re-engineer the old tools" opportunities in the aftermath. R1 in 2025 is the canonical example for reasoning. What will be next?
