* The River of the Problem

A problem that seemed already solved: how do we make attention's O(n²) even cheaper?

Nine years. Since 2017, when Transformer hoisted the banner "Attention Is All You Need," every generation of researchers has been running the same arithmetic — a sequence of length n requires each token to compute similarity against every other token, so both computation and memory scale as n². 8K context is tolerable; 32K starts to hurt; 128K is an engineering disaster, yet users and applications keep pushing higher.

The way each generation has passed the baton is fascinating: no one overturns the previous generation — instead, they find a new layer of cost buried inside the "secondary overhead" left behind. First came saving memory (avoid materializing the n×n intermediate matrix), then saving compute (swapping FP16 for FP8 / INT8), then harvesting hardware dividends (Blackwell GPUs natively support 4-bit floating point). Each layer peeled away exposes the next, which promptly becomes the new bottleneck.

By 2026, the cost-shaving has reached an awkward impasse: hardware offers MXFP4 / NVFP4 4-bit float instructions with 4× the theoretical throughput of FP16, but who dares use them? Applying 4-bit precision directly to QK^T drops the attention-score cosine similarity from 0.988 to 0.714, and the model collapses. This is exactly the embarrassment DMA sets out to fix — "the hardware gives you 4× speed but you're afraid to use it." Can we identify the positions that cannot afford a precision drop, keep those at 8-bit, and compress everything else to 4-bit, capturing the hardware dividend without sacrificing accuracy?

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Transformer: Replace Recurrence with Attention

*** What Problem It Saw

Before 2017, the workhorse for sequence modeling was RNN/LSTM. RNNs have a fundamental constraint — tokens must be processed one at a time, each step waiting for the previous hidden state. This is inherently sequential; GPU compute sits largely idle. Attention mechanisms already existed as auxiliary components (e.g., Bahdanau attention), but always as a side dish to the RNN main course.

*** Its Solution

Elevate attention to the main course: every token directly attends to every other token, resolved in a single matrix multiplication. The formula is Softmax(QK^T / sqrt(d)) V. All pairwise scores are computed in one shot — exactly what GPUs love.

*** Why It Worked (and What Debt It Left)

Parallelism took off, training throughput took off, quality took off — this became the root of every subsequent LLM. But it planted a time bomb: QK^T is an n×n square matrix; every element must be computed, and storing it requires n×n memory. When sequences are short nobody cares, but once length exceeds 4K, that n² factor is an inescapable disaster.

** 2018 | Online Softmax: Compute Softmax While You Stream

*** What Problem It Saw

Classical softmax requires two passes: first scan the entire row to find the maximum (to prevent exp overflow), then scan again to compute numerator and denominator. This blocks tiling of attention — you must see the full row before you can begin. Any attempt to process attention in chunks hits this wall.

*** Its Solution

Milakov & Gimelshein buried a mathematical trick in an obscure NVIDIA technical report: maintain a running maximum m and a running normalization sum l, updating them incrementally with each new batch of data, such that the final result is numerically identical to the two-pass approach. This is online softmax.

*** Why It Worked (and What Debt It Left)

It compresses softmax from "two passes" to "one streaming pass." The trick attracted little attention at the time because standalone softmax wasn't a bottleneck. Its real power had to wait four years, until someone used it as a LEGO brick — it is the mathematical prerequisite that lets FlashAttention fuse the entire attention operation into a single kernel.

** 2022 | FlashAttention: Stream Attention Through SRAM

*** What Problem It Saw

By 2022, n² memory had become the hard bottleneck for training long-context LLMs. The prevailing approach to "cheaper attention" was algorithmic surgery — sparse attention, linear attention, low-rank approximations — but all of these alter the mathematical definition of attention and introduce quality loss. Dao's insight was: the algorithm doesn't need to change; the bottleneck is the HBM ↔ SRAM data movement, not arithmetic.

*** Its Solution

Tile Q, K, and V into small blocks; load only one block at a time into the GPU's SRAM (fast on-chip memory); use online softmax to incrementally accumulate the attention output. The full n×n intermediate matrix is never materialized. Everything is fused into a single CUDA kernel, reducing HBM accesses from O(n²) to O(n).

*** Why It Worked (and What Debt It Left)

Mathematically exact — no quality loss unlike sparse or approximate methods — while winning on both memory and speed. This is arguably the most impactful engineering paper for LLMs in the past decade. But it revealed a new problem: once HBM is no longer the bottleneck, where does the bottleneck go? The answer: arithmetic itself. FP16 Tensor Cores computing QK^T can only go so fast. To squeeze further, you have to touch precision.

** 2024 | SageAttention / INT-FlashAttention: Compress QK to INT8/FP8

*** What Problem It Saw

FlashAttention claimed the memory dividend, but arithmetic was still FP16. Meanwhile, LLM inference had broadly embraced low-bit quantization (weight INT4, KV cache INT8), yet the two matrix multiplications inside attention — QK^T and PV — were still running in FP16/BF16. This was a conspicuous unclaimed dividend.

*** Its Solution

Quantize Q and K online to INT8 (SageAttention) or FP8 (INT-FlashAttention), compute QK^T using low-bit Tensor Cores, then dequantize back to floating point for softmax. Speed improves another 2-3× over FlashAttention, with precision loss negligible on most tasks.

*** Why It Worked (and What Debt It Left)

INT8/FP8 has enough dynamic range to faithfully represent attention scores, so this is essentially plug-and-play with minimal quality loss. But when people grew greedier and tried pushing to 4-bit, things broke — 4-bit representation is far too sensitive to the tail outliers in attention scores, and naive quantization catastrophically distorts the softmax probability distribution. This set the stage for the next generation: smoothing.

** 2024–2025 | SageAttention v2/v3: Patching 4-Bit with Smoothing

*** What Problem It Saw

Directly quantizing QK to 4-bit (MXFP4/INT4) drops cosine similarity from 0.988 to 0.714 (Table 2 in this DMA paper), collapsing model quality. Yet NVIDIA Blackwell GPUs natively support MXFP4 / NVFP4 instructions with twice the throughput of FP8 — too tempting a target to ignore.

*** Its Solution

Borrow SmoothQuant's idea: apply a smoothing transform before quantization, transferring outliers from Q to K (or V) so that both tensors have a distribution more amenable to quantization. With outliers tamed, 4-bit precision loss falls back into an acceptable range.

*** Why It Worked (and What Debt It Left)

Smoothing directly attacks the outlier problem that makes 4-bit quantization painful, so precision is recovered. But it carries an engineering cost: the smoothing step is an additional floating-point GEMV that must run either before or inside the attention kernel. When the DMA authors profile this (Table 7), they find that extra GEMV eats back a large fraction of the speedup that 4-bit otherwise provides. The natural next question: can we use 4-bit without smoothing at all?

** 2026 | DMA: No Smoothing — Partition Instead. Important Tiles Stay at 8-Bit; the Rest Drops to 4-Bit

*** What Problem It Saw

DMA directly challenges the previous approach: smoothing exists to make *all* tokens work at 4-bit. But is every token in attention truly equally important? Sparse-attention research (SparseAttention, StreamingLLM, etc.) has repeatedly demonstrated that attention scores are heavily concentrated near the diagonal — in a local window — and in a handful of "sink tokens." In other words, most positions don't need to be rescued by 4-bit — only a small subset of critical positions needs higher precision; 4-bit is fine everywhere else.

*** Its Solution

DMA partitions the entire n×n attention matrix by tile. Tiles near the diagonal (interactions between nearby, important tokens) are computed in MXFP8 (8-bit); tiles far from the diagonal are computed in MXFP4 (4-bit). The full map requires neither smoothing nor sacrifices the speed of 4-bit Tensor Cores.

At the same time, DMA does something that follow-up FlashAttention work often neglects: it fuses quantization, micro-scale transformation, 4-bit encoding and packing, and the attention computation itself into a single Triton kernel. The smoothing GEMV in the SageAttention series made this kind of end-to-end fusion difficult; removing smoothing makes fusion straightforward.

*** Why It Worked (and What Debt It Left)

The core insight: rather than using smoothing to force *all positions* into 4-bit territory, accept that *some positions genuinely need 8-bit* and partition by importance. The idea sounds simple once stated, but it rests on two prerequisites:
1. Hardware must natively support mixed-precision Tensor Core instructions (only available on Blackwell and later).
2. The spatial distribution of "important" attention scores must follow a predictable pattern (the diagonal window).

Debt left behind: the diagonal window is hand-crafted, assuming "important token = local token." For long-context retrieval tasks, important tokens may appear at the sequence beginning (sink tokens) or at arbitrary positions in the middle. The next generation will likely require *dynamic token-importance routing* — deciding at runtime which tiles take 8-bit and which take 4-bit.

* Frontier Extensions

This paper appeared on arXiv only in April 2026, and no follow-up work explicitly citing and improving on it has emerged yet. Two directions are foreseeable:

1. *Dynamic mixed-precision routing*: replace the static diagonal window for "which tiles use 8-bit" with an importance score computed dynamically from the query/key at runtime — analogous to a Mixture-of-Experts router, but operating at attention-tile granularity.

2. *Even lower bit-width mixing*: beyond MXFP4, incorporate FP6, MXINT4, binarization, and other formats to build a multi-level 8/6/4/2 precision pyramid. The next GPU generation after Blackwell already has additional low-bit instructions on its roadmap.

As of 2026-05, DMA is the first publicly published work to run hardware-native MXFP mixed-precision in an end-to-end attention kernel.

* Insight

Reading the full arc, what is truly changing is not the attention algorithm itself, but *the understanding of where the bottleneck lies*.

- In 2017, the perceived bottleneck was "the algorithm isn't parallel enough."
- In 2022, it was "not enough memory."
- In 2024, it was "bit-width is too wide."
- In 2026, DMA says: the bottleneck is "we applied uniform precision to positions of unequal importance."

This is a thoroughly classical engineering move: discover *heterogeneity* inside a problem that was previously treated as homogeneous, then allocate resources according to that heterogeneity. MoE does exactly this (each token routes to a different expert), as does speculative decoding (easy tokens take the small model) and mixed-precision training (different layers use different precision). DMA pushes the same pattern down to the granularity of individual attention tiles.

Prediction for the next step: this river will enter a phase of *adaptive, runtime decision-making*. Every static, hand-crafted choice — diagonal window, fixed number of sink tokens, fixed sparsity pattern — will be replaced by some lightweight runtime router. The future of attention may look less like a fixed kernel and more like a dynamic circuit.

* Takeaways

Lessons for finding problems:

1. *Read the profiler, not the paper title.* The reason DMA identified smoothing as the bottleneck is that the authors did the kernel breakdown profiling in Table 7 and saw how much time the GEMV was consuming. Many "performance problems" are hand-waved into existence; only actually measuring reveals where the money leaks.

2. *The hardware roadmap is the research roadmap.* The moment MXFP instructions appeared on Blackwell GPUs, a paper like DMA was already inevitable somewhere in this river. The research window is often opened by hardware releases — arrive two years early and nobody can use your work; arrive two years late and someone else has planted the flag.

3. *Accepting heterogeneity is cheaper than enforcing uniformity.* When a problem is handled uniformly, there is often a low-hanging fruit of "partition by importance" waiting to be picked — MoE, speculative decoding, mixed-precision training are all variations on the same underlying theme. Whenever you see uniform processing, it is worth asking: is this actually uniformly important?

The actionable takeaway: the next time you encounter a design with uniform precision, uniform bandwidth, or uniform time allocation, run a lightweight importance profiling pass, identify the top-k% critical positions, give them more resources, and cut everything else. That is often the embryo of a publishable paper.
