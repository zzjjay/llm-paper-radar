* The River of Problems

A hundred-billion-parameter model — how do you compress it from 16-bit floats down to 4-bit integers and still have it say anything sensible?

It sounds like switching to a coarser ruler: once the ruler is coarser, the measurements come out rougher. The trouble is that the activations inside the model are not uniformly distributed — a handful of "troublemakers" (outliers) are lurking in there, with values tens of times larger than everything else.

Measure that whole collection with a coarse ruler, and the outliers consume all the precision; the smaller values all get rounded to zero. The model breaks.

Everyone in this river is trying to do the same thing: smooth out, scatter, or hide those outliers so that even a coarse ruler can measure accurately. Over a decade the field has moved from "hunt down each outlier individually" to "smash outliers apart with a matrix" to "redesign the smashing for the block structure of new hardware" — each generation inherits the loose thread left by the last.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

The river has one simple leitmotif: outliers are hard to handle. But over a decade, the meaning of "handle" has been redefined again and again.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Admit You Will Quantize Wrong, Then Compensate

*** What Problem It Saw

The earliest attempts simply rounded weights from 16-bit down to 4-bit. The model immediately broke.

The reason: weights are interdependent. Round weight 1 with an error of 0.01, and everything downstream of it sees a corrupted input — the error propagates.

*** Its Solution

GPTQ does not pretend rounding is error-free. It accepts the error and uses a mathematical trick — the inverse of the Hessian matrix — to compute "this column was rounded wrong; which other columns can absorb the residual?"

Like a bookkeeper: column A is over by 100; column B goes under by 100. The total stays correct.

*** Why It Works (and What It Left Behind)

GPTQ turned quantization from a one-shot operation into a feedback-driven process — a genuine paradigm shift.

But it only touches weights. Activations (the intermediate values computed during a forward pass) are untouched. And the truly difficult outliers live in the activations.

** 2023 | SmoothQuant: Can We Relocate the Outliers?

*** What Problem It Saw

A 4096-dimensional activation vector might have 4090 channels sitting in [−1, 1] and 6 channels spiking to 50.

Try measuring that with a ruler scaled to [−2, 2]. The six spikes blow the scale; the other 4090 values all collapse to zero.

*** Its Solution

Key observation: activations have outliers, but the weights at the same positions tend to be well-behaved. So sell some of the activation's "difficulty" to the weights — divide activation channel i by s_i (shrink it), and multiply the corresponding weight row by s_i (grow it slightly, but still within what quantization can handle).

Mathematically equivalent (divide then multiply = identity), but quantization-friendliness is completely different.

*** Why It Works (and What It Left Behind)

This was the first time "difficulty" was transferred across tensors. But SmoothQuant can only scale per channel — channel i gets one scale, channel j gets another, and channels never mix.

If an outlier in some channel is simply too large (scaling it down too aggressively hurts other values in that channel), you are stuck.

** 2024 | QuaRot: Scatter the Outliers with a Rotation Matrix

*** What Problem It Saw

SmoothQuant suppresses; QuaRot scatters. Per-channel scaling is a one-dimensional operation — it has no recourse against a pathologically skewed channel. What if you mixed the entire vector together so that nobody is unusually large?

*** Its Solution

Multiply the activation vector by a Hadamard matrix (a special ±1 rotation matrix).

The magic of Hadamard: applied to a vector whose energy is concentrated in a few dimensions, it spreads that energy approximately evenly across all dimensions. A vector like [50, 0, 0, 0] becomes something close to [12.5, 12.5, 12.5, 12.5] — no outlier, quantizer is happy.

Better still: because Hadamard is orthogonal, its inverse is its transpose, so it can be "folded" into the adjacent weight matrix. Zero extra cost at inference time.

*** Why It Works (and What It Left Behind)

QuaRot was the first leap in this river — from per-channel to cross-channel. But Hadamard is a fixed, data-agnostic matrix. Different models have very different activation distributions; one universal matrix is not optimal for all.

** 2025 | SpinQuant: Can We Learn the Rotation?

*** What Problem It Saw

Rotation is useful; a fixed one is suboptimal. Why not learn one?

*** Its Solution

Treat the rotation matrix as a trainable parameter. Use a small calibration dataset and backpropagation to optimize it so that the quantized model's outputs stay as close as possible to the original model's.

Constraint: orthogonality must be maintained (rotation matrices are orthogonal matrices). This means standard Adam cannot be used directly; optimization must be performed on the Stiefel manifold (the geometric space of orthogonal matrices) — technically demanding.

*** Why It Works (and What It Left Behind)

A learned rotation genuinely outperforms a fixed Hadamard. But two loose threads remain:
1. The search space is too narrow — only orthogonal matrices, which are a small subset of all invertible matrices.
2. It still assumes traditional quantization: one shared scale per tensor. Meanwhile, the hardware landscape was quietly shifting.

** 2025 | Egiazarian & Shao: MX Is Here — Rotation Must Change

*** What Problem It Saw

A consortium of hardware vendors (Microsoft, AMD, Arm, Intel, Meta, NVIDIA) jointly introduced the MX format: tensors are cut into small blocks (e.g., 32 elements each), and every block carries its own scale.

Intuitively this should be better — finer-grained scales, better precision. But researchers discovered that applying QuaRot-style global rotation on top of MX caused a sharp performance collapse.

Why? Global rotation spreads energy across the entire vector. MX computes scales independently per block — so if you scatter block 1's outlier into blocks 2, 3, and 4, those blocks now have unexpectedly large values. Each of their scales must grow to accommodate the intruder, degrading the precision of everything else in those blocks.

Rotation and MX's block structure were fighting each other.

*** Its Solution

Replace the global rotation with a block-diagonal rotation — each block has its own small rotation matrix, and blocks never communicate. Energy scatters only within an MX block, never across block boundaries.

*** Why It Works (and What It Left Behind)

Finally, rotation and MX coexist without conflict. But the price is that energy can no longer flow across blocks. If a particular MX block contains an exceptionally hard outlier, it is permanently trapped there — spread among 32 elements in the best case, each still absorbing a significant share.

A theoretically solvable problem that remains unsolved because of this wall.

** 2026 | LATMiX: Can the Wall Come Down?

*** What Problem It Saw

The LATMiX authors derive a mathematical inequality (Theorem 3.3) showing that MX quantization error is governed by two terms:
1. The expected maximum value within each block (intra-block distribution).
2. The spectral norm of the inverse of the transformation matrix (inter-block structure).

Prior methods each optimized only one side:
- QuaRot / SpinQuant optimize inter-block (global rotation), ignore intra-block → collapse on MX.
- Egiazarian / Shao optimize intra-block (block-diagonal rotation), ignore inter-block → error has a non-trivial lower bound.

The two objectives look contradictory, but the theory says they can be jointly optimized — the key is to drop the "must be a rotation" constraint.

*** Its Solution

Three layers of relaxation:

1. *From rotation to general invertible matrix*: A rotation is an orthogonal matrix — a tiny corner of the space of invertible matrices. LATMiX learns any invertible matrix (the general linear group GL), giving far greater expressive power.
2. *From invertible matrix to affine transformation*: Add a translation term v, so T(x) = Ax + v. The translation can center the activation mean, further reducing quantization error.
3. *Parameterize via LU or QR decomposition*: Directly parameterizing an invertible matrix is tricky (invertibility must be enforced). The authors represent A via LU decomposition (PA = LU) or QR decomposition (A = QR), plus a volume-preserving regularizer (det(A) ≈ 1) to ensure numerical stability.

Optimization uses ordinary SGD/Adam — no constrained manifold optimization required. After training, A is folded into the weight matrices; inference has zero extra cost.

*** Why It Works (and What It Left Behind)

LATMiX does not invent a new scattering operation. It expands the search space for "scattering" from a narrow subset (orthogonal matrices) to the full space of invertible affine transformations. By construction: any rotation is representable, so the floor cannot be worse; and non-rotation solutions are reachable, so the ceiling is higher.

Empirically, on MXFP4 and MXINT4 across 8 Llama/Qwen models of varying sizes, LATMiX consistently outperforms QuaRot, SpinQuant, FlatQuant, and OSTQuant. The gap is especially pronounced for small models (1B–4B) — small models have less redundancy to absorb errors, so outliers hit harder.

Loose threads that remain:
- One training run takes ~2 hours on 4 × A100 for Qwen3-14B — considerably more expensive than calibration-based methods like GPTQ.
- LU slightly edges out QR; QR requires matrix-exponential backpropagation, making optimization harder.
- No exploration yet of how LATMiX interacts with more aggressive formats (varying MX block sizes, mixed precision).

* Frontier Extensions

LATMiX is an April 2026 preprint — the current leading edge of this river.

Looking downstream, several directions are clearly visible:

1. *Co-optimize the transformation and the quantization format itself.* Lee et al. (2025)'s AMXFP4 already adjusts the mantissa/exponent allocation of MX; the future may involve jointly optimizing "transformation + format."
2. *MX-aware quantization-aware training (QAT).* LATMiX is post-training quantization — applied after a model is already trained. Starting training with MX from scratch might reduce the need for such elaborate post-hoc transformations.
3. *Cross-layer or cross-block shared transformations.* Currently each transformer block learns independent T1 and T2. Even though these can be folded away, training cost scales linearly with the number of layers. Sharing or factoring transformations could cut that cost.

* Insights

On the surface this river is about "finding a better matrix to scatter activation outliers," but what is actually changing underneath is something different.

*First layer*: from specialization to generalization.
GPTQ handles only weights; SmoothQuant adds per-channel scaling; QuaRot uses a fixed Hadamard; SpinQuant learns an orthogonal matrix; LATMiX learns a general invertible affine transformation. Each generation expands the assumed solution space. The underlying paradigm: rather than cleverly constructing a special-case solution (rotation, Hadamard, diagonal scaling), just learn a general one and let the optimization machinery of deep learning find it.

*Second layer*: hardware formats are defining algorithms upstream.
Before 2024, every quantization method tacitly assumed "one scale per tensor." The moment MX appeared, the then-strongest method QuaRot immediately collapsed on it — not because QuaRot was wrong, but because its implicit assumptions conflicted with the new hardware. All of LATMiX's theoretical effort is essentially answering: "If scales are computed per block, what should the transformation look like?" Hardware is not downstream of algorithms; it is an upstream constraint.

*Third layer*: theoretical analysis is becoming important again.
LATMiX does not invent a trick and then validate it — it derives an inequality first and then designs the method. Theorem 3.3 tells you directly which two quantities govern the error; the rest is figuring out how to jointly minimize both. This "bound first, method second" research style is surprisingly rare in the engineering-heavy literature of the large-model era.

Where does the river most likely flow next? My guess is joint hardware-format / quantization-method co-design. Today, formats are fixed first and methods adapt to them. The future may look more like NAS — given a set of models and a precision target, jointly search over "MX block size + bit allocation + transformation form."

* Takeaways

What this river suggests about how to do research:

1. *Confront the contradiction; don't route around it.* LATMiX matters because it identified a conflict that everyone had silently accepted as a forced binary choice (global scatter vs. MX block independence), then proved both can be satisfied simultaneously. Many breakthroughs follow exactly this pattern — others say you can't have both; you ask why not.
2. *Derive the bound first, then design the method.* Write the problem as mathematics, look at which terms appear in the inequality, then ask "can I compress all of them at once?" This is more efficient than brainstorming tricks.
3. *Follow the hardware.* The most recent leaps in this line of work were all triggered by a new hardware format (MX). In systems and algorithms research, the hardware roadmap is a free map of research directions — every hardware upgrade invalidates some old assumption and opens the door for a new one.

The practical takeaway:
When doing model compression work, you should no longer default to "QuaRot/Hadamard is good enough" — if your target hardware uses the MX format (AMD MI355, Intel Gaudi3, NVIDIA Blackwell, etc.), you should directly consider learning an affine transformation. And this paradigm will very likely generalize to other block-structured quantization settings (KV cache quantization, attention quantization, streaming activation quantization).
