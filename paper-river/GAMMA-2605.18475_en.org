* The River of the Problem

Cramming a tens-of-gigabytes model into a consumer GPU — or even a phone — without letting it get dumb.

That is what the *quantization* river has been doing for more than a decade. The simplest version: compress every weight from 16-bit float down to a 4-bit integer, cutting the footprint by 4×. It runs; accuracy is still acceptable.

Below 4 bits, though, things suddenly get hard. At 3 bits, at 2.5 bits, models start *collapsing* — some layers withstand the squeeze with no complaint; others fall apart the moment you touch them.

So an apparently obvious idea emerges: *don't give every layer the same number of bits*. Give more bits to the important layers, fewer to the robust ones, and keep the average at 3 bits. That is *mixed-precision quantization*.

Sounds simple. But the question that stumped an entire generation follows immediately: *which layers matter? How do you know? How do you find out automatically?*

Every generation answered in its own way: "search," "derive a formula," "learn an allocator." Each answer had a fatal weakness — too slow to be practical, not accurate enough, or requiring a full restart whenever the target budget changed.

- The 2018 cohort said: "Let the network pick its own bit-widths, end-to-end." — but you have to *retrain the whole model*.
- The 2020 cohort said: "No need to learn; just compute a Hessian score." — but the score is *static*, and changing the budget means *recomputing* everything.
- The 2025 cohort said: "Just run evolutionary search." — but it is *slow*, and changing the budget means *re-searching* from scratch.
- The 2026 GAMMA paper says: "Learn — but *learn only the bit assignment, never touching the model weights*; and the learned scores can be *reused across budgets*."

Every step forward along this river is a direct fight against the *specific pain points left by the previous generation*.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2018 | DNAS: Let the Network Pick Its Own Bit-Widths

*** What Problem It Saw

Before 2018, mixed-precision quantization for a CNN relied on two approaches:

- Hand-tuning — experts relying on intuition: "convolutional layers are sensitive, give them 8 bits; BN layers are robust, give them 2 bits."
- Brute-force search — try every combination of bit-widths for every layer. A 50-layer network with 4 bit-width choices per layer yields 4^50 ≈ 10^30 combinations. Completely intractable.

Neither scales.

*** Its Solution

DNAS *softened* the act of "choosing a bit-width."

Originally, "layer i uses b bits" was a discrete choice — either you pick it or you don't. DNAS replaced this with a *probability distribution* over bit-widths: this layer is 2-bit with probability 0.1, 4-bit with probability 0.7, 8-bit with probability 0.2, summing to 1.

Using the *Gumbel-Softmax* trick, the discrete choice is replaced by a differentiable continuous relaxation. The entire network can then be trained end-to-end with gradient descent — jointly learning the weights *and* the per-layer bit-width distributions. After training, the bit-width with the highest probability in each layer is selected as the final assignment.

A soft penalty term is added: "if average bit-width exceeds the target, inflate the loss" — naturally nudging the optimizer toward lower bit-widths.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Buried)

Differentiable means *learnable*. Learnable means *the same machine-learning toolchain can handle it*. This was a paradigmatic victory.

But the pitfall lay in *requiring quantization-aware training (QAT)* — you are not just learning the bit-width assignment; you are also retraining the full model weights so the model adapts to quantization.

In the CNN era this was manageable: tens of millions of parameters, a few days of training.

In the LLM era, parameter counts are 1000× larger. Retraining a 70B model once burns hundreds of H100s for weeks. *QAT had become a luxury item.*

The DNAS path is simply unworkable at LLM scale.

** 2020 | HAWQ-V2: Stop Learning — Just Compute

*** What Problem It Saw

DNAS could run on CNNs, but even there QAT was already painful enough. Dong et al. wondered: can we *skip training entirely* and just compute the bit-width assignment?

If a *static metric* can tell us "how sensitive this layer is to quantization," we can rank layers by sensitivity, assign bit-widths accordingly — zero training, results in minutes.

*** Its Solution

HAWQ-V2's core idea: use the *Hessian matrix* to measure sensitivity.

The Hessian is the matrix of *second-order derivatives* of the loss with respect to the weights. The intuition is:

- If the Hessian is *steep* at some layer (large eigenvalues), a small perturbation to the weights there causes a large change in loss → the layer is *sensitive* → quantization error must be small → *give it more bits*.
- If the Hessian is *flat* (small eigenvalues), large weight perturbations barely matter → the layer is *robust* → *give it fewer bits*.

HAWQ-V2 uses the *trace* of the Hessian (sum of diagonal elements) as the per-layer sensitivity score. The trace is estimated via Hutchinson's method, far cheaper than computing the full matrix.

Once sorted by sensitivity, bit-widths are assigned in descending order within the given budget.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Buried)

Completely training-free; done in a few hours. The metric has a mathematical grounding — the Hessian trace connects directly to the second-order Taylor expansion of the loss.

But two fundamental pitfalls:

- *The trace is a static per-layer metric.* Each layer's sensitivity is computed independently, so *inter-layer coupling* is invisible — quantization error in one layer propagates along the forward path and interacts with quantization error in downstream layers. This *cross-module dependency* is entirely ignored.
- *Changing the target bit-width requires recomputing.* The trace itself does not depend on the budget, but the assignment strategy does. A 3-bit budget and a 2.5-bit budget are two different integer assignment problems that must be solved separately. In industrial deployment — where the same model ships to cloud (4-bit), desktop (3-bit), and mobile (2-bit) — this means *starting from scratch three times*.

** 2024–25 | LLM PTQ Arrives in Force

*** What Problem It Saw

LLM scale exploded. Once GPT-3 landed, QAT was out of the question for everyone. The entire field was forced toward *post-training quantization (PTQ)* — using only a small calibration dataset, without altering a single weight, directly compressing FP16 into low-bit integers.

GPTQ, AWQ, AQLM, and QTIP — a wave of industrial-grade PTQ methods — pushed "losing as little accuracy as possible at a fixed bit-width" to the extreme:

- *GPTQ* (2022): approximate-Hessian-based column-wise weight reconstruction
- *AWQ* (2024): identify critical channels and protect them selectively
- *AQLM* (2024): additive vector quantization packing multiple weights together
- *QTIP* (2024): trellis-coded quantization

*** Its Solution

All of these are *uniform-bit* methods — every layer uses the same bit-width (4-bit, 3-bit, or 2-bit). Above 4 bits, their accuracy loss is nearly negligible.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Buried)

Uniform-bit at 4 bits and above is practically lossless — sufficient for industrial deployment.

But below 3 bits, *uniform-bit suffers a cliff-edge collapse* — Llama3.1-8B at uniform 3-bit drops from 80% to 70%; at uniform 2-bit it crashes to 40%.

Yet the most commercially valuable deployment zone is precisely 2–3 bits — that is the range where a 70B model fits inside a 24 GB consumer GPU.

The PTQ infrastructure was now mature, but "how not to collapse below 3 bits" remained unanswered. The problem was pushed to a new crossroads: *mixed-precision at LLM scale*.

** 2025 | AMQ: If You Can't Learn and Can't Compute Accurately, Just Search

*** What Problem It Saw

By 2025, both old roads for LLM mixed-precision were blocked:

- The DNAS branch (learn): QAT is infeasible at LLM scale.
- The HAWQ branch (compute): Hessian trace misses cross-module dependencies and yields limited gains at LLM scale; more critically, *changing the budget requires recomputation*.

Yet below 3 bits, mixed-precision is non-negotiable for deployment.

*** Its Solution

AMQ abandoned both "learn" and "compute," turning instead to *evolutionary search*.

The idea is blunt but forceful:

- Maintain a population of candidate bit-width assignments.
- Each individual is one "how many bits per layer" configuration.
- Use a PTQ backend (e.g., AQLM) to actually quantize each individual and evaluate its accuracy.
- Apply selection, crossover, and mutation over many generations.

The surviving fittest individual at the end is the bit-width assignment for that budget.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Buried)

Evolutionary search is *gradient-free*, relies on no proxy metric, and uses actual PTQ accuracy as its fitness signal. At LLM scale it is more accurate than Hessian-based methods.

But three new pitfalls:

- *Hugely expensive.* Every generation requires full PTQ evaluation; the entire search takes hours to days.
- *Budget only approximately satisfied.* The bit-width constraint can only be *approximately* met during search; the final assignment often deviates from the target and needs post-hoc correction.
- *No reuse across budgets.* A solution found for a 3-bit budget is worthless for a 2.5-bit budget — everything must be *re-searched from scratch*.

Just like HAWQ-V2, the curse of "change the budget, redo everything" remained unbroken.

* Frontier Extension

** 2026-05 | GAMMA: Resurrect the "Learn" Branch, Plus Cross-Budget Reuse

*** What Problem It Saw

GAMMA lines up the pain points of all three prior generations:

| Approach | Strength               | Fatal Weakness                                    |
|----------+------------------------+---------------------------------------------------|
| DNAS     | Differentiable, learnable | Requires QAT; infeasible at LLM scale          |
| HAWQ-V2  | No training, fast      | Static proxy; recompute per budget; misses coupling |
| AMQ      | Accurate (no proxy)    | Hugely expensive; budget only approximate; re-search per budget |

The observation: among the three branches, *DNAS's "differentiable and learnable" is the most elegant* — driven directly by the optimization objective, capable of capturing cross-module dependencies, and requiring no hand-crafted proxy. It was blocked by exactly one specific constraint: "requires QAT."

If we could make the DNAS machinery *learn only the bit-width assignment without touching the model weights*, we would retain the differentiability advantage while being able to run at LLM scale.

*** Its Solution

GAMMA *transplants the DNAS differentiable framework entirely into a PTQ pipeline*. Four steps:

1. *Candidate pool + frozen weights.* For every linear module, pre-quantize 2-, 3-, 4-, and 8-bit *candidate weights* using a PTQ backend (QTIP / AQLM). All candidate weights are *frozen*.

2. *Learn only the bit-selection mask.* Each module receives a lightweight Gumbel-Softmax mask that outputs a probability distribution over "select b bits for this module." The only parameters being learned are these mask parameters (on the order of a few MB), not the full model weights (tens of GB).

3. *Hidden-state reconstruction as the loss.* Instead of task loss (which overfits on a small calibration set), use *teacher-forced layer-wise hidden-state reconstruction* — force the i-th layer of the mixed-precision model to *match* the i-th layer output of the FP16 model. This loss directly measures how quantization error accumulates along the forward path; *cross-module dependencies are captured naturally*.

4. *Augmented Lagrangian hard constraint.* Unlike DNAS's soft penalty ("if over budget, inflate the loss" — which often fails to converge to the target), GAMMA uses an augmented Lagrangian: Lagrange multipliers λ1 and λ2 are themselves learned, *dynamically tightening the constraint* so that the expected average bit-width *exactly* converges to b_target.

5. *Stage II: integer programming discretization.* Stage I produces soft distributions; a 0-1 knapsack-style integer linear program then projects those soft distributions onto a *strictly budget-satisfying* discrete assignment.

A *miraculous byproduct* emerges here: the mask probabilities learned in Stage I are fundamentally a *cross-budget-stable sensitivity ranking*, not a budget-specific assignment. When the budget changes, *Stage I does not need to be retrained* — only Stage II's integer program needs to be re-solved, cutting the time from hours to minutes.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Buried)

Reading through the whole river, you feel a *confluence* of satisfaction:

- Takes DNAS's differentiable framework, but removes QAT.
- Takes HAWQ's "post-training, no retraining" paradigm, but removes the static proxy.
- Takes AMQ's "driven by actual quantization quality" philosophy, but removes repeated search.

Every historical lesson is absorbed; every historical pain point is surgically addressed.

*Empirical results*: on Llama3.1-8B, Qwen3-8B/14B, *GAMMA at 2.5-bit ≈ uniform 3-bit accuracy*. This means, at the same accuracy level, the model footprint is cut by another 17% — a 70B model drops from 24 GB to ~20 GB, fitting comfortably inside an RTX 3090's 24 GB.

New pitfalls buried:

- The candidate pool must be pre-quantized and stored — approximately K× storage overhead (K candidate bit-widths) during training, discarded at deployment.
- Stage I still depends on a calibration set; generalization to out-of-distribution data is not fully verified (Section 5.2 includes partial experiments but leaves room for future work).
- The "score reuse across budgets" property is elegant, but has only been validated within the budget range tested in the paper (2.5–4 bits). Whether it extends to more extreme bit-widths — such as the 1.58-bit BitNet regime — is an open question.

** As of Now, This Is the State of the Art on This Line

GAMMA was submitted in May 2026. It is the first paper to simultaneously achieve all three properties — *learnable + no QAT required + cross-budget reuse* — for mixed-precision LLM quantization. Future work will most likely extend along these directions (speculative; not necessarily already published):

- *Candidate pool compression*: storing K full candidate bit-widths is heavy — can they be generated on the fly?
- *Cross-architecture transfer*: can scores learned on Llama-8B be directly applied to Llama-70B or Qwen-14B?
- *Activation quantization included*: currently weight-only; activations remain in high precision.
- *Joint sparsity*: mixed-precision + structured sparsity co-allocation.

* Insights

Reading the whole river reveals not just a technical progression, but a process of *constraints being dismantled one by one*.

Each generation's advance is essentially *identifying which specific constraint had blocked the previous generation*, then operating surgically on that constraint alone, preserving everything else.

- DNAS's constraint was "requires QAT" → HAWQ-V2 removed it, but introduced "static proxy."
- HAWQ-V2's constraint was "static proxy misses coupling" → AMQ removed it, but introduced "expensive search."
- AMQ's constraint was "search is expensive and must restart per budget" → GAMMA removed it, returning to "learn," but this time "learn only the mask, not the weights."

Notice that GAMMA did not *invent* anything fundamentally new — Gumbel-Softmax dates to 2016, augmented Lagrangian to 1969, knapsack ILP is even older operations research, and hidden-state reconstruction has been used since the GPTQ era. GAMMA's contribution is *precisely identifying which specific constraint was the true bottleneck of eight years of mixed-precision quantization*, then assembling already-existing tools in a *new configuration*.

A deeper trend visible across the river: *the boundaries between the "learn / compute / search" paradigms are blurring*. GAMMA is simultaneously learning (mask trained with gradients), computing (hidden-state reconstruction is a deterministic objective), and searching (Stage II is ILP). Once you stop insisting on belonging to one camp, your degrees of freedom expand dramatically.

Where is the next step most likely to go? My assessment: *cross-model sensitivity transfer*. If the mask learned on Llama-8B can be directly applied to Llama-70B or Qwen-14B, then Stage I would not need to be rerun at all — that would be truly *train once, deploy anywhere*. GAMMA's "score reuse across budgets" is already the first step, but only reuses within the same model; the next step is reuse across models.

* Takeaways

*Identifying the real constraint* matters far more than *inventing a new method*.

GAMMA's core innovation is not a mathematical trick — it is *peeling "whether QAT is required" away from the "differentiable bit-width search" paradigm*. Once you see clearly that "the DNAS paradigm is elegant; it is only strangled by the single constraint of QAT" — the rest of the work is mechanical: freeze the weights, change the objective, add the constraint.

Implications for research:

- When a direction has stalled for several years, first *list exactly which constraint is blocking the current best method*, then ask *whether that constraint is fundamental or merely a historical accident*. It is usually the latter.
- When you want to apply an old technique to a new problem, *systematically check each assumption of the old technique to see whether it still holds in the new setting*. Often one or two assumptions have quietly expired — but because nobody asked explicitly, the entire technique gets dismissed as "inapplicable."
- Properties that look like "mere engineering details" — such as *cross-budget reuse* — are often the true bottleneck for real deployment. The paper's casual mention that "scores can be reused" converts a multi-hour ad-hoc workflow into a minutes-long automated pipeline; the industrial value of that single sentence far exceeds the accuracy numbers reported in the paper.

The bottom line is "actually usable": this means you can —

- When deploying a 70B model on consumer hardware, *stop repeatedly ab-testing between accuracy and footprint*. One GAMMA training run plus multiple ILP solves, and all target-budget configurations are ready within minutes.
- Handle a non-standard requirement like "3.7-bit average budget" without training a new solution from scratch.
- Transfer GAMMA's design pattern to *KV cache quantization, attention sparsity allocation, MoE expert pruning*, and any other problem that faces "allocate resources under a budget" — the same recipe of "freeze the large part, learn a small mask, augmented Lagrangian constraint, ILP discretization" is likely to transfer.
