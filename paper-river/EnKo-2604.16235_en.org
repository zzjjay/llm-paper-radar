* One-Line Positioning

This line of research asks: when we want a general-purpose large model to serve only one language, are all those "other languages" inside the model a burden or a foundation?

The target paper delivers its 2026 answer: strip out the tokens of superfluous languages, and Korean task performance not only holds up — machine translation actually improves, especially because the model stops "mid-sentence switching into English or Chinese."

* Forward Narrative: How the Problem Grew Layer by Layer

** Starting Point: One Model for All Languages (2019, mBERT)

In 2019 Google set out to do one thing: instead of training a separate model for every language, let a single BERT absorb all 104 of them. Mix text from all languages together, feed it in, share one vocab and one set of parameters. That was mBERT.

The results were surprisingly good — it could even perform cross-lingual transfer: trained on English annotations, it still worked reasonably on Tamil. This set the tone for the entire field: *multilinguality is a free lunch* — parameter sharing lets low-resource languages ride on the coattails of high-resource ones.

But a hidden risk went unnoticed: those 104 languages share a single, finite vocab (mBERT has 110 K tokens). Cramming in Korean Hangul, Chinese Hanzi, Arabic, Hindi… leaves each language very few "slots." Nobody cared at the time, because "it runs anyway."

** Turning Point: Scale Can't Save Everything (2020, XLM-R)

A year later Facebook released XLM-R, expanding training data from Wikipedia to 2.5 TB of CommonCrawl, growing the model from 170 M to 550 M parameters, and enlarging the vocab to 250 K. Performance went up again.

But the XLM-R paper surfaced an uncomfortable finding: *with fixed model capacity, adding more languages degrades per-language performance*. They named it the *curse of multilinguality* — what you thought was a free lunch is really everyone sharing a single pot of rice; one more mouth means less for everyone.

Their solution was blunt: make the model bigger. Enough capacity fits all languages, and the curse is broken.

That answer was defensible in 2020. But it left a sharper question for the deployment engineers — *I only serve Korean users; why am I paying the electricity bill for 90% of parameters I'll never use?*

** Detour: Instead of Fattening the Model, Cut What Isn't Needed (2020, Load What You Need)

That same year Abdaoui et al. proposed the exact opposite: if my service only uses French and Arabic, why keep the token embeddings for the other 102 languages in mBERT?

Their approach was almost too simple to be research: take a target-language corpus, count which tokens actually appear, delete the rest from the vocab, and drop the corresponding embedding rows. The model shrank by half; downstream task performance barely moved.

This was the embryonic form of *vocabulary pruning*. But the work left two loose ends:
- Experiments were run only on small models like mBERT; the large language models of 2024 did not yet exist.
- Evaluation metrics were mostly classification accuracy; nothing was said about the subtler question of generation quality.

What came next dealt with exactly those two loose ends.

** Tightening: Shrink on Demand at Inference, Not a One-Time Training-Time Cut (2024, Lee & Kim)

Four years passed, and the era of large language models arrived. Generative models are more fragile than classifiers — the output is a softmax over the entire vocab at every step, so a larger vocab means a more expensive softmax and a more diffuse distribution.

Lee & Kim 2024 noticed something: even if you ultimately generate only Korean, the model still runs a full softmax over all 150 K+ tokens at every prediction step. The tens of thousands of Chinese, Japanese, and Hebrew tokens that will *never* be selected are wasting FLOPs on every single step.

They proposed *dynamic vocabulary pruning*: trim the softmax candidate set at inference time based on context, computing probabilities only over the slice likely to be needed.

But this only saved compute. It *did not answer the more fundamental question — why does the model "want to go" to those tokens in the first place?*

** Side Channel: Models Switch Languages on Their Own (2024, Marchisio et al.)

Almost simultaneously, a separate thread was interrogating multilingual models. Marchisio et al. formally introduced the *language confusion* metric: prompt the model to answer in Korean, then measure how much non-Korean text appears in the output.

The results were ugly. Even SOTA models like Llama-3 and Qwen would suddenly produce a few English words — or entire English passages — while responding to Korean prompts. This is not a bug; it is the model's natural behavior under a shared vocab and shared embedding space — Korean tokens and English tokens coexist in the same semantic space, and when they are close enough, the model "boards the wrong train."

They introduced Word-level Pass Rate (WPR) — the proportion of target-language words in the output — as the evaluation metric. This paper later adopts it directly.

But the Marchisio thread only diagnosed the disease; *it offered no cure*. They left the problem on the table for others to solve.

** Landing: Connecting the Two Threads, Running a Physical on Korean (2026, This Paper, EnKo)

In April 2026, Hoyeol Kim and Hyeonwoo Kim brought those three threads together:
- From the Abdaoui thread, they inherit the idea of pruning the vocab by language.
- From the Lee & Kim thread, they inherit the insight that a pruned vocab makes softmax leaner too.
- From the Marchisio thread, they borrow the WPR metric to test one specific claim: *does removing non-target-language tokens cure language confusion at the source?*

They designed three vocabulary configurations:
- Original: the model as released, unchanged.
- EnKo: retain only English + Korean tokens; strip everything else.
- EnKoZh: retain English + Korean + Chinese tokens (because models like Qwen are heavily pretrained on Chinese and may rely on Chinese latent representations).

They then ran 14 open-source models (Qwen3 0.6B–14B, Gemma-3 270M–12B, Llama-3, Tri, Aya-23, etc.) across four benchmark categories: general knowledge (KMMLU), cultural knowledge (HAERAE / CLIcK), instruction following (LogicKor / KoMTBench), and machine translation (WMT24++).

The results fall into three parts.

*First: general and cultural knowledge — almost no degradation.* Across Qwen3 variants the gap between configurations is <0.01; Gemma-3-12B's linguistic evaluation even ticked up slightly. Cutting 36% of the vocab leaves Korean capability unscathed — confirming Abdaoui's original intuition: superfluous tokens really are superfluous.

*Second: instruction following varies by architecture.* Qwen3, with its deep Chinese pretraining, drops when Chinese tokens are removed (EnKo) but recovers when they are kept (EnKoZh) — evidence that its reasoning capability is partly *parasitic on the latent representations of Chinese tokens*. Llama-3.1-8B shows the opposite pattern: the more aggressively it is pruned, the better it performs. This is a new finding: pruning's effect on reasoning is not monotonic; it depends on the language distribution during pretraining.

*Third, and most dramatic: machine translation broadly improves.* Llama-3.1-8B's COMET score rises from 0.5879 to 0.6342; Aya-expanse-8B's rises from 0.6957 to 0.7496. Removing irrelevant languages actually sharpens translation — because the output distribution is no longer distracted by tokens from languages that should never appear.

*WPR is the smoking gun:* Qwen3-4B's WPR jumps from 0.8882 to 0.9923, a gain of 0.1041. The language confusion problem that Marchisio laid on the table is largely solved by this unexpected surgical intervention.

One mildly awkward finding rounds things out: pruning 36% of the vocab yields only a *0.89% reduction in inference latency*. The embedding table is not the real bottleneck — attention is. This path saves memory, not compute.

* Distilled Insights

** What Is Really Changing Beneath This Thread

On the surface it looks like "pruning techniques are evolving." The deeper shift is *a cognitive reversal about multilingual sharing*.

The 2019–2020 cohort believed *multilinguality is an asset* — sharing lets low-resource languages benefit from rich ones. The 2024–2026 findings expose the other side of that story: in generative models, *multilinguality is also a source of contamination*. Excess language tokens do not merely occupy memory; their embeddings coexist with target-language tokens in the same semantic space and actively "lure" the model onto the wrong path — that is the micro-mechanism behind language confusion.

What the EnKo thread is therefore really doing is pushing *"general-purpose model + single-language deployment"* — a half-measure — toward a new paradigm of *"general pretraining + single-language post-hoc excision."* The model is still pretrained on all languages (capturing knowledge, alignment, and reasoning), but undergoes a targeted removal before deployment. This is neither traditional *monolingual-from-scratch* nor *multilingual-all-the-way*; it is a third path.

** Where This Is Most Likely to Go Next

Three openings remain:

1. *Language pruning inside attention layers.* The paper's last finding gives it away — vocab pruned by 36% but latency reduced by only 0.89%; the bottleneck is attention. If one can identify the sub-structures within attention heads that "specialize in certain languages" and prune those, real speed gains will follow. See Voita et al. 2019 for early work on head analysis in NMT.

2. *Dynamic language detection.* EnKo's pruning is a one-time pre-deployment cut, but users occasionally send mixed-language prompts. Someone will build a hybrid scheme that detects the input language on the fly and temporarily activates the corresponding token set — extending Lee & Kim's dynamic pruning from "by candidate probability" to "by language family."

3. *Formalizing pruning as regularization.* The MT improvement in this paper was unexpected. If someone can prove that "removing irrelevant language tokens is equivalent to imposing L1 regularization on the output distribution," token pruning ceases to be a deployment trick and becomes a proper training-time tool.
