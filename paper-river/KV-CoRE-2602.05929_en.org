* The River of the Problem

Every time a large language model generates a token, it must glance back at the "memory" of every
previous token — that memory is the KV-cache. The problem: as context grows long, this memory
balloons to several GB, and memory bandwidth simply cannot feed it fast enough; the model is left
stalling while it waits to read the cache. Compressing this memory has been, by a wide margin, the
single largest problem in LLM inference optimization over the past seven years.

Where is the difficulty? This memory is *data-dependent* — feed the same model Chinese text versus
code, and the compressible redundancy inside the cache looks completely different. Yet the vast
majority of compression methods ignore the data entirely and look only at model weights. The result
is that they either cannot compress enough, or they compress and immediately lose quality. What
every generation of researchers along this line has been searching for is: "how do we honestly
account for the data dimension?"

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Vaswani et al. - Attention Is All You Need: Throwing Out the RNN

*** What problem did it see

The dominant paradigm was RNN/LSTM: sequences had to be computed token by token, no
parallelism was possible, and training was painfully slow. Long-range dependencies would
routinely vanish in the gradient.

*** Its solution

Drop the sequential constraint entirely. Every position attends to every other position
simultaneously — attention. All pairwise relationships emerge from a single matrix
multiplication.

*** Why it worked (and where the new hole was buried)

Dominant at training time: parallelizable, long-range-dependency-preserving. But it left a tail
that went largely unnoticed — inference is autoregressive: each new token still attends to all
previous tokens. To avoid recomputing history, every past Key/Value must be cached. This cache,
the KV-cache, grows linearly with context length. Nobody worried much about this in Vaswani's
paper — contexts were only 512 tokens back then. But nearly all of the next seven years' worth of
stories would revolve around that tail.

** 2019 | Shazeer - Multi-Query Attention: Brute-Force Sharing

*** What problem did it see

As models scaled, the head count rose from 12 to 32, 64, 128 — and the KV-cache grew with it
linearly. During decoding, GPU memory bandwidth simply could not keep up; compute units sat idle
waiting for data, and utilization collapsed. Shazeer noticed that the bottleneck was not compute
but *reading the cache*.

*** Its solution

The simplest possible fix: all heads share one single K and one single V. Instead of storing h
copies, store just 1. KV-cache shrinks to 1/h.

*** Why it worked (and where the new hole was buried)

Bandwidth pressure immediately eased; decoding speed jumped. But the cost was severe: all heads
sharing one K,V is equivalent to neutering multi-head attention's expressive capacity, and model
quality dropped visibly. It was an all-or-nothing cut — no account taken of heads that genuinely
need independent information.

** 2023 | Ainslie et al. - Grouped-Query Attention: A Middle Ground

*** What problem did it see

MQA was too extreme. But the original MHA was too memory-hungry. A knob to tune between the two
extremes was needed.

*** Its solution

Grouping. Partition the h heads into g groups; each group shares one K,V. At g=h it degenerates
to MHA; at g=1 it degenerates to MQA. LLaMA-2, Mistral, Qwen, and other mainstream models all
adopted GQA.

*** Why it worked (and where the new hole was buried)

Elegant engineering; quality loss controllable. But fundamentally still a "decided at training
time" architectural choice — how many groups, how much compression, must be fixed before
pretraining. Already-deployed MHA models are out of luck, and it is still one-size-fits-all: it
does not look at the actual data.

** 2023 | Yu & Wu - Features are low-rank, but weights are not!: A Critical Turning Point

*** What problem did it see

Every low-rank compression method up to that point — whether targeting attention or FFN — was
trying to decompose *weight matrices* into low-rank factors. Yu and Wu ran what looks like a
simple experiment in their AAAI'23 paper: measure the rank of Transformer weights versus the rank
of Transformer activations separately.

*** Its solution

The result overturned intuition — *weights are not low-rank; activations are*. In other words,
the model's parameters need full-rank expressivity, but the intermediate tensors produced when
running real data (including K and V) have a rank far below that of the weights.

*** Why it worked (and where the new hole was buried)

This single statement split the field in two. It told everyone doing low-rank compression: you
have been compressing the wrong thing. What should be compressed is the *data-dependent KV
activations*, not the *data-independent W_K, W_V weights*. But Yu & Wu themselves only
identified the fact — they did not provide a concrete recipe for "how to do this for LLM
KV-caches." It would take several more years for this insight to percolate into engineering
practice.

** 2024 | DeepSeek - Multi-head Latent Attention: Burning Low-Rank into the Architecture

*** What problem did it see

GQA was already the state of the art, but the DeepSeek team wanted something more aggressive —
preserve MHA's expressivity while shrinking the KV-cache further.

*** Its solution

Introduce a latent variable layer: project the hidden state down to a low-dimensional latent
vector c; the KV-cache stores only c, and the full K, V are recovered at attention time via
up-projection matrices. This is equivalent to forcing K and V to share a low-rank bottleneck.

*** Why it worked (and where the new hole was buried)

Mathematically, it acknowledges that KV is inherently low-rank and crystallizes that fact into
the architecture. But it is still "designed at train time" — the vast majority of open-source
models trained with MHA/GQA cannot benefit. The post-hoc compression camp must pick up the baton.

** 2024 | PALU / MHA2MLA: Patching Existing Models

*** What problem did it see

LLaMA, Mistral, Qwen, and other existing MHA/GQA models had already been deployed and were
powering countless applications. Retraining from scratch is prohibitively expensive. Could
KV-cache be compressed *without retraining*?

*** Its solution

Apply SVD to the W_K, W_V projection weight matrices inside the model; retain the top-k singular
values — effectively projecting K and V into a lower dimension. That is what PALU does. MHA2MLA
goes further and "translates" an MHA model into the MLA shape.

*** Why it worked (and where the new hole was buried)

Works in practice, but theoretically stumbles into Yu & Wu's trap — it is still compressing
weights, not activations. The consequence: it holds up on simple data but degrades on real
inference workloads, because "the low-rank structure of the weights" and "the low-rank structure
of activations on real data" are two different things.

** 2021 / 2024 | DRONE / SVD-LLM: Truly Targeting the Data

*** What problem did it see

If activations are the truly low-rank quantity, then the compression matrix should be derived from
*data*, not from weights. DRONE proposed this idea at NeurIPS 2021 (before Yu & Wu gave it an
explicit label); SVD-LLM made it more engineering-ready in 2024.

*** Its solution

Fix a calibration dataset; compute K = X·W_K across that dataset in full; then find the optimal
rank-k approximation of K (minimizing Frobenius error). DRONE derives a closed-form solution;
SVD-LLM uses an incremental Cholesky variant that is memory-friendly. The two are mathematically
equivalent — both recover the same optimal compression matrix.

*** Why it worked (and where the new hole was buried)

This is the first moment in this line where the data truly speaks — the compressed matrix is
*jointly optimal over data and model*. But the output is one thing only: the optimal compression
matrix W_K·V_k.

The remaining hole is subtle: *nobody knows how much redundancy exists in each layer or each
dataset*. The compression matrix gives you "the best compression," but not "a portrait of
compressibility." As a result, all existing methods apply a uniform compression ratio across all
layers, because there is no data telling you that a particular layer can be compressed much harder
than another.

** 2026 | KV-CoRE: Pulling Out the Singular Spectrum as a Diagnostic Instrument

*** What problem did it see

DRONE and SVD-LLM only compute the "optimal compression matrix" — the full singular value
distribution is never explicitly exposed. Compressibility is a continuous concept: if singular
values decay fast, the rank is low and compression is easy; if they decay slowly, the rank is high
and compression is hard. Without the singular spectrum, you cannot say things like "this layer is
3× more redundant than that one" or "Arabic data is 5× more redundant than English." The entire
KV-compression field lacked a *diagnostic tool*, forcing every compression strategy to apply a
uniform strike.

*** Its solution

Two pieces sewn together:

First, *apply SVD directly to activations*. But the activation matrix K has shape (l × d), where
l is the total token count across a dataset — potentially tens of millions of rows — so a
direct SVD is infeasible. KV-CoRE gives an incremental algorithm: maintain only the (d × d)
covariance matrix C = K^T·K; for each token, accumulate k_t^T·k_t; at the end, eigendecompose
C to recover the full singular spectrum of K. Same memory footprint as SVD-LLM, but *additionally*
yields the complete singular spectrum, not just the optimal projection matrix.

Second, *use NER (Normalized Effective Rank) as the compressibility metric*. erank(K) =
exp(-Σ p_i log p_i), where p_i are the normalized singular values. Divide by the true rank r of
the matrix to get NER ∈ [1/r, 1]. Low NER = singular spectrum concentrated in a few large values
= highly compressible.

*** Why it worked (and where the new hole was buried)

It decouples "compression" from "diagnosis." For the first time, someone can give quantitative
answers to questions like:

- Keys are generally more compressible than Values (NER-K is significantly lower than NER-V)
- Middle layers are often the least compressible; early and late layers carry more redundancy
- Low-resource languages such as Arabic and Finnish show anomalously low NER — likely because
  their token embeddings were not well trained
- The Pearson correlation between NER and post-compression perplexity degradation reaches 0.88
  (V) / 0.64 (K)

The new hole left behind: you now know how much each layer in each dataset should be compressed —
*but exactly how to dynamically and adaptively enact that compression policy* is not something
KV-CoRE addresses. It passes the ball to the next generation of "data-aware, layer-differentiated"
compression methods.

* Frontier Extensions

KV-CoRE was released in February 2026 and has no public follow-up work yet. From the holes it
leaves, the most direct next step is a dynamic scheduler that "automatically determines the
per-layer, per-scenario compression ratio based on NER" — an end-to-end system taking NER as an
input signal and producing a compression ratio as an output policy. A second line is
"data-centric model development": using NER to inversely diagnose pretraining data coverage issues
(e.g., anomalously low NER on a low-resource language → that language is underfitted in
pretraining), then using NER to guide data sampling decisions.

* Insight

On the surface, this line is "iterative improvement of KV-cache compression techniques." At a
deeper level, *it is a cognitive shift in where to make the cut*.

The cut site moves progressively closer to the data:

1. First generation (MQA, GQA, MLA) cuts at the *architecture* — preset, data-agnostic structural
   constraints
2. Second generation (PALU, MHA2MLA) cuts at the *weights* — already frozen, but still
   data-independent
3. Third generation (DRONE, SVD-LLM) cuts at *dataset-level activations* — the data truly speaks
   for the first time
4. KV-CoRE pushes the third generation one step further — not only makes the cut, but *draws the
   full map*, showing you where to use what granularity of cut

The underlying pattern: *every generation makes its decision at a later point in the pipeline*.
Architecture → weights → activations → activation diagnosis. The later the decision point, the
more information is available, but also the higher the engineering complexity. This line is not
finished — the foreseeable next generation will achieve "online diagnosis + online compression,"
dynamically adjusting the per-layer compression ratio based on the real-time NER of the current
prompt.

One layer deeper: *the Yu & Wu 2023 insight (compress activations, not weights) is the true
watershed of this river*. All work after it is an attempt to cash in that insight in engineering —
DeepSeek bakes it into the architecture, DRONE/SVD-LLM apply it in post-hoc processing, KV-CoRE
turns it into a quantifiable diagnostic metric. One clean observation spawned an entirely new
research path.

* Takeaway

*Decoupling diagnosis from optimization often catalyzes new research directions*. The compression
field had always bundled "find the optimal compression matrix" together with "assess
compressibility" — once you had the optimal matrix, you were done. KV-CoRE separates the latter
into a standalone metric — NER — and immediately opens up a cascade of byproduct directions:
"data compressibility analysis," "data-model alignment diagnosis," "low-resource language
identification," and more.

This suggests a general heuristic: when a field is focused only on "the optimal solution," ask
"can we pull out the byproducts of the process (singular spectrum, gradient direction, error
distribution) and use them as standalone diagnostics?" That question is often the entry point for
the next generation of work.
