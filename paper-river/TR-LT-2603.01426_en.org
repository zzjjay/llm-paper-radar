* The Problem River

Feed a large model a 100K-token document — it must cache the key and value vectors
for every token at every layer. That cache (the KV cache) often dwarfs the model
weights themselves. VRAM runs out; throughput collapses.
So everyone has been chasing the same question: *can we throw away 80–90 % of the KV
entries and have the model act as if nothing happened?*

The answer over the past few years has been: yes — drop 80–90 % and scores barely
budge. That sounds like a miracle. But miracles don't survive scrutiny. This 2026
paper steps forward and says: "The score you're watching is not what's happening
inside the model's mind — you're only seeing what it says at the very end."

This river flows from "how to drop KV entries" all the way to "what actually breaks
inside the model when you do."

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Transformer: Ditched RNNs, but Quietly Planted a New Landmine

*** What problem did it see?

Previous sequence models using RNNs had to compute step by step — the next token
couldn't be processed until the previous one was done. All that GPU parallelism was
wasted. And the longer the dependency, the harder it was to remember.

*** Its solution

Throw out the recurrent structure entirely. Every token attends directly to every
other token. Fully parallel; dependency distance is no longer a bottleneck.

*** Why it worked — and where the new trap was buried

Parallelism achieved; long-range dependencies captured. But the cost hid in two
places: attention is O(N²) in computation, and — more critically —
*during inference, to avoid recomputation, every token's K and V must be cached.*
The longer the sequence N, the larger the cache. A 100K-token context can consume
tens of gigabytes. That is the starting point of the war everyone has been fighting
for the past decade.

** 2019–2020 | Sparse Transformers / Longformer / BigBird: Don't Look at Everything — Pick and Choose

*** What problem did they see?

Transformer's O(N²) attention made long documents a luxury. But when humans read,
they don't compare every word to every other — most attention is local.

*** Their solution

Pre-specify a sparse pattern: a sliding window (attend only to nearby tokens) plus
a few global tokens (anchor points that everyone attends to). Computation drops from
O(N²) to O(N).

*** Why it worked — and where the new trap was buried

Short windows suffice for most situations; global tokens backstop critical
information. But "which tokens matter" is *fixed in advance* — when the model
actually runs, the important tokens may fall entirely outside the window you drew.
Static sparse patterns can't follow live semantics. The question then escalates: can
the model *decide on the fly* which tokens to keep?

** 2023.05 | Scissorhands: Important Tokens Stay Important

*** What problem did it see?

Fixed sparsity is too rigid. But to go "dynamic," you'd have to recompute the full
attention distribution at every step — which brings O(N²) right back.

*** Its solution

An observation it called *Persistence of Importance*: genuinely important tokens
consistently score high throughout history. So there's no need to recompute from
scratch every step — just track cumulative scores continuously and periodically
evict tokens that have been "cold" for a long time.

*** Why it worked — and where the new trap was buried

First time "dynamically selecting important tokens" became an engineerable solution.
But Persistence is merely an *empirical observation* without theoretical guarantees —
why does "important in the past" mean "safe to evict the less important ones in the
future"?

** 2023.10 | H2O: Turned Intuition into an Oracle — and Became Everyone's Baseline

*** What problem did it see?

Scissorhands had the right intuition but wasn't clean enough as engineering, and
hadn't elevated the eviction strategy to a principled criterion.

*** Its solution

Formally named the persistently-attended minority of tokens *Heavy Hitters*.
Applied a greedy algorithm: at each step, inspect cumulative attention scores and
evict the token with the lowest score. Theoretically shown to approximate the
optimum under certain assumptions.

*** Why it worked — and where the new trap was buried

H2O is clean, argued for, and easy to implement. It became the field's *default
baseline* overnight. But the entire argument rests on a hidden assumption:
*"attended to less = less important = safe to discard."*
For the next five years, no one directly challenged this premise — every improvement
only refined how to execute it. This is exactly the sentence this paper ultimately
challenges.

** 2024 | StreamingLLM / SnapKV / PyramidKV: Patching H2O's Details

*** What problem did they see?

Running H2O strictly — "evict whatever gets low attention" — revealed that the very
first few tokens are highly attended in every sentence and cannot be evicted:
so-called *attention sinks*. H2O also used the same budget across all layers and
heads indiscriminately, which is wasteful.

*** Their solutions

StreamingLLM: always retain the first K tokens (sinks) plus a sliding window.
SnapKV: let queries actively guide which KV entries to keep.
PyramidKV: deeper layers get smaller KV budgets — a pyramid-shaped allocation.

*** Why they worked — and where the new trap was buried

Score-drop curves were flattened a little further. But all these methods are still
evaluated under *the same ruler*: LongBench, InfiniteBench, RULER — give the model
a question, see whether it answers correctly. No one asked: what is happening inside
the model? When it does answer correctly, did it genuinely *retrieve* the
information, or did redundancy and lucky guessing cover for the damage?

** 2025 | AdaKV / Expected Attention / CurDKV / FINCH: The End of Refinement

*** What problem did they see?

Fixed budgets lack flexibility; plain attention scores are biased approximations;
evicting whole chunks is coarser-grained but more stable.

*** Their solutions

AdaKV: adaptive per-head budgets.
Expected Attention: score tokens using the expected attention from future tokens —
more accurate than current attention scores.
CurDKV: select KV entries via a ranking / decomposition criterion.
FINCH: chunk-level pruning.

*** Why they worked — and where the new trap was buried

Engineering steadily improved. "90 % compression with barely any score drop" quietly
became an industry consensus. But everyone kept changing *how to choose what to
evict*; no one changed *how to measure whether the model truly survived eviction*.
The evaluation metrics never moved — long-context QA EM/F1. The field was
self-referential: measuring its own progress with the same ruler it had always used.

** 2026 | TR-LT / GER (This Paper): Attention Is a Router, Not a Warehouse

*** What problem did it see?

The authors did something with all these "barely-any-drop" methods that no one else
had done: *instead of only checking whether the model answered correctly, they
checked whether the model's internal representations were broken.*

Three unsettling findings emerged:

1. Standard benchmarks conflate three fundamentally different things:
   - *Retention*: is this token's KV entry still in the cache?
   - *Accessibility*: can attention route to it?
   - *Utilization*: once routed, does it actually influence the output?
   The H2O family of methods only guarantees the first, yet the field tacitly
   assumed all three were covered.
2. At moderate compression ratios, the model "answers correctly" largely because
   *redundancy substitutes for the damaged routing* — not because compression caused
   no harm, but because the harm was masked.
3. As compression approaches 90 %, a *cliff* appears: hallucination rates spike,
   and this corresponds to a specific physical quantity surging —
   the *Global Eviction Ratio (GER)*: the fraction of answer-critical tokens
   that are evicted consistently across all heads.

*** Its solution

Rather than proposing a new compression method, the paper re-axiomatizes the
evaluation problem itself.

It borrows from the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (Frankle & Carlin 2019): the
original LTH says a dense network contains a sparse sub-network that can match the
full network's performance. The authors lift this from the *parameter level* to the
*inference-time token-routing level*, proposing the
*Token-Route Lottery Ticket (TR-LT)*: the dense attention graph contains a sparse
cross-layer, cross-head *pathway*; as long as that pathway survives, semantics can
flow through; if that pathway is severed, keeping the token in the cache is useless.

GER is the measure of how severely that pathway has been disrupted.

Two failure modes are identified:
- *Representational Erasure*: the answer token is evicted from every head — it
  simply vanishes.
- *Representational Rigidity*: the token is still present, but routing consensus
  across heads collapses; flexibility is lost, and once an error occurs it cannot
  be corrected.

Architecture differences are also observed: LLaMA exhibits "early consensus +
late divergence"; Qwen exhibits "funnel-style late convergence" — the same
compression ratio inflicts damage at different locations in the network.

*** Why it works — and where the new trap is buried

This framework is the first to separate "the model can still answer correctly after
compression" from "compression caused no harm." But the new pitfalls are
substantial:
- Can GER become an optimizable objective? Is it only a measurement tool, or can
  it guide compression design?
- Is TR-LT an empirical phenomenon or does it have theoretical guarantees?
- The architectural differences imply *no universal compression method exists* —
  so how does the field conduct apples-to-apples comparisons?

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026, this paper is barely two months old with no direct public follow-up.
But three directions are foreseeable:

1. *GER-aware compression*: use GER as a target function to redesign eviction
   strategies. Expect a class of methods that appear to have lower compression
   ratios but lower GER — roughly the same scores on old benchmarks, but a
   noticeably flatter hallucination curve.
2. *Cross-architecture compression theory*: if the LLaMA vs. Qwen differences are
   general, future KV compression methods will need to be model-aware, analogous
   to mixed-precision in quantization.
3. *Routing visualization tools*: TR-LT is an inherently visual object; diagnostic
   tooling that lets you observe the token-routing network in real time during
   compression may emerge.

* Insights

Looking back along the entire river, only half the story is about technical
evolution. The other half is about *evaluation blind spots being slowly illuminated*.

The hidden assumption buried in the H2O era — "low attention score means safe to
discard" — was *inherited* by every subsequent piece of work for five years rather
than *questioned*. Every new paper improved on H2O's execution, but no one looked
back to ask whether H2O's premise was correct. The reason is simple: everyone
shared the same benchmarks, and as long as the benchmarks don't change, the premise
is never challenged.

This is a textbook case of *evaluation locking the direction of innovation*.
The paper's real contribution is not the GER metric — it is breaking the ruler:
"You thought nothing was broken because you only watched what the model said out
loud; the interior was already compromised, you just weren't looking."

Zoomed out to the bigger picture: the shift from "watching outputs" to "watching
routing" is LLM research quietly moving from *behaviorism* to *mechanicism* —
a direct continuation of the mechanistic interpretability thread.

Where things are most likely to go next: *all the old "barely-any-drop" methods
will be re-evaluated with metrics like GER*, and the rankings may be reshuffled
dramatically. A number of current SoTA claims may be reclassified as benchmark
hacking.

* Takeaways

What this paper teaches about *how to find problems*:
*When an entire field is iterating on a single baseline,*
*asking whether that baseline's premise is correct is usually more valuable than
taking one more step in the same direction.*

Practically speaking, this means you can —
- Whenever you see a research line where "metrics keep improving," first ask
  *whether the metric itself is distorted*;
- Before adopting any new tool or method, clearly distinguish *existence*,
  *accessibility*, and *utilization* — three things that are easy to conflate in
  KV compression, in RAG, in context engineering, and in memory systems alike;
- When designing evaluations, always prepare a *mechanism-level backup metric*:
  even when the primary metric looks great, use it as a sanity check.
