* Target Paper

*ButterflyMoE: Sub-Linear Ternary Experts via Structured Butterfly Orbits* (Karmore, 2026)

In one sentence: an MoE model with N experts traditionally stores N full weight matrices, so memory grows linearly with N; this paper argues that experts never need to be stored individually — each expert is simply a different orientation of a single shared "master matrix," obtained through a few butterfly rotations. N experts collapse from O(N·d²) to O(d² + N·d·log d), saving 150× at 256 experts.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** Origin: 1991 — "Experts Are Not One Model, They Are a Division of Labour"

MoE did not begin in 2017; Jacobs proposed it back then. He was solving something refreshingly simple: a single neural network trained on all tasks gets dragged in every direction at once. His answer was to maintain a pool of small experts, each learning one class of inputs, plus a gating network to decide which expert steps forward.

The idea never caught fire at the time — computers were not large enough, and splitting into experts actually slowed things down. But the seed was planted: *experts are specialised, routable, and non-interfering*. That assumption went unquestioned for twenty years.

** 2017 — Shazeer Puts It Inside the Transformer

Google wanted enormous language models that year. The obstacle was stark: more parameters → more computation → intractable training. Shazeer dusted off Jacobs' old idea and made one decisive change: each token activates only the top-k experts.

This decoupled *parameter count* from *compute*. You could have hundreds of billions of parameters, yet each token passes through only a small slice. MoE became a viable option for large models for the first time.

But Shazeer left a trap open: *active compute dropped, yet all expert weights still had to reside in memory*. The router must choose among all experts at every step, so you can never evict the unchosen ones.

** 2022 — Switch Transformer Pushes Sparsity to the Extreme

The Fedus team took the next step: assign exactly one expert per token. Routing became simpler, training more stable, and the model scaled to 1.6 trillion parameters. MoE entered the mainstream.

But the memory trap widened dramatically: expert counts grew from a handful or dozens to hundreds or thousands. Each expert is a full d×d matrix. *Total memory is almost entirely occupied by experts.* A single GPU cannot hold them all; deployment requires tens of machines. MoE became inaccessible to edge devices, and serving costs exploded.

By this point the problem was unambiguous: MoE memory grows *linearly* with N. Everything that followed circled around that single line.

** 2023 — QMoE: Compress Each Weight Below 1 Bit

Frantar's approach was direct: if each expert is too large, compress each *weight*. They devised a special quantization scheme, pushing weights below 1 bit, achieving 10–20× memory reduction with only a 2% accuracy drop.

Impressive on the surface. But ButterflyMoE identifies the flaw immediately: *you have only changed the constant factor; the scaling is untouched.* It is still O(N·d²). Double N and memory doubles. 1024 experts on an edge device remain infeasible — just slightly less infeasible than before.

This is ButterflyMoE's first target: every quantization method saves the *constant*, but nobody saves the *N*.

** 2025 — MoBE / PuzzleMoE: Finally Touching the Structure

Chen's MoBE made a fresh move: instead of storing each expert independently, share K basis matrices and express each expert as a linear combination. Memory drops 24–30%.

Zhao's PuzzleMoE went further: identify redundant experts and merge them directly, plus 3-bit quantization, halving memory.

Both teams were scratching the right itch — there is clearly redundancy among experts, and independent storage is wasteful. But ButterflyMoE sees that neither escapes the old framework: *in MoBE, if K grows with N, scaling is still linear*; PuzzleMoE treats expert identity as atomic — it can merge experts but cannot *generate* them.

At this stage the question had been sharpened to its finest edge: can an expert simply not be stored? Can "expert i" be *computed* from a tiny index plus a shared base, rather than retrieved from memory?

** Side Branch: From SmoothQuant to QuaRot — the Rotation Thread

While the main line struggled with MoE memory, a parallel line worked on suppressing activation outliers to enable quantization.

Xiao's SmoothQuant (2023) found that a few channels in activation tensors are wildly large (10–100× the median), and those channels blow up quantization. Per-channel scaling mitigated the problem. But ButterflyMoE notes its limitation: *scaling each channel independently cannot move energy between channels*.

Ashkboos's QuaRot (2024) upgraded the idea: a global Hadamard rotation spreads energy across all channels, flattening outliers. This was the key cognitive leap — *rotation can reshape numerical distributions*. But QuaRot's rotation is fixed, global, and uniform across all experts.

By the time ButterflyMoE arrived, this thread had accumulated everything needed: rotations suppress outliers, rotations can be learned, and rotations can be structured (butterfly matrices represent a d×d rotation with only d·log d parameters).

** Side Branch: Dao's Structured Matrices

Dao's 2019 butterfly paper introduced learnable butterfly matrices — a sparse structure that expresses a d×d rotation with O(d·log d) parameters. Monarch (2022) extended the family further.

Both papers used butterfly/Monarch as drop-in replacements for dense layers — substituting them directly for attention or FFN weight matrices. *Nobody thought to use them to define expert identity.*

** Concurrent: BitNet Pushes Weights to Ternary

Ma's BitNet b1.58 (2024) demonstrated that weights compressed to {-1, 0, +1} still yield usable models. The 1.58-bit floor was established.

This was the final puzzle piece: the master matrix can be ternary.

** 2026 — ButterflyMoE: All Threads Converge

Karmore locks all the threads together.

Store one shared *ternary master matrix* (BitNet's extreme compression). Each expert is no longer an independent weight block — it is that master matrix repositioned by a set of learned *butterfly rotations* (Dao's structured matrices + QuaRot's concept of learnable rotation).

Each expert requires only O(d·log d) rotation parameters. Summed over N experts: O(d² + N·d·log d). For the first time, N drops from an independent linear term to a sub-linear one, sitting beside a logarithm.

Activation outliers are treated as a bonus: each expert's rotation is learned separately, tuned to the activation distribution that particular expert encounters — finer-grained than QuaRot's single global rotation.

* Core Insight

On the surface this evolutionary line is about "how to reduce memory." At a deeper level it is a shift in the conception of *what an expert is*.

From Jacobs through PuzzleMoE, everyone implicitly assumed that *an expert is an independent, addressable, storable block of weights*. You could compress it, merge it, or share its basis — but its existence was physical, and it occupied its own room in memory.

ButterflyMoE is the first to say: an expert is an *action*, not an *object*. "Expert i" means "apply butterfly rotation set i to the shared master matrix." Expert identity transforms from "a chunk of data sitting in RAM" to "a small program that generates it."

This is the same inversion that functional programming calls *function as data* → *data as function*. It is the same move NeRF makes replacing voxel grids with an MLP, or neural codecs make replacing codebooks with a network. *Storage is replaced by computation* — as long as that computation is cheap enough (O(d·log d) genuinely is).

** Where Does This Go Next

If "experts are actions" holds, several directions open naturally:

1. *Compress the rotations themselves.* N experts each carry their own rotation parameter set, totalling N·d·log d. Could rotation parameters be partially shared? For instance, if all experts' rotations are outputs of a small hypernetwork conditioned on a few tokens encoding the expert id, the total becomes O(d²) for the master plus a small network — nearly independent of N.

2. *Discover experts during training.* If experts are fundamentally actions, there is no reason to fix N before training begins. When new data arrives that existing N actions cannot cover, grow a new rotation parameter set — *expert growth on demand*.

3. *Cross-model transfer.* One master matrix, different rotation sets for different tasks. A single base, with n tasks' "postures" layered on top. MoE becomes the underlying language for multi-task learning.
