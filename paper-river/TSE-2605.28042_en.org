* The Target Paper

*Extracting Small Translation Specialists from LLMs by Aggressively Pruning Experts*
(arXiv:2605.28042, UCLA, 2026-05-27)

In one sentence: take a general-purpose MoE like GPT-OSS-20B, discard more than
half its experts using routing statistics alone, and the resulting smaller model
loses almost nothing on translation; add a little SFT and you can prune
75%–90%.

* The Thread Traced Backwards

The real protagonist of this thread is not the technique of expert pruning — it
is a question that echoes across every generation: *how many parameters does
translation actually need?* Each generation over the past twenty years has
pushed that upper bound lower.

Supporting strand (explanatory, not in the main causal chain):

- *Bogoychev 2021 (2010.11859)* — showed on conventional Transformer MT that
  "FFN is the least critical component; attention is what matters" → gave
  researchers the confidence to aggressively cut FFN layers / experts
- *Frankle 2021 / Dettmers 2022 / Hinton 2015* — the three grand traditions of
  pruning, quantization, and distillation; the target paper treats them only as
  background

* Forward Narrative: How the Problem Was Progressively Cornered

** Starting Point: The Problem Shazeer Saw in 2017
The 2017 pain point was simple: making a model smarter meant adding parameters,
but adding parameters raised FLOPs proportionally — nobody could afford to run
it. Shazeer's counter-move: have each token activate only a small subset of FFNs
("experts"), leaving the rest dormant. A learned gating network decides who
wakes up. The result: 137B parameters, but per-token compute identical to a
small model.

What was left unresolved: the implementation was hand-coded on top of LSTM and
incompatible with the Transformer that was rapidly taking over the field;
engineering constraints confined it to a handful of machines.

** GShard 2021: Pack It into Transformer and Scale to 600B
Lepikhin's problem: for MoE to matter, it had to slot into any Transformer
architecture and partition automatically across thousands of TPUs. They wrote an
XLA-level SPMD annotation API, embedded MoE layers into an encoder-decoder with
top-2 gating, and trained a 600B-parameter many-to-English multilingual
translation model in four days on 2,048 TPUs.

What was left unresolved: 100 languages sounds like a lot, but they were all
high- or medium-resource languages; how to source low-resource parallel data and
filter out toxicity was left untouched.

** NLLB 2022: Cover 200 Languages, but the Model Became a Beast
NLLB's problem: the real world has thousands of languages and prior models were
essentially useless for low-resource ones. They crawled massive low-resource
parallel corpora, built a 54.5B-parameter MoE replacing every fourth FFN with an
MoE layer, and achieved BLEU+44% over the previous SOTA.

What was left unresolved: running 54.5B requires four 32 GB GPUs. When
translating Greek→Japanese, the experts responsible for Swahili and Korean are
completely irrelevant — yet to keep load-balancing loss from collapsing during
training, every expert must participate in every forward pass. *Those parameters
sit in memory doing nothing.*

** Koishekenov 2023: The First Aggressive Expert Cull
Koishekenov's problem: the situation above — "most experts in memory are useless
for this particular translation pair" — should be fixable. Their approach was
disarmingly simple: for each language pair, run the model over a small
calibration set and count how many times each expert is selected by the top-1
gate. Discard the least-selected ones. No retraining required; pruning 80% of
experts per language pair caused almost no BLEU degradation, and NLLB-200 now
ran on a single 32 GB GPU.

An even more interesting side-finding: decoder experts clustered by language
family — Indo-European languages shared one pool of experts, Sino-Tibetan
languages another. This hinted that *something like genuine "language experts"
really does exist inside MoE models.*

What was left unresolved:
1. This had only been verified on NLLB, a model trained from scratch specifically
   for translation using an encoder-decoder architecture; the routing geometry of
   modern decoder-only MoE LLMs (GPT-OSS, Qwen3-MoE) is completely different.
2. "Top-1 frequency" is a coarse signal — it counts occurrences but ignores gate
   weight magnitudes and expert output norms.

** Two Parallel 2026 Groundwork Papers
*** REAP (Lasby 2026, arXiv 2510.13999) — A Smarter Pruning Signal
REAP's problem: the prevailing approach at the time was expert merging — average
less-important experts into the surviving ones. They showed this causes
"functional subspace collapse," destroying input-dependent routing information.
Pruning is therefore preferable to merging. Their importance signal is also
sharper: each expert's importance = gate weight × expert output norm. On models
like Qwen3-Coder-480B, 50% compression still preserved SOTA performance.

But REAP is task-agnostic — its calibration data is general mixed text. If you
only care about *one specific downstream task* (say, translation), you can
actually prune far more aggressively.

*** Bandarkar 2026b (arXiv 2510.04694) — Dissecting Multilingual MoE Routing
Bandarkar's problem: does the "experts cluster by language family" phenomenon
that Koishekenov observed in NLLB also hold in models like Qwen3-30B-A3B and
GPT-OSS-20B — general LLMs that happen to be good at translation? They conducted
large-scale routing analysis and concluded:
- *Early and late layers*: experts specialize by language (these handle "which
  language")
- *Middle layers*: experts are language-agnostic (these handle "what to express")

This structural finding directly supplied a pruning blueprint for the next step.

** Target Paper 2605.28042: All Threads Converge
Seen in this light, the Martin/Bandarkar/Peng paper is almost inevitable — it
braids all four strands together:

1. *From Koishekenov*: borrows the core paradigm of routing-statistics-based
   pruning
2. *Transfers it from NLLB to GPT-OSS-20B / Qwen3-30B-A3B*, answering the first
   open question Koishekenov left behind
3. *Upgrades top-1 frequency to routing mass* — for each token, sum the gate
   weights assigned to every selected expert directly; not just counting
   selections, but weighting by magnitude. This inherits the spirit of REAP
   (though REAP itself is used as a comparison baseline)
4. *Applies layer-differentiated pruning rates*: drawing on Bandarkar 2026b's
   finding — prune middle layers more aggressively, spare the first and last
   layers. This is their distinctive contribution.

Without any retraining: 50% pruning is nearly lossless, 70% incurs only minor
loss.
Add a round of SFT: 75% is lossless, and 90% remains usable in some settings.

The Discussion section surfaces several intriguing side-findings:
- *Shared subnetworks* exist across language directions — calibrating only on
  German data yields a pruned model that still works on Russian
- Calibration data generalizes across languages; task-level commonality is more
  stable than language-level commonality
- The "foreman experts" forced into activation by load-balancing loss during
  training are a distinct population from the "specialist experts" actually
  responsible for translation; pruning eliminates mostly the former

** Frontier Extensions: The Rest of 2026
- *Chen 2026 (2601.14050)*, *Routing Isolation (2604.03592)* — continue digging
  into multilingual routing along Bandarkar's direction
- *REAM (2604.04356)* — directly challenges REAP: "pure pruning loses
  information; a merge+prune hybrid is better"
- *Myth of Expert Specialization (2604.09780)* — questions the entire thread
  from the opposite direction: "apparent expert specialization is merely an
  artifact of embedding geometry, not genuine semantic division." If this
  critique holds, the fact that task-targeted pruning in the target paper still
  works so well is itself a counter-argument that demands explanation

* The Deeper Shift Underneath This Thread

On the surface this looks like "pruning techniques getting progressively
finer-grained," but underneath it is a three-level cognitive progression:

1. *From "how many parameters can I pack in" to "of the parameters I packed,
   how many am I actually using"*
   — Shazeer/GShard occupy the first half, answering "how big can we go";
   Koishekenov marks the turn into the second half, asking "how much is actually
   activated." MoE is the pivot — it is the first architecture that makes "used
   vs. unused" *measurable and therefore prunable.*

2. *From general-purpose compression to task-directed compression*
   — Early pruning, quantization, and distillation all treated "unimportant" as
   meaning "unimportant averaged over all tasks." Koishekenov was the first to
   bind "importance" to a concrete downstream task (a specific language pair).
   The target paper pushes this to its limit: once you fix a task, 90% of
   parameters are bystanders.

3. *From "model structure is a black box" to "model structure is modular"*
   — MoE makes explicit which parameters handle which responsibilities. This
   opened a door for interpretability research — Bandarkar-style dissection of
   "what are early, middle, and late layers each doing" is only possible because
   MoE provides "experts" as observable, discrete units. The next horizon may be
   "a model as a module library" — assemble whichever experts you need for a
   given capability. Pruning is merely the earliest product of this modular
   intuition.

* Where This Is Most Likely Headed Next

- *Per-user / per-conversation pruning*: today it is "I need translation, discard
  non-translation experts"; tomorrow it may be "I am currently writing code,
  discard non-coding experts" — dynamic pruning, assembled at inference time.
  This requires reducing calibration cost to seconds.

- *Training MoE models that are pruning-friendly by design*: today's paradigm is
  "train first, prune later"; the next step is building training objectives that
  explicitly encourage "for each task, a sparse prunable subnetwork exists" —
  essentially transplanting the lottery ticket hypothesis into task space.
  Shi et al. 2026 / Wang et al. 2026b are already pursuing this direction.

- *Cross-cutting with quantization and distillation*: after pruning 90% of
  experts, can the remaining 10% be further 4-bit quantized? Can they be
  distilled into a dense model? This is the next 10× on the memory frontier.

- *The counter-argument will force deeper explanations*: if the "Myth of Expert
  Specialization" critique holds — specialization is just an embedding-geometry
  coincidence — then why does the target paper's task-targeted pruning still
  work so well? That tension itself will drive a new wave of interpretability
  work aimed at locating where "true task subnetworks" live, as opposed to the
  regions that merely *look like* them in routing statistics.

- Shazeer → GShard → NLLB → Koishekenov → target paper is a chain of
  *explicit citations and direct critiques/improvements* within the target paper;
  confidence is high
- REAP is *used as a comparison baseline in the target paper*; the relationship
  is unambiguous
- Bandarkar 2026b shares an author with the target paper (Bandarkar); its key
  findings are cited in "Assumption 2.3" of the target paper and are a direct
  intellectual source
- Bogoychev 2021 is cited in the target paper as motivational work; the
  relationship is "inspiration," not "critique and improvement"
- Chen 2026 / REAM / Myth of Expert Specialization in the frontier-extensions
  section were retrieved from arXiv by the research agent as contemporaneous
  related work; *they have not been confirmed by the target paper itself*.
  Their relationship to the target paper is "parallel / subsequent"; since the

  inference is speculative
- The conclusion that middle layers handle "language-agnostic tasks" while early
