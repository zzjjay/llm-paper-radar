* The Problem River

Assigning a quantization precision to every layer of a large model — which layers use 4bit, which use 2bit — so that total accuracy loss is minimized is a combinatorially explosive problem: as layer count grows, the space of possible precision assignments is exponential, making exhaustive search completely infeasible.

The community has therefore sought a "divide-and-conquer" shortcut: measure each layer's "pain from precision loss" in isolation, then sum those individual pain scores to estimate the total loss of any arbitrary assignment. This shortcut rests on a premise — *layer losses are additive* — meaning a given layer's pain score stays roughly constant regardless of what precision the other layers receive.

But does this premise actually hold? Nobody has ever truly verified it. People either assume it holds (using per-layer sensitivity scores to rank precision) or assume it doesn't (arguing that layers interact in complex "chemical" ways that require joint modeling). The papers along this line represent a tug-of-war over whether this premise is valid.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2019 | HAWQ: Assign Each Layer a Single "Sensitivity Score"

*** What problem it saw

Deep networks destined for edge devices must be compressed to very low bit-widths. But if every layer uses the same precision (say, all 4bit), the model degrades badly — different layers tolerate quantization noise to vastly different degrees: some survive aggressive compression unharmed, others collapse at the slightest perturbation.

The trouble is that picking the right precision for each layer would require trying every combination, and with many layers the search space is exponential — brute force is simply out of the question.

*** Its solution

HAWQ observed that a layer's sensitivity to quantization noise is related to the second-order derivatives of the loss (the Hessian) at that layer: the larger the maximum Hessian eigenvalue, the "steeper" the loss surface, and the more the loss spikes when weights are perturbed. Computing the maximum eigenvalue of the Hessian for each layer in isolation is enough to rank all layers by "how much they fear compression" — without ever running the full combinatorial search.

*** Why it works (and what trap it sets)

It works because it replaces a combinatorial optimization with linear-cost per-layer scoring. But it buries two traps: first, using only the maximum eigenvalue discards the rest of the Hessian spectrum, making scores somewhat crude; second, even after scoring, the mapping from scores to actual bit-widths must be chosen by hand — there is no automated rule, and every new task or compression budget requires fresh tuning.

** 2020 | HAWQ-V2: Sharper Sensitivity Scores and Automated Bit Selection

*** What problem it saw

HAWQ's "maximum eigenvalue" scoring is like judging a student's overall ability by a single best grade — the information is thin. And even after scoring, translating sensitivity into concrete bit choices (4bit vs. 2bit) still required manual intervention, needing re-tuning for every task and every compression budget.

*** Its solution

HAWQ-V2 proved that the Hessian trace — the average effect of all eigenvalues — is a better sensitivity measure than a single maximum eigenvalue, more faithfully representing a layer's true sensitivity. It also constructed a Pareto frontier: given a total model-size or bit-budget constraint, the method automatically finds the optimal bit allocation that balances sensitivity scores against compression ratio, removing the need for manual bit selection.

*** Why it works (and what trap it sets)

The trace is more robust than a single eigenvalue because it cannot be skewed by one extreme curvature direction. The Pareto frontier automates bit selection. Yet both HAWQ and HAWQ-V2 are fundamentally measuring each layer's sensitivity "behind closed doors" — when testing layer 5, the actual quantization state of layer 6 is completely ignored. The "layers do not interact" assumption is harmless when the bit budget is generous, but nobody verified what happens when it tightens.

** 2025.04 | QEP: Errors Don't Stop When Measured — They Snowball

*** What problem it saw

The HAWQ family measures "how much does the loss rise if we quantize this layer alone?" But at inference time all layers are chained: quantization error from layer 1 is fed as input to layer 2, layer 2's error feeds layer 3, and so on. Measuring each layer's sensitivity in isolation completely ignores this downstream propagation and amplification, especially at low bit-widths (below 4bit), where accumulated error far exceeds what summing per-layer sensitivities would predict.

*** Its solution

QEP (Quantization Error Propagation) builds an explicit "error propagation channel" between layers: instead of discarding the error signal after quantizing layer 1, it passes it to the next layer and applies targeted compensation there, preventing the snowball effect. A tunable propagation strength prevents over-compensation (which would cause overfitting) and keeps computational overhead in check.

*** Why it works (and what trap it sets)

It works because it acknowledges that layers are not independent and specifically corrects for the "error accumulates" mechanism, earning the largest gains at extreme low bit-widths. But QEP's interaction model is still chain-shaped — A propagates to B, B propagates to C, one direction, one step at a time. It does not answer a deeper question: if A and C are quantized simultaneously (skipping B), is there a "combinatorial effect" between them beyond what the chain predicts?

** 2025.09 | CoopQ: Treat Layer Interactions as a Cooperative Game

*** What problem it saw

QEP captures chain propagation, but CoopQ's authors argue the true layer relationships are richer — the total loss from quantizing any arbitrary subset of layers simultaneously is not simply the sum of their chain-propagation effects. Like a sports team, the combined effect of two players on the field cannot be predicted from "A passes to B" alone; other synergies may exist. Below roughly 4bit average bit-width, these ignored combinatorial effects noticeably hurt performance.

*** Its solution

CoopQ treats each layer as a player in a cooperative game and treats quantization as removing a player from the game. It uses Shapley values — a classical game-theoretic measure of each player's marginal contribution — to estimate the true per-layer contribution and the pairwise interactions among layers, rather than only measuring the loss when a layer is removed in isolation. The Shapley-aware sensitivity estimates are then fed into an integer program that translates them into concrete 2bit/4bit per-layer assignments.

*** Why it works (and what trap it sets)

Shapley values can in principle capture the effects of any subset combination, making them more comprehensive than chain propagation; this pays off most under extreme compression. But CoopQ leaves a hidden assumption unvalidated: to make Shapley estimation tractable, CoopQ (and every prior method) must first assume that "per-layer or pairwise sensitivity scores, measured in isolation, can reconstruct the true total loss of any combination." Whether this assembly is actually valid has never been directly measured.

** 2026.07 | Target Paper: Before Building Models, Measure How Well Assembly Actually Works

*** What problem it saw

Whether one believes the HAWQ camp (isolated per-layer scores can be assembled into total loss) or the CoopQ camp (joint modeling of layer interactions is necessary), both sides have been racing toward ever-greater complexity — trace, propagation, Shapley values — without anyone turning back to do the most basic thing: directly measure how well per-layer or pairwise sensitivity scores, obtained in isolation, actually reconstruct the true joint loss f(S) over arbitrary subsets. If this premise is mostly valid, the increasingly elaborate interaction models may be solving a much smaller problem than assumed.

*** Its solution

The author conducts a systematic empirical check at 4bit precision: measure the true joint loss f(S) for many layer subsets, then ask how much variance is explained by the sum of per-layer terms alone. The answer: 85–93 % of variance is explained by per-layer terms, and after a simple monotone transformation, rankings produced by this additive model disagree with true rankings by at most 2 % — meaning the HAWQ camp's "isolated measurements can be assembled" assumption is, to a large degree, correct, and the complex interactions CoopQ worried about matter less than expected.

But "mostly correct" is not "exactly correct," and the remaining variance has a systematic structure. The author identifies a "saturation" effect as the culprit and proposes a coverage model: f(S) = c(1 − ∏_{i ∈ S}(1 − a_i)), where a_i is each layer's break-rate. The intuition: quantizing a layer is like pouring water into a bucket; each layer contributes its own "break probability," and the water level (total loss) saturates as more layers are included — it does not grow without bound. This saturation shape is precisely the nonlinear residual that the per-layer linear model misses. Fitted using only each layer's break-rate, the coverage model reproduces the true variance curve to within a few percentage points.

*** Why it works (and what trap it sets)

It works because instead of assuming a form for layer interactions (independent / chain / game-theoretic) and then testing whether the results are good, the paper first honestly measures "how much is missed by isolated measurement," finds that the missed portion has a shape simple enough to capture with a single nonlinear transformation (product-to-saturation curve) rather than exponential combinatorial enumeration or explicit propagation mechanisms. Bit allocations produced by this coverage model achieve lower KL divergence than competing methods on models from 30B to 355B parameters; under extreme bit budgets, gradient-sensitivity (HAWQ-style) allocations cause the model to generate non-terminating output, while coverage-model allocations continue to produce valid text.

The trap it leaves: whether this "saturation" shape is specific to quantization near 4bit, and whether a similar simple residual structure appears in other compression settings (pruning, distillation), is not addressed.

* Insight

The real shift along this line is not "models keep getting more complex" but a methodological swing back: the first four papers each assume a form for layer interactions (independent / chain / game-theoretic) and then test whether outcomes improve; the target paper reverses the order — first directly measure what the true joint loss looks like, then ask whether a simple model can describe it. The answer turns out to be: "the interactions are not as complex as debated, but they are not zero either" — what was actually being missed is a saturation-curve effect that can be stated in a single sentence, not an effect requiring exponential combinatorial modeling.

The most likely next step is to carry this methodology — "measure true values first, then fit the simplest form" — to other compression axes (inter-layer interactions in pruning, teacher-student alignment in distillation) and test whether similarly "complex interaction assumptions" are equally overstated there.

* Takeaway

When a field splits into two camps — both building increasingly elaborate models atop the same unverified shared assumption ("can isolated measurements be assembled?") — the highest-leverage move is usually not to pick a side and produce an even more complex third model, but to directly measure whether the shared assumption is true in the first place. In practice: whenever you see "method A assumes the simple premise holds, method B insists complex modeling is necessary," ask first — is there a controlled, small-scale experiment that can directly test the premise itself, rather than continuing to pile complexity in both directions?
