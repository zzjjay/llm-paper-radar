* The River of the Problem

A model says "I answered this way because..." — but how much can you trust that "because"?

This is a research thread about *whether explanations can be trusted*. A decade ago, people discovered that deep models were black boxes, and set about making them "explain themselves." But critics quickly pointed out: the explanations models produce may be nothing more than stories confected after the fact, with no connection to the actual decision mechanism. The entire thread is a succession of researchers trying to pin down *when a model's self-explanation is genuine and when it is fabricated*.

By 2024, researchers had begun to "lie-detect" LLM self-explanations. Then this 2026 paper poked a new hole: everyone is now compressing models through quantization — squeezing 16-bit weights down to 4-bit or 8-bit — and the question becomes whether that compression further degrades an already fragile self-explanation capacity. In other words, *how much more fictional is the "because" that your deployed, compressed model gives you compared with the full-precision model's "because"?*

The difficulty is that at every step along this thread, "what counts as a faithful explanation" is itself a slippery concept. The definition has been refined back and forth over ten years, and each refinement has revealed a new gap in the previous testing approach.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Doshi-Velez & Kim: Laying Down Rules for "Interpretability"

*** What Problem It Saw

Before this paper, "interpretable ML" was a marketing term. Everyone claimed their method was interpretable, but no one could answer: "What makes your explanation good? How do you measure it?" One paper said "I visualized attention weights," another said "I extracted decision-tree rules," a third said "I had the model write its own explanation" — were these comparable? Which was more interpretable? There was no objective standard.

Worse, the field harbored a tacit conceptual sleight of hand: a researcher would draw a pretty visualization, readers would think "oh, I get it," and the author would claim "my method is interpretable." But "readers feel they understand" and "readers actually understand what the model is doing" are completely different things.

*** Its Solution

Instead of asking "what is interpretability," it asked "why do we need interpretability." It decomposed the goals of interpretability into categories (debugging, ethical auditing, scientific discovery, etc.) and designed concrete evaluation experiments for each. It proposed three evaluation tiers:
- Application tier (have real users apply the explanation to a real task; measure task performance)
- Human tier (run user studies with simplified proxy tasks)
- Functional tier (use surrogate metrics without human involvement)

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It was the first to make "interpretability" something that could be argued about and scored. But it left a large gap: it said "evaluation should check fitness for purpose," without addressing whether "what the model says" and "what the model actually computes" are the same thing. That gap would wait for Jacovi & Goldberg to fill.

** 2018 | e-SNLI: Building a Training Ground for "Models Explaining Themselves in Plain Language"

*** What Problem It Saw

Doshi-Velez had laid the rules, but they couldn't be applied — the NLE direction lacked even training data. To teach a model to output a natural-language explanation alongside an NLI judgment, you need a dataset: each sample annotated with (premise, hypothesis, label, human-written explanation). That dataset did not exist.

*** Its Solution

Take the existing SNLI dataset (570k sentence pairs) and hire annotators to add an explanation to every example. "Why does 'A man is playing guitar' entail 'A person makes music'? Because a man is a type of person, and playing guitar is a form of making music." One by one.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

This is the foundational dataset for NLE research — every subsequent "teach a model to write explanations" method started from here. But it assumed: *as long as the model's explanation resembles the human-annotated explanation, it is a good explanation*.

That assumption contains exactly the conflation Doshi-Velez had warned about. A model might learn to "output explanations that look plausible" while those explanations have nothing to do with the features actually driving its decisions. The evaluation metrics of e-SNLI (BLEU, human ratings) only check "does it look like something a human would write," not "is it actually what the model used." That hole was waiting for Jacovi to puncture.

** 2020 | Jacovi & Goldberg: Pulling "Looks Right" and "Is Right" Apart

*** What Problem It Saw

NLE had been popular for two years, and papers were full of "our explanations are more faithful." Jacovi looked closer and found the word "faithful" was being used in ten different senses. The most damaging conflation: many papers used "humans find this explanation reasonable" as evidence that "the explanation is faithful."

A pointed example: a model is actually using "not" as a shortcut to flip its judgment, but its explanation says "because the event described in the premise contradicts the hypothesis." A human reads it, nods, and rates it highly. But the explanation does not reflect what the model actually did — *it is plausible, but it is false*.

*** Its Solution

Formally separate two concepts:
- *Plausibility* = how good humans think the explanation is
- *Faithfulness* = whether the explanation truly reflects the model's decision process

Then provide an operational guideline for faithfulness: "If two decision processes are the same, the explanations should be the same; if different, they should differ." This apparently tautological definition is the root of every subsequent "faithfulness test."

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It corrected the field's vocabulary. From then on, any paper using "faithful" had to specify how it was measured and could no longer muddy the waters.

But it offered only a philosophical definition, not a concrete test method. "Explanations should differ when decision processes differ" — but how do you operationalize "decision processes differ"? You cannot see inside the model's computation. Atanasova picked up that baton.

** 2023 | Atanasova et al.: A Lie Detector for NLE via Counterfactual Editing

*** What Problem It Saw

Jacovi said "if the decision process changes, the explanation must change too." But researchers cannot directly observe a model's decision process. What to do?

*** Its Solution

Take a detour: change the input directly and watch whether the explanation follows. It designed a set of counterfactual tests, for example:
- *Counterfactual Test*: add a perturbation to the input that flips the model's prediction (e.g., insert a negation). If the model's prediction truly flips from entailment to contradiction, the explanation must acknowledge "because a negation was added." If it keeps repeating the original explanation, the explanation has decoupled from the actual decision.
- *Input Reconstruction Test*: mask out the parts of the input that the explanation mentions. If the explanation genuinely reflects what the model used, masking those parts should change the prediction.

This collection of "perturb-then-observe" tests forms an operational lie-detector for NLE.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It grounded Jacovi's philosophical definition in practice. But the test subjects were primarily *fine-tuned mid-sized models* — you fine-tune BART to output a label plus an explanation, then test that fine-tuned model.

By 2023, however, the world had changed: people no longer fine-tuned; they prompted large models with "please give your answer and reasoning." This prompt-based self-explanation behaves differently from fine-tuned NLE: the model may have decided its answer at the very first token, with the "explanation" serving only as post-hoc rationalization. Did Atanasova's tests still apply? Madsen came to answer that.

** 2024 | Madsen et al.: Pointing the Lie Detector at Prompt-Based LLM Self-Explanations

*** What Problem It Saw

In the LLM era, "self-explanation" looks like this: a user asks "Is this review positive or negative? Please explain your judgment," and the model outputs "This is positive, because..." The explanation sounds fluent, detailed, and well-reasoned. But Turpin 2023 had already sounded the alarm: if you plant a bias in the prompt ("I notice that answer A is always correct"), the model will silently conform to that bias while the explanation never mentions it — explanation and actual reasoning are completely decoupled.

Does that mean LLM self-explanations are universally unreliable? Or only unreliable in special cases like planted biases? No one had tested this systematically.

*** Its Solution

Port Atanasova's counterfactual test suite to prompt-based LLM self-explanations. Have LLMs give answers and explanations across multiple tasks, then apply counterfactual perturbations:
- Change key words in the input that the explanation mentions, and check whether the explanation changes
- Test whether counterfactual edits trigger explanation changes

The results showed that the faithfulness of LLM self-explanations is *highly task-dependent* — adequate on some tasks, pure post-hoc confabulation on others. In other words, Madsen produced a preliminary map of "which scenarios allow you to trust LLM self-explanations."

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It upgraded the NLE-era tooling to the LLM era. But every model it tested was a *full-precision fp16 model*.

The problem is that industrial deployments of LLMs almost universally apply quantization — cutting weights from 16-bit to 8-bit or even 4-bit, saving half the memory or more. Quantization had already been shown to degrade models on many downstream tasks, to impair multilingual capability, and to erode emergent abilities.

So — would it also further degrade an already fragile self-explanation capacity? Madsen's map described "ideal-condition" LLMs; the LLMs users actually encounter are quantized. Are the self-explanation faithfulness of these two the same thing? That is the question Wang 2026 picked up.

** 2026 | Wang et al. (QuantSE): Twisting the "Quantization" and "Self-Explanation Faithfulness" Threads Together

*** What Problem It Saw

Madsen told us "full-precision LLM self-explanations are sometimes false." The quantization literature told us "quantization makes models worse on certain dimensions." But no one had tested both together: *how much more fabricated is the "because..." from the 4-bit quantized model I actually deploy compared with its full-precision counterpart?*

This question is not academic curiosity — it carries real-world pressure. A company deploying LLMs in customer service, medical consultation, or loan approval must use quantized versions (for cost and latency), while also having to give users a "why." If quantization quietly degrades the credibility of that "why" without anyone noticing, that is a hidden harm to end users.

*** Its Solution

A complete cross-product evaluation:
- Three mainstream quantization methods: LLM.int8 (Dettmers 2022), GPTQ (Frantar 2023), AWQ (Lin 2024)
- Multiple bit-widths (4-bit, 8-bit)
- Multiple model families and sizes (Llama 3, Qwen 2.5, Gemma 3, etc.)
- Two types of self-explanation: NLE (having the model write reasons) and counterfactual (having the model say "if the input changed this way, the answer would change")
- Faithfulness measured with the Atanasova/Madsen test suite + LLM-as-judge for quality + a real human user study for coherence/trustworthiness

Conclusions per the abstract: quality drops by *up to 4.4%* on average, faithfulness drops by *up to 2.38%* on average, and coherence and trustworthiness in the user study drop by *up to 8.5%*. Larger models are more robust to quantization on faithfulness but not necessarily on quality. No single quantization method dominates across all metrics.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It formally introduced "real-world deployment" as a variable in self-explanation faithfulness research. From now on, any claim about "how good LLM self-explanations are" must be qualified with "under what quantization setting."

The pit it leaves: an 8.5% drop in trustworthiness — is that large or small? For a customer-service use case it may not matter; for medical or legal applications it could be catastrophic. The engineering practice of running faithfulness regression tests at the user level, by use case, has no standardized method yet. The paper itself admits: "We recommend validating per use case" — which translates to: "We cannot give you a universal answer; each project must redo this test suite for itself."

* Extensions at the Frontier

The target paper was published in January 2026; as of today (May 2026) only four months have passed. No follow-up work directly focused on "the impact of quantization on self-explanation faithfulness" has been found in public sources. Several foreseeable extension threads:

- *Convergence with the "quantization amplifies CoT unfaithfulness" thread*: Turpin 2023's CoT-unfaithfulness line, combined with the quantization variable, is a natural next step
- *Practical tooling for per-use-case validation*: Wang already calls for "per-use-case validation"; the obvious next move is for someone to package this test suite into an SDK so deployers can run it with a single command
- *Training-time countermeasures*: Doi et al. 2025's "instilling faithfulness during training" thread raises the question of whether faithfulness gained in training survives quantization — a new problem waiting to be studied

If you encounter the latest developments along this thread, please update this river.

* Insights

Reading through this river, three things surface:

*1. The word "good" gets stripped of another layer of skin every five years*

In 2017: "good explanation = humans feel they understand." In 2020: wrong — "good explanation = truly reflects the model." In 2024: still not enough — "must also hold in prompt-based settings." In 2026: still not enough — "must also hold under quantized deployment." Each layer is correct, yet each leaks. The thread is not fundamentally about technical progress; it is about *evaluation granularity being forced finer and finer by reality*.

The next layer to be stripped may be: "good explanation = still holds under quantization + long context + multi-turn interaction + tool calls." When the next wave arrives, today's "good" will look coarse.

*2. Faithfulness is a "system property," not a "model property"*

The biggest conceptual shift happens in the Madsen → Wang step: previously, everyone treated faithfulness as an intrinsic model property — model A is more faithful than model B, full stop. But Wang reveals: the same model, at a different bit-width, yields a different faithfulness score. This means *"can you trust what this model says" must be answered at the level of the complete system — model + quantization + inference setting*. Saying "I'm using Llama 3 70B" is not enough; you have to say "I'm using Llama 3 70B + AWQ 4-bit."

*3. Explanation itself is "something that can be damaged by compression"*

This is the most counterintuitive point. Intuitively you might expect quantization to damage "model capability" — accuracy drops slightly, generation quality gets rougher. But Wang measures something subtler that quantization also damages: *the model's capacity to report accurately on its own internal state*. In other words, the model may still get the answer right, but the "reason" it gives for getting it right has already drifted.

This hints at something deeper: explanation ability and task ability are not the same thing; they are supported by different parts of the model's weights, and quantization damages them asymmetrically. If this observation is solidified, it will feed back into how we design training objectives for "interpretability-aware optimization."

* Implications

*1. Finding problems: look for the intersection between "already-mature tools" and "newly emerged real-world variables"*

Wang et al. did not invent any new method — the faithfulness tests were others', the quantization was others'. They were simply the first to twist the two threads together. This style of "intersection research" has extremely low cost and extremely high value.

This means you can: take a well-established evaluation tool from one domain and apply it to a new variable from another domain — you will almost certainly unearth something useful. Examples: "long context × bias," "multimodal × privacy," "tool calling × hallucination."

*2. Reading papers: looking at who a paper argues against is more efficient than looking at what it claims*

Reading this river in reverse, you find that the core contribution of every paper is hidden in "where the previous paper went wrong." Atanasova's method is essentially "Jacovi said something that cannot be operationalized; here is the method." Madsen's work is essentially "Atanasova tested fine-tuned models, not LLMs; here is the fix."

This means that when you read a new paper, you can jump straight to the related work section and find the prior paper it criticizes most sharply — that paper is basically the "parent paper." Once you understand the parent paper's limitation, you understand 80% of the new paper's value. The remaining 20% is execution details.

*3. Doing research: honestly admitting "per-use-case validation" is more valuable than pretending to have found a universal answer*

The most intellectually honest moment in the Wang paper is the line in the conclusion: "We recommend validating per use case." It does not pretend to have found a universal rule; it honestly acknowledges "our measurements show this does not generalize — each deployment scenario must be tested independently."

This means *the honest characterization of what you have found is itself part of the research contribution*. In a sea of "we achieved SOTA" papers, a paper that proves "this problem cannot be solved with a single method" is rarer and more memorable.
