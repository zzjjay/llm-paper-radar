* The Problem River

Can a small model actually learn how a large model *reasons* — not just copy the answers, but genuinely think?

Here is where the difficulty lies: a large model can produce a long chain of "first this, then that" thinking, but if you ask a small model to directly imitate that chain, it cannot manage — its capacity is simply insufficient, and forcing it in often disrupts what it already knows. For a decade, generation after generation of researchers has been adjusting the same fundamental act: how much should the teacher teach, how much should the student work out on its own, and exactly where should teaching happen? This timeline is the story of the teacher's hand slowly withdrawing.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Hinton Classic KD: Large Model Feeds Probability Distributions to the Small Model

*** What problem did it see?

In 2015 neural networks had just started to balloon. A large model could hit 90% on ImageNet but was far too heavy to run on a phone. How could you compress it into a small model without losing too much accuracy?

*** Its solution

Don't just tell the small model "this image is a cat" (hard label). Give it the large model's probability distribution over all classes: "90% cat, 5% leopard, 2% tiger, …" These soft labels encode the inter-class similarity that the large model has learned. The small model uses KL divergence to match that distribution.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

Soft labels carry far more information than hard labels — they tell the student "the answer is cat, but it looks more like a leopard or tiger than a car." Following this gradient, the student absorbs the teacher's implicit structural knowledge.

But the paradigm rests on a hidden assumption: *the student should align fully with the teacher's distribution*. For image classification this is harmless — the output space is just a few thousand classes. Move it to generative tasks — where the output is a long sequence of tokens and every step is a distribution over a vocabulary of tens of thousands — and the problem arrives.

** 2016 | Sequence-Level KD: Match the Whole Sequence, Not Each Token's Distribution

*** What problem did it see?

If you transplant Hinton's recipe into machine translation — making the student match the teacher's full vocabulary distribution at every target token position — the gradient is too scattered for the student to learn. Worse, at some positions the teacher may have multiple equally valid continuations ("I like" could be followed by "it" or "this"), and matching distributions token by token pushes the student toward a "blurry average" that loses all sharpness.

*** Its solution

Lift the unit of supervision from "token distribution" to "complete sequence." The teacher beam-searches a set of high-quality translations and uses those sequences as new training data for the student. The student is not matching the teacher's distribution — it is *fitting the optimal sequences the teacher selected*.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

A sequence-level objective is more focused — the student only learns "the path the teacher judged best" and is not confused by a crowd of equally valid candidates. This became the default recipe for generative KD for the following decade.

But — all the training samples the student ever saw were *written by the teacher*. Once the student begins generating on its own, a small deviation at step one compounds at step two on top of the already-drifted state, spiraling further and further off course — the classic *exposure bias*. The student learned to recognize words in "the teacher's handwriting" but was never corrected in "its own handwriting."

** 2023 | MiniLLM: Let the Student Write First; Teacher Corrects on the Student's Sentences

*** What problem did it see?

By the LLM era, exposure bias had become lethal. Models like GPT and LLaMA routinely produce hundreds or thousands of tokens, so a student trained on the teacher's "perfect samples" veers off the moment it opens its mouth in a real setting, because it was never supervised on its own output distribution.

A subtler issue: traditional KD uses *forward* KL (forcing the student's distribution to cover all of the teacher's modes), which demands that the small student imitate every possible output of the teacher — something it simply cannot do. The result is a blurry distribution that vaguely resembles everything and clearly resembles nothing.

*** Its solution

Two things change at once:
1. *On-policy* — let the student generate its own rollouts; the teacher provides feedback on *what the student itself wrote*.
2. *Reverse KL* — switch from "cover all of the teacher's modes" to "converge onto the teacher's high-probability modes," encouraging the student to produce *confident, sharp* outputs.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

On-policy training aligns the training distribution with the inference distribution — the student encounters the states it will actually produce, so corrections land where they matter. Reverse KL tells the student not to be greedy; focus on a few high-probability modes.

But reverse KL can cause *mode collapse* — the student becomes overly conservative and loses diversity. And the paradigm still faced the question: forward KL or reverse KL? That tension became the entry point for the next paper.

** 2024 | GKD: Slide Between Forward and Reverse KL; Student Learns on Its Own Samples

*** What problem did it see?

MiniLLM bet on reverse KL, but reverse KL collapses modes; forward KL scatters. Each is biased; neither is a silver bullet. What is needed is a loss that can be tuned anywhere between the two extremes.

*** Its solution

Use the *generalized Jensen-Shannon divergence* JSD(β) — at β=0 it reduces to forward KL, at β=1 to reverse KL, with arbitrary interpolation in between. Pair it with on-policy sampling: the student generates, the teacher computes JSD(β) on the student's outputs.

JSD(β)(P||Q) = β·KL(P||βP+(1-β)Q) + (1-β)·KL(Q||βP+(1-β)Q)

Turning a single β shifts from "mode covering" to "mode seeking."

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

GKD turns "which divergence to use" into a hyperparameter — elegant from an engineering standpoint. It became the de facto standard for on-policy KD.

Yet GKD's objective still reads: align the student's distribution with the teacher's at *every single token position*. The problem is — *the student is simply different from the teacher*. A university student's approach to solving a problem cannot possibly match a middle schooler's step for step; forcing the middle schooler to imitate the university student at every intermediate step also corrupts the methods it already had.

This is TSD-KD's true entry point: *why should the teacher exert pressure at every token?*

** 2026 | TSD-KD: Teacher Acts Only at Critical Points; Student Reasons in Its Own Words

*** What problem did it see?

The authors plot a figure (paper Fig. 1): they measure the per-token entropy of the student across a reasoning trace and find two facts:
1. *Entropy is highly non-uniform across tokens*: a small number of tokens carry very high entropy (the student is very uncertain here — these are "reasoning branch points"), while most tokens carry very low entropy (the student is confident — these are "routine steps").
2. *High-entropy tokens cluster at the beginning of the sequence*: the critical forks in reasoning occur in the first few dozen tokens; what follows is just following through.

Every prior method — from Hinton to GKD — fed every token equally into the loss. This is like a parent interrupting a child at every step of a problem, regardless of whether the child already knows that step. The result: the student loses its own ability to organize its reasoning and is forced into a clumsy imitation of the teacher's phrasing.

The deeper problem: *the student and the teacher simply reason at different levels*. The paper makes this explicit — "a university student and a middle schooler work through the same problem in genuinely different ways." Forcing the student to align with the teacher at every token is equivalent to forcing a change of cognitive style.

*** Its solution

Three components, all token-selective:

1. *Indirect distillation* (applied at the front of the sequence)

   Applied only to the "opener" — the short stretch of high-entropy tokens at the start of the sequence. The mechanism changes:

   - At each position the student *proposes* its own top-k candidate tokens.
   - The teacher does not force its own distribution; it only *ranks* the k candidates.
   - The student learns to "align my ranking with the teacher's ranking" — using the Plackett-Luce model (the same one used in DPO) to compute a ranking likelihood.

   Key shift: from "teacher speaks, student transcribes" to "student speaks, teacher critiques." The student reasons in *its own words*; the teacher serves only as a ranking oracle.

2. *Direct distillation* (selectively applied)

   KL divergence against the teacher's distribution is still used, but *only on specific tokens* — the selection criterion: tokens where the student is very uncertain (high entropy) but the teacher is very confident (low entropy).

   The logic: if both sides are low-entropy, no teaching needed; if the student is confident and the teacher is not, the teacher has no solid ground to teach from; if the student is uncertain and the teacher is confident, *that is exactly where teaching belongs*.

3. *Entropy regularization* (confidence floor)

   On those "teachable tokens," an additional term encourages the student's output entropy to *decrease* — preventing the situation where the teacher's intervention leaves the student even less confident than before.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

The fundamental shift here: *the teacher's hand withdraws from "pressing down on every move" to "pointing the way at a fork in the road."*

- Letting the student use its own words sidesteps the distortion of "a middle schooler mimicking a university student's phrasing."
- Token-selective distillation concentrates the student's limited learning budget where it actually matters.
- Ranking supervision is a weaker signal than distribution supervision, leaving the student room to explore.

Experimentally: state-of-the-art on 10 reasoning benchmarks, +54.4% over baseline, +40.3% over the second-best method, and on four tasks the student actually exceeds the teacher by 20.3% — that last figure is telling: when the teacher stops imposing itself, the student can evolve solutions better suited to its own capabilities.

Remaining holes: how to automatically choose opener length, how to dynamically tune the threshold for "teachable tokens," whether this recipe generalizes to non-reasoning tasks — all of these are the next round of open questions.

* Frontier Extensions

TSD-KD is a February 2026 paper; no direct follow-up work has appeared yet. But several parallel threads are visible from the same period:

- *Speculative KD* (Xu et al. 2025): borrows the speculative decoding idea — student proposes, teacher approves token sequences — a different implementation of the same core intuition as TSD-KD's "student proposes, teacher ranks."
- *Keypoint-based KD* (Feng et al. 2024): also asks "which tokens should we learn from," but selects via rule-based filtering rather than entropy filtering.
- *Unreasonable effectiveness of on-policy KD* (Agarwal et al. 2025): further demonstrates the superiority of the on-policy paradigm on reasoning tasks — one of TSD-KD's supporting arguments.

Predictable next steps: push the "student leads, teacher judges" relationship to its limit — e.g., let the student propose an entire reasoning trace, with the teacher feeding back only on whether the final answer is correct (which is essentially RL, the DeepSeek-R1 line); or teach the student to learn for itself when to ask the teacher.

* Insight

What is truly evolving along this river is not "which divergence to use" or "what data to train on" — it is the *power relationship between teacher and student*.

Every generation asks the same question: how much should the teacher control? The answer has retreated steadily from "control everything" to "control what matters." Behind this lies a fact that has been slowly coming into focus: *the student is not a shrunken copy of the teacher*. A 1.5B model is not a 1/50 replica of a 70B model — it has its own capability surface, its own uncertainty distribution, its own optimal solution paths. Forcing it to replicate the teacher's trajectory grinds away its own strengths.

TSD-KD marks an inflection point in this shift in understanding: for the first time, *the student's own confusion distribution* is placed at the center of the loss function. It says — teach where the student is lost; let the student handle what it already knows. This sounds like an obvious principle from pedagogy, but ten years of KD literature defaulted to the assumption that the teacher should be present at every step.

Where does this go next? Two directions are already faintly visible:
1. *Teacher exits the process entirely and evaluates only the outcome* — this is what the RLHF/GRPO line is doing; a different road to the same destination.
2. *Student learns to self-distill* — no teacher required at all; the model trains on its own high-quality outputs (self-rewarding, self-improvement).

The ultimate destination of the knowledge distillation field may be to dilute the role of "teacher" until it disappears entirely.

* Practical Takeaways

If you are working on "small model learning from large model" — whether LLM distillation, RAG fine-tuning, or any student-teacher setup:

- *Measure the student's entropy distribution first.* Run a pass on your task and see where the loss actually lands. You will almost certainly find that 80% of the loss comes from 20% of the tokens; the rest is noise.
- *Do not apply pressure on every token.* Selective losses (filtered by entropy, filtered by teacher-student entropy gap) almost universally outperform uniform loss.
- *Downgrade "distribution matching" to "ranking matching."* When the student's capacity is far below the teacher's, hard distribution matching is harmful; weak ranking supervision preserves the student's own expressive ability.
- *Trust the student.* If the student is already confident at a given token, do not "correct" it with the teacher's distribution — you are probably just corrupting something it already had right.

A broader transfer: in any "expert guiding a novice" setting — writing coaching, code review, teaching — the lesson from this KD timeline is consistent: *the more finely the teacher controls, the more shallowly the student learns.*
