* The River of the Problem

Can the weights of an LLM be compressed to just 2 bits per value — while the model still performs as well as the original?

Here is what makes this hard: a 7B model in FP16 needs 14 GB of VRAM; at 2 bits it would theoretically fit in 1.75 GB, small enough for a phone. But once you drop below 2 bits, the model basically falls apart — perplexity explodes and inference becomes incoherent. For a decade, generation after generation of researchers has run into the same wall: "2 bits is the line between life and death."

What makes it even more painful is that the field offers two roads, and both are blocked. The first is *post-training quantization* (PTQ) — take a trained FP16 model and quantize it after the fact. Cheap, but it collapses below 3 bits. The second is *training a low-bit model from scratch* (the extreme QAT variant, e.g. BitNet) — model quality holds up, but starting from zero costs millions of dollars, which is out of reach for most practitioners.

This research river is the story of successive waves of people trying to break through that 2-bit wall — and ideally without forcing everyone to retrain from scratch.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022–2023 | GPTQ / AWQ: The Promise and Ceiling of Post-Training Quantization

*** What Problem It Saw

By 2022, LLMs had scaled to hundreds of billions of parameters and deployment costs were exploding. The most direct idea: keep FP16 for training, then quantize to INT8 or INT4 at deployment. GPTQ optimized each layer's weights independently, minimizing the output error after quantization — at 4 bits the quality loss was nearly invisible, and the field celebrated.

*** Its Solution

Partition the weight matrix into column groups, quantize column by column, and use the Hessian matrix to guide error compensation — quantization error in earlier columns is absorbed by later ones. AWQ went further: it discovered that a small fraction of "salient weights" determines model quality, and gave those weights special treatment.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Pit)

4 bits still provides 16 slots, enough to cover most of a weight distribution. But — LLM weight distributions are *heavy-tailed*: the vast majority cluster near zero, while a small number are very large. When you compress to 2 bits, only 4 slots remain, and no matter how you place them, critical information is lost. Fairy2i's paper puts it sharply: "Finite discrete codebooks in the real domain cannot adequately approximate the heavy-tailed distribution of LLM weights."

By 3-bit GPTQ was already struggling; at 2 bits it collapsed — perplexity jumping from 6 to above 10. PTQ hit a dead end at the 2-bit wall.

** 2023-10 | BitNet: If You Can't Compress After Training, Train Compressed

*** What Problem It Saw

The fundamental problem with PTQ: during FP16 training the model has no idea it will later be compressed to 1 bit, so it stores information "however it likes." Trying to rescue that information after the fact is bound to fail.

*** Its Solution

BitLinear — a drop-in replacement for ~nn.Linear~ whose weights are binarized to {-1, +1} by a sign function during the forward pass, with gradients flowing through via a straight-through estimator during backpropagation. The entire Transformer is trained end-to-end; the weights are *inherently* 1-bit from birth.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Pit)

The model *learns* to allocate information under a 1-bit constraint during training. The results were striking: a 1.3B BitNet approached the quality of a same-size FP16 model.

But the new pit was glaring: you must train this model *from scratch*. Llama-2, Mistral, Qwen — every existing pretrained asset is rendered useless. Training a 7B model from zero costs millions of dollars; the community simply cannot keep up.

** 2024-02 | BitNet b1.58: Add a Zero, Close the Gap with FP16

*** What Problem It Saw

Pure binary {-1, +1} is missing something: an *"off" channel*. Every weight must be one thing or the other, with no ability to say "I don't need this feature." That rigid topology limits expressiveness, and at larger scales it cannot match FP16.

*** Its Solution

Add one value: {-1, 0, +1}. Each weight averages log₂(3) ≈ 1.58 bits. The extra zero lets the model "sparsely select features."

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Pit)

The impact was stunning — BitNet b1.58 at the 3B scale became the first model to match FP16 Llama. The slogan "the era of 1-bit LLMs" was coined in this paper.

But two pits remained. First, *training from scratch is still required* — the pretrained-model ecosystem is still out of reach. Second, the 2-bit encoding space has 4 slots, but ternary only uses 3, *wasting an entire slot*. That gap gave the next wave of researchers their opening.

** 2025-08 | iFairy: Move the Battlefield to the Complex Domain

*** What Problem It Saw

The iFairy authors fixed their gaze on that "wasted fourth slot." If you want to fill all 4 codewords of a 2-bit budget in the real domain, your choices look like {-2, -1, 0, 1} or {-1.5, -0.5, 0.5, 1.5} — either asymmetric, or requiring genuine multiplication. There is no way in the real domain to construct a 4-symbol codebook that is *both symmetric and multiplication-free*.

The deeper critique: every QAT paper silently assumed "FP16 is the ceiling." Nobody asked whether that ceiling itself could be raised.

*** Its Solution

Rewrite the entire Transformer in *complex* arithmetic. Weights are no longer real numbers but points in the complex plane. The quantization codebook is chosen to be the *fourth roots of unity*: {+1, +i, -1, -i} — the four vertices of a square on the unit circle.

This codebook has three elegant properties:
1. *Perfect symmetry*: the four points are equally spaced in angle — information-theoretically optimal
2. *2-bit fully utilized*: four codewords cover all 4 states of a 2-bit budget
3. *Multiplication-free*: every codeword has at least one zero in its real or imaginary part, so multiplication reduces to *addition, sign flipping, and real-imaginary swapping*

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Pit)

Complex weights carry not just magnitude but also *phase*. Under the same 2-bit budget, a complex model is fundamentally more expressive than a real one. iFairy outperformed same-bit-count real binary/ternary models at the 3B scale.

But the pit it left was in the same vein as BitNet's — and arguably worse: *you must train a complex Transformer from scratch*. And because a complex Transformer is an entirely new architecture, there are no pretrained weights anywhere to borrow from. The community faced an awkward choice: either abandon the entire Llama ecosystem and train a complex model from zero, or keep muddling along in the real domain.

** 2025-12 | Fairy2i: "Translate" Real Pretrained Weights into Complex

*** What Problem It Saw

The Fairy2i authors stated iFairy's pain point directly: "A major bottleneck of iFairy and similar approaches is their incompatibility with existing real-valued pretrained models. They generally require training a complex model from scratch, which is impractical for the vast majority of practitioners."

The problem was precisely located: iFairy's codebook is correct, but the path to that codebook is blocked. Can we find a shortcut that *avoids retraining*?

*** Its Solution

Three steps, each one solving a specific bottleneck:

*Step 1 · Lossless Real → Complex Translation (Wide-Linear Reparameterization)*

The authors prove a mathematical equivalence: any real-valued linear mapping can be *exactly* emulated by a "wide-linear mapping" in the complex domain: y = Ux + W·x̄ (where x̄ is the conjugate of x). Given the four blocks R₁₁, R₁₂, R₂₁, R₂₂ of an original real weight matrix R, they provide a closed-form solution for how to compute U and W from those four blocks. Theorem 1 guarantees the transformation is *exact and unique*.

Intuition: take a 4096×4096 real matrix, view it as a 2048×2048 complex matrix plus a conjugate term, and the result is mathematically identical to the original. Every piece of knowledge in the pretrained model is translated into the complex domain without retraining a single parameter.

*Step 2 · Phase-Aware Quantization (PhaseQuant)*

Each complex weight is projected to the nearest point in {±1, ±i} based on its angle in the complex plane. Two axis-aligned scale factors — one for the real axis, one for the imaginary — restore the magnitude. During training, full-precision master weights are kept and STE lets gradients pass through.

*Step 3 · Recursive Residual Quantization*

One round of quantization leaves too much error? *Quantize the residual again.* Formally: R⁽ᵗ⁺¹⁾ = R⁽ᵗ⁾ − Ŵ⁽ᵗ⁾, then quantize again with {±1, ±i}. The final weight is the sum of T low-bit terms. At T=2 this yields an effective 2-bit representation.

Key insight: these T terms are *input-independent* and can be computed in parallel. Latency over T is nearly O(1) — adding more terms does not slow inference proportionally.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Pit)

The three steps interlock: Step 1 lets you *use* existing pretrained assets; Step 2 ensures *storage* is genuinely low-bit (each codeword is essentially ±1 or ±i — all 0/±1 integers); Step 3 ensures *accuracy* can be recovered. The result on LLaMA-2 7B: FP16 C4 perplexity is 6.63, Fairy2i-W2 (2-bit) is 7.85; the same-budget AQLM scores 8.54, QuIP# scores 11.01; even 3-bit GPTQ only reaches 10.61. A 2-bit method beating a 3-bit strong baseline.

The new pits are also visible. First, although the T residual terms can be computed in parallel, storage is T× — at T=2 you are effectively storing 2 bits per real parameter. Second, the GPU kernels for complex operators have not fully caught up; the advertised multiplication-free property requires custom LUT kernels to actually materialize. Third, validation so far is limited to the LLaMA-2 7B family; whether larger scales and other architectures (MoE, long-context) hold up remains open.

* Frontier Extensions

This is work from December 2025; as of the current date (2026-05) no paper specifically critiquing or improving Fairy2i has appeared publicly. But along this line, several directions are already in motion:

- *Microsoft BitNet v2 / MiniCPM4*: the real-domain camp continues pushing forward, attempting larger scales and longer contexts at 1.58-bit, racing the complex-domain camp
- *Hardware*: LUT-based CPU kernels such as BitNet.cpp and T-MAC are already making 1.58/2-bit inference on CPUs faster than GPU FP16; complex-domain kernels are still catching up
- *Higher-order complex quantization*: the natural extension is finer complex codebooks (e.g. eighth roots of unity), trading 3 bits for higher precision

Fairy2i can be said to have pushed the combination of "complex low-bit + practical usability" to the threshold of industrial applicability for the first time. But whether the complex or real road ultimately dominates is still being fought.

* Insights

Reading this line of work reveals three layers:

*Layer 1 (surface)*: bit-width walks from 16 → 4 → 2 → 1.58 → 2 (complex), always compressing further. Each generation does the same job with fewer bits.

*Layer 2 (mechanism)*: What is actually happening is not "compression" but a *change of representational medium*. FP16 uses continuous real numbers; BitNet retreats to discrete real numbers; iFairy moves the battlefield to the complex plane. "Complex numbers" sounds like a detour, but it is actually trading *dimensionality* for *codebook granularity* — under the same bit budget, in two dimensions (the complex plane) you can arrange a codebook that is more symmetric, more efficient, and multiplication-free. This is a victory of geometry over algebra.

*Layer 3 (path dependence)*: A technically correct solution is not the same as a winning solution. iFairy is mathematically beautiful but lost on the ecosystem — because "retrain from scratch" is a threshold the vast majority of practitioners cannot cross. Fairy2i's real contribution is not inventing a new codebook, but *building the bridge that connects an already-excellent codebook to the existing ecosystem*. Research outcomes often depend not on "who is newer" but on "who can actually be used."

Where to look next? Three directions are worth watching:
1. *1-bit complex* — if 2-bit complex works this well, can 1-bit complex (only {+1, -1} but exploiting phase) retreat one step further?
2. *Hardware co-design* — once complex LUT has native support on NPUs, the power-efficiency gap between FP16 and 2-bit complex may open up by 10×
3. *Cross-architecture transfer* — can Fairy2i's "mathematical translation" idea be applied to MoE, Mamba, or Diffusion models? That is a much larger vein to mine

* Takeaways

This line of work offers three concrete lessons for researchers:

1. *Don't just watch algorithmic accuracy — watch ecosystem friction.* iFairy appeared four months before Fairy2i, yet the community remembers Fairy2i. The reason is not technical; it is "usability." The next time you evaluate an idea, ask first: "What is the switching cost for existing users?" This means, *before writing a paper, ask whether it can appear as a drop-in patch for existing systems.*

2. *Changing dimensions beats squeezing dimensions.* While everyone was fighting over the last bit on the real axis, iFairy moved the battlefield to the complex plane and immediately gained a symmetric codebook. When a path looks exhausted, the answer is rarely "optimize a little more" — it is usually *change the coordinate system*. This means, *when you face a seemingly saturated optimization problem, actively ask: can I go up one dimension?*

3. *Mathematical equivalence is the cheapest bridge.* Fairy2i's entire "translation" step is a zero-training-cost mathematical transformation, yet it unlocks the entire pretrained-model ecosystem. The strongest bridges are often not newly built, but are found by *proving that two apparently different things are actually the same*. This means, *finding a mathematical isomorphism between two incompatible worlds is one of the highest-leverage moves in research.*
