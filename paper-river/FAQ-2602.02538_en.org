* One-liner

For five years, practitioners compressing large models to INT4 have made decisions staring only at "this one layer." FAQ lets them peek a few layers downstream before pulling the trigger — for the first time.

SmoothQuant found that slow and instead mathematically migrated the monsters to the weight side.
AWQ felt neither predecessor could distinguish the importance of individual weights, and introduced "protect only the top 1% critical weights."
FAQ's complaint about AWQ: when AWQ judges which weights are critical, it looks only at the current layer — by the time downstream layers actually use those weights, the wrong ones have already been crushed.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** Act I: Who Is the Monster? (LLM.int8, 2022)

Before this story begins, quantization was considered a solved problem. Swap FP16 weights for INT8, and the model runs fine. Then large language models arrived.

In 2022, Tim Dettmers noticed something strange: quantize OPT-175B to INT8 and the model collapses — comprehensively, with several percentage points of accuracy simply evaporating. He examined the activation values and found monsters lurking inside: the activations in certain channels were *100× larger* than everything else.

Stuffing such data into INT8's 256 bins, the monster alone saturates the entire range, squashing the remaining 99% of normal values into the first bin. All resolution is gone.

LLM.int8's fix is blunt: identify the monsters and *leave them in FP16*. Everything well-behaved goes to INT8. Mixed precision.

The problem left behind: anyone who has run a mixed-precision kernel knows that splitting, merging, and decision logic all add overhead. LLM.int8 is accurate, but actually slower than FP16.

** Act II: Move the Monster (SmoothQuant, 2022)

SmoothQuant appeared the same year. Its authors are the same group who would later write AWQ. Their reaction to LLM.int8: "You were right to identify the monsters, but you shouldn't exile them to FP16 — you should tame them before quantizing."

Observation: weights are well-behaved, roughly Gaussian, and quantize without pressure. Activations are the monsters.
Approach: multiply each channel by a scaling factor s to shrink the activation; simultaneously multiply the corresponding weight by 1/s, leaving the matrix product unchanged. This effectively migrates the quantization difficulty from activations onto the weight side.

With activations now tame, the whole model runs in INT8. No longer slower than FP16.

The problem left behind: W8A8 is fine, but pushing toward W4 (4-bit weights, more aggressive compression) means the weight side has already absorbed SmoothQuant's blow — taking a second hit down to 4-bit starts to break things. A more refined approach is needed.

** Act III: Not All Weights Are Equal (AWQ, 2023)

AWQ's insight: only *1% of weights are critical*; the remaining 99% can be compressed however you like.

How to find that 1%? Don't look at the weights themselves — look at the activations. Whichever input channels have large activation magnitudes correspond to important weights, because their contribution to the output is large.

Once the critical weights are identified, multiply them individually by a scale to enlarge them, reduce the quantization error, then divide back out. The entire network runs in INT4 — no mixed precision, no FP16 outliers, blazingly fast.

AWQ received the MLSys 2024 Best Paper award and became the de facto standard for 4-bit quantization.

The problem FAQ comes to poke at: when AWQ judges "which channels in this layer are important," it looks *only at this layer's* activations.

** Act IV: The Current Layer Is Not Enough (FAQ, 2026)

The FAQ authors argue that AWQ's judgment has two flaws:

*Bias*: a channel that looks unimportant by this layer's standard may be exactly what a downstream layer depends on. Compress it flat based on the current layer's criterion, and downstream inference breaks.

*Accumulation*: every layer makes a locally optimal decision, and errors propagate forward layer by layer. When layer 30 collapses, the damage may have been sown back at layer 3.

On top of that, the small calibration dataset AWQ uses to "read activations" can diverge from the real deployment distribution, skewing the decisions further.

FAQ's fix: when quantizing layer i, *peek at the activations of the next 3 downstream layers*. Blend the current layer's activations with the future layers' activations at a ratio of 0.85:0.15, and use this blended signal to compute the scale.

In terms of the equation, going from AWQ to FAQ, exactly one thing changes:
- AWQ: scale is computed from ~a_i~
- FAQ: scale is computed from ~0.85*a_i + 0.05*(a_{i+1}+a_{i+2}+a_{i+3})~

That is all. No new framework, no training, no gradients. Just a few extra forward passes during calibration to collect downstream activations.

Why a window instead of a single future layer? To avoid overfitting to one particular layer. A window average is more stable.

Why not greedily search for the best γ and window size per model? Too expensive. The authors search once, land on γ=0.85 and window=3, and apply it universally across all models.

* Core Insight

On the surface this lineage is a sequence of quantization techniques; at a deeper level it is the *expansion of the judgment signal*.

LLM.int8 judges by activation magnitude.
SmoothQuant judges by activation distribution.
AWQ judges by ranked activation magnitudes.
FAQ judges by a joint signal across the current and future layers.

Each step asks the same underlying question: *Does the local decision I make right now actually align with what downstream layers will want?*

After four years, the quantization community has finally acknowledged something neural networks have always known: a network is end-to-end. A single layer's decision has never belonged to that layer alone.

* Where Things Might Go Next

FAQ handles looking forward; nobody has yet handled looking backward — perhaps the next paper will use *bidirectional* activation information (past j layers + future j layers).

More aggressively: if you are going to look downstream anyway, why not use the gradient of the downstream loss with respect to the current layer's scale? But that leads straight back to the backward pass that FAQ deliberately avoids. OmniQuant went down that road; FAQ's selling point is "I can beat you without gradients." So the next step will most likely not return to gradient-based methods, but instead keep mining *cross-layer signals using only forward passes*.

Another direction: FAQ assumes all layers share the same γ=0.85 and window=3. But shallow and deep layers, attention blocks and FFN blocks, have different degrees of dependence on the future. A follow-up paper may derive a *layer-adaptive* γ.
