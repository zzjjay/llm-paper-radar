* The Problem River

One sentence: *How do you compress a large model to store every number in just 4 bits without making it dumb?*

This sounds like simple frugality — memory is expensive, so use less. But the devil is in the details. A model contains billions of numbers (weights), and inference produces a massive stream of intermediate results (activations). Originally each number is stored in 16 bits; now you want to slash that to 4 bits — like compressing a ruler's scale from tens of thousands of graduations down to just 16. Most numbers cluster tightly in the middle, but there are always a few "squatters" — abnormally large values (outliers) — and the moment they appear, they blow the ruler's range wide open, squashing all those middle values onto the same graduation and destroying their information.

This river has swallowed generation after generation of researchers because the "squatter" enemy keeps shapeshifting:

- Early on, people only quantized weights; squatters were manageable because weights are static and visible.
- Later, activations had to be quantized too (only then can you truly save memory and gain speed), and squatters suddenly multiplied and grew fiercer — and became dynamic, changing with each input.
- Just when the community had finally tamed squatters in traditional left-to-right, token-by-token autoregressive LLMs, *a fundamentally new language model architecture — the Diffusion LLM (DLLM) — appeared, and every old trick stopped working.*

This is exactly where STaR-Quant stands: in a Diffusion LLM, squatters are not only spatially bizarre but also jump around across time. This is a new form that nobody has directly tackled before.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022 | GPTQ: Making One-Shot Quantization Work at Billion-Parameter Scale for the First Time

*** What Problem It Saw

Before GPTQ, compressing a trained model meant either fine-tuning on data all over again (expensive and slow), or applying post-training quantization (PTQ — compress without retraining) and accepting terrible accuracy. When models reached 100B+, existing PTQ methods simply collapsed — the compressed model became incoherent gibberish.

*** Its Solution

GPTQ's core intuition: quantizing one weight introduces an error, so let the *remaining unquantized weights* actively "lean" a little to cancel that error. Like pulling the heaviest book from a stack — it throws the whole stack off balance, so you nudge the neighboring books to restore equilibrium. It uses something called the Hessian (second-order information) to precisely calculate "which direction and how much to lean," and applies a mathematical trick (Cholesky decomposition) to make this compensation tractable at hundred-billion scale — a 175B model compressed in 4 GPU hours.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It works because it stops quantizing each weight in isolation and instead lets the whole layer share the error burden. But it only touches *weights* — activations remain high-precision. Yet saving memory is just the first step: to genuinely speed up inference and save more, you need to *quantize activations too*. And inside activations lurk outliers far more vicious than anything in weights. That passes the ball to the next paper.

** 2023 | SmoothQuant: Can't Compress Activations? "Migrate" the Difficulty to Weights

*** What Problem It Saw

The moment you try to quantize activations jointly (W8A8 — both weights and activations at 8 bits), accuracy collapses. The reason: activation outliers with values hundreds of times larger than normal blow up the quantization range. Weights, by contrast, are well-behaved and easy to compress. One side is immovable, the other has plenty of slack — can they trade burdens?

*** Its Solution

SmoothQuant's trick is as elegant as a magic act: in the matrix multiplication `Y = X·W`, dividing a channel of activation X by a scalar s and multiplying the corresponding channel of weight W by s leaves Y *completely unchanged*. So it "divides down" the activation outliers, migrating their difficulty onto the weights. Activations become smooth and compressible; weights become slightly harder to compress but had margin to spare. The difficulty moves from the side that can't bear it to the side that can.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It works because it exploits the asymmetry — weights are easy, activations are hard — and performs a zero-cost difficulty transfer. But what should s be? SmoothQuant chooses it empirically and statistically, which is coarse. Which weights are truly the critical pressure points? It doesn't give a precise answer. That's what AWQ supplies.

** 2023 | AWQ: Not All Weights Matter — Find the 1% That Do

*** What Problem It Saw

SmoothQuant migrates difficulty to weights uniformly. But AWQ discovers that only about *1% of weights are truly salient* — disturbing them collapses accuracy, while the other 99% can be compressed freely. Even more subtly: the way to identify which weights are critical is not to look at how large the weights themselves are, but at how large *the activations multiplied against them* are.

*** Its Solution

AWQ follows the activation distribution to locate the 1% of weight channels paired with large activations, and applies a protective scaling specifically to those channels so they suffer less during quantization. And it does this *without back-propagation or reconstruction* — pure statistics — so it doesn't overfit to calibration data and stays robust across tasks. On this front it is both more stable than GPTQ and about 1.45× faster.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It works because it concentrates "protection resources" precisely where they matter most. But both SmoothQuant and AWQ are fundamentally about *scaling* — flattening outliers. After flattening, the outliers are still there, just shorter. At the more aggressive 4-bit level (only 16 graduations on the ruler), flattening is no longer enough — you need the outliers to *vanish entirely*.

** 2024 | QuaRot: Stop Flattening Outliers — Scatter Them Instead

*** What Problem It Saw

To achieve genuine all-4-bit quantization (W4A4 — activations and KV cache compressed to 4 bits too), scaling-based methods fall short. Flattened outliers remain concentrated in a few channels, and a 4-bit ruler is too short to accommodate them.

*** Its Solution

QuaRot takes a different angle: apply a *Hadamard rotation* to the activation vectors. The magic of rotation is that it *spreads the energy* originally concentrated in one or two channels *evenly across all channels*. What was a spike piercing through the paper becomes a uniform gentle bump across the whole surface — nobody sticks out. And this rotation can be "folded" into the weights, adding no extra computation at inference time, allowing every matrix multiplication to run in pure INT4.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It works because it exploits computational invariance — rotation doesn't change the model's output but redistributes numerical values. But QuaRot's rotation handles "ordinary" outliers well; for *massive outliers* with magnitudes exceeding 100, extremely concentrated, a single rotation can't spread them thin enough.

** 2024 | DuQuant: Rotation + Zigzag Permutation, Purpose-Built for "Massive Outliers"

*** What Problem It Saw

DuQuant identifies a hard case QuaRot can't handle: *massive outliers* with magnitude greater than 100, concentrated specifically in certain FFN layers, appearing only on some tokens. A single rotation hands their energy to neighbors — but the neighbors can't absorb it either.

*** Its Solution

A two-stage transformation: first, *block-wise rotation* to distribute the massive outlier's energy to neighboring channels; then *zigzag permutation* — reordering channels so that the load across blocks is balanced and no single block gets saturated with outliers; finally, one more rotation to smooth things out. The effect is "rotate to scatter + reshuffle + scatter again," spreading massive outliers everywhere so nothing dominates.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It works because it acknowledges that one transformation isn't enough and treats outliers with divide-and-conquer. But — and this is critical — from GPTQ to DuQuant, *everyone has been working on the same kind of language model: autoregressive LLMs that produce one token at a time, left to right*. Their activation distributions have outliers, but those outliers are at least "one-shot" and structurally stable. What happens when a fundamentally new model architecture appears?

** 2025 | DLLMQuant: Every Old Trick Fails Simultaneously on Diffusion LLMs

*** What Problem It Saw

Diffusion LLMs (DLLMs — represented by LLaDA and Dream) are a different species. Instead of writing text left to right, they work like developing a photograph: you start with an entire sentence, half of it blanked out ([MASK] tokens), then *iteratively denoise*, filling blanks into real words step by step, potentially filling several at once while attending bidirectionally to context.

Applying quantization methods designed for autoregressive LLMs (e.g., AWQ) directly, DLLMQuant measured *AWQ dropping 16 points on LLaDA W4A4*. It diagnosed three root causes: (1) token distributions differ across denoising steps, and traditional calibration captures only a single snapshot; (2) quantization errors *accumulate and amplify step by step* through iterative denoising; (3) already-filled "unmasked tokens" are stable while still-pending "masked tokens" are probabilistic and drifting — mixing the two in one quantization pass produces an awkward distribution that fits neither.

*** Its Solution

DLLMQuant is the first DLLM-specific quantization framework, prescribing three targeted remedies: *TMAS* (calibration sampling across time steps + mask states, ensuring coverage of different moments and token states), *IA-AQ* (using bidirectional attention signals to allocate quantization resources), and *CGQ* (folding mask state and token determinacy scores into error compensation).

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It works because it is the first to directly confront the two unique dimensions of DLLMs — *token state* (masked vs. unmasked) and *time* (denoising step). But its handling of these two dimensions is still not clean enough: masked and unmasked tokens are treated differently in calibration and compensation, yet *they are still compressed into the same quantization transformation space* — even though their outlier patterns look quite different. Forcing them into one space means neither can be smoothed properly. And for "error accumulation along the time axis," there is no dedicated, explicit interception mechanism. Those two gaps are exactly where STaR-Quant begins.

** 2026 | STaR-Quant: Separate Spatial Compression, Intercept Temporal Error

*** What Problem It Saw

STaR-Quant makes two precise cuts in the DLLM quantization problem, corresponding to two "dimensions":

1. *State dimension (spatial)*: Within a single denoising step, masked tokens (uncertain positions awaiting prediction) and unmasked tokens (known context) have *clearly different* activation distributions and outlier patterns (illustrated clearly in the paper's Fig. 1). Using *one* unified quantization space for both is like asking a single ruler to simultaneously accommodate two completely different numerical distributions — neither gets smoothed properly. DLLMQuant differentiated between them in calibration and compensation, but ultimately shared one transformation space.
2. *Time dimension*: Denoising is iterative — the output of step t feeds into step t+1. Quantization error is not a one-off event; it *snowballs along the denoising trajectory*. Prior analysis (Xu & Yang) points to the step where softmax weights multiply values inside attention as the most error-prone at low bit-width and the most likely relay point for propagating errors forward.

*** Its Solution

Two modules, one for each cut:

- *SGAT (State-Guided Activation Transformation)* — treats the spatial dimension. It splits the hidden dimension into *three subspaces*: one "shared" (used by both masked and unmasked tokens), one "masked-exclusive," and one "unmasked-exclusive." Masked tokens pass through the "shared + masked-exclusive" transformation; unmasked tokens pass through "shared + unmasked-exclusive." Each group's outliers are absorbed and smoothed in their own dedicated subspace without interfering with the other. Key insight: *the activation side splits into two paths, but the weight side retains only one unified transformation* — otherwise you'd have to store a separate transformed weight for each token type, doubling inference overhead. The inspiration comes from FreeAct (Liu et al., 2026), which proposes making activation transformations state-conditioned rather than uniform.
- *TAC (Temporal Attention Compensation)* — treats the time dimension. Rather than modifying the attention computation itself, TAC inserts a *lightweight block-wise affine correction* (block-wise affine: divide into small blocks, each multiplied by a small matrix M_b and added a bias d_b) on the quantized attention representation, just before it enters the output projection. Compensation parameters are solved in closed form by "aligning the first- and second-order statistics (mean and covariance) of the full-precision and quantized versions," with singular-value smoothing and shrinkage toward the identity matrix to prevent amplifying noisy directions. This amounts to, at every critical node where errors would snowball, "nudging" the quantized representation back close to its full-precision counterpart at each step.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It works because it addresses the two DLLM-specific dimensions — state and time — each with a dedicated module, and does so lightly: SGAT does not double the weight footprint, TAC inserts only a single correction before the attention output projection. Ablations confirm the two modules are complementary: removing TAC drops the average score from 57.07 to 55.43; removing SGAT drops it to 56.39. In final W4A4 benchmarks on LLaDA-8B / LLaDA-1.5-8B / Dream-7B, average scores all exceed DLLMQuant (+2.78 / +2.62 / +1.69 respectively), while achieving up to 1.69× speedup and 3.14× memory reduction.

Pits dug for the future: (1) TAC's compensation parameters estimate covariances from a small calibration set — when blocks are too large (g=32/64) the covariance estimate degrades and accuracy drops, leaving an unresolved tension between calibration data volume and compensation capacity; (2) the entire approach stays at 4 bits; the more aggressive 2-bit regime is untouched; (3) the subspace partitioning in SGAT (how much to allocate to shared / masked / unmasked) uses a fixed split that may not be optimal.

* Frontier Extensions

As of the time of writing, STaR-Quant (2026-06) is a very recent entry on the "Diffusion LLM quantization" branch, and *no explicit follow-up that critiques or improves it has been identified yet* (honest caveat: it is so new that it may not yet have been cited). But several roughly contemporaneous papers on the same problem sketch the river's next likely course:

- *Quant-dLLM* (arXiv:2510.03274, ICLR 2026) — pushes DLLM quantization to the *extreme of 2 bits*, using masked calibration simulation (MCS), data-aware arbitrary-order quantizers (DAQ), and adaptive block-wise mixed precision (ABMP). This lands squarely on the "still stuck at 4 bits" pit that STaR-Quant left open.
- *Quantization Meets dLLMs* (arXiv:2508.14896, v3 updated 2026-03) — the first systematic PTQ benchmark for DLLMs, evaluating GPTQ / AWQ / SmoothQuant / QuaRot / DuQuant on DLLM models side by side, independently confirming that "W4A4 on DLLMs remains difficult due to activation outliers" and providing a public common yardstick for this research line.
- *NPU/hardware-oriented approaches for diffusion LM inference* (e.g., early-2026 work on KV quantization + block-wise online smoothing BAOS) — address "inter-step distribution drift" from the hardware side, echoing STaR-Quant's temporal diagnosis from a chip rather than an algorithm perspective.

(Relationship note: the above three papers are *parallel / roughly contemporaneous* with STaR-Quant, not downstream critiques of it. Please do not read them as direct successors.)

* Insight

Reading the whole line together reveals a recurring *cognitive upgrade pattern*: every generation's breakthrough comes from seeing the structure of the enemy (outliers / errors) *one layer more finely than the generation before*.

- GPTQ saw that errors *can be transferred* (weights cancel each other).
- SmoothQuant saw that difficulty *can be transferred across tensors* (the activation ↔ weight asymmetry).
- AWQ saw that importance *is uneven* (the 1% pressure points).
- QuaRot / DuQuant saw that outliers *can be spatially redistributed* (rotation / permutation), not just flattened.
- DLLMQuant saw that DLLMs introduce *two entirely new structural dimensions* (token state, denoising time).
- STaR-Quant goes one step further and sees that these two dimensions *should not be handled together* — the spatial dimension needs "separate-path compression" (SGAT), the temporal dimension needs "step-by-step interception" (TAC), each with its own tool.

So what this river is really carrying is not "ever more elaborate quantization algorithms," but *ever sharper dissection of the structure of errors and outliers*. Every advance is, at its core, discovering a dimension that "was previously lumped together but should actually be handled separately." STaR-Quant's name, "State-Time," announces this directly — its contribution is to formally disentangle the "state" axis from the "time" axis.

Where does the river most likely flow next? (1) *Lower bit-widths*: 2-bit is already appearing in Quant-dLLM; going from 4→2 will force new structural discoveries; (2) *Adaptive partitioning*: SGAT currently uses a fixed subspace split and TAC a fixed block size — the next step will almost certainly make these *automatically adapt per layer and per denoising step*; (3) *Quantization-aware training for DLLMs* (QAT for DLLM) — baking "compressibility" into DLLM pretraining as an objective, rather than patching after the fact.

* Takeaway

The biggest lesson this evolutionary line offers for "how to do research and how to find problems": *truly good problems are usually hiding inside the sentence "everyone assumed these two things are the same — they're actually not."*

- SmoothQuant's insight: activations and weights are not the same. AWQ's insight: 1% of weights and the other 99% are not the same. STaR-Quant's insight: masked tokens and unmasked tokens are not the same; different denoising steps are not the same. Every paper's core is *splitting apart something that was being handled as one.*
- An actionable heuristic for finding new topics: take a mature method from a mature field, feed it a *newly appeared architecture / data modality / scenario*, and stare hard at where it breaks down — the breakage point is the dimension that was "assumed identical but is actually different." DLLMQuant did exactly this: feed AWQ to LLaDA, stare at the 16-point collapse, and find DLLM's special structure.
- The payoff lies in usefulness: this means *you don't need to invent fundamentally new mathematics* to do consequential work — you only need to find a dimension where "predecessors cooked everything in one pot but it should have been separate dishes," then equip each dish with a suitable, preferably lightweight tool. (STaR-Quant's SGAT doesn't double the weights; TAC inserts just one correction — both deliberately control overhead.) *Identifying the right splitting dimension + equipping each dimension with a lightweight tool* is itself a reproducible research paradigm.
