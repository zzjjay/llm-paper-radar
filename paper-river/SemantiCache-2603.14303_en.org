* The River of the Problem

Every time an LLM generates a new token, it must consult a "memory" of every
preceding token — that memory is the KV cache. The longer the context, the
larger the cache, the more GPU memory explodes, and the slower inference
becomes. The question is: can we compress this memory so the model stays
lean without becoming dumb?

Why is this hard? Every token in that memory *might* be needed by some future
attention computation. Drop any one of them and you may drop the crux of a
sentence. That is why this research thread, since 2023, has been caught in
one central tension: the harder you compress, the more performance degrades.
A dozen papers have been wrestling with the same trade-off — what makes a
token "truly important," and how do you safely discard or merge the less
important ones without reconstructing the original text.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Transformer + KV Cache: the origin of the problem

*** What problem did it see

The attention mechanism requires every new token to compute similarity against
all historical tokens. Recomputing the Keys and Values of every history token
from scratch at each step would be an O(n^2) disaster.

*** Its solution

Cache the already-computed K and V, and reuse them at every subsequent step.
That is where KV cache comes from.

*** Why it worked (and what new hole it buried)

It reduced each generation step from O(n) recomputation to O(n) cache reads,
eliminating redundant computation. But the cache itself must live in GPU
memory permanently and grows linearly with context length — tolerable at 8K,
but it starts bursting GPUs at 32K or 128K. The starting point for the entire
research thread is: can this ever-growing cache be put on a diet?

** 2023 | H2O: "just kick out the unimportant tokens"

*** What problem did it see

In practice, the vast majority of tokens receive near-zero weight in
subsequent attention distributions — they sit in the cache doing nothing but
taking up space. So why not keep only the genuinely "important" minority and
discard the rest?

*** Its solution

Define a simple metric: cumulative attention score — the total attention mass
each token has received across all past queries. High-scoring tokens are
"heavy hitters" and are retained; low-scoring tokens are evicted. The cache
budget is fixed, and rank determines who stays and who goes.

*** Why it worked (and what new hole it buried)

The intuition is sound — sparse attention is the normal regime for
transformers. But H2O's fatal flaw is *irreversibility*: once a token is
evicted, its information is gone forever. If a later query suddenly needs it,
the model can only hallucinate. This became the scar that every subsequent
eviction-based method had to live with.

** 2024 | StreamingLLM: "the first few tokens are sacred"

*** What problem did it see

H2O mysteriously crashes during long streaming inference. On investigation,
researchers found that models assign anomalously high attention to the very
first tokens — even when those tokens carry no meaningful content. The reason
is that softmax must allocate weight somewhere; when nothing is genuinely
important it dumps the mass onto the beginning. This is *attention sink*.

*** Its solution

Simple and blunt: always keep the first few sink tokens, add a sliding window
over the most recent N tokens, and discard everything in between.

*** Why it worked (and what new hole it buried)

The model no longer crashes and can stream over infinitely long sequences.
The cost is that the entire middle context vanishes — feed it a 50-page
document and ask about page 20, and it can no longer see page 20. This
exposed the fundamental limit of "drop by position": importance and position
are not the same thing.

** 2024 | SnapKV: "what to drop depends on what will be asked"

*** What problem did it see

Both H2O and StreamingLLM are *task-agnostic* — they have no idea what the
user will ask next. But in practice, the same history has different important
tokens depending on the question.

*** Its solution

Introduce an "observation window" — treat a few tokens at the end of the
prompt as a proxy for the question, use them to score history tokens, and
decide what to keep based on those scores. What gets retained is precisely
what is useful for the current query.

*** Why it worked (and what new hole it buried)

Performance improves noticeably because a task signal is now available. But
the paradigm is unchanged: still eviction, still at single-token granularity.
Evicted tokens are still dead. That opening is where the CaM school enters.

** 2024 | CaM: "don't kick — merge"

*** What problem did it see

Eviction always risks evicting the wrong token. Since every token still
carries some information, could we *avoid losing it* by folding its content
into a token that is being kept?

*** Its solution

For tokens selected for eviction, compute their attention similarity to
retained tokens and merge their K/V into those retained tokens using weighted
summation. What was formerly "deletion" becomes "folding."

*** Why it worked (and what new hole it buried)

Information no longer drops off a cliff, and long-context tasks are
noticeably more stable than H2O-style methods. But CaM still decides merge
targets at single-token granularity — a modifier inside a noun phrase might
get folded into a completely unrelated token, scrambling grammatical
relationships. SepLLM targets exactly this: the idea of *boundaries*.

** 2025 | SepLLM: "punctuation is a natural summary slot"

*** What problem did it see

Looking at attention heat maps reveals a peculiar pattern: commas, periods,
newlines — tokens that appear meaningless — consistently receive
disproportionately high attention scores. They behave like paragraph-level
summary points that the model learned on its own.

*** Its solution

Exploit this observation: compress each run of non-separator tokens into the
separator token that immediately follows it, letting the separator serve as
a "condensed version of this segment." At inference time, only the
separator's K/V are kept; all others are discarded.

*** Why it worked (and what new hole it buried)

For the first time, "semantic structure" entered KV compression. Using
separators as anchors is more respectful of natural language units than
dropping by position or by token. But the granularity is still too coarse:
cramming an entire segment into a single token flattens all internal
structure — multiple parallel clauses, multiple entities — in one stroke.
That is precisely the gap SemantiCache steps into.

** 2026.03 | SemantiCache: "compress along semantic hierarchy, not token-by-token"

*** What problem did it see

Surveying all prior methods together, they share the same mistake: either
they operate at single-token granularity (H2O / SnapKV / CaM), or at
arbitrary fixed-length chunks (approaches like MoBA, Native Sparse Attention),
or they are too coarse and cram a whole segment into one token (SepLLM).
All of this is *semantic fragmentation* — splitting a modifier from its noun,
severing a conditional clause from its main clause.

*** Its solution

Import the way humans remember long texts and turn it into a three-stage
pipeline:

1. *Semantic Chunking.* Borrow SepLLM's insight and use separators as
   natural semantic boundaries to split the sequence into chunks. The
   separator tokens' own K/V are kept intact; only the content inside each
   chunk is compressed.
2. *Greedy Seed-Based Clustering (GSC).* Within each chunk, pick the first
   unassigned token as a seed and absorb every subsequent token whose cosine
   similarity to that seed exceeds threshold τ into the same cluster. This
   is a single-pass O(l^2/2) lightweight clustering that requires no
   iterative refinement the way k-means does.
3. *Cluster Merging + Proportional Attention.* Each cluster is mean-pooled
   into one "semantic core." But merging a cluster of |S| tokens into one
   dilutes its influence in attention — so a log(|S|) term is added to the
   logits before softmax, equivalent to multiplying the attention score by
   cluster size. In other words, "this core represents 5 tokens" — so after
   softmax it receives 5× the weight.

*** Why it worked (and what new holes remain)

The three pieces fit together tightly:
- *Splitting* does not break semantic boundaries (inherits SepLLM but
  preserves intra-chunk detail)
- *Merging* does not throw away redundancy (inherits CaM but at finer
  semantic granularity)
- *Reweighting* prevents merging from diluting influence (this is new —
  no prior work explicitly addressed "how many tokens should a core speak for
  in the attention formula")

Empirically, decoding reaches 2.61× speedup while LongBench / NIAH
performance approaches full-cache quality.

Remaining holes: (a) threshold τ is fixed — the optimal value likely differs
across layers and heads; (b) GSC's "first unassigned token as seed" is
order-sensitive — reordering the sequence changes the clustering outcome;
(c) the method relies on separators, which makes it awkward for languages
or code that lack explicit punctuation.

* Frontier Extensions

In the wake of SemantiCache (concurrent with 2026 Q1-Q2), the same line is
already branching:

- *AdmTree* (Li et al., 2026): expands SemantiCache's "one-level clustering
  within chunks" into an *adaptive semantic tree* — multi-level aggregation
  where each level's granularity is chosen automatically. This directly
  answers the unresolved question of "what should τ be": replace a single
  threshold with a hierarchy.

- *COMI: Coarse-to-fine Context Compression* (Tang et al., 2026): selects
  which tokens to retain through the lens of marginal information gain,
  providing a more information-theoretic basis than a cosine-similarity
  threshold.

- *"Read as Human"* (Tang et al., 2026): formally encodes human reading
  strategies — close reading (careful attention to key segments) + skimming
  (quick reading of the rest) running in parallel. This is a more systematic
  realization of SemantiCache's motivating intuition of "mimicking human
  memory."

The entire thread has shifted from "how to lose as little as possible" toward
"how to compress along the hierarchy of human cognition."

* Insight

Compressed to one sentence: *The history of KV cache compression is the
history of realigning the "unit of compression" from "arbitrary token" to
"natural language unit," one step at a time.*

Looking one level deeper, what we see is researchers iterating over the very
definition of "importance":
- Generation 1: *attention score* as importance → but the model's own
  attention distribution is biased (the sink phenomenon).
- Generation 2: inject *task/query signal* → but still a binary decision at
  token level.
- Generation 3: shift the frame to *merge instead of delete* →
  information conservation, but the unit is still wrong.
- Generation 4: accept that *language itself has structure* → let the
  compression unit align to that structure (separator chunks, semantic
  clusters).
- Then the inevitable new question: if merging dilutes information, what
  then? Answer: add log(|S|) reweighting before softmax — an elegantly
  small operation that writes "how many tokens this core represents" directly
  into the attention formula.

Where might this go next? Two directions seem likely:
1. *Learn τ* — let the model learn the optimal clustering granularity for
   each layer and each head, replacing hand-tuning. AdmTree has already
   opened that path.
2. *Compression-retrieval integration* — SemantiCache is still a
   "compress once and done" approach. The natural next step is to fuse it
   with long-context retrieval: if a query later needs the fine-grained
   detail inside a merged core, it can look up the original K/V on demand.
   This is effectively adding a cache-of-the-cache layer on top of
   compression.

* Takeaways

Lessons for how to find good problems:

1. *Pushing along the same trade-off curve is the wrong direction.* The first
   three generations of methods all refined "how much to drop / how to
   select" — and the performance-memory curve flattened. The real
   breakthrough is SemantiCache's *change of coordinates*: stop asking "which
   token is best to discard" and ask "what should the minimal unit of
   compression be."

2. *The phenomena others find too messy to examine are often the gold mine.*
   SepLLM exists because someone was willing to seriously ask "why do
   punctuation tokens attract such high attention" — a question many would
   have dismissed as a model artifact and moved on. A stable anomaly that
   everyone overlooks is frequently the exact pivot point for the next
   solution.

3. *Aligning to the natural structure of the domain is almost always the
   terminal direction.* The endpoint of this thread — and the core argument
   of SemantiCache — is that natural language is not a sequence of tokens
   but a hierarchical structure, and compression should not work against that
   structure. Any work that brings a domain's natural structure into its
   algorithm is pointed in the right direction; the only question is timing
   and execution.

Practically speaking: the next time you tackle a "drop / compress / select"
problem, pause and ask — *is the unit of operation I am using the domain's
natural unit, or just the convenient engineering unit?* If it is the latter,
there is a SemantiCache waiting to be written right there.
