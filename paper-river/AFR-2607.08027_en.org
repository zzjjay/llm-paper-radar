* The River of the Problem

How do you decide which parameters in a pretrained large model can be deleted and which cannot?

The question sounds simple, but it conceals a tension: the model's pretrained knowledge must be protected, yet downstream tasks demand targeted adaptation. Generation after generation of methods have circled the same question — "what signal tells us whether a parameter matters?" — starting with raw training gradients, then discovering that gradients are useless for pretrained models and switching to whether features are preserved, then combining both signals, and finally realising that forcing this per-weight scoring logic onto structured pruning (deleting entire neurons) causes the signals to fight and cancel each other. This river is the story of how "importance signals" were wrung into shape, twisted, and corrected all the way down.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1990s | OBD / OBS: Let Mathematics Tell You Which Deletion Hurts Least

*** What Problem It Saw

Early pruning simply deleted the smallest weights — pure intuition, no theoretical grounding. It frequently deleted the wrong things, causing accuracy to drop in baffling ways.

*** Its Solution

LeCun's Optimal Brain Damage and Hassibi's Optimal Brain Surgeon approached the problem rigorously: since the goal is to minimise the change in the loss function, use a second-order Taylor expansion to compute each weight's true contribution to the loss (via the Hessian matrix rather than the weight's magnitude).

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because it was the first time "deletion cost" was formalised mathematically — no more guessing. But the pit was fatal: the Hessian is quadratic in the number of weights, so even moderately large networks made it intractable, and pruning had to be embedded in a slow iterative "prune a little, retrain a little" loop.

** 2015 | Magnitude Pruning (Han et al.): Crude but Genuinely Useful

*** What Problem It Saw

The Hessian is too expensive; it cannot be applied to networks at realistic scales.

*** Its Solution

Abandon the precise second-order information and simply use weight magnitude as importance — a small weight has a small effect on the output, so deleting it should not matter.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked surprisingly well and for a time became the de facto standard for pruning, later extended to RNNs. But two burdens never went away: the network must be trained to convergence first (pretraining dependence), and the "prune a little, retrain a little" iteration must be repeated many times (heuristic scheduling that must be re-tuned for every new architecture).

** 2019 | SNIP (Lee et al.): Prune Once Before Training Begins

*** What Problem It Saw

The cost of iterative prune-retrain cycles comes mainly from the repetition — every pruning round requires a retraining round, and every architecture change means redoing the entire schedule.

*** Its Solution

SNIP reframed the question: instead of waiting for training to converge before judging importance, why not — before training starts — score each connection by its "sensitivity to the loss" in a single pass? One forward-backward pass computes the product of each weight and its gradient; low-sensitivity connections are pruned immediately, and then normal training proceeds from start to finish.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because it completely decoupled "pruning" from "training": no pretraining required, no iterative retraining, no architecture-specific schedules — one shot, very lightweight.

But a new pit appeared in the pretrained-model era: SNIP's assumption is that "the network starts from random initialisation," where gradient signals are meaningful. Pretrained models are different — they have already converged to a good point, and the optimal solution for a downstream task is typically nearby. At this point most weight gradients are nearly zero (∂L/∂θ ≈ 0), so SNIP's sensitivity signal degrades to noise and can no longer identify the truly important parameters.

** 2024 | ReFer (Nitta et al., MIRU): Don't Look at Gradients — Look at Whether Features Are Preserved

*** What Problem It Saw

SNIP and similar foresight-pruning methods all bet on "gradients still carry signal," but for pretrained models that bet is wrong — at a converged point, gradients carry very little information and noise dominates.

*** Its Solution

ReFer switched to an entirely different measurement target: instead of asking "is this weight sensitive to the loss?" it asks "if this weight is deleted, how much does this layer's output feature change?" Concretely, it applies SVD (singular value decomposition) to each layer's output features and measures each weight's influence on the singular values, preferring to retain weights whose removal would cause large structural changes in the feature space. This directly protects "the feature representations learned during pretraining" — the more essential thing — rather than going the roundabout way through gradients.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because it bypassed the fundamental obstacle of "distorted gradients in pretrained models" and directly measured the thing that matters most to downstream performance — the feature space itself.

But the pit is symmetric: ReFer is entirely focused on protecting pretrained knowledge and completely ignores whether the current downstream task requires adaptation. When the task is hard and the model needs substantial adaptation, the tighter ReFer's protection, the less the model can learn, and performance drops sharply.

** 2025 | AFR (Nitta et al., MIRU): Two Signals, Each Covering Its Own Ground

*** What Problem It Saw

SNIP understands tasks but not pretrained-knowledge protection; ReFer understands knowledge protection but not task adaptation — the two methods are precisely complementary, and neither should be discarded.

*** Its Solution

AFR (Adaptive Feature Retention) normalises both scores (z-score) and adds them directly: the ReFer term handles "will deleting this weight damage the geometric structure of the features learned during pretraining?" and the SNIP term handles "how sensitive is this weight to the current downstream task's loss?" Normalisation ensures the two scores are on the same scale so neither dominates due to differing numerical ranges.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because it expressed the two originally opposing goals — "protect knowledge" and "adapt to task" — simultaneously in a single scoring formula, balancing both sides during per-weight pruning.

But a new pit appeared at the step from "per-weight" to "structured" pruning: AFR was designed from the start to score and delete individual weights (unstructured pruning). Unstructured pruning achieves high accuracy but creates a scattered sparsity pattern that cannot be accelerated on real hardware. What industry actually wants is structured pruning that removes entire neurons or channels — but structured pruning requires aggregating the scores of all weights within a neuron into a single score, a scenario AFR's formula was never designed for.

** 2026 | This Paper (Kobayashi et al.): Three Pits Erupt Simultaneously When AFR Scores Are Aggregated to the Neuron Level

*** What Problem It Saw

The authors tried the most naive approach — taking the absolute value of each weight's AFR score within a neuron and averaging — and found accuracy fell off a cliff (on Llama-3-8B at 50% pruning ratio, from unstructured AFR's 60.62 down to naive averaging's 52.25, even below many off-the-shelf structured pruning methods). Digging deeper, three problems were simultaneously at work:

1. *Mismatched distributions*: ReFer scores span a wide range; SNIP scores, because pretrained-model gradients are universally near zero, are crammed into a small interval near [0, 1]. The normalisation in the formula is a linear stretch, which for SNIP's densely packed small values amplifies noise after stretching, polluting ReFer's otherwise clean signal.
2. *Direction information discarded*: Weight scores carry signs, and the sign encodes "was this weight increasing or decreasing during optimisation?" For structured pruning, if all weights in a neuron move in the same direction, the neuron has a unified role and makes a structural contribution; if directions are mixed, the weights cancel each other and the contribution is limited. But taking absolute values before averaging erases this "directional consistency" information at the very first step.
3. *Outliers dominate the average*: Plotting the score distribution within a single layer, the authors found that the vast majority of scores are packed into a very narrow interval, yet extreme outliers exist at both ends — the range spans from −2884 to 9803, but after removing the most extreme 2% it drops to just 48.8. Ordinary averaging is extremely sensitive to outliers; the presence or absence of a few such points can directly reverse the importance ranking of two neurons.

*** Its Solution

Three targeted fixes, applied in sequence:

1. *Power transformation*: Apply x^b (b > 1; the empirically optimal value is 2.7) to SNIP scores. This transformation strongly suppresses values near 0 (x^b ≪ x) while leaving values near 1 largely unchanged (x^b ≈ x). It effectively applies different amplification factors to the "noise-dense region" and the "signal region" — noise is pushed down, signal is preserved, and subsequent normalisation is no longer hijacked by noise.
2. *Average first, then take absolute value*: Reverse the order of operations in the formula from "absolute value → average" to "(signed) average → absolute value." If the weights in a neuron are directionally consistent, the signed average remains large in magnitude; if directions cancel, the average tends toward zero. In this way, "directional consistency" is encoded directly in the magnitude of the aggregated score, without needing a separate metric.
3. *Quantile-based outlier removal*: For the score set of each neuron, retain only values between the 2nd and 98th percentiles before aggregating. The authors conducted a dedicated statistical analysis to determine the 2% threshold — kurtosis drops 94.2% going from the 1%–2% range, but only a further 54.9% going from 2%–3%, indicating that 2% already removes most of the outliers' destructive effect while further trimming starts to cut into the central 50% of the data (IQR), constituting information loss rather than denoising.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

The shared logic behind all three fixes is: stop treating "aggregation" as a mindless arithmetic mean, and instead first align the distributions of the two score types, preserve the directional semantics of weights, and isolate anomalies before aggregating — the aggregation step itself is unchanged; what changes is the quality of the data fed into it. The result is that structured pruning at a 50% pruning ratio loses only 0.32 points compared to unstructured AFR, in exchange for a genuine 1.57× inference speedup.

The remaining pits, which the authors themselves acknowledge in their conclusion:
(1) Only FFN layers are handled; attention modules have not yet been included.
(2) The pruning ratio is set globally and uniformly, without adaptive allocation based on per-layer importance.
(3) The power-transformation exponent b = 2.7 was found through systematic ablation and is purely empirical; no theoretical explanation is given for why this specific value is optimal.
(4) For vision-language models like LLaVA, the visual encoder has no downstream task loss to draw on, so the method degrades to pure ReFer (losing the SNIP half). The question of "what to do when a signal is missing" is not truly solved in this paper — it is simply sidestepped.

* Insight

On the surface this line looks like "pruning signals growing ever more complex" (Hessian → weight magnitude → gradient → feature geometry → fusion → purified fusion), but what is truly changing is a deeper cognitive shift: *the criterion for pruning has never been singular — it is continuously redefined as the constraints accumulate: "is the model pretrained?", "does the downstream task matter?", "can the result be hardware-accelerated?"*

- SNIP assumes "training from scratch"; once that assumption breaks (pretrained models), the entire signal is distorted.
- ReFer corrects that assumption but overcorrects, ignoring task adaptation entirely.
- AFR stitches together two complementary signals that had never been considered simultaneously, at the cost of introducing a new question: how to fuse them.
- This paper discovers that "how to fuse" re-erupts when the application scenario shifts (from per-weight to per-neuron), and it does so through three symptoms that look unrelated but share a common root (distribution mismatch, direction loss, outlier leverage) — the same fusion formula, applied at a different granularity, reveals completely different pitfalls.

This suggests a general pattern: *a method that works at one granularity is not guaranteed to work at a coarser granularity.* The step from "per-weight" to "per-neuron" looks like merely adding an aggregation step, but that aggregation amplifies every statistical flaw that was previously hidden (misaligned distributions, discarded signs, outlier leverage). The most likely next step is to systematise the "distribution alignment + sign preservation + outlier removal" purification pipeline and apply it to other cross-granularity aggregation scenarios — such as the extension to attention-head-level structured pruning that this paper itself identifies.

* Takeaways

This evolutionary line most resembles a cycle of "signals moving from simple to composite, and composite signals requiring refinement": every time someone stitches two signals together to solve one problem, the seam becomes the source of the next problem.

Implications for how to do research and how to find problems:
- *Switch the application scenario and re-examine the hidden assumptions of old formulas.* AFR's normalise-and-sum formula is fine in the per-weight setting, but when moved to neuron-level aggregation, the seemingly innocuous implementation detail of "take absolute value then average" suddenly becomes a performance bottleneck. Good research topics are often hiding in "take a validated method into a new scenario and discover an assumption that was never tested."
- *Statistical diagnosis before algorithmic design.* Before proposing any fix, the authors plot score histograms, compute kurtosis, compute SNR, and perform quantile analysis — pinning down the "lesion" with data visualisation first, then choosing the appropriate tool (power transformation, sign preservation, quantile clipping) to treat it. This "diagnose first, then prescribe" order makes it far easier to explain why the fix works than simply stacking a more complex model on top.
- *Leave a graceful degradation path rather than pretending the problem does not exist.* For the LLaVA visual encoder's lack of downstream task loss, the authors do not fabricate a proxy gradient — they honestly degrade to "ReFer only." Acknowledging that a method has limited capability in certain sub-scenarios is more honest than forcing it to appear "complete" in all scenarios, and it makes it much easier for subsequent work to precisely locate and improve upon the limitation.
