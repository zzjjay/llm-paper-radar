* The Problem River

A 70-billion-parameter model starts life at 16 bits per weight. To fit it on your laptop, you need to compress it to 2 or 3 bits — a 5× to 8× reduction. The question is: after all that squeezing, does it still understand language?

That is the fight *post-training quantization (PTQ)* has been picking with humanity for thirty years. The difficulty is not the word "compression" — any high-schooler can round a pile of floats to the nearest grid point. The hard part is *which grid points to choose and which weight lands on which point*, so that the model's outputs barely change. This is a *discrete optimization* problem: you must make billions of "which bin does this weight fall into" decisions simultaneously, and gradient descent — our most familiar hammer — is completely blind to discrete choices.

Every generation has wrestled with that discreteness. In 1993 someone said "use the Hessian matrix to decide which weights matter most" — that line of thinking ran straight through to GPTQ in 2022, scaling it up to large models. But it hit a wall: scalar quantization (each weight handled independently) collapsed in accuracy below 3–4 bits. So another camp switched to *vector quantization* — packing weights into groups and looking them up in a codebook — which recovered accuracy at the cost of engineering complexity and hardware unfriendliness.

GSQ (2026) turns back and asks: wait, is scalar quantization fundamentally limited, or have we simply *not optimized it properly*? The answer is surprising: hand the discrete assignment over to Gumbel-Softmax — a differentiable sampling trick invented a decade ago for entirely different purposes — and scalar quantization can match vector methods.

What this river has really been evolving is not the "compression ratio" but *how humanity tames discrete optimization*.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1993 | OBS: Let the Hessian Decide Which Weights to Cut

*** What Problem It Saw

People in the 1990s already noticed that neural networks were "over-parameterized" — many weights are redundant, and pruning some of them does not hurt accuracy. But *which ones to prune* had no good answer. The most naive approach is "prune the smallest-magnitude weights," but that ignores a crucial fact: a small-magnitude weight might sit exactly on a sensitive direction of the loss surface, and removing it could crash the whole model.

*** Its Solution

Hassibi and Stork argued: you need to compute precisely "how much does the loss rise if we remove weight i?" Under a second-order Taylor expansion, that cost equals w_i² / (2 · [H⁻¹]_ii) — the denominator is the diagonal entry of the inverse Hessian. A weight's "cost" depends not only on its own magnitude but also on how steep the loss surface is in that direction.

After pruning one weight, you must *compensate* — redistribute that perturbation along the Hessian's eigendirections to the remaining weights so the loss moves as little as possible.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfalls It Left)

The intuition: you are not solving "which weight is small" but "in which direction is the loss surface least sensitive." The Hessian is the local curvature of the loss surface.

The pitfall left behind: the inverse Hessian requires O(n²) storage and O(n³) inversion. A 1993 network had a few thousand weights — manageable. A 2022 network has hundreds of billions — not a chance. For thirty years, the theory was locked inside small networks.

** 2022 | OBQ: Swap "Pruning" for "Rounding"

*** What Problem It Saw

Pruning sets a weight to zero — a special kind of discrete choice. Quantization replaces a weight with the nearest grid point (e.g., {-1, 0, +1}) — also a discrete choice. Structurally they are identical: you make one discrete decision per weight, and afterward the loss should move as little as possible.

But before 2022 nobody unified them. Pruning followed its own path (OBS, Magnitude Pruner); quantization followed its own (plain round-to-nearest plus some patching).

*** Its Solution

Frantar and Alistarh generalized the OBS cost formula: replace "change weight i to 0" with "change weight i to its nearest grid point q_i." The cost formula changes from w_i² / (2·[H⁻¹]_ii) to (w_i - q_i)² / (2·[H⁻¹]_ii). Everything else is inherited: choose the lowest-cost weight first, then compensate the others.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfalls It Left)

The elegance is unification: two separate problems are now two special cases of one. Results on vision models are excellent — ResNet quantized to 4 bits with almost no loss.

New pitfall: the Hessian is still O(n²). For ResNet (tens of millions of parameters) it is barely feasible; for GPT-3 (175 billion parameters) it is unthinkable. The largest model OBQ's paper tested was BERT-base — three orders of magnitude smaller.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Making OBQ Run on 175 Billion Parameters

*** What Problem It Saw

OBQ had two engineering bottlenecks that prevented it from running on LLMs: (1) after quantizing each weight, it had to recompute "which weight has the lowest cost next" — O(n) selections each costing O(n), totaling O(n²) selection overhead; (2) updating the inverse Hessian required O(n²) storage.

*** Its Solution

Frantar et al. made three clever moves:

1. *Fixed order.* Instead of "always pick the lowest-cost weight next," scan columns left to right. The paper proves that for each row of a weight matrix, column-order quantization is nearly as good as greedy selection — because the Hessian entries are identical within a row.
2. *Block-wise updates.* Pre-compute the inverse Hessian updates via Cholesky decomposition and apply them lazily in blocks rather than after every single weight.
3. *Row-parallel execution.* A weight matrix has thousands of rows, each quantized independently — fully parallelizable.

With these three tricks, OPT-175B is quantized on a single A100 in *4 hours* at 4 bits with almost no accuracy loss.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfalls It Left)

GPTQ became the de facto standard for LLM quantization immediately upon release. Combined with AWQ ("protect the top 1% outlier weights, freely quantize the rest") it dominated 2023–2024.

But it has a *ceiling*: at 3 bits and 2 bits, accuracy drops off a cliff. GSQ's paper makes this vivid — above 4 bits GPTQ is nearly indistinguishable from the original, but below 3 bits scalar quantization collapses universally. That "error wall" at 4 bits became the starting point for the next generation.

** 2023–2024 | The Second Wave (QTIP / AQLM / GPTVQ): Abandon Scalars, Build Codebooks

*** What Problem It Saw

The fundamental difficulty of 3-bit scalar quantization: each weight can only pick from 8 values (2³=8). No matter how cleverly you choose those 8 values, the one-dimensional "resolution" is fixed. To achieve higher fidelity you must *pack multiple weights together* — for example, 8 weights as a single 24-dimensional vector, selecting the nearest entry from a 256-entry codebook — so that 256 points cover a 24-dimensional space far more finely than 8 points cover 1 dimension. This is *vector quantization*.

*** Its Solution

- *AQLM* (Egiazarian 2024): additive vector quantization — multiple codebooks summed to represent one vector.
- *QTIP* (Tseng 2024): trellis-coded (Viterbi-style) method that makes the codebook structured and lookup cheap.
- *GPTVQ* (van Baalen 2024): transplants GPTQ's column-scan idea onto vector quantization.

Accuracy breakthrough: QTIP at 2 bits nearly matches 4-bit GPTQ accuracy.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfalls It Left)

The intuition: high-dimensional weight distributions have structure (some directions correlated, some sparse); vector quantization exploits that structure in ways one-dimensional quantization cannot.

The new pitfalls come in two layers:

1. *Engineering.* Vector decoding kernels are extremely hard to write — every token inference requires a codebook lookup and decoding step. Few people have written them to run fast on GPU, and hardware vendors are reluctant to support them. GSQ's paper quotes Tseng himself: QTIP provides *almost no speedup* over BF16 (even though memory is saved) because decoding consumes the bandwidth.
2. *Deployment.* The open-source ecosystem (llama.cpp, Ollama) has grown up around the scalar GGUF K-Quant format, serving millions of local-inference users who only speak that format. Vector methods require an entirely separate stack — nobody wants to switch.

The field was split in two: accuracy or deployability? Enter GSQ.

** 2026 | GSQ: Scalar Quantization Strikes Back — Discrete Choices Made Differentiable

*** What Problem It Saw

The GSQ team asked a question nobody wanted to ask: *Is scalar quantization hitting a wall in principle, or have we simply not optimized it properly?*

Looking back at GPTQ: its core operation is "column scan + Hessian compensation," but it *never truly learned which grid point each weight should occupy* — it rounds to the nearest grid point and then applies a continuous compensation. In other words, GPTQ mixes the discrete decision (which bin) with the continuous decision (scale factor, compensation), and the discrete part is never "optimized" at all — it is simply rounded.

What if we *explicitly optimized* the discrete assignment?

*** Its Solution

Borrow a tool from the other side of deep learning, circa 2017: *Gumbel-Softmax*. This is a trick that makes "choose one item from a finite set" approximately differentiable — assign each candidate a trainable logit, add Gumbel noise, pass through softmax, with temperature τ controlling softness. At high τ the selection is soft (gradients flow through); as τ anneals toward zero it collapses to a hard one-hot.

GSQ assigns a logit to every candidate grid point for every weight coordinate, and jointly with each group's scale, softens the discrete assignment via Gumbel-Softmax and minimizes layer reconstruction error end-to-end. Temperature is annealed during training; at convergence the soft selection collapses to hard discrete values.

A key engineering detail: at low bit-widths (2–3 bits) there are only 4–8 candidate grid points, so the logit overhead is small and the full assignment can be softened *globally*. At 5–8 bits the candidate count explodes, so the authors switch to a *local-shift* formulation — only allowing ±2 grid-point shifts around the GPTQ initialization.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfalls It Left)

The intuition: GPTQ's 3-bit wall is not due to insufficient information in a scalar representation — it is due to never truly optimizing the discrete assignment. When grid assignment, scale, and compensation are all *jointly* optimized in a differentiable manner, scalar quantization can match vector methods.

Experimentally, for Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct at 2 bits, GSQ outperforms the strongest scalar baseline (EfficientQAT) by 4.76 points on average zero-shot accuracy and trails QTIP by only 1.33 points — while using *symmetric scalar quantization with no zero-point*, fully compatible with existing GGUF/llama.cpp kernels.

More importantly, GSQ can take an existing GGUF K-Quant model as its starting point, optimize it, and write the result back to the same K-Quant format — meaning users change nothing and quantization quality improves automatically. On Qwen3-8B Q2_K, the average score rises from 50.03 to 56.28.

Remaining pitfalls:

1. *Training cost.* The Gumbel-Softmax logit parameters are 2–5× the count of quantized weights; joint whole-model optimization is still too expensive for trillion-parameter MoE models — currently GSQ on Kimi-K2.5 proceeds block by block.
2. *Objective function.* GSQ currently minimizes layer reconstruction MSE, but differentiability in principle allows direct optimization of task loss. That path has not yet been explored.
3. *Hardware acceleration.* Kernel compatibility is an advantage, but it also means there is no room to further exploit hardware — the next question is whether new hardware primitives should be designed for learnable discrete representations.

* Frontier Extensions

GSQ v1 was posted in April 2026 (v2 revised May 15, 2026) — just one month ago as of this writing. A search finds no published papers explicitly criticizing GSQ or building directly on top of it; the latest station on this river is GSQ itself.

Directions one can reasonably anticipate (based on hints in the paper's limitations section):

1. *Task-aware quantization.* Replace the Gumbel-Softmax optimization target from layer-reconstruction MSE to downstream task loss (or even RLHF reward), so that quantization decisions "know" what the model ultimately needs to do.
2. *Joint quantization with KV cache / activations.* GSQ currently quantizes only weights; KV cache and activations remain BF16. Extending the same differentiable discrete optimization to activations and KV cache could halve memory again.
3. *MoE expert-awareness.* The paper already applies GSQ to Kimi-K2.5, but uses the same strategy for every expert. A natural next step is assigning different bit-budgets or different grid shapes to different experts.

* Insights

*The central pattern of this river: every generation fights "discreteness," but with different strategies.*

- *Evade discreteness* (1993–2022, OBS → GPTQ): Accept that discrete choices cannot be optimized; use second-order expansion to estimate costs and greedily rank them — disguising a discrete problem as a continuous "sort by cost" problem.
- *Route around discreteness* (2024, QTIP/AQLM): Replace 1D discrete choices with ND vector choices. The problem is no easier; the higher dimension just makes continuous approximation more accurate — still fundamentally evasion.
- *Confront discreteness head-on* (2026, GSQ): Use Gumbel-Softmax to directly render discrete choices differentiable. This is a tool already validated in another corner of deep learning (variational inference, neural architecture search, MaskLLM), but nobody thought to carry it into LLM quantization.

*Second observation: engineering compatibility matters more than theoretical accuracy.*

QTIP's accuracy is unbeatable, but the open-source ecosystem won't adopt it — because the llama.cpp / Ollama / GGUF infrastructure has already taken root. GSQ's true killer feature is not "scalars can match vectors" but "I can improve your existing GGUF model in place, without changing its format." This is a recurring pattern in engineered systems: *new methods must be able to parasitize the old ecosystem*, disrupting from within rather than demanding a clean break.

*Third observation: old tool + new context = breakthrough.*

Gumbel-Softmax was not invented in 2026 — it has existed since 2017, originally for discrete latent variables in variational autoencoders. Over nine years it matured in NAS and MaskLLM, yet the LLM quantization community consistently ignored it, because GPTQ's success had locked in a local paradigm. Breakthroughs tend to come from *cross-domain tool transfer*, not from invention within the same field.

*Where is this most likely to go next?*

If GSQ's core insight is "discrete choices can be made differentiable," then this way of thinking will spread to other discrete problems in LLM post-training: pruning (already addressed by MaskLLM), KV cache quantization (not yet systematically tackled), MoE expert routing (naturally discrete), and speculative decoding draft-model selection (more speculative still).

* Takeaways

*For "finding problems":* When a field splits into two camps each claiming the other is wrong, a third path is usually waiting to be discovered.

The scalar-vs-vector quantization debate ran for two years, and the community largely accepted the narrative that "scalar hits a wall, vector takes over" — until GSQ stood up and asked: "Did scalar really hit a wall, or did you just not try hard enough?" That willingness to *question consensus*, combined with one overlooked old tool (Gumbel-Softmax), broke the deadlock.

*For "doing research":* When implementing a method, don't let "engineering convenience" calcify into a principled constraint.

GPTQ uses round-to-nearest plus Hessian compensation because joint optimization seemed infeasible. But the NAS community had already turned "infeasible" into "feasible" with Gumbel-Softmax a decade earlier. Had the GPTQ authors been aware of this, scalar quantization's ceiling might have been shattered in 2022. *Engineering compromises harden into research biases.*

*Practical implications:*

- If you run local LLMs (llama.cpp / Ollama): this means you can *very soon* receive higher-accuracy GGUF quantized models that are fully format-compatible — no need to change your inference kernel at all.
- If you do quantization research: Gumbel-Softmax as a "differentiable discrete optimization" tool is worth applying to other settings — MoE routing, speculative decoding, structured pruning. Differentiability is itself a degree of freedom; once you have it, you will not want to give it up.
- If you do systems design: prioritize new methods that *parasitize the existing ecosystem* over methods that demand users switch formats. GGUF's hundreds of millions of downloads is the true reason GSQ made the engineering choice to write results back into K-Quant format.
