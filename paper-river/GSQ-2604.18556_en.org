* River of Questions

A 70B-parameter model in full precision occupies 140GB of GPU memory — too large for a single card, and entirely out of reach for consumer-grade hardware. Squeezing each weight down to 2-3 bits would let the model fit on a single 24GB consumer GPU.

But low-precision compression has a peculiar split. The field has two camps. *Scalar quantization* rounds each weight independently to an entry in a small number table — simple, supported by existing inference kernels, and the foundation of the GGUF / llama.cpp / shipping ecosystem. *Vector / trellis quantization* packs groups of weights into a codebook — higher accuracy, but requires new lookup-table kernels and rewriting the deployment stack.

The tension: at 2-3 bpp the scalar camp's accuracy collapses while the vector camp leads by a wide margin. Yet the vector camp's kernels are sparsely maintained, the ecosystem is fragmented, and nobody wants to rewrite their entire inference stack just for that accuracy bump.

*Can scalar quantization catch up at 2-3 bpp?* That's where GSQ enters. Trace this line backward and you'll find: deciding which weight should take which value is fundamentally the same problem LeCun was thinking about 35 years ago.

* Evolution Narrative

** 1989 | OBD: lifting pruning from "look at weight magnitude" to "look at impact"

*** What it saw

Pruning back then went like this: train a network, look at the absolute weight values, remove the small ones. Intuitively reasonable — if a weight is near zero, deleting it shouldn't matter much.

But LeCun didn't buy it. He gave a counterexample: a weight with small value but on a critical path, whose removal cascades deactivation downstream; versus a weight with large value but redundant with others, where removal is easily compensated. Magnitude alone can't distinguish the two.

*The real question is: how much does the loss rise if you prune this weight?*

*** Its approach

Treat the loss as a function of weights, L(w). On a trained network the first-order gradient is nearly zero (otherwise it hasn't converged). So the second-order Taylor expansion reduces to:

#+begin_example
ΔL ≈ (1/2) * Δw^T * H * Δw
#+end_example

where H is the Hessian. Pruning weight w_i means Δw_i = -w_i. The "importance" of each weight — the loss rise from removing it — falls out analytically.

OBD added a simplifying assumption: *diagonal Hessian* — weights treated as mutually independent. This drops computation from O(n^3) to O(n), making it runnable on 1989-era hardware.

*** Why this worked (and what new pitfalls it introduced)

"Look at impact, not magnitude" was the right move — 30 years of subsequent saliency-based work stands on this foundation. But *the diagonal-Hessian assumption was a trap*: neural network weights are highly coupled; pruning one perturbs the rest. OBS came along three years later to puncture it.

** 1992 | OBS: since weights are correlated, compensate when you prune

*** What it saw

Hassibi ran the head-to-head: pruning with OBD to some ratio breaks the model fast; pruning with a correlation-aware method to the same ratio holds up. The "importance" scores OBD gave under the diagonal assumption were just wrong.

More fundamentally: *OBD did nothing to the remaining network after a removal*. Deleting one weight shifts the optimal operating point — the surviving weights were trained to "cooperate with that pruned one," and suddenly one is missing, leaving all of them slightly off.

*** Its approach

Two changes:

1. Use the *full inverse Hessian* H^{-1}, no diagonal assumption. The loss impact of pruning w_i comes out precisely as w_i^2 / (2 * [H^{-1}]_ii).

2. When w_i is pruned, update all remaining weights:

#+begin_example
Δw_remaining = -(w_i / [H^{-1}]_ii) * H^{-1}_{:,i}
#+end_example

This is the "prune one, compensate the rest" rule.

*** Why this worked (and what new pitfalls it introduced)

OBS was validated in the 90s: at the same prune ratio, loss rise dropped by an order of magnitude compared to OBD.

Two pitfalls remained:

1. *Scale problem*: H is n × n where n = number of weights. For 100M-parameter models, H^{-1} simply doesn't fit.
2. *Pruning ≠ quantization*: OBS sets weights to 0. Quantization moves them to "the nearest discrete grid point" — not removal, but approximation.

The first pitfall waited 30 years (hardware + algorithms catching up together); the second waited 30 years (deep learning waiting for LLMs to make quantization a hard requirement).

** 2022-08 | OBQ: rework OBS from "prune" to "quantize"

*** What it saw

OBS solved a binary decision — keep a weight or remove it. Quantization solves *which grid point to pick*: at W4, each weight picks one of 16 values.

Frantar noticed: the OBS math doesn't actually require Δw = -w_i. Change it to Δw_i = (quantized(w_i) - w_i) — move the weight to its nearest grid point — and you can still use the Hessian to compute "loss impact of this move" and compensate the rest.

*** Its approach

OBQ's core loop:

1. For each row of weights, compute "loss rise from moving each weight to its nearest grid point"
2. Pick the smallest-rise one and snap it to the grid
3. Apply a Hessian-inverse compensation update to the remaining unquantized weights
4. Strike the corresponding row/column from the Hessian, update H^{-1}
5. Go to 1

Each weight quantized triggers an adjustment of the rest. By the time all weights are quantized, the cumulative loss rise has been minimized as much as the loop allows.

*** Why this worked (and what new pitfalls it introduced)

OBQ validated W4 with near-zero loss on 100M-scale models. But *it can't run LLMs*: each H^{-1} update is O(n^2) memory, O(n^3) time. What takes a night at 100M takes years at 100B.

** 2022-10 | GPTQ: make OBQ feasible at billion-parameter scale

*** What it saw

The OBQ algorithm itself was right; the bottleneck was raw compute. To run OPT-175B you had to slash the redundancy without breaking the algorithm's core.

Frantar et al. spotted three exploitable properties:

1. *Column order doesn't change the final result* — OBQ greedily picks "smallest-rise" but any fixed order is equivalent in expectation.
2. *Compensation updates are highly redundant* — most of each H^{-1} update isn't actually consumed by the next step.
3. *Cholesky decomposition pre-stages everything* — the full evolution path of H^{-1} can be computed once up front.

*** Its approach

GPTQ's three-punch combo:

1. *Fix the column order*: quantize left-to-right, no greedy "smallest-rise" selection.
2. *Lazy block update*: cut each row into 128-column blocks, accumulate compensation within a block, apply one big update between blocks.
3. *Cholesky-based inverse Hessian*: H = LL^T decomposition lets you read off H^{-1} columns directly without recomputation.

Time complexity drops from OBQ's O(n^3) per row to GPTQ's O(n^2) per row. OPT-175B-scale goes from "years" to "4 GPU-hours".

*** Why this worked (and what new pitfalls it introduced)

GPTQ became the starting point for 3 years of subsequent PTQ work: AWQ, SmoothQuant, SqueezeLLM, Marlin kernel all build on its algorithm or its quantized outputs.

Pitfall remaining: *GPTQ treats quantization as a per-weight independent decision*. Each weight looks at its own nearest grid point, picks it, and freezes. The grid itself is round-to-nearest uniform, pre-determined, not part of the optimization.

At 4-bit this barely matters — 16 grid points is dense enough. But at 2-bit (4 points) or 3-bit (8 points), "independent decision + fixed grid" leaves money on the table. You can sense it: at such sparse grids, each weight's best assignment depends on its neighbors (they share a per-group scale), but GPTQ can't go back and jointly optimize.

The vector-quantization camp (QuIP#, AQLM, QTIP) capitalized on this gap: codebook-based joint decisions over multiple weights, beating GPTQ substantially at 2-3 bpp. The cost: inference kernels have to be rewritten.

** 2026-04 | GSQ: bring joint optimization to scalar quantization at 2-3 bpp

*** What it saw

The landscape GSQ's authors saw:

- *Scalar camp*: GPTQ / AWQ dominate at 4-bit, fall noticeably behind at 2-3 bit.
- *Vector camp*: QTIP / AQLM lead at 2-3 bit, but kernel ecosystem is fragmented, deployment cost is high.
- *Middle ground*: can you do joint optimization *within* the scalar framework, capture the vector camp's accuracy gains, and keep the scalar camp's ecosystem?

GPTQ's specific limitations: (1) each weight picks a grid point independently; (2) the grid itself is fixed (per-group scales exist, but the scales aren't jointly learned). Together these are suboptimal at low bit-widths — but both are fundamentally argmin discrete decisions, not amenable to direct gradient descent.

*** Its approach

Bring in *Gumbel-Softmax relaxation* — an old trick (Jang/Maddison 2016): relax "pick one of K discrete options" into a continuous distribution over the K-simplex, controlled by a temperature parameter, making the whole decision process differentiable.

What GSQ does:

1. Each weight w_i isn't quantized directly — instead it outputs a length-K logits vector (K = number of grid levels, e.g. K=8 at W3)
2. Use Gumbel-Softmax sampling to get a soft assignment
3. Parameterize the per-group scale and learn it jointly
4. Differentiate the whole objective (reconstruction error or task loss) w.r.t. logits + scale, optimize end-to-end
5. Anneal the temperature → 0 after training, soft assignment collapses to a hard discrete choice
6. Project the result back to the symmetric scalar grid of GGUF K-Quant, dropping seamlessly into existing kernels

*** Why this worked (and what new pitfalls it introduced)

Result: at W2/W3, most of the gap to QTIP is closed, *and* the output is GGUF-compatible — meaning every existing llama.cpp deployment pipeline runs it without code changes. It also scales successfully to Kimi-K2.5 trillion-parameter MoE, validating scalability.

Potential new pitfalls:

1. *Moderate calibration cost*: per-coordinate logits optimization is more expensive than GPTQ's one-shot closed-form solve, requiring gradient optimization. The paper says cost is acceptable, but it's a tier higher than AWQ.
2. *Grid shape is still constrained*: projection back to GGUF K-Quant means the final grid must be symmetric scalar, losing some of the non-uniform grids Gumbel-Softmax could theoretically have learned.
3. *Local optima from joint optimization*: Gumbel-Softmax training instability is a known issue, lacking the closed-form stability guarantees of GPTQ.

* Frontier Extensions

As of writing (2026-05), GSQ is a 2026-04-20 preprint with a v2 released on 2026-05-15. Within this short window no published work has explicitly critiqued or extended GSQ.

Plausible next directions (speculative, no papers yet):

- *End-to-end vs grid-projection gap*: can you skip the GGUF projection and ship custom kernels that consume GSQ's learned non-uniform grid? How much more accuracy is available?
- *Gumbel-Softmax annealing stability*: can you replace the continuous relaxation with a straight-through estimator or other discretization trick, cutting calibration cost?
- *Joint learning across MoE experts*: GSQ already scales to Kimi-K2.5, but each expert is quantized independently. Can you jointly learn the grid across experts?

* Insights

A few patterns that emerge from reading this lineage:

1. *Discrete-decision optimization has been quietly "softening" generation by generation* — OBD is hard-threshold pruning, OBS adds compensation, OBQ becomes "pick the nearest grid point + compensate," GPTQ adds block-internal compensation, GSQ goes fully continuous relaxation. Each generation makes a previously non-differentiable decision smoother, opening more optimization headroom.

2. *Accuracy and deployment ecosystem are a perpetual tension* — QTIP / AQLM picked "accuracy first + rewrite kernels"; GSQ picked "find accuracy in the cracks + keep existing kernels". GSQ may not be theoretically optimal, but it picked a position that's *shippable*. This "find the algorithmic sweet spot under system constraints" is the typical shape of production-oriented work.

3. *Old tricks get reactivated* — Gumbel-Softmax was designed in 2016 for categorical VAE; 10 years later it's brought over to solve PTQ's discrete-decision bottleneck. Many research breakthroughs aren't "invent new method" but "transplant a mature method from elsewhere because that domain's problem matches".

4. *5 layers of lineage isn't the bottom* — this line can be traced further: OBD itself stands on 1980s Taylor expansion + Hessian analysis traditions. Any "weight-impact scoring" work can be traced here. But further back gets more general and less quantization-specific; stopping at OBD is a reasonable "contextual boundary".

* What This Inspires

This means you can:

- When doing PTQ engineering, first ask "am I on GSQ's left (scalar accuracy limited) or right (vector deployment hard)" — picking the wrong quadrant means you shouldn't be using the corresponding method.
- When you see any "argmin discrete decision + non-differentiable" bottleneck, instinctively reach for Gumbel-Softmax / Straight-Through-style relaxation tools. GSQ did it for PTQ; similar bottlenecks exist in MoE routing, sparse attention, codebook learning.
- When evaluating a PTQ paper's real value: check whether it *can be projected back into mainstream inference stacks*. If it can't, no matter how impressive the accuracy numbers, the paper-to-production gap requires another round of kernel engineering investment.
