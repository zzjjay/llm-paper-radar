* The River of the Problem

Fitting a 9-billion-parameter model onto an ordinary laptop — FP16 demands 18 GB, but
quantizing to 4 bit brings it down to just 5–6 GB. This has been a seven-year
bottleneck: parameter values are not uniformly distributed, so the naive approach of
"dividing the range into 16 buckets" always wastes slots. How do you represent a set of
weights with fewer bits while the model barely notices the difference?

Every generation fights the same monster — *outliers*. A handful of unusually large
weights stretch the dynamic range wide, while the dense mass of small weights gets
crammed into one or two coarse buckets. As long as the monster lives, quantization can
only pile on algorithmic patches (GPTQ applies Hessian corrections; AWQ guards
per-channel scales). Until someone asked: what if we twisted the distribution itself so
the monster disappears?

PolarQuant is a 2026 milestone on this "twist the distribution" route. Its story begins
with GPTQ, seven years earlier.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2023 | GPTQ: Correcting Quantization Error with Second-Order Information

*** What Problem It Saw

Before GPTQ, the mainstream approach to quantizing large models was absmax — find the
largest absolute value in a group of weights, then linearly map the range onto integers
such as {-8, …, 7}. The problem is that real weight distributions are approximately
Gaussian: the vast majority of values cluster near zero, while a few outliers stretch
the dynamic range enormously. The result is that 13 of the 16 quantization slots fall
in empty air, 3 cover the dense region, and quantization error piles up precisely
where it hurts most. Quantize a model to 4-bit this way, and perplexity simply
collapses.

*** Its Solution

Work layer by layer. For each weight matrix, quantize columns left to right; after each
column is quantized, use that layer's empirical Hessian (X^T X) to "spread" the
quantization error introduced by that column back onto the columns not yet quantized,
so they can pre-compensate. This idea is borrowed from "optimal brain surgeon": the
mistakes you have already made can still be partly repaired by what comes later.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pitfall It Leaves)

It genuinely made 4-bit quantization practical — for the first time, INT4 large-model
inference became a real option in the field. But the cost is heavy: you must collect
calibration data (feed a small text corpus through the model), compute the Hessian, and
invert it. The larger the model, the slower this gets. And — the point that mattered
most to subsequent researchers — *this method carries no theoretical guarantees*. It
works on most models, but you cannot predict when it will work; you have no idea how
far it strays from the optimum at more aggressive bit-widths like 2-bit or 3-bit.

The next question grows right out of that gap: can we derive a worst-case bound from
information theory?

** 2023 | QuIP: Scramble the Weights Before Quantizing

*** What Problem It Saw

GPTQ's problem is that "the outliers are still there; you just spread their damage
algorithmically." QuIP's authors asked the opposite question: what if you could make
the outliers *disappear* before quantization even starts?

More precisely, the theoretical analysis of quantization error involves a quantity
called "coherence" — a measure of how strongly the weights align with the eigenvectors
of their corresponding Hessian. The stronger the alignment, the harder quantization
is (many important directions carry large weight values). GPTQ never addresses this
explicitly; it only corrects after the fact.

*** Its Solution

Multiply by a random orthogonal matrix on each side before and after quantization:
first compute U W V, quantize it, then recover with U^T (·) V^T. The orthogonal
transform leaves the model's output mathematically identical, but it "decouples"
the weights from the Hessian — in expectation over random directions, coherence drops
dramatically. This is called *incoherence processing*. Quantizing the resulting,
"flatter" weights (QuIP uses lattice quantization) then yields provable worst-case
error bounds — the first time such guarantees had been brought into LLM-scale
quantization.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pitfall It Leaves)

The intuition: a random orthogonal matrix scatters any "spiky" distribution into
something closer to isotropic. Important directions no longer concentrate on a few
coordinates, so "no individual coordinate is very large," and quantization stops
hurting.

The pitfall: a dense random orthogonal matrix is large and expensive. A single
4096×4096 layer already requires storing 16 M fp16 numbers. And at extreme bit-widths
like 2-bit, simple scalar quantization is still insufficient.

The next question: can we replace the expensive dense U with something structured and
nearly free?

** 2024 | QuIP#: Walsh-Hadamard Replaces Random Orthogonal, Plus a Lattice Codebook

*** What Problem It Saw

QuIP's random orthogonal matrix is mathematically beautiful but engineeringly heavy.
Moreover, the scalar codebook QuIP uses at 2-bit is wasteful — representing each weight
with only 4 levels means you must look for tighter codes at the vector level.

*** Its Solution

Two moves:
1. Replace the random orthogonal with a *randomized Hadamard transform (RHT)* — the
   Walsh-Hadamard matrix H_d is a ±1 matrix computable with bitwise operations, requiring
   virtually zero storage and zero overhead. Flanking it with random ±1 diagonal
   matrices (sign randomization) makes it statistically equivalent to a random
   orthogonal matrix.
2. Upgrade scalar quantization to *E8 lattice quantization* — pack 8 weights together
   and find the nearest lattice point in the densest 8-dimensional lattice (E8).
   Each weight averages 2-bit, but because an 8-dimensional codebook is far denser than
   eight independent 2-bit codes combined, error drops noticeably.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pitfall It Leaves)

Hadamard is "the poor person's random orthogonal": it is cheap enough to be almost
free, yet its theoretical properties (worst-case coherence bound) are nearly identical
to those of a truly random orthogonal matrix. This is the crucial turning point where
*mathematical elegance and engineering economy meet*.

But QuIP#'s vision is still confined to the weights themselves. During inference,
activation values have their own outliers — some channels are a hundred times larger
than others, and quantizing those activations to 4-bit breaks everything. If you want
a complete pipeline with both weights and activations at 4-bit, QuIP# offers no help.

The next paper's job: elevate Hadamard rotation from the weights to the entire
inference path.

** 2024 | QuaRot: Lifting Hadamard Rotation to the Compute Graph

*** What Problem It Saw

QuIP# makes weights incoherent, but at inference time the input activations still
carry heavy channel-wise outliers. In LLaMA-family models, a few dimensions of the
residual stream have variance hundreds of times larger than the rest. Quantize those
channels to 4-bit and the entire layer's output is destroyed.

If you could "rotate away" these outlier channels — distributing energy evenly across
all channels — you could enjoy the benefits of incoherence during inference as well.

*** Its Solution

QuaRot's core observation: for two adjacent linear layers W1 and W2 in a Transformer,
inserting an orthogonal matrix R and its inverse R^T between them leaves the model
output *completely unchanged*: W1 (R^T R) W2 = W1 W2.

So, insert them. Use the Hadamard matrix H as R at the appropriate positions throughout
the network, so activations and hidden states are always in a "rotated" representation
as they propagate. Then absorb H mathematically into the adjacent W matrices — at
inference time the rotation is invisible, but *the distribution of activations has been
permanently scrambled*, and outliers vanish for good.

Now not only the weights but also the KV cache and activations can all be quantized
to 4-bit.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pitfall It Leaves)

The beauty of rotation is that it "changes nothing" mathematically, yet "changes
everything" numerically. This is a very deep trick — a free lunch, provided you are
willing to perform "graph surgery."

But graph surgery means QuaRot must intrude into the model architecture: you must know
where each layer lives, where to insert the matrices, and which linear layer to absorb
them into. For custom architectures or unusual normalization layers, integration
requires case-by-case adaptation. Moreover, the rotation matrix is shared across a
whole layer — for dynamically generated objects like the KV cache, information theory
suggests there is still room to do better.

The next paper's question: can we push rotation to a finer granularity and fully
exploit the knowledge of what the distribution looks like after rotation?

** 2025 | TurboQuant: Polar Decomposition + Gaussian Codebook for the KV Cache

*** What Problem It Saw

QuaRot's treatment of the KV cache was "rotation + scalar quantization," but the
authors observed that the coordinates of a rotated KV vector are actually *very close
to Gaussian*. Given that the distribution is now known, why not use *the quantizer that
Lloyd-Max proved in 1960 to be MSE-optimal for exactly that distribution*?

Going one step further: what a vector really wants to compress are two independent
things — its *length* and its *direction*. Length is a single scalar; direction is a
point on the unit sphere. Storing them separately lets each be encoded with its own
optimal scheme.

*** Its Solution

For each KV vector b:
1. Extract the L2 length r = ||b||; the remainder b/r is a point on the unit sphere.
2. Apply a randomized Hadamard rotation H to b/r. *Theoretical fact*: for a point
   drawn uniformly from the unit sphere, the marginal distribution of each coordinate
   after an orthogonal rotation converges asymptotically to N(0, 1/d) — a central-limit
   theorem for spherical projections.
3. Multiply each coordinate by sqrt(d) to obtain N(0, 1).
4. Quantize using the optimal L = 2^b centroids that Lloyd-Max computes for the
   standard normal distribution.

Storage: one fp16 length r per block, one int8 code per weight.
Decoding: table lookup → divide by sqrt(d) → inverse Hadamard → multiply by r.

This is "polar quantization" — "polar" comes from polar coordinates (length and
direction separated). TurboQuant uses rate-distortion tools from information theory to
prove that this scheme approaches the optimum for online KV quantization.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pitfall It Leaves)

The entire mathematical chain is tight:
- Rotation turns any distribution into Gaussian → Gaussian has a known optimal quantizer
  → you obtain a scheme that is *theoretically MSE-optimal* on the transformed distribution.
Not empirical patching, not a worst-case bound — but *attaining the rate-distortion limit
on the post-transform distribution*.

However, TurboQuant only addresses the KV cache. Dynamically generated KV vectors and
statically stored model weights are two different problems: weights can be processed
offline at leisure; KV vectors must be handled online for every new token. On the weight
side, the distribution-aware path had not yet been explored — everyone was computing
Hessians, tuning channels, or learning rotations.

The leap the next paper makes: port TurboQuant's three steps — sphere normalization +
Hadamard + Gaussian codebook — into weight quantization.

** 2026 | PolarQuant: Porting Polar Quant to Weights, and Uncovering a Counter-Intuitive Fact

*** What Problem It Saw

TurboQuant proved that polar quant is near-optimal for the KV cache. But on the weight
quantization side, the field had been stuck with "patch-and-pray" or "assume-and-hope"
approaches — either relying on calibration data for remediation (GPTQ/AWQ) or simply
*assuming* weights are already Gaussian (NF4, though in practice they are not). Nobody
had verified: if you *force* the weights to become Gaussian (via Hadamard rotation) and
then apply a Lloyd-Max-optimal codebook, what happens?

*** Its Solution

Directly transplant TurboQuant's polar-coordinate pipeline, but applied to weight blocks:
1. Partition the weight matrix into blocks of length d = 128.
2. L2-normalize each block; store the scale r_i as fp16.
3. Apply Walsh-Hadamard rotation H_128 to the unit vector —
   *key empirical fact*: the K-S distance between the rotated coordinates and N(0, 1/d)
   is < 0.01, meaning *actual LLM weights are close enough to uniform on the sphere that
   the post-rotation Gaussian approximation is very good*.
4. Multiply by sqrt(d) to obtain N(0, 1), then look up the Lloyd-Max quantization code.

On Qwen3.5-9B: absmax Q5 perplexity 6.90; PolarQuant Q5 is 6.40; FP16 is 6.37 —
*nearly lossless*, and *requiring zero calibration data*.

Furthermore, PolarQuant is a *composable* preprocessing step:
- PolarQuant Q5, dequantized and re-quantized with torchao INT4, beats direct absmax INT4
  by 0.12 perplexity points.
- It is orthogonal to AWQ (one operates per-channel, the other per-block); combined they
  achieve 3.6× compression with a perplexity only 0.06 above FP16.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pitfall It Leaves)

The most interesting finding is the *ablation study*: the authors decompose the scheme
into two contributions —

A. Sphere normalization + Hadamard rotation (making the distribution Gaussian)
B. Using the carefully derived Lloyd-Max Gaussian codebook (rather than equally spaced
   absmax bins)

A alone: error drops by 98 %.
B on top of A: a further 2 %.

In other words: *virtually all the credit goes to the Hadamard rotation*. The
seemingly sophisticated "Gaussian-optimal centroids" are almost decorative. This implies
that everything the field spent the past three years carefully engineering into
distribution-aware codebooks — NF4 and its relatives — contributes far less to final
quality than the almost-free operation of "rotate first."

New pitfalls:
- Paper v2 was voluntarily withdrawn on 2026-04-20, with the note "Found some errors,
  I need to fix." Whether the issue is numerical or whether the ablation conclusion
  holds up is currently unknown — meaning the "98 %/2 %" figure should be treated with
  caution for now.
- Validation is limited to a single model, Qwen3.5-9B. Whether the Gaussian
  approximation at d = 128 is equally good across different architectures needs more
  evidence.
- Dequantization at inference time requires an extra inverse Hadamard transform. The
  author reports roughly 8 seconds of extra model-loading time, but the maturity of
  dequantization kernels across platforms (especially Apple Silicon) still needs time.

* Frontier Extensions

Because PolarQuant itself was withdrawn in April 2026, the line of "subsequent work
directly citing and improving PolarQuant" is currently *empty* — withdrawn papers rarely
attract serious citations.

But on the broader problem line of "weight + distribution-aware + Hadamard," foreseeable
directions include:

1. A revised PolarQuant resubmission — the author explicitly said they intend to fix it.
   Whether the revision preserves the "98 % from rotation" conclusion is the key thing
   to watch.
2. Replacing the fixed Hadamard with a *learned rotation* to create a weight-side
   SpinQuant — SpinQuant has already shown learned > fixed Hadamard on the activation
   side; the weight-side counterpart is a natural extension.
3. *Applying the ablation insight retroactively to older methods*: if Hadamard is the key,
   then adding a block-wise Hadamard before GPTQ or AWQ's fine-grained compensation
   could be a one-line change that captures most of the gain for free.
4. Pushing to 3-bit / 2-bit. Q5 is already near-lossless; the real frontier where
   PolarQuant should be challenged is the 2-bit territory currently held by QuIP# and AQLM.

*Honest disclaimer*: directions 1–4 above are *reasonable downstream inferences* about
this river, not published facts. The statement "no citations after withdrawal" reflects
a current arXiv search snapshot and may miss parallel work I have not found.

* Insights

This river ran for three years, and what actually happened was not "the algorithms got
cleverer" — it was that *the coordinate system changed three times*:

1. *Algorithm layer*: GPTQ applies Hessian corrections — treating quantization as an
   optimization problem and patching errors after the fact.
2. *Geometry layer*: QuIP / QuIP# / QuaRot introduce orthogonal rotations — transforming
   weights and activations into a new coordinate system where outliers diminish.
3. *Distribution layer*: TurboQuant / PolarQuant upgrade the goal from "fewer outliers"
   to "distribution becomes a known-optimal shape" — Gaussian, because Lloyd-Max's 1960
   work gives it a *closed-form optimal quantizer*.

Each upgrade of the coordinate system made the approach *simpler, cheaper, and
theoretically tighter*. GPTQ requires calibration data plus a Hessian inversion;
PolarQuant needs only a single matrix multiply — and is actually more accurate.

This illustrates something familiar yet easy to forget: *before attacking a hard
problem, first move it into the right coordinate system*. If you can recast the problem
into a form that has already been solved (Lloyd-Max on N(0,1) is a closed case), you
inherit the gift a mathematician left 60 years ago.

PolarQuant's ablation — 98 % vs. 2 % — is the *empirical proof* of this insight:
what actually does the work is the geometric act of "twisting the distribution into the
right shape," not "quantizing more finely once the shape is right." The latter is merely
the cherry on top.

Where to go next? Three directions:
- Push to 2-bit. Q5 is already lossless; the new battleground is more aggressive
  bit-widths.
- Push into training. Everything so far is post-training; making weights "naturally
  easy to quantize" during training is a deeper level.
- Push to non-linear transforms. Hadamard is just one of many possible transforms; if a
  learned non-orthogonal transform could twist the distribution into something even more
  quantization-friendly than Gaussian (sharper, more regular), the next Lloyd-Max-inspired
  opportunity lies right there.

* Takeaways

For "how to do research / find problems," this river offers the most direct guidance:

1. *Cross-domain borrowing beats deep local excavation*. PolarQuant invented nothing —
   polar quantization came from TurboQuant, Hadamard from QuIP#, Lloyd-Max from 1960.
   Its sole contribution was *porting a mature recipe from KV cache to weights*. A complete
   paper whose core is "transfer + one ablation experiment." The lesson: whenever you see
   a clean toolchain working in one sub-domain, always ask — which adjacent problem in the
   same field has nobody yet applied this toolkit to?
2. *Ablations are more likely to yield insight than designs*. PolarQuant's real value is
   not "PolarQuant is an impressive method" but "it turns out 98 % of the contribution
   comes from the simplest step." A well-designed ablation lets you *throw away nineteen
   twentieths of the paper's complexity* and extract the one line of code that actually
   does the work in practice.
3. *Withdrawal is a real cost*. The retraction of v2 is a reminder that in LLM
   research, where numerical results are passed along in a chain, reproducibility
   matters more than elegance. Before reusing a stunning "near-lossless" result, check
   the random seed, the benchmark selection, and whether the baseline is truly
   comparable — then decide whether to build on it.

Actionable takeaway:
- The next time you do any LLM weight quantization, *add a block-wise Hadamard before
  quantizing* — it is free, has no side effects, and may capture most of the available
  gain.
- When you receive a distribution-aware method, *force an ablation*: separate "the
  distribution transform" from "the optimal codebook on that distribution" and measure
  each contribution independently. That is worth more than piling on new tricks.
- Watch for the revised resubmission of PolarQuant — if it fixes the errors, it will
  be the simplest, cheapest, and closest-to-SoTA weight quantization scheme of the
  past two years.
