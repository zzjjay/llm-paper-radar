* The River of the Problem

For every small block of weights in a language model, who gets to decide "how many bits should this be stored in"? Should researchers set the rules by intuition, or should the training process learn the answer itself?

The difficulty cuts both ways. Looking ahead to training: bit-widths are discrete (1/2/4/8 bit), non-differentiable — gradient descent has no direct way to say "drop this group from 4 bit to 2 bit." Looking back from post-training: if you first train a model in FP16 and then decide where to compress, the weight distributions have already been shaped for high precision, and the "importance" visible at compression time may not reflect where high precision was genuinely needed during training. So for the past few years, the field has been spinning in two grooves: either fix the precision and simplify the problem, or allow non-uniform precision but only by hand-picking a few layers after training is done. The truly difficult thing — letting the model learn, during training itself, how many bits each small group of weights deserves — has never been accomplished.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2023 | GPTQ: Post-training second-order calibration for uniform low-bit compression

*** What problem it saw

Large models are slow and memory-hungry, but retraining a smaller model from scratch is prohibitively expensive. Is there a way to directly "slim down" an already-trained model without significant accuracy loss?

*** Its solution

Run a calibration dataset through the model, use second-order (Hessian) information, and layer by layer minimize the output difference between the original and quantized model, compressing weights from FP16 down to 4 bit or lower — but with the entire model using the same single bit-width.

*** Why it worked (and what new problem it buried)

It captured "which weight errors get amplified," so compression was no longer blind hacking. But it left one assumption unchallenged: since we are compressing, compress everything to the same bit-width. This treated "bit-width" as a single model-level hyperparameter rather than a per-weight property.

** 2023–2024 | BitNet / BitNet b1.58: Train from scratch at ultra-low precision from the start

*** What problem it saw

Post-training compression is inherently a patch: the weights were optimized for high precision, so forcing them into low bit-width always carries unavoidable loss.

*** Its solution

Instead of "train first, then compress," start from random initialization and train entirely at 1.58-bit (ternary) throughout, letting the weights learn to express themselves clearly even at such coarse precision.

*** Why it worked (and what new problem it buried)

The weights co-evolved with the final precision level rather than being an approximation of high-precision weights. But this welded the "uniform bit-width throughout" assumption even more rigidly in place. The BitNet line proved that training from scratch at low precision is viable, yet never asked: does every position actually need the same low precision?

** 2018–2020 | HAQ / HAWQ / EdMIPS (DNAS): The CNN era discovers that one precision cannot fit all

*** What problem it saw

Vision researchers noticed earlier: in convolutional networks, some layers are extremely sensitive to precision while others can be compressed almost arbitrarily without harm. Applying the same bit-width to every layer is wasteful — precision-starved layers drop accuracy while precision-rich layers waste bandwidth.

*** Its solution

Search for the right bit-width per layer: use reinforcement learning (HAQ) to estimate each layer's cost-benefit ratio, Hessian sensitivity (HAWQ) to measure how much perturbation each layer propagates, and differentiable architecture search (EdMIPS/DNAS) to let bit-width choices also be guided by gradients.

*** Why it worked (and what new problem it buried)

For the first time, "bit-width" moved from a model-level constant to a per-layer variable. But these ideas were born on CNNs and image classification, granularity stopped at the layer level, and the search was always a process separate from main-task training — search bit-widths first, then train, or run them as separate stages. Nobody brought this into the LLM pretraining / QAT main loop.

** 2025 | Unsloth dynamic quantization / NVFP4 / MixLLM / SliM-LLM: Non-uniform precision enters LLMs, but via heuristics

*** What problem it saw

LLMs face the same situation: the LM head and certain sensitive layers are extremely picky about precision, while the bulk of weights are quite robust. The CNN-era finding that "precision should be non-uniform" was something the LLM community also wanted.

*** Its solution

Unsloth uses hand-crafted heuristics to keep "notable" layers at higher precision; NVIDIA's NVFP4 makes micro-tiles finer (16 elements per group) but still manually selects a few layers to keep in BF16; MixLLM and SliM-LLM assign precision to different features/groups based on saliency scores.

*** Why it worked (and what new problem it buried)

These methods genuinely captured the fact of non-uniform precision needs in LLMs and outperformed one-size-fits-all approaches. But in every case, "where to use high precision" was decided post-training by rules or scores — still humans engineering heuristics, not the optimization process discovering the answer, and always applied as post-processing on an already-trained model.

** 2026-04 / 2026-06 | GSQ / dMX: Making precision allocation itself differentiable and learnable

*** What problem it saw

Hand-picking layers is ultimately guesswork. Since bit-width is a discrete choice, can we treat it like architecture search — turn it into a continuous problem optimizable by gradients, and let "what precision to use" emerge on its own rather than being guessed by humans?

*** Its solution

GSQ introduces trainable logits over candidate precision levels for each weight, samples a "soft quantization" result via Gumbel-Softmax, makes reconstruction loss differentiable with respect to precision choices, and anneals to discrete precision at convergence. dMX parameterizes each layer's floating-point format as a continuous scalar, keeps it continuous during training, discretizes via annealing before inference, and uses a regularization term to push average bit-width toward a target budget.

*** Why it worked (and what new problem it buried)

Both methods genuinely turned "choosing precision" into a gradient-descent-able optimization problem, no longer relying on human experience. But both still operate in the post-training phase: the optimization objective is per-layer reconstruction loss (GSQ) or a KL-divergence-style layer-selection intuition (dMX), granularity is "layer" rather than "group," and crucially the optimization is done on a model whose weights are already fixed — the weights never had a chance to "know" which precision they would ultimately be assigned to.

** 2026-07 | VBQ (target paper): Train from scratch and let every 64-weight group learn its own bit-width

*** What problem it saw

Per-layer optimization is still too coarse — within a single layer, different groups of weights can differ dramatically in importance. And as long as precision is decided after training, the situation is always "weights locked in, precision chasing to fit" rather than "weights and precision growing together." The real problem was never solved: can we, during from-scratch training, let each small group of weights directly learn its own bit-width against the final task loss?

*** Its solution

Partition weights into groups of 64; for each group, use Gumbel-Softmax relaxation to differentiably select precision from {1, 2, 4, 8} bit; combine with alternating optimization (one round updates weights, the next updates precision assignments); train entirely from scratch; optimize task loss directly rather than reconstruction loss.

*** Why it worked (and what new problem it buried)

Because the granularity reaches the group level and training is joint from the start, the model can bind "how fine-grained does this group need to be" together with "what should this group learn" from the very first steps of training, rather than fixing weights first and adapting precision afterward. The paper observes highly non-uniform outcomes: 69% of groups converge to 1 bit, the LM head averages roughly 1.09 bit, while the first MLP block retains close to 2.5 bit — revealing that precision requirements are an inherently local, highly structured property that uniform or per-layer allocation both obscure. But the paper openly acknowledges new open problems: the search has only been validated at up to 1.5B parameters, and scaling to larger models "remains an unsolved problem." The paper also explicitly does not make direct head-to-head comparisons against post-training compression methods like GPTQ, AWQ, QuIP#, or AQLM — it addresses "how to allocate precision when training from scratch" and has not yet demonstrated that it can serve as a stronger post-training compressor.

* Insight

The real transformation happening along this line is not the surface-level number game of "bit-width going from 8 down to 1." Rather, it is the steady demotion of "precision" as a concept — from a model-level hyperparameter that humans must decide in advance, to a local property on equal footing with the weight values themselves, one that can be learned by optimization. Every step of progress is fundamentally doing the same thing: moving the decision of "how to allocate precision" from coarser granularity and later stages toward finer granularity and earlier stages. The CNN era first proved "layers differ from each other"; the LLM era initially transplanted that conclusion via manual heuristics; GSQ/dMX then made it a differentiable problem; and finally VBQ pushed it to the limit of "jointly decided from the very first step of training, at the granularity of individual groups."

The most probable next direction is to continue along this axis of "finer granularity + earlier in the pipeline": for instance, pushing granularity down to individual weights rather than groups of 64, or verifying whether this kind of joint learning remains stable in pretraining runs beyond 1.5B parameters — a question the paper itself names as open.

* Takeaway

This evolutionary line offers a reminder: whenever you see a system containing a "globally uniform hyperparameter" — whether it is bit-width, learning rate, or some threshold — resist the urge to immediately tune its value. First ask: is the "uniformity" itself the root of the problem? Turning it from a constant into a learnable, finer-grained variable is typically more productive than continuing to tune the constant. This means that every time you encounter a "one-size-fits-all" design choice, you can instinctively ask: can this "one size fits all" also be something that is learned?
