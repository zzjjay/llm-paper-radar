* The Problem River

The bottleneck of LLM inference is matrix-vector multiplication. Weight matrices contain hundreds of millions to tens of billions of numbers, and every generated token demands a full read-and-multiply pass over all of them. How do you make this both fast and lossless?

The difficulty is that two pressures converge from opposite directions:
- *Hardware direction*: compress weights from 16-bit down to 1-bit to save memory and bandwidth. But the harder you compress, the more accuracy you lose. How far can you push before the model breaks?
- *Algorithm direction*: standard matrix multiplication is O(n^2), and decades of research have never broken through that wall. But what if the weights can only take values from {-1, 0, 1}? Is it still O(n^2)? Is there really no shortcut?

The two paths ran in parallel, neither helping the other — until someone noticed that once hardware had compressed weights to ternary, the old foundation of algorithms cracked open, and a log factor could be excavated. Then someone else noticed that the gains excavated by the algorithm couldn't land inside a real hardware kernel — they were a paper victory. This river is the story of how two separate tracks collided.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2023.10 | BitNet: Training a 1-Bit Transformer from Scratch

*** What Problem It Saw

LLM inference is slow, expensive, and hot. FP16 weights cost 2 bytes per number; a 70B model needs 140 GB for weights alone. Post-training quantization to INT8 saves half, but pushing further — to 4-bit or 1-bit — causes a cliff-edge accuracy collapse. The model was never trained for low precision; forcing it into a {-1, +1} world naturally leaves it confused.

*** Its Solution

Abandon post-training quantization — train with 1-bit from the very first step. Replace every ~nn.Linear~ in PyTorch with ~BitLinear~: during the forward pass, weights are binarized to ±1; during the backward pass, gradients still flow through and update a full-precision "latent" weight. This way the model learns from day one how to live in a 1-bit world.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

Because the model adapts to low precision rather than being forced into it after the fact, it retains more capability and exhibits scaling laws similar to FP16. But pure 1-bit expressivity still has a ceiling: each weight can only "fire or not fire" — it cannot say "this connection is irrelevant." A noticeable capability gap remains on large models, motivating the next step.

** 2024.02 | BitNet b1.58: Add a Zero, Ignite the Ternary Wave

*** What Problem It Saw

1-bit BitNet manages at small scale but struggles as you push into tens of billions of parameters. The problem is not the training framework — it is the vocabulary. A binary on/off switch cannot express "this edge is unimportant," which is an extremely common situation.

*** Its Solution

Add exactly one value: 0. The weight set grows from {-1, +1} to {-1, 0, +1}. Three values need log2(3) ≈ 1.58 bits to encode, hence the name b1.58.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

That zero gives the model a built-in "pruning" button: unimportant connections can be zeroed out. Starting from 3B parameters, b1.58 matches same-size FP16 LLaMA; at 70B it is 4.1× faster and matrix-multiply energy is 71× lower.

But b1.58 is only a *model architecture* — it says nothing about how to actually run those ternary matrices fast. cuBLAS/cuDNN on GPU is written for FP16/INT8; nobody has optimized for ternary. The result: the model weights are small, but inference is not much faster. Two engineering relay races begin: bitnet.cpp takes the hardware track; RSR takes the algorithm track.

** 2024.10 | bitnet.cpp: The Hardware-Facing CPU Kernel

*** What Problem It Saw

b1.58 models are available, but running them with HuggingFace + PyTorch means doing bfloat16 multiplications on ternary matrices — none of the benefits of ternary are realized.

*** Its Solution

Hand-write SIMD kernels for x86 and ARM, compressing "ternary weight × float activation" down to a handful of instructions. Same-size models run 2.4×–6× faster on CPU.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

Ternary multiplication does not need a real multiplier — table lookups and additions suffice. But the approach is fundamentally still O(n^2): every matrix element must be visited once. It never asks the question "could we visit fewer elements?"

** 2024.11 | RSR: The Algorithm Track — First Break Through O(n^2)

*** What Problem It Saw

Everyone before — FP16, INT8, BitNet, bitnet.cpp — had silently assumed that matrix-vector multiplication must be O(n^2). This was decades of received wisdom. But with only three distinct weight values, that wisdom has a gap: *many columns are identical*.

More precisely: slice the matrix into horizontal strips of k rows each. Within each strip, look at each column's k-element pattern. With k=8 and ternary, there are only 3^8 = 6561 possible patterns. If the matrix is n=10,000 columns wide, the pigeonhole principle guarantees that on average each pattern appears 1.5 times — heavy repetition.

Old approach: compute the dot product of every column with the input vector v independently, repeating the work n times. RSR's insight: compute each *unique pattern* exactly once, then broadcast the result to all columns that share that pattern.

*** Its Solution

Two phases:
- *Offline preprocessing*: slice the matrix into strips, apply counting sort within each strip to cluster columns with identical patterns, and record the column permutation and group boundaries.
- *Online inference*: using the precomputed groupings, gather and sum the relevant elements of input v, compute one multiply-accumulate per *unique* pattern, then scatter the results back.

Complexity drops from O(n^2) to O(n^2 / log n) — a log factor on paper.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It exploits the fact that "weights are fixed after training" — one preprocessing pass can be reused indefinitely. It exploits "low bit-width implies natural repetition" — the narrower the bit-width, the more repeated patterns.

But the implementation is NumPy + C++ lab code: fine for benchmarking in isolation, but impossible to insert into a real LLM inference pipeline. The Python interpreter, generic sorting, variable-length scatter lists, and non-coalesced memory accesses all silently devour the log factor the algorithm had earned. Theoretical 29×, actual gain in HuggingFace: essentially zero. That is the starting point of RSR-core.

** 2026.03 | RSR-core (This Paper): Making the Log Factor Land

*** What Problem It Saw

The RSR algorithm itself is not the problem — *RSR written in Python* is too slow, slow enough to erase every advantage it earns. The specific bottlenecks:

- Interpreter dispatch: every matmul call detours through Python
- Generic comparison sort O(n log n), even though the pattern space is a finite discrete set that does not need comparison sorting at all
- Scatter implemented as a Python list of lists — memory jumps everywhere
- Ternary requires scatter in both positive and negative directions; the original implementation uses two lists, causing dense branching
- Multiple linear layers sharing the same input (Q/K/V) each invoke the kernel separately, wasting work

*** Its Solution

Rewrite the entire RSR pipeline as native kernels:

1. *Replace generic sort with counting sort*: the pattern space has only 2^k or 4^k buckets; one pass in O(n + buckets) is enough.
2. *Compact metadata*: column-permutation indices compressed to 16-bit; scatter represented with bitmasks instead of variable-length index lists; on CUDA, one group's full metadata fits in a single 64-bit word loaded in one instruction.
3. *Fuse gather and aggregate on CPU*: a single scan over the input vector accumulates directly, avoiding any intermediate array. Scalar unrolled loads with software prefetch outperform hardware gather instructions for RSR's random-access pattern.
4. *CUDA: row block → thread block, warp-level group parallelism*: each warp uses a shared-memory local buffer to avoid write conflicts; zero-contribution groups are dropped at preprocessing time.
5. *Fuse activation quantization with matrix multiply; batch Q/K/V together*: eliminates one Python dispatch hop and exposes a larger parallel pool to multi-core execution.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

This is not "algorithmic innovation" — it is *translating an existing algorithm from paper to silicon*, where every optimization corresponds to a hardware feature that the previous implementation had wasted. Result: 62× faster than ternary LLM inference with HuggingFace bf16 on CPU; 1.9× faster token generation on CUDA; zero accuracy loss.

Remaining limitation: the CUDA speedup is only 1.9× because random-access scatter patterns are hostile to GPU memory hierarchies. The algorithmic next step likely requires redesigning pattern groupings to respect GPU memory coalescing — an open problem.

* Frontier Extensions

As of now (2026-05), RSR-core is the most recent engineering milestone combining algorithmic and hardware compression on this line. Visible next directions:
- GPU-friendly RSR variants: the 1.9× on CUDA is far from exhausting the algorithm's potential; the bottleneck is random access. Pattern layout must be redesigned to match GPU coalesced memory access.
- Cross-layer pattern sharing: currently each linear layer is preprocessed independently. If different layers share a common pattern dictionary, the preprocessing artifacts can be compressed further.
- RSR-aware training: adding a loss term during training that encourages repeated column patterns could amplify the log factor further. BitNet and RSR have not yet formally converged on this idea.

* Insights

On the surface this river is "LLM inference optimization"; one layer deeper it is *two independent paths — hardware compression and algorithmic compression — that at a certain point each unlocked the other's door*.

- When weights are FP16, nobody thinks "can the algorithm be faster?" because the matrix looks "dense" and "continuous" — structural redundancy is hidden by representational precision.
- When weights are compressed to ternary, structural redundancy is exposed: columns have only 3^k possible patterns, so for large n they repeat heavily. For the first time in decades, the O(n^2) wall has a crack.
- But an algorithmic victory in theory gets silently consumed by interpreters, general-purpose libraries, and memory access patterns in practice. The distance from "algorithm" to "kernel" is often longer than the invention of the algorithm itself — and far less celebrated in academia.

A deeper thread: *compression creates new redundancy*. FP16 has no redundancy but is expensive. INT4 has some. Ternary has massive redundancy. Redundancy flips from "a problem" to "a resource the accelerator can exploit" — that is the core cognitive reversal in RSR.

A prediction for what comes next: the next breakthrough may not be "shave off another bit," but rather "redesign the training objective so that redundancy emerges in a more structured shape that GPUs can digest." The bit count is approaching a floor (1.58 is very close to 1); the remaining space lies in *the shape of the redundancy*.

* Takeaways

1. *When reading a paper, start with who it criticizes*: RSR-core's single phrase "existing implementations operate at the application level" precisely locates the upstream RSR paper. Always read the "however" in the introduction first.
2. *The gap between algorithm and engineering is larger than you think*: a theoretical 29× becoming 0× in production is the norm, not the exception. An algorithm with a log factor is not necessarily faster than an engineering implementation with a small constant factor. This means that "kernelizing someone else's algorithm" is a severely underrated, high-leverage opportunity.
3. *Hardware trends change what algorithms are feasible*: ternary weights opened the first crack in the O(n^2) wall. The next hardware shift — in-memory compute, new memory hierarchies — will open new cracks. Watch the hardware roadmap to choose algorithmic problems.
4. *Redundancy is defined by the representation*: switching representations can turn "an unstructured matrix" into "a highly repetitive matrix." When you are stuck on a problem, ask: "Is my current representation hiding structure that a different representation would reveal?"
