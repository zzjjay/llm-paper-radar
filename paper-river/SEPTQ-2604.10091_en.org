* The Problem River

Cramming a 70-billion-parameter model onto a gaming GPU — giving each weight only 2 bits of representation — and still expecting it to produce coherent output. That is the bone every researcher in quantization has been gnawing at for the past four years.

Where is the difficulty? Drop one bit of precision and the representation space is cut in half; go from 4-bit to 2-bit and perplexity routinely jumps from two digits to three or four — the model starts babbling. Worse, a small minority of numerically large "salient weights" account for nearly all of the model's performance. Round them carelessly and the whole model collapses.

The real question underneath all of this is: *at an extreme low-bit budget, which weights are salient? How do you identify them? How do you protect them?* Pruning research was asking the same question thirty-plus years ago; the LLM era has just given it new vocabulary.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1992 | OBS: Pruning Connections with Second-Order Precision

*** What Problem It Saw

The neural networks of that era were tiny, but after training they contained large numbers of connections that appeared superfluous. The naive approach was blunt: sort by absolute weight value and zero out the smallest. The problem is that *the magnitude of a weight does not equal its importance*. A very small weight at a critical node can destroy the network when removed; a very large weight on a redundant path can be removed harmlessly.

*** Its Solution

Hassibi and Stork proposed a smarter criterion for importance. They used the second derivative of the loss with respect to the weights (the Hessian matrix) to estimate "if I zero out weight i, by roughly how much will the loss increase?" They then removed whichever weight caused the smallest increase, and immediately adjusted all remaining weights to compensate — if you knock out one leg, let the others shift to rebalance.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Buried)

Second-order derivatives provide genuine local sensitivity, far more reliable than weight magnitude. The cost is a massive Hessian matrix — barely tractable for small networks and unthinkable for large ones. The paradigm of "use second-order information to find salient weights + compensate remaining weights" lay dormant for thirty years, waiting to be rediscovered in the LLM era.

** 2022 | OBQ: Replace "Zero Out" with "Round" — OBS Reborn as Quantization

*** What Problem It Saw

In the LLM era the goal shifted — not pruning (zeroing weights) but quantization (compressing each weight from FP16 to a 4-bit integer). But the essence is the same: *disturbing any weight raises the loss; the question is which weight to disturb first, and how to compensate afterward.*

The OBS framework fits perfectly — just replace "zero out a weight" with "round a weight to the nearest quantization grid point." The sensitivity calculation and the compensation update remain identical.

*** Its Solution

OBQ processes one weight at a time: compute the sensitivity of all unquantized weights, quantize the one with the lowest sensitivity, then update all remaining weights. Repeat until every weight is quantized. Effective at the scale of ResNet.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Buried)

Theoretically elegant — every step makes the locally optimal choice. Engineeringly catastrophic: quantizing one weight requires updating every other unquantized weight, giving roughly O(d_row × d_col³) complexity. Applied to OPT-175B, runtime is measured in days.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Engineering OBQ into Something That Actually Runs

*** What Problem It Saw

OBQ's method is correct; its granularity is wrong. The Frantar team realized you don't need to update the entire matrix after every single weight — you can batch the operations.

*** Its Solution

Three engineering optimizations stacked together:
1. *Column-wise processing*: Abandon per-weight selection; quantize columns left to right. Proven empirically that column order barely affects the final result.
2. *Lazy batch-update*: Process 128 elements within a column together; perform the global update only after the entire block of 128 is done. Saturates memory bandwidth.
3. *Cholesky decomposition*: Compute the Hessian inverse in a numerically stable way, preventing the numerical collapse that occurs at LLM scale.

Result: the first time anyone achieved near-lossless 4-bit quantization on a 175B model, completing in a few hours.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Buried)

GPTQ is essentially "engineered OBQ" — mathematics turned into runnable code. It defined the scaffolding on which every subsequent PTQ method would be built.

But two new problems were left open:
1. Pushing to 2-bit still collapsed — with only 4 representable values (00, 01, 10, 11), no amount of compensation can recover quality.
2. *The Hessian is blind to activations.* During inference some channels have very large activation values, making their corresponding weights critically important — GPTQ cannot see this.

** 2023–2024 | Three Parallel Branches: AWQ, SpQR, QuIP

The field forked. All three branches extend GPTQ but each diagnoses a different problem.

*** AWQ: Salience Should Follow Activations, Not the Hessian

AWQ (Lin et al., 2024) argues: judging "which weight matters" from the weight itself and the Hessian is insufficient — you need to look at activations. If a channel's input activations are consistently large, the corresponding weights are salient and must be quantized carefully. AWQ multiplies each channel by a per-channel scale to widen the range of salient weights, diluting their quantization error.

The pitfall: a scale that is too large crushes non-salient weights; one that is too small fails to protect the salient ones. It is a knob that can never be perfectly tuned.

*** SpQR: Pull Outlier Weights Out and Store Them at Full Precision

SpQR (Lee et al., 2023a) takes a different route: rather than compressing outlier weights, *don't compress them*. Identify roughly 1% of "dangerous" weights and keep them in FP16; compress the remaining 99% to 3–4 bit.

The cost: outlier identification is dynamic and local — the search runs block by block as quantization proceeds. This is slow, and the "important" weights found locally may not be the globally most critical ones.

*** QuIP: Stir the Weights and Hessian into Incoherence

QuIP (Chee et al., 2023) operates at the matrix level: before and after quantization, multiply by a pair of random orthogonal matrices to render the weights and Hessian incoherent — making each weight's distribution more Gaussian and allowing the quantization grid to be used more efficiently. This was the first method to achieve stable results at 2-bit.

The cost: the pre- and post-multiplication by orthogonal matrices is itself computationally heavy.

** 2026 | SEPTQ: Strip Away the Complexity — Go Global and Static

*** What Problem It Saw

SEPTQ looks back at the rubble of those three branches and notices they are all solving the same sub-problem: *how to identify salient weights?* Yet each solution adds new complexity: AWQ's scale tuning, SpQR's local dynamic search, QuIP's orthogonal stirring.

And 2-bit performance is still not good enough.

SEPTQ poses a pointed question: *Do we actually need all this complexity? Or do we just need a single global, statically computed salience mask?*

*** Its Solution

Two plain steps:
1. *Step 1: Global static mask.* Across the entire weight matrix, compute an importance score for each element using the Hessian diagonal and the weight value (essentially the OBS sensitivity approximation), then mark the top-p% (p=1% in the paper) as "retained." This is computed once, then frozen.
2. *Step 2: Column-wise quantization + compensation.* Reuse GPTQ's column-processing framework; quantize non-retained positions, leave retained positions untouched; apply GPTQ's lazy batch-update for compensation.

Two key empirical claims support the design:
- *Static vs. dynamic*: The authors show graphically that a mask computed once statically and a mask updated dynamically during quantization have nearly identical distributions — the extra dynamic computation is wasted.
- *Global vs. local*: Salient weights cluster in the matrix (certain rows/columns are densely salient); selecting top-p% globally is more accurate than selecting block by block.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Left)

It works because the three preceding branches over-engineered a fundamentally simple idea. SEPTQ's implicit claim is: "All you need is a top-p% mask and GPTQ's engineering optimizations, and 2-bit becomes tractable."

The numbers at 2-bit are striking: OPT-66B compressed to 2-bit on the C4 dataset — SEPTQ perplexity 11.96; GPTQ 253.49; QuIP 17.64.

The new problems are visible:
- The retained 1% is stored in FP16, so the effective bit-width is not truly 2-bit but roughly 2.16-bit. The paper adds a p=0.1% variant (~2.01-bit) to address this, but the trade-off never disappears.
- "Importance score = Hessian diagonal × weight²" is still an OBS approximation — at the 1-bit extreme this approximation will very likely break down.

* Frontier Extensions

After SEPTQ, the field is moving in two directions:

** Pushing to 1 Bit

OmniQuant, AQLM, QuaRot, and similar methods continue pushing toward 1.5-bit or even 1-bit. SEPTQ's "static + global" philosophy will likely need to be revisited at 1-bit — because with only +1 / −1 to choose from, the marginal benefit of a salience mask shrinks sharply.

** Redefining "Importance"

More recent work (SliM-LLM, Wanda, etc.) has begun questioning the OBS sensitivity formula itself — it assumes local independence of weight perturbations, but Transformer attention and MLP layers are strongly coupled, so local sensitivity may be a misleading proxy for global loss. This line is exploring replacements for the Hessian diagonal: activation covariance, attention flow, and other more globally informed signals.

** Extensions of SEPTQ Itself

Since its KDD 2025 publication, the "static mask + GPTQ framework" design has been adopted by follow-up work — decoupling the mask computation from the subsequent GPTQ column processing so the mask step can be cached offline and reused, further reducing quantization time.

* Insight

Reading this lineage, what emerges is not "quantization technology is improving" but a deeper cognitive shift:

*The concept of "importance" itself is being repeatedly redefined.*

- OBS era: "Importance = second-order sensitivity"
- GPTQ era: "Importance = Hessian + column order"
- AWQ era: "Importance must reflect activation distribution"
- SpQR era: "Importance is local outlierness"
- QuIP era: "Importance can be eliminated through matrix transformation"
- SEPTQ era: "Importance should be computed once, globally and statically"

Every generation picks apart the previous generation's definition of importance. This tells us: *what is really evolving in this field is not the technique but the understanding of which property of a weight makes it incompressible.*

A second observation: *simplification is often the ultimate advance.* After three branches had each added complexity (scale tuning, dynamic search, orthogonal stirring), SEPTQ achieved state-of-the-art by removing things rather than adding them. This is a classic signal of domain maturity: when everyone is adding, the next winner usually subtracts.

Where does it go from here? 1-bit is the engineering floor, but at 1-bit the concept of "importance" starts to dissolve — with only two choices per weight, the meaning of "which weights to retain" disappears. The likely direction is: *abandon the OBS second-order approximation and enter a new framework of joint activation-weight optimization.* In other words, the paradigm that OBS launched thirty years ago may finally be ready to be replaced.

* Implications

For research practice:

*Lock onto one enduring problem and watch the methods iterate around it.* "How do you identify salient weights in a neural network" has been alive from 1992 to 2026 without changing. What changed is scale, vocabulary, and solution technique. Find a question that survives thirty years and you have found a research thread worth following.

*Recognize when "adding things" is nearing its end.* When every paper in a field is adding new modules, new constraints, or new loss terms, the next landmark paper will probably do the opposite — strip everything down to the core. SEPTQ is exactly that kind of work: it saw through the accumulated complexity of its predecessors.

For practical engineering:

*When quantizing your LLM, start with the "global static mask + GPTQ framework" combination that SEPTQ represents* before reaching for dynamic, local, or matrix-transformation techniques. If the simple approach beats the baseline, use it — don't be intimidated by the complexity in the papers.

*What are the "salient weights" in your own domain?* This line of thinking extends well beyond quantization. Model pruning, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (which layers does LoRA target?), inference acceleration (which tokens to skip?) — all are asking some version of "what is important?" Transplanting SEPTQ's "compute once, globally, statically" approach to your own problem may be the seed of a paper.
