* The River of the Problem

Why does inference in large language models get slower and more memory-hungry the longer a conversation goes? Because the model must carry the "memory" (KV vectors) of every single token in the dialogue. A hundred conversational turns can easily amount to tens of gigabytes.

So a natural idea surfaces: *can we simply discard useless tokens and keep only the important ones?*

It sounds straightforward. But "which tokens are useless"—a question that seems purely engineering—has been studied for five years, and the methods have grown fancier with every generation: from "keep the first few plus the last few," to "look at historical attention scores," to "train a small model to predict importance." Each generation believed it had found the fatal flaw of its predecessor.

Until this paper stepped forward to say: this entire line of thinking may be fundamentally unworkable.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Transformer: The Root of All Trouble

*** What problem did it see?

RNNs are too slow. To compute the 100th word, you must run through all 99 preceding words one by one. The hardware sits idle waiting.

*** Its solution

Drop sequential dependency; let every token attend to all prior tokens simultaneously — *self-attention*. The entire batch can then be computed in parallel during training.

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it dug)

Parallelism made training fast and models much stronger. But inference introduced a complication: generating the N-th token requires the Key/Value representations of all N-1 preceding tokens. Recomputing everything from scratch at each step costs O(N^2) — explosive.

The fix is the *KV cache*: compute each token's K/V once, store it, and reuse for all future steps. This seemed elegant — until context lengths grew from 2K to 32K to 1M. That stored cache became a memory monster.

*This monster is the starting point for every paper that follows.*

** 2023 | StreamingLLM: Cut the Middle, Keep Head and Tail

*** What problem did it see?

Long conversations (customer support, multi-turn agents) immediately accumulate thousands of tokens; KV cache memory cannot keep up. The simplest idea: drop some tokens. But which ones?

*** Its solution

The authors observed a curious phenomenon: the model assigns extremely high attention scores to the first few tokens (especially token 1), even when those tokens are semantic garbage like "the" or "<s>". They called this the *attention sink*.

The strategy follows directly: *keep the first 4 tokens + keep the most recent N tokens*, discard everything in between.

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it dug)

Attention sinks are a side effect of softmax normalization — there must always be somewhere for excess attention weight to go. Remove those sink tokens and the model collapses. Preserve them, and quality holds mostly intact.

But the cost is obvious: *all information from the middle is gone*. If the user mentions a critical name at token 500, and you want to reference it at token 5000 — sorry, it was discarded.

The next paper aims to fix exactly this.

** 2023 | H2O: Keep Whoever Has Been "Attended To" the Most

*** What problem did it see?

StreamingLLM's approach is too blunt. Not all middle tokens are useless — some are key pieces of information that get revisited repeatedly (names, terminology, numbers). How do you identify these "important middle tokens"?

*** Its solution

The authors proposed the *Heavy Hitter Oracle*: for each token, accumulate the attention scores assigned to it by all subsequent queries from its birth to the present. Tokens with high accumulated scores are "heavy hitters" — keep them; the rest are evicted.

Analogy: in a meeting, whoever gets quoted most often is the key contributor.

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it dug)

Intuitively, tokens that have been frequently attended to in the past will likely be attended to again. This assumption holds on many tasks.

But two new problems emerge:
1. *Cumulative scores are biased*: tokens born earlier accumulate scores over a longer time window and enjoy an unfair advantage
2. *Past does not equal future*: what if the user suddenly shifts topic and asks about a previously ignored detail?

** 2024 | SnapKV: Don't Watch a Whole Lifetime — Just Take One Last Look

*** What problem did it see?

H2O's cumulative scores contain too much "stale information." If a long prompt discusses topic A at the start and asks about topic B at the end, tokens about A carry artificially inflated historical scores — even though they are about to become irrelevant.

*** Its solution

Look only at the attention from the *last observation window of prefill* — that is, at the moment the prompt finishes being processed, which tokens is the model "glancing at"? Keep those.

Analogy: instead of reviewing an employee's five-year performance record, just look at what they did in the past week.

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it dug)

The attention at the end of prefill is the freshest signal closest to actual generation, and it beats cumulative scores on many benchmarks.

New pit: *all of these methods are betting that "future queries will look like current queries."* But what the LLM will need to answer next is, fundamentally, an unknown function. Using present attention to predict future needs — is this broken at the root?

This suspicion is the opening that brings the learned methods onto the scene.

** 2024 | Learned Methods (DMC / TRIM-KV / ChunkKV): If Humans Can't Figure It Out, Let the Model Learn It

*** What problem did it see?

All heuristics — position, cumulative score, prefill attention — are human guesses. Machine learning has an old saying: *if it can be learned, don't hand-code it*. Since "which tokens matter" is a prediction problem, why not train a small model to learn it?

*** Its solution

The details differ across papers, but the skeleton is the same:
- Input: each token's K/V vectors
- Output: an importance score
- Training: use some proxy objective (reconstruction loss / distillation / supervision) to teach the model to score

For example, DMC learns a "merge or keep" decision; TRIM-KV learns a retention gate; ChunkKV assumes attention follows a Gaussian distribution and computes expected values.

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it dug)

All papers reported state-of-the-art results, appearing to capture patterns that heuristics missed.

But TRIM-KV's own 2025 follow-up quietly acknowledged: the trained gate *converged to a solution that approximated a position heuristic*.

In other words — after all that training, the learned result was approximately equivalent to "keep the first 4 + keep the last N."

This awkward finding needed a paper to systematically nail it down.

** 2026.01 | SIP (This Paper): Push the Learned Approach to Its Peak, Then Declare It Broken

*** What problem did it see?

Previous learned-method papers shared a common flaw — *sloppy evaluation*:
- No multiple random seeds
- No comparison against "random token selection," the weakest possible baseline
- Tuned on a handful of benchmarks and published

If what was learned is merely a proxy for position, why had no one run a clean controlled experiment to settle the question?

*** Its solution

Author Brady Steele designed the *strongest possible learned approach* — called SIP (Speculative Importance Prediction):
- 1.7M parameters (not trivially small)
- Multi-step lookahead: predicts importance not just one step ahead but several
- Cross-attention mechanism
- Every available trick applied

Then evaluated with a setup that is *honest to the point of being harsh*:
- 5 random seeds (to measure variance)
- 4 retention budgets (to measure behavior at different compression ratios)
- 3 tasks (to measure generalization)
- Baselines include: pure random selection, StreamingLLM (first 4 + last N), H2O, SnapKV, and raw prefill attention itself

*Result*: SIP cannot beat random selection. At best it ties H2O, but is significantly outperformed by the strongest positional heuristic.

*** Why this approach works (and what new question it raises)

This is the most elegant part of the paper — it does not just say "learning doesn't work," it provides a mechanistic explanation:

> In the non-query-aware setting (using only KV vectors themselves, without knowledge of future queries), importance prediction has a *circular dependency*:
>
> Which tokens matter depends on what gets generated next
> What gets generated next depends on which tokens are retained
>
> There is simply no way to break out of this loop.

So the KV vectors themselves do not contain sufficient signal to predict what an as-yet-unseen query will need. No matter how elaborate the scorer, it cannot conjure that information.

New question left behind: *either abandon compression, or bring the query into the picture.*

* Frontier Extensions

This paper appeared in January 2026, but the echoes are already spreading.

** Same-Direction Evidence: Guo et al. 2025 "Hold onto that thought"

Almost concurrently, another research group reached similar negative conclusions from a different angle. They verified on larger models that what learned methods "learn" can almost always be reproduced by a simple "retain late-position tokens" rule. The two papers mutually corroborate each other.

** Opposite-Direction Breakthrough: TokenButler (Wang et al. 2025)

If non-query-aware learned methods are doomed to fail, what about *query-aware* ones?

TokenButler takes the query at each generation step as input and *dynamically* scores the historical KV entries. This sidesteps the circular dependency SIP identified: the query has already appeared, so there is nothing to predict — just compute directly.

Result: query-aware learned methods begin to consistently outperform heuristics.

This in turn validates SIP's diagnosis — *the circular dependency is the true bottleneck*. The way to break it is not a smarter model, but a later decision point.

* Insight

Looking at the entire trajectory flattened out, what actually happened was not "an iteration of compression techniques" but rather a *process of discovering the signal boundary*:

1. *Phase 1 (2023)* — People assumed KV caches were riddled with redundancy and could be slashed freely
2. *Phase 2 (2023–2024)* — Discovered that slashing must be principled. First came positional justification, then attention-based justification
3. *Phase 3 (2024)* — Believed the justification could be learned even better; the learned-method wave crested
4. *Phase 4 (2026)* — SIP nailed it with rigorous experiments: the signal in KV vectors has already been squeezed dry by simple heuristics; more complex models only rediscover "position"

This is a script that repeats throughout machine learning: *a seemingly learnable task turns out to be information-theoretically impossible to learn*. When linear regression fails to beat a decision tree, the problem is not the model — the data simply does not contain more learnable patterns. The situation here is analogous: the KV vectors themselves do not reveal what a future query will ask, and no scorer, however large, can manufacture that information.

*Where does the field go next?* Three directions are already clear:
- *Query-aware*: defer the decision until the query is available (the TokenButler route)
- *Change the level*: stop fighting over "token selection" and pivot to quantization (KIVI), cross-layer sharing (MiniCache), or prompt compression
- *Accept incompressibility*: acknowledge that some scenarios simply must pay the memory cost, and shift engineering focus back to batching / paged attention

* Takeaways

This river offers three landing points for researchers:

1. *Negative results have value, but they must be executed cleanly*
   SIP's contribution is not a new method — it is the use of a rigorous 5 seeds × 4 budgets × 3 tasks evaluation to puncture the consensus that "learned methods beat heuristics." Practical takeaway: *if you suspect a subfield is deceiving itself, first make your controlled experiments airtight, then speak up*

2. *Beware the proxy-objective trap*
   The learned scorer converged to a positional heuristic because the training objective (reconstruction loss / distillation) is one step removed from the true goal ("what will a future query need?"). A model can only optimize the signal you actually give it. Takeaway: *before training a model to solve a problem, ask yourself — how many layers of proxy separate my training signal from the true objective? Each layer risks letting the model slide toward a trivial solution*

3. *The only escape from a circular dependency is to change the timing*
   SIP's diagnosis is elegant: which tokens matter depends on the future query; the future query depends on which tokens are retained — a closed loop. Breaking it requires not a larger model but *pushing the decision point back until the missing information is available* (query-aware). Takeaway: *whenever you face a chicken-and-egg problem, don't reach for more compute first — ask whether the decision can simply be deferred until the information exists*
