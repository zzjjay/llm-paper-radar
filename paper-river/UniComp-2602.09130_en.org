* The River of Questions

Once you compress a large model, what exactly has it lost?

It sounds like a one-liner — "runs faster, accuracy dropped a few points." But a decade of research has kept revealing: what does that "accuracy" actually measure? The knowledge the model has memorized? Its ability to chain multi-step reasoning? Its performance in another language? Whether it suddenly starts hallucinating, leaking private information, or getting jailbroken? Prune an 8B model by 50% and MMLU drops only 2 points — but GSM8K (mathematical reasoning) collapses from 76 to 19. Can you still call that a good compression?

This is the river this piece follows: *evaluating whether a compression method is actually good* is itself a problem that evolves even more slowly than the compression methods it judges. Each generation of evaluation frameworks believes it has seen clearly; the next generation reveals it was blind in one eye.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022-2023 | SparseGPT / GPTQ: Compress in one pass, report WikiText perplexity, call it done

*** What problem did it see?

Rewind to early 2023. Models in the 100B+ range like GPT-3 had just become too large for ordinary research groups to run, and the field urgently wanted to shrink them. Frantar et al. published SparseGPT (pruning 50% of weights in a single pass) and GPTQ (compressing weights from 16 bits to 4 bits).

Both papers were genuinely impressive — SparseGPT was the first to make one-shot LLM pruning without retraining feasible, and GPTQ reduced the accuracy loss of 4-bit quantization to an acceptable level.

But how did they prove they hadn't "broken" the model? By reporting perplexity on WikiText and tacking on a few MMLU scores.

*** Its solution

Not really a "solution" — it was the default move. Inherit the ruler from the pretraining era: lower perplexity is better. Perplexity measures "given a piece of text, how uncertain is the model about the next token?"

*** Why it worked (and where it buried new problems)

Perplexity is intuitive, cheap to compute, and comparable across models. MMLU is a widely accepted "general knowledge" benchmark. So at that point in 2023, "perplexity barely increased + MMLU barely dropped" looked like sufficient evidence that the compression was clean.

But the hole was deep: perplexity only tests text-continuation ability, and MMLU only tests knowledge lookup. Whether the model can solve math problems, respect safety guardrails, or behave normally in Chinese — none of that is visible to perplexity or MMLU. Did those capabilities collapse after compression? Nobody had checked.

The next paper exists to clean up that mess.

** 2024 | LLMCBench (Yang et al.): Test "can it still answer questions after compression" more thoroughly

*** What problem did it see?

Yang et al.'s LLMCBench (2024) was the first systematic attempt to place multiple compression methods (pruning, quantization) on multiple knowledge benchmarks in a large-scale head-to-head comparison. They identified the first generation's flaw: every original paper only reported the handful of numbers its authors had cherry-picked, making cross-method comparison impossible.

A concrete example: if you wanted to know "on LLaMA-7B, is 4-bit quantization or 50% pruning a better trade-off?" — before LLMCBench, you had to run it yourself.

*** Its solution

Build a standardized test bed: fix a set of models (LLaMA family), fix a set of benchmarks (MMLU, ARC, HellaSwag, PIQA — knowledge/commonsense MCQs), and run SparseGPT, Wanda, GPTQ, and AWQ through all of them, producing an overall conclusion of "which method is best at which compression ratio."

Their core finding: on knowledge-style MCQs, quantization consistently outperforms pruning.

*** Why it worked (and where it buried new problems)

For the first time it gave "comparative evaluation of compression methods" a real baseline — no researcher had to reinvent the wheel from scratch. But UniComp directly identifies two blind spots:

1. *Distillation is missing.* Pruning and quantization are covered, but knowledge distillation is absent. Distillation is a parallel compression route (training a small model to mimic a large one), one of the three major paradigms alongside pruning and quantization — leaving it out makes the comparison fundamentally incomplete.
2. *Still only knowledge MCQs.* Multi-step reasoning (GSM8K, MATH) is not tested, multilingual performance is not tested, instruction-following is not tested, and trustworthiness dimensions like safety, privacy, and fairness are entirely absent.

The papers that followed each went to plug a specific one of those holes.

** 2024-2025 | Single-Focus Deep Dives: Reasoning, calibration, and trustworthiness each get one shot

*** What problems did they see?

After LLMCBench, researchers recognized that knowledge-style MCQs were too blunt an instrument to reveal the real damage compression does to a model. Three separate waves of researchers dug deeper:

- *Liu et al. (2025)*: Effects of compression on *reasoning*. Their key observation: with the rise of CoT (chain-of-thought) reasoning models, the choice of quantization scheme has an enormous impact on reasoning performance — the same model quantized with different schemes can differ by tens of points on GSM8K.
- *Zhang et al. (2026)*: Applying pruning + quantization to distilled DeepSeek-R1, examining reasoning robustness.
- *Williams & Aletras (2024)* / *Zeng et al. (2024)*: Discovering that *calibration data* itself influences post-compression NLU and multilingual performance — the same pruning method fed different calibration samples produces wildly different results.
- *TrustLLM (Huang et al., 2024)*: Not a compression paper, but it constructed a standard framework for evaluating LLM trustworthiness across six dimensions: truthfulness, safety, fairness, robustness, privacy, and ethics.

*** Their solutions

Each took a "narrow but deep" evaluation approach: pick one dimension (reasoning / calibration / trustworthiness), pull the existing compression methods through it, and see what happens.

*** Why they worked (and where they buried new problems)

Each paper illuminated a specific blind spot — "it turns out quantization damages reasoning ten times more than it damages knowledge." But the limitation is equally obvious: each study stares at its own finger. A practitioner reading all these papers still cannot answer: "In my deployment scenario, weighing performance + trustworthiness + efficiency together, is pruning, quantization, or distillation the best deal?"

That gap is exactly UniComp's reason for existing: lay all the fingers on the table at once.

** 2026 | UniComp (this paper): Spread three compression paradigms × three evaluation dimensions into one big table

*** What problem did it see?

By early 2026 the situation was:
- Compression methods scattered across three paradigms (pruning, quantization, distillation), each with its own benchmarks;
- Evaluation dimensions scattered across five or six angles (knowledge, reasoning, multilingual, instruction-following, trustworthiness), each with its own paper;
- Not a single paper had ever placed all three paradigms × all five or six dimensions in one comparison.

Practitioners making deployment decisions had to assemble the puzzle themselves, with a high probability of getting it wrong.

*** Its solution

Blunt-force completeness — fill in every cell:

- *Methods*: Pruning (SparseGPT, Wanda) + Quantization (GPTQ, AWQ) + Distillation (Minitron, Low-Rank Clone) — six representative methods, all compressed to roughly 50% scale.
- *Evaluation*: Performance (knowledge / reasoning / multilingual / instruction-following) + Trustworthiness (TrustLLM's six dimensions, 30+ datasets) + Efficiency (runtime speedup, inference efficiency, compression cost) — 13 metrics total.
- *Models*: LLaMA-2/3.1 (multiple sizes), Qwen-2.5/3 (including MoE), DeepSeek-R1 distilled versions (reasoning models).

A single net spanning *40 datasets × 6 methods × multiple model architectures*.

*** Why it worked (and where it buried new problems)

Once the table was laid out, several key findings surfaced immediately:

1. *Knowledge Bias*: Every compression method "preserves factual memory well but damages reasoning." LLaMA-3.1-8B pruned 50% with Wanda still retains 85% of MMLU performance, but GSM8K collapses from 76 to 19 and MATH-500 from 30 to 7.6. This is the first time the field has quantified that gap so clearly.
2. *Performance ≠ Trustworthiness*: A compression method that preserves MMLU well does not necessarily preserve safety, privacy, or fairness — the two are decoupled. This means any deployment decision based solely on performance benchmarks carries real accident risk.
3. *Calibration data can recover 50% of lost reasoning performance*: Using reasoning-aware calibration data (CoT samples) during pruning pulls reasoning performance about halfway back. This is an immediately actionable "old method + new insight" optimization.
4. *Minitron-Depth vs. Width*: Even within NVIDIA's own distillation pipeline, removing depth (cutting the latter half of layers) performs much worse than cutting width — evidence that "later layers are especially critical for reasoning."

New gaps left open: we now know that "reasoning collapses hardest after compression," but *why* does it collapse hardest? Which parameters form the critical circuits for reasoning? Could they be identified and shielded during compression? UniComp has mapped the symptoms comprehensively; the mechanism remains a black box.

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026, UniComp is the most recent unified framework on this evaluation trajectory. Predictable next steps include:

1. *Embedding evaluation inside the compression process.* Current evaluation is "compress first, test afterward." The future may be "test while compressing" — a lightweight evaluation proxy continuously telling the compression algorithm "the weight you just touched damaged reasoning."
2. *Mechanistic interpretability + compression.* Following Anthropic's "circuits" line of work: identify which sub-circuits are responsible for multi-step reasoning and mark them as compression-protected regions.
3. *Task-specific on-demand compression.* If your application has no need for multilingual capability, allow it to degrade and redirect the budget toward protecting reasoning. UniComp-style comprehensive evaluation is exactly the prerequisite for this kind of "choose what to preserve" approach.

* Insight

On the surface this river is about "evaluation methods becoming ever more comprehensive." At its core it is an *epistemological iteration* — our understanding of what "good compression" even means has been repeatedly overturned.

Every generation of researchers believed its ruler was adequate. SparseGPT authors felt perplexity was enough; LLMCBench felt multiple knowledge benchmarks were enough; UniComp feels 13 metrics × 40 datasets is enough. But the moment the next generation adds one more dimension, the previous generation's "enough" is immediately exposed.

The deeper pattern: *evaluation always lags behind capability.* A model develops a capability first (e.g., multi-step CoT reasoning), then someone realizes "compression might be damaging this capability," then an evaluation framework is built. That means the capabilities UniComp has not yet tested — long context, tool use, multimodal reasoning — will become the blind spots of the next paper.

Where is the most likely next move? Two directions:

1. *Evaluation moved upstream* — no longer "a post-compression checkup," but "the compression algorithm itself treats evaluation targets as part of its loss function."
2. *Personalized evaluation* — general head-to-head comparisons (UniComp-style) as baselines, but every real deployment scenario needs its own "mini-UniComp calibrated to my downstream task."

* Takeaways

For researchers:

- When you see a paper claiming "our method performs well," first ask "what ruler did they use?" — the ruler itself is often the topic of the next paper.
- Survey and evaluation papers are often dismissed as "not real research," but UniComp shows that *choosing the right dimensions and laying out the right grid* is itself a method for discovering new regularities. Its "knowledge bias" finding is more valuable than any single compression method's incremental gain.
- Look in your own field: which widely reported "we didn't lose performance" papers are still using a ruler from five years ago? That is where the opportunity lies.

For engineers:

- *Don't pick a model based on MMLU alone.* If your application involves multi-step reasoning, multilingual use, or safety-sensitive scenarios, UniComp's table directly tells you which compression methods are off the table.
- *4-bit AWQ is the most reliable current default* — near-lossless on both knowledge and multilingual preservation, making it the most sensible starting point for LLaMA/Qwen deployments. But if reasoning matters, budget for a ~50% performance drop, or use reasoning-aware calibration to recover half of it.
- *Pruning 50% depth (cutting the latter half of layers) is a landmine* — within the same distillation approach, cutting width is significantly better than cutting depth. This is one of the most immediately actionable conclusions UniComp provides.
