* The River of the Problem

Why does compressing a large model's numbers from 16 bits to 8 bits — which sounds like merely halving storage — actually blow up the entire model in practice?

The story starts simply enough: hardware keeps getting faster, but what GPUs truly "fly" on is 8-bit or even 4-bit arithmetic. If both weights and activations can be computed in 8 bits, memory usage halves and throughput doubles. The reality, however, is that Transformers always harbor a handful of numbers that are grotesquely large — values in the tens of thousands, even hundreds of thousands. Forcing them into 8 bits' 256 slots either truncates them (precision explodes) or stretches those 256 slots so wide that every other normal value collapses to zero.

This is the *outlier* problem. Nearly seven years of quantization research have essentially been a crowd of people circling a few "troublemakers": first trying to dodge them, then trying to suppress them, and finally trying to understand — why do they appear in the first place?

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022 | LLM.int8(): Giving the "Troublemakers" a Name for the First Time

*** What Problem It Saw

People assumed that compressing 16-bit floats to 8-bit integers (INT8) would cost only a small amount of precision. Dettmers tried it and found: once a model exceeds 6.7B parameters, INT8 simply collapses. Perplexity jumps from the teens into the thousands.

Stranger still — the collapse is not uniform. The damage concentrates in an extremely small number of dimensions (fewer than 0.1%). Activation values in these dimensions can reach hundreds, while all other dimensions hover around ±1.

*** Its Solution

Since the troublemakers occupy only a few dimensions, *keep just those dimensions in FP16* and quantize everything else to INT8. This is essentially a divide-and-conquer patch — called mixed-precision decomposition.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

It sidesteps the issue: outliers remain, they simply avoid being harmed by quantization. But this "special treatment" breaks the regularity of GPU kernels, eating away at the speed advantage. More critically, it never answers *why* those dimensions become outliers in the first place.

The pit it leaves: can we quantize outliers directly instead of dodging them?

** 2023 | SmoothQuant: Relocating the Difficulty

*** What Problem It Saw

LLM.int8 is a patch applied at inference time. Xiao et al. aimed for something more thorough — quantizing all activations. But activation distributions are too peaky (a few dimensions are large, the rest are small), and quantization precision cannot hold.

They fixed their attention on one fact: *weight distributions are very smooth*. If the "spike" of activations could be transferred to the weights, letting weights absorb some of the difficulty, activations would become quantizable.

*** Its Solution

Mathematically, for each channel, divide the activation by a scalar =s_j= and multiply the corresponding weight column by =s_j=. The result is fully equivalent (modulo a reshape), but activations become flatter and weights become slightly spikier — overall quantizability improves.

Think of it as a seesaw: originally one side is heavy, one side is light; shifting the center of gravity a bit makes both sides manageable.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

Clever, but it only *transfers* the difficulty rather than eliminating it. The residual stream (the main trunk between each block) must remain in BF16, because quantizing it causes the model to immediately collapse (the TWEO paper reports that GPT-2 XL perplexity jumps from 14.81 to 1876.70).

The pit it leaves: why is the residual stream so fragile? Why can't we stop activations from spiking at the source?

** 2023 | Quantizable Transformers (Bondarenko): The First Attempt to Explain "Why"

*** What Problem It Saw

The first two works were treating symptoms. Bondarenko et al. wanted to ask: where do outliers actually come from?

They proposed the *no-op hypothesis*: the attention mechanism sometimes wants certain tokens to "do nothing." To achieve this, the softmax output must be nearly all zeros — but softmax can never produce true zeros (exponentials are always positive). The model can only make the logits entering the softmax extremely large or small, indirectly approximating zero. This "extreme large and small" propagates downstream and becomes outliers.

*** Its Solution

If softmax is the culprit, change it:
- *Clipped softmax*: allow the softmax output to truly equal zero
- *Gated attention*: add a gate to attention so that an entire token can "switch off"
- *softmax+1*: add 1 to the denominator, giving "zero attention" an escape route

This was the first time anyone tried to cure outliers at the architecture level rather than patching them after the fact.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

It does provide partial relief. But TWEO later disproves it: testing on Swin-T, these methods reduce the peak from the raw 1000+ down to 478–976 — still several orders of magnitude away from FP8-capable levels.

The deeper problem: the no-op hypothesis *assumes outliers are data/task driven* — that they appear because the model "wants to perform a no-op." This assumption quietly became the default premise for everyone who followed.

The pit it leaves: what if outliers are not data-driven?

** 2023 | Vision Registers (Darcet): "Finding a Home for the Troublemakers"

*** What Problem It Saw

Darcet et al. observed a peculiar phenomenon in ViTs: outliers are not scattered — they *concentrate almost entirely on a few irrelevant tokens*, typically patches from uninformative corners of the image. These tokens act like "garbage bins" where the model dumps its excess energy.

*** Its Solution

Then *proactively provide garbage bins* — prepend a few meaningless "register tokens" to the input and let the model deposit all outliers into these dedicated containers. The original tokens stay clean.

Analogously, on the text side, *PrefixQuant* prepends fixed prefixes to each prompt specifically to absorb outliers.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

Works well on ViTs — but it remains an extension of the "data-driven" assumption: outliers come from "garbage positions" in the data, so providing dedicated containers suffices.

The pit it leaves: is this assumption correct? If you run a pretrained model on pure Gaussian noise, do outliers still appear? (This is precisely the key experiment TWEO later runs.)

** 2024 | DeepSeek-V3 / Kimi: Forcing FP8 Training Through Sheer Engineering

*** What Problem It Saw

By 2024, FP8 hardware (H100 Tensor Cores) was right there in front of everyone, and FP8 training promised to halve memory. But everyone who tried it found that direct FP8 training inevitably collapses.

The DeepSeek team accepted this reality: since the root cause couldn't be fixed, keep the "sensitive" modules (embedding, normalization, MoE gate, LM head) in BF16, use FP8 for the main linear layers, and add tile-wise / block-wise quantization to contain local outliers within small regions.

*** Its Solution

A finely tuned mixed-precision engineering stack:
- Sensitive layers in BF16
- Insensitive layers in FP8
- Tile-wise quantization (independent scale per 128×128 tile)
- Extensive custom operators

Through sheer engineering force, DeepSeek-V3 successfully pre-trained a 671B MoE in FP8.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

It runs — but the *engineering barrier is extremely high*. Every new architecture requires re-tuning which modules are sensitive, and the FP8 speed gains are eaten up by repeated precision conversions. Ordinary teams cannot replicate it; the paper itself is criticized as "hard to popularize."

The pit it leaves: why, in 2024, are we still using workarounds for a phenomenon first identified in 2022? Is there a way to make outliers *simply not appear* at the source?

** 2025 | TWEO: "Outliers Have Nothing to Do with Data"

*** What Problem It Saw

The TWEO authors ran two "disproving experiments":

1. *Feed a pretrained Qwen2.5-0.5B random Gaussian noise* — by all prior theories, without real data there is no no-op behavior, no "garbage positions," so outliers should not appear. Result: *outliers appear anyway*.
2. *Feed real data to a randomly initialized Qwen2.5-0.5B* — by prior theories, data-driven outliers should appear immediately. Result: *all activations stay below 10; no outliers*.

The conclusion is definitive: outliers are *unrelated to data and depend only on the learned weight structure*.

Every assumption held over the preceding six years — LLM.int8, SmoothQuant, the no-op hypothesis, the register hypothesis — was built on a false premise.

*** Its Solution

Since the cause is the weight structure (mechanical), the fix should also be mechanical — *regardless of how outliers are generated, directly suppress the magnitude of activations during training*.

#+begin_example
L_total = L_task + lambda(t) * L_TWEO

L_TWEO = (1/L) * sum_l  E[ ( |A^(l)| / tau )^p ]

  where A^(l) is the output of the l-th transformer block (post-residual)
        tau = 3, p = 4, lambda = 0.01
#+end_example

Intuition:
- |A| = 0.5τ → penalty ≈ 0.0625  (normal values barely touched)
- |A| = τ    → penalty = 1
- |A| = 10τ  → penalty = 10000   (outliers crushed hard)

p=4 makes normal values insensitive to the loss while giving outliers nowhere to hide. τ is a soft scaling factor, not a hard threshold — gradients are continuous and training remains stable.

Crucially, the loss acts on *the entire block's output* and touches neither attention, nor MLP, nor softmax — *zero architectural intrusion*.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

It operates at the mechanistic level. Using SVD analysis of the MLP =y = BAx=, one can prove that a necessary and sufficient condition for an outlier to appear is that some row of the downstream matrix *is aligned with a left singular vector of the upstream matrix*, while the input *is aligned with the corresponding right singular vector*. All three factors must coincide to produce an extreme value.

Empirical validation: for ViT-B the predicted peak is 884.29, the measured peak is 880 — error < 0.5%. The theory closes the loop.

Results: peak drops from 10000+ to < 20. Genuine per-tensor FP8 training *now works* (performance on par with BF16, throughput +36%). The residual stream can now be quantized too (W8A8 static) — a setup previously considered completely impossible is now state-of-the-art.

The pit it leaves: training adds one extra loss term and requires annealing of λ — but the paper reports that a single set of hyperparameters generalizes across ViT and LLM architectures, so tuning cost is minimal. At a deeper level: if outliers arise from weight co-linearity, is co-linearity itself the model's way of "saving parameters"? Does suppressing it destroy some form of expressive capacity we do not yet understand? Current experiments show no performance drop, but this remains an open question.

* Frontier Extensions

TWEO was uploaded to arXiv in November 2025, and as of the retrieval date (May 2026) no publicly available follow-up work directly builds on or challenges it. Foreseeable directions include:

- *Proving or disproving the boundaries of the mechanical hypothesis*: does it hold for all Transformers on all tasks? Are there counterexamples?
- *Extending L_TWEO to RNN / SSM / Mamba* and other non-Transformer architectures — these also suffer from outlier problems.
- *Combining with FP4*: now that FP8 is solved, the next frontier is FP4 (available on NVIDIA Blackwell). Can a peak below 20 survive FP4 quantization?
- *Geometric analysis of trained weights*: why does co-linearity emerge naturally? Is it a preference of the optimizer or a property of the loss landscape?

(These are reasonable extrapolations, not published results. They should be verified and updated as progress is made.)

* Insights

On the surface, this whole river is about "how quantization technology progressed." At its core, it is about *how long a wrong assumption can survive*.

From 2022 to 2025, virtually every top-tier lab defaulted to the belief that "outliers are data-driven" — LLM.int8 never explained it, SmoothQuant never questioned it, Bondarenko supplied a plausible story (no-op), and Darcet followed that thread to the register solution. The assumption was never validated, but because it "sounded reasonable" and "guided workable solutions," no one shook it.

What TWEO did was blunt yet thorough: run two controlled experiments (Gaussian noise + random initialization), uproot the entire assumption, then use SVD to lay out the true mechanism. This is the classic pattern of "diagnosis being worth more than treatment" — prior generations were treating; TWEO was diagnosing.

*What was really changing along this line*: from "patching at the symptom level" → "giving the wrong explanation for the symptom" → "diagnosing the correct root cause." Each step was necessary — without SmoothQuant's failure, no one would have asked "why can't the residual stream be quantized?"; without Bondarenko's wrong hypothesis, no one would have asked "is the hypothesis itself correct?"

*Where to go next*: once the diagnosis is right, structural optimization follows. Since outliers are a product of training dynamics and weight geometry, (1) the training objective can avoid them from the start (TWEO has already shown this path), and (2) perhaps the optimizer itself (certain properties of AdamW) is the true cultivator of co-linearity. The next iteration may not add another loss term but instead change the optimizer.

* Takeaways

Three lessons for doing research:

1. *Test your assumptions before optimizing your solution.* Across the entire river, everyone was optimizing solutions while nobody validated assumptions. A 200-line controlled experiment (random noise vs. real data) is worth more than five years of engineering optimization. Always ask yourself: has that thing "everyone takes for granted" actually been verified?

2. *"Sounds reasonable" is the most dangerous state.* The no-op hypothesis was coherent; the register story was tidy — so neither was questioned for two years. When an explanation *just barely* accounts for what you observe, be wary: it may have no predictive power at all and is merely fitting existing observations.

3. *Simple solution + clean principles = the most durable work.* TWEO adds a single L_p loss term — no new operators, no new architecture, universal across LLM and ViT. The reason such an "apparently naive" solution can work is that it rests on a foundation of *first diagnosing the problem correctly*. When the diagnosis is right, the solution is usually simple; when the solution is complicated, the diagnosis is usually wrong.

The bottom line: next time you see a field in which everyone is using increasingly complex methods to solve a long-standing problem, pause and ask — has the earliest assumption ever been verified? Verifying it may be ten times more valuable than building the next engineering optimization.
