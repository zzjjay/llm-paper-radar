* The Problem River

You have a large model. You want to compress it to 4 bits before deployment — save memory, save money, run faster. But after compression it gets dumber.

So someone devises a clever fix: let the uncompressed "full-strength" version serve as a teacher, and let the flattened "student" imitate the teacher's outputs, gradually recovering the lost capability. The trick works well — the outputs match tightly.

But this paper asks a sharper question: *if the student says exactly what the teacher says, does it also think the way the teacher thinks?*

That sounds philosophical, but it is a deadly engineering problem. If a model merely "gets the answer right" while its internal representations have become unrecognizable, you will not see the problem on easy questions — but the moment it faces multi-step reasoning or hard coding tasks, it collapses. This is especially true for reasoning models that have been carefully sculpted by reinforcement learning: their internal structure is particularly fragile. You think you just changed the clothes; you actually dismantled the skeleton.

The core question of this river: *to measure "how well the student has learned," should we look at outputs or at internals?* This question did not appear in 2026; it has been flowing through deep learning for a decade. This paper is the wave that splashes when that long river empties into the new estuary of "4-bit large models."

* Evolutionary Narrative

This river has two main headwaters that converge in 2026 at the estuary of "4-bit large models." We trace the main trunk — knowledge distillation — first, then explain why the tributary "how to fairly compare internal representations" was necessary, and finally watch how they merge into this paper.

** 2015 | Hinton KD: Let the Small Model Peek at the Teacher's Subtext

*** What problem it saw

Back then everyone realized that high performance required stacking models — training an ensemble, or training one enormous network. But none of that could go to production: too expensive, too slow. How do you pack all that capability into a deployable small model?

*** Its solution

Hinton's insight is elegant: don't train the small model against ground-truth labels — train it against the large model's *probability distribution*.

An analogy. A multiple-choice question has the ground-truth answer "this is the digit 2." But the teacher model's output is richer: "I'm 90% sure it's 2, but 8% it might be 3, and only 0.001% that it's 7." That subtle distinction — "looks like 3, nothing like 7" — is what Hinton calls *dark knowledge*. It leaks the teacher's understanding of the world: 2 and 3 really do resemble each other.

Raise the softmax temperature to amplify those tiny probabilities, then train the student to imitate the softened distribution (using KL divergence). What the student learns is not just the answer but the teacher's "texture of judgment."

*** Why it works — and what pit it leaves

It works because soft targets carry far more information per example than hard labels. On MNIST a student can even recognize digit classes it has never seen labeled, inferring them purely from the teacher's soft targets.

But the pit: *it supervises only the final output layer.* All those intermediate layers doing their own thing — no signal constrains them. For deep-and-thin students this is a hard limitation: deep networks are already hard to optimize, and gradient from a single output layer cannot propagate back far enough. That pit directly motivated the next paper.

** 2015 | FitNets: Align Not Just Answers but "Intermediate Notes"

*** What problem it saw

FitNets targeted exactly the pit Hinton left: with only output supervision, deep-and-thin students cannot be trained. Deep means strong non-linearity and difficult optimization; thin means narrow layers and limited capacity. A single output-layer supervision signal cannot reach the depths.

*** Its solution

Add intermediate supervision. Pick one of the teacher's hidden layers as a "hint," and make the student's corresponding "guided layer" predict the teacher's features at that layer (L2 loss). Because the student layer is narrower, a regressor bridges the dimension mismatch.

If Hinton had the student copy the teacher's final answer, FitNets has the student copy the teacher's scratch-paper notes as well.

*** Why it works — and what pit it leaves

It works because intermediate supervision gives deep networks an "anchor," effectively laying a gradient pathway in stages. The result: a student with 10× fewer parameters outperforms the teacher on CIFAR-10.

New pit: *forcing the student to point-wise replicate the teacher's entire feature tensor is too rigid.* The teacher's features contain many details that are useful only to the teacher; the student has neither the need nor the capacity to reproduce each one. The regressor is patching over a mismatch. This is compelling "equal activations," not "learned equal capability."

** 2017 | Attention Transfer: Copy the "Highlights," Not the Whole Book

*** What problem it saw

Attention Transfer saw FitNets' rigidity: replicating full high-dimensional feature tensors demands that the student match every channel and every value, including noise unique to the teacher.

*** Its solution

Don't transmit features; transmit *attention maps*. Collapse a feature tensor along the channel dimension (e.g., sum of squared activations per channel), yielding a 2D spatial map that shows *where* the network is attending. The student only needs to align this map.

Back to the analogy: FitNets had the student copy the teacher's whole notebook; Attention Transfer only asks the student to copy what the teacher underlined with a highlighter. Dimensionality drops by an order of magnitude and is independent of network width.

*** Why it works — and what pit it leaves

It works because "where the network attends" is a more fundamental and robust quantity than "the exact value of each neuron."

But the pit remains: what is transmitted is still a "concrete map." Whether two networks have learned similar capabilities does not fundamentally depend on what one activation map looks like — it depends on *how they organize relationships among samples.* That is the opening for the next paper.

** 2019 | Similarity-Preserving KD: Don't Transmit Coordinates; Transmit "Who Belongs with Whom"

*** What problem it saw

Tung & Mori took aim at all the preceding papers at once: whether FitNets' features or Attention Transfer's attention maps, the underlying demand is that the student *reproduce the teacher's representation space* — point by point, in the teacher's coordinate system. But the student has different capacity and structure; why should that coordinate system be appropriate?

*** Its solution

Reframe the question: what matters is not the "absolute coordinates" of activations but their *relational structure* — which inputs does the teacher treat as similar?

The key observation: semantically similar inputs elicit similar activation patterns. So for each batch of b samples, compute a b×b pairwise similarity matrix (inner products of activations), one for the teacher and one for the student, and train the student to match this *similarity matrix* rather than the activations themselves.

The analogy: earlier methods made the student memorize the coordinates the teacher assigned to every point. This method only requires the student to preserve "which points are close and which are far" — preserve the shape of the relationships, regardless of where they land.

This marks the turning point of the KD main trunk: *response-based (Hinton) → feature-based (FitNets, AT) → relation-based (Tung & Mori).*

*** Why it works — and what pit it leaves

It works because relational structure is the true substance of "understanding," and it naturally tolerates the student using its own coordinate system.

But a theoretical pit remains: *how should the similarity matrix be computed "fairly"?* Using raw inner products, a rotation or rescaling of representations changes the matrix. You might conclude two representations differ when they only differ in basis. Answering "do two sets of internal representations actually resemble each other?" requires a metric immune to irrelevant transformations. That question belongs to the other tributary.

** Tributary: 2017–2019 | From CCA to CKA — How to "Fairly" Compare Two Representations

*** What SVCCA / PWCCA saw

Earlier, Raghu (SVCCA, 2017) and Morcos (PWCCA, 2018) wanted to quantify "how similar are two sets of neural activations?" Their intuition was right: you should not look at axis-aligned neurons, because features can be distributed arbitrarily. So they used CCA (Canonical Correlation Analysis) — invariant to affine transformations and neuron permutations. PWCCA further weighted CCA directions by their contribution, suppressing noise directions.

*** What CKA saw

Kornblith (CKA, 2019) identified a fatal flaw in the CCA family: *invariance to "any invertible linear transformation."* That sounds desirable, but there is a theorem: as long as the representation width ≥ the number of data points, an invertible linear map can align any two sets of representations. Modern wide networks live exactly in that regime. As a result, CCA-class methods return meaningless values — they cannot even pass the basic sanity check of matching corresponding layers trained with different initializations.

*** Its solution

CKA makes a seemingly small but decisive subtraction: *require invariance only to orthogonal transformations (rotations, permutations) and isotropic scaling — not to invertible linear maps.*

Instead of aligning the representations directly, it compares the relationship between their respective Gram matrices, building on HSIC with normalization. Removing "invertible-linear invariance" cures the degeneracy — CKA passes layer-correspondence checks that CCA fails.

*** Why this matters

A good similarity metric must be immune to symmetries that "genuinely don't change meaning" (you can rotate, permute, or uniformly scale a hidden layer's basis without changing the network's function) while remaining sensitive to "genuinely important differences" (which directions carry most variance; whether two networks have learned corresponding features). Invertible-linear invariance is over-generalization — it erases real structure. CKA's narrower invariance sits in the sweet spot.

Note that Tung & Mori's relation matrix and CKA's Gram matrix are two independent discoveries of the same idea: "compare relationships, not coordinates." CKA supplies the theoretical foundation. That is why, a decade later, this paper reaches for CKA rather than simple MSE.

** Tributary: 2022 | Toy Models of Superposition — Why Internal Geometry Is So Fragile

*** What problem it saw

This paper does not critique anyone; it reveals a fact that makes "protecting internal geometry" a matter of survival.

Elhage et al. found that neural networks can *represent more features than they have dimensions*, by packing those features into *approximately orthogonal (not strictly orthogonal)* directions. High-dimensional space can accommodate far more "almost orthogonal" vectors than strictly orthogonal ones, at the cost of inter-feature interference that sparsity and ReLU non-linearity then clean up.

*** Why this redirects the whole river

(The causal step below is my reasoning; the original paper does not address quantization — noted honestly.)

If features live in "approximately orthogonal" directions, the angles and magnitudes between directions have *no slack* — the interference budget is already at its limit. Quantization (4 bits!) perturbs weights and activations at a magnitude comparable to that thin interference margin. Strictly orthogonal storage has headroom; superposition-based storage does not. So even a small perturbation can cause a direction to "cross-talk," flipping which feature it primarily encodes.

This is precisely why a "geometry-preserving" metric like CKA is needed to diagnose quantization damage: geometric distortions that accuracy metrics cannot reveal, CKA can illuminate. Both tributaries — "how to compare" and "why it is fragile" — are now in place, waiting for the main trunk to reach the 4-bit estuary.

** 2025-06 | NVFP4: Fitting 4 Bits with a Two-Level Jack

*** What problem it saw

The main trunk has now reached "large-model deployment." FP4 has only ~8 representable levels (E2M1, range approximately −6 to 6). Real tensors mix large and small values; with a single coarse scaling factor most levels are wasted and direct quantization destroys accuracy.

*** Its solution

NVIDIA's NVFP4 uses *two-level micro-scaling*:
- Elements are stored as 4-bit E2M1 values;
- Every 16 elements share one FP8 (E4M3) block scale — E4M3 has mantissa bits, so scaling is *fractional*, unlike MXFP4 which is restricted to powers of 2;
- A FP32 tensor-level scale keeps all block scales within E4M3's range.

Smaller blocks (16 vs. MXFP4's 32) → twice as many opportunities to match local dynamic range; fractional scaling → rounding error drops sharply.

*** What pit it leaves

The format-level precision is now adequate, but 4 bits is still 4 bits. Residual loss remains, especially in models that went through multiple post-training stages (SFT → RL → model merging). How to recover that last increment of accuracy?

** 2026-01 | QAD: Let the Full-Precision Model Teach Itself

*** What problem it saw

The NVIDIA Nemotron team found that plain QAT (retraining with task loss) is *unstable* on these post-trained models — it especially tends to destroy the reasoning capability built up by RL.

*** Its solution

QAD (Quantization-Aware Distillation): use the original BF16 model as a *frozen teacher*, and train the NVFP4 quantized student to match the teacher's output distribution with KL divergence at temperature T=1, in a single-stage post-training run.

Note that this is *self-distillation* — the original model teaches itself; the paper finds this beats using a larger external teacher. In essence, Hinton's recipe (KL + soft targets) is applied unchanged, eleven years later, to "quantization accuracy recovery." It sidesteps QAT instability, rescues RL-trained reasoning models (several points ahead of QAT on AIME25 and GPQA-D), and ships as the product Nemotron-Nano-3-30B-A3B-NVFP4.

*** What pit it leaves

The pit is buried in its proudest achievement: *it aligns only the output logits.* We are back to the pit Hinton left at the very start of this river — only the final layer is supervised; nobody watches the internals. Eleven years later, the pit is back, but the stakes are higher: at 4 bits, in RL reasoning models, once internal geometry drifts the consequences are collapse of reasoning and coding ability. And the KL objective itself cannot see that drift. This is the starting point of the target paper.

** 2026-06 | CKA-QAD: Matching Outputs Is Not Enough; the Internals Must Match Too

*** What problem it saw

The paper punctures QAD's comfort zone in one sentence: *"Output-layer alignment can mask internal representation degradation that the KL objective itself cannot detect."*

It provides quantitative evidence: many different intermediate-activation geometries can produce logits that match the teacher's — meaning output alignment places almost no constraint on internals. The more stinging finding: plain-KL QAD's *average CKA can actually fall below that of PTQ with no training at all.* In other words, you go through the trouble of distillation, the outputs align, and the internal geometry ends up more disordered than if you had done nothing. RL post-trained models drift especially severely.

*** Its solution

Connect both tributaries to the main trunk. On top of QAD's KL loss, add a *lightweight CKA regularizer*: align the Gram matrices of the student's and teacher's *decoder-block outputs*, layer by layer.

Why CKA rather than FitNets-style L2 feature matching? Because NVFP4 uses block-level scaling, which induces activation scaling drift — and linear CKA is invariant to orthogonal transformations and isotropic scaling (exactly the "subtraction" Kornblith made in 2019). Forcing "equal activations" via L2 would penalize the student for innocent scale differences; CKA only requires that the *geometric shape* be preserved.

Key engineering decisions:
- Loss = top-k KL (k=8192) + linear CKA (computed in feature space, avoiding the N×N Gram matrix);
- No manual λ tuning — use a stop-gradient ratio between the KL and CKA losses for *dynamic balancing*;
- Hooks attached to decoder-block outputs (all 52 blocks of Nemotron are matched); prenorm activations, attention weights, and KV-cache are excluded;
- Overhead is minimal: +0.5% step time, +7.0% peak memory, total <15%.

*** Why it works — and what it may leave behind

It works because it finally converts "the tool for diagnosing internal geometry" directly into "a training-time constraint." Verified on two models: on Qwen3-4B, AIME25 goes from 68.5→72.3, GPQA-D from 59.5→61.1, LiveCodeBench from 57.9→59.8; final-layer CKA is restored from 0.86 to 0.97.

What it may leave behind (my speculation): the relationship between CKA gain and accuracy gain is not linear — concurrent work has already observed this. A rise in CKA does not translate proportionally to a rise in benchmark scores; saturation exists. The authors also explicitly state this is *not meant to be a general-purpose distillation loss* — it is a targeted remedy for the specific scenario of NVFP4 quantization accuracy recovery, and may not transfer unchanged to other settings.

* Frontier Extensions

This paper is from June 2026, standing at the furthest point of this river. *No subsequent work has been found that explicitly cites and critiques it.* But two *concurrent (early-2026) parallel works* have independently walked in the same direction — not descendants of this paper but independent discoverers of the same insight, together marking the direction in which the river is changing course. (Honest notation: these are parallel relationships, not citation relationships.)

- *Self-Distillation Manifold Alignment* (arXiv 2604.15794): applies "aligning internal manifolds" to accuracy recovery after *pruning + NF4 quantization.* It likewise uses CKA to quantify student-teacher manifold alignment, and explicitly shifts the explanatory frame *from output distributions to the spatial structure of internal hidden states.* A useful honest footnote: it finds CKA improvement correlates strongly with performance recovery, but the *precise quantitative mapping has not been established* — a rise of x% in CKA does not mean a rise of x% in accuracy; the relationship may be non-linear or saturating.

- *Relational CKA (RCKA) for VLMs* (arXiv 2601.22709): in vision-language models, explicitly notes that *logit distillation cannot transmit the rich relational structure among visual tokens*, and uses RCKA to align the kernel matrix of penultimate-layer visual tokens. This is Tung & Mori's relational distillation echoing into the multimodal era.

Taken together, the signal is clear: in 2026, *"aligning internal representational geometry rather than merely aligning outputs"* is shifting from a diagnostic tool (CKA as health check) to a training objective (CKA as regularizer), and it is surfacing simultaneously in quantization, pruning, and multimodal compression.

* Insights

Tracing the whole river at once, the surface story is technical iteration; underneath is a *migration of understanding*.

*First insight: this is the "eleven-year return" of Hinton's pit.* In 2015, Hinton's KD supervised only outputs, leaving the "nobody watches the internals" pit open. The FitNets / AT / SP-KD line gradually moved inward over the era of small models, filling that pit. Then in 2026, QAD transplanted Hinton's original recipe into 4-bit large models — *and brought the pit along with it.* What CKA-QAD does is, in essence, re-teach the lessons that FitNets→SP-KD learned in the small-model era to the new domain of large-model quantization. History does not repeat; it rhymes.

*Second insight: measurement tools inevitably become optimization objectives.* CKA was born as a "health check" — judging whether two networks resemble each other. But a good enough metric will always eventually be moved from "post-hoc evaluation" to "in-training constraint." This happens repeatedly in this river: CCA was a diagnostic tool; SP-KD turned "relational similarity" into a loss. CKA was a diagnostic tool; this paper turns it into a regularizer. *Whatever can be measured precisely will eventually become something that can be optimized.*

*Third insight: the true enemy of compression is not error but the illusion of equivalence.* The paper's deepest line is "many different internal geometries can produce the same outputs." This means the fundamental difficulty of low-bit recovery is not "the error is large" but "it looks correct" — the optimizer finds the path of least resistance: align the outputs, leave the internals to drift. And Superposition tells us the internals have no error tolerance to spare. So between "surface compliance" and "internal health" lies exactly the gap the optimizer is most inclined to exploit. *You get only what you optimize for; whatever is not written into the loss, the optimizer will always sacrifice.*

*Where does the river flow next?* Three directions are visible: (1) from "aligning decoder-block outputs" toward "aligning finer-grained internal structures" (attention and KV-cache are currently excluded, but RL reasoning may depend on them precisely); (2) clarifying the "non-linear saturation" relationship between CKA and accuracy, so one knows when to stop aligning; (3) the "preserve internal geometry" principle spreading from quantization to all forms of compression (pruning, low-rank, MoE routing) — concurrent work has already validated it in pruning and VLMs.

* Inspiration

What this river teaches about *how to do research* comes down to one action: *find the places where the metric passes but something in you is uneasy.*

QAD's outputs matched tightly; everyone was satisfied — except someone asked the extra question "but what about the internals?" The entire paper grows out of that one follow-up. The best research questions are often not "how do I push this metric higher" but "this metric is passing — is something else quietly failing?" When everyone in a field is optimizing the same number, *the dimensions that number conceals are the gold mine.*

This suggests you can:
- *Run an internal health check on any "end-to-end passing" system.* Beyond output / accuracy / loss, is there a metric that reflects "internal health"? If so, wherever it diverges from the primary metric is where the next paper (or the next bug) lives.
- *Carry old tools to new estuaries.* CKA (2019) and KD (2015) are old ideas. The value of this paper is not invention but recognizing that the new scenario of "4-bit RL models" happens to need a previously overlooked property of the old tool (CKA's invariance to scaling ↔ NVFP4 block-scale drift). When reading classic papers, ask: "which property of this becomes suddenly valuable in today's new problem?"
- *Be wary of self-distillation as self-reassurance.* When you have a system imitate one of its own versions (self-distillation, self-consistency, self-reward), be especially alert to "output alignment masking internal drift" — because the more the teacher and student resemble each other, the more easily you relax after seeing only the output match.
