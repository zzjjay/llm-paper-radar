* The River of Problems

How bloated have code LLMs become? GPT-3 175B requires 325 GB of VRAM and five A100s just to load; CodeGen 16B still needs 72 GB. A single GPT-3 training run emits more CO2 than three transatlantic flights from SF to NY. And the big cost is not training — *inference* accounts for 80–90% of total energy during deployment, and by Google's own accounting, inference alone consumes 60% of total ML energy.

So "shrinking these giants" has become one of the hottest races in SE/NLP in recent years. Compression techniques are individually mature — distillation, pruning, quantization, NAS — each with a decade of baggage. But look at the actual landscape:

- Almost all SE compression work targets *encoder-only models* (the BERT family), and at most tackles clone detection;
- Any given paper applies *only one technique* — either distillation, or pruning, or quantization;
- *No one has systematically asked: when you chain multiple techniques together, does ordering matter?*

This is where CTT enters: it does not invent new compression algorithms — *it asks how a compression pipeline itself should be sequenced* — and anchors that question to a larger story: can we, like the economic concept of a "carbon tax," penalize wasteful model architectures and reward deployment-friendly ones?

Looking back, the backbone of this research line is a group of people gradually widening the scope of the same question: *from one model type to three, from one technique to four, from measuring accuracy alone to measuring accuracy + latency + memory + CO2*.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2019 | DistilBERT: KD Becomes the "Main Road" for Compression

*** What problem did it see?

By 2019, BERT-base was already 110M parameters / 420 MB; BERT-large was 340M / 1.3 GB. Cloud-hosted demos were fine, but every *on-device, IDE-plugin, or mobile* deployment scenario was completely blocked. The mainstream "compression" thinking at the time was pruning and quantization, but both felt like engineering hacks: are the pruned connections truly unnecessary? What do you do about the accuracy drop after INT8 quantization? Nobody had clean answers.

The deeper question: is BERT large because it genuinely needs that many parameters, or is there redundancy baked into its training objectives (MLM + NSP)? No one had systematically answered this.

*** Its solution

The DistilBERT team proposed: since BERT clearly has headroom on many tasks, have a small student model *mimic* its output distribution — not just mimicking hard labels, but mimicking the teacher's entire softmax distribution (soft labels). This is Hinton's 2015 KD, but DistilBERT is the first to treat it as the "main road for compressing BERT."

In practice: remove half of BERT's Transformer layers, then train with three losses — MLM loss (preserve language capacity) + KD loss (mimic the teacher) + cosine loss (align student hidden states with teacher). Result: model shrinks to 60%, runs 60% faster, retains 97% downstream task performance.

*** Why it works, and what trap it laid

The fundamental reason it works is that *soft labels carry far more information than hard labels*. When the teacher says "this sentence is 65% positive, 30% neutral, 5% negative," the student learns the entire semantic geometry, not just a single label.

But DistilBERT left two large potholes:

*First*, it is a *general NLP model*, not a code model. Code token distributions, syntax, and contextual structure differ substantially from natural language; whether this recipe transfers is untested.

*Second*, it is a single technique — KD only. No interaction with pruning, quantization, or NAS. Pruning removes parameters, quantization compresses precision, NAS reshapes architecture — *four levers are pulling the same cart, but nobody has pushed all of them together*.

** 2022 | Compressor: Compressing a Code Model to 3 MB

*** What problem did it see?

By 2022, CodeBERT and GraphCodeBERT had become standard in SE, but they were as large as BERT — CodeBERT at 500 MB. Developers wanting to ship *vulnerability detection* inside an IDE plugin or CI node found 500 MB completely infeasible.

The Compressor team (Jieke Shi et al.) tried DistilBERT's recipe on code and found two problems: *first*, how do you choose the student architecture? DistilBERT hand-picked "half the layers," which need not be optimal for code tasks; *second*, KD alone *hits a wall around 30 MB* — still an order of magnitude away from the 1–3 MB target for plugins.

*** Its solution

Compressor extended DistilBERT's KD framework in two steps:

1. *Genetic search for the student architecture* — instead of hand-tuning layer count and width, let evolutionary search find them;
2. *The search objective is a multi-objective Pareto front of "model size + task loss"* — you can get a 3 MB model, or a 10 MB model with better accuracy, as you prefer.

Result: CodeBERT compressed from 500 MB to 3 MB (160× compression), with minimal accuracy loss on binary classification tasks such as vulnerability and clone detection.

*** Why it works, and what trap it laid

Its real contribution is not technical novelty — genetic search + KD are both off-the-shelf — but *being the first to nail down the fact that a code model can be compressed to 3 MB*. Every subsequent SE compression paper treats that 3 MB as the floor.

But the potholes are clear:

- *Too narrow a task scope*: validated only on binary classification (vulnerability/clone detection); seq2seq and autoregressive tasks like code generation and summarization are untouched;
- *Too narrow an architecture scope*: only encoder-only models; BART/T5-style encoder-decoders and GPT-style decoder-only models completely ignored;
- *Energy never measured*: the model is smaller, but how much electricity does one inference actually save? How much CO2? Nobody computed it;
- *Genetic search is slow*: a full search run takes days of GPU time, poor engineering usability.

** 2024 | Avatar: The First Time "Carbon Emissions" Is Put on the Table

*** What problem did it see?

The same team (Jieke Shi) returned in 2024. They identified the two most painful gaps Compressor left: slow search and unmeasured energy.

The more important cognitive shift: *the research community was beginning to collectively realize that "smaller model" and "greener model" are not the same thing*. A poorly designed small model can actually consume more power than a large one (due to cache misses, poor kernel scheduling, etc.). So *latency, memory, and CO2* must become first-class optimization targets, not afterthoughts derived from parameter count.

*** Its solution

Avatar did three things:

1. *Replaced genetic search with SMT (Satisfiability Modulo Theories)-guided search* — an interpretable, exact solver that can directly constrain the feasible region ("latency ≤ X, memory ≤ Y") without blind exploration;
2. *Chained KD and quantization*: first KD to get a compact student, then INT8 quantization to compress it further;
3. *First time CO2 emission is reported as a metric*: used the CodeCarbon library to measure inference carbon, giving the phrase "greener language models for code" actual numbers.

Result on clone detection: model compressed to 3 MB while *inference CO2 is one order of magnitude lower* than the uncompressed model.

*** Why it works, and what trap it laid

Avatar is the first work to give *"green"* a concrete meaning in SE compression — not an abstract environmental slogan, but a measurable quantity like grams of CO2 per inference. It is also the first to show that *combining two compression techniques beats using either alone*.

But Avatar still left the two questions CTT would later pursue unanswered:

- *Does ordering matter?* Avatar does KD → quantize, never tested in reverse. It assumes this order is correct;
- *Why stop at two techniques?* Pruning and NAS are never brought in. The question "how should all four be ordered" goes unasked.

And — it is still encoder-only.

** 2025 | ALPINE: Drops the Language Dependency, But Abandons KD

*** What problem did it see?

The ALPINE team (Saad et al.) approached from a different angle: Avatar's route, while effective, requires *fully fine-tuning a student model* during the KD phase. For CodeBERT-scale (500 MB) models this is manageable, but for 1B+ code LLMs (CodeGen 16B, StarCoder), even loading the teacher into VRAM for KD is difficult, let alone fine-tuning a student.

A second observation: existing methods are tightly language-specific — a student trained on Python is useless on Java — but code models are inherently *cross-lingual*. Can we build a language-agnostic pruning strategy?

*** Its solution

ALPINE's approach is to *bypass KD and go with pure pruning + post-hoc quantization*:

1. *Adaptive pruning* — use statistical criteria to automatically identify which attention heads and FFN dimensions are unimportant across all languages, and remove them;
2. *Low-overhead quantization plugin* — apply 8-bit quantization after pruning, no retraining required.

Result: 58.1% memory saving, maintained performance across multiple languages, no KD fine-tuning needed.

*** Why it works, and what trap it laid

The core reason it works: *KD transfers performance from teacher to student, but the teacher itself is expensive; if you can operate directly on the teacher (prune + quantize), you never need the student as an intermediate*. This is important engineering realism for very large models.

But the potholes are significant:

- *Still encoder-only* — five years into this line, everyone is still compressing encoder-only models;
- *Dropping KD means dropping the "performance recovery" mechanism*: pruning and quantization are both subtractive; once accuracy drops, there is no way back;
- *NAS untouched*: no architectural-level changes;
- *Ordering still unaddressed*: the sequence of pruning and quantization is never systematically validated.

By this point the shape of the entire research line is clear: *five years in, everyone has been adding or swapping techniques, but nobody has systematically tested the meta-question of "how should the techniques be ordered."* And the field has remained locked in the encoder-only comfort zone throughout.

** 2026.04 | CTT: Giving the Compression Pipeline a Principled Ordering

*** What problem did it see?

The CTT team (Alam et al.) opens by critiquing each predecessor by name ([43] = ALPINE, [49] = Avatar, [50] = Compressor):

#+begin_quote
"Unlike prior pipelines [43, 49, 50], which are limited to single architectures and do not examine how ordering impacts results under deployment budgets, CTT provides a unified framework..."
#+end_quote

The specific problems they identified:

1. *Architecture coverage*: Five years of SE compression work almost exclusively targets encoder-only models. But today's code LLMs fall into three distinct families — encoder-only (clone detection), encoder-decoder (summarization), decoder-only (generation) — with fundamentally different inference bottlenecks (attention-dominated vs. cross-attention-dominated vs. autoregressive serial). Success on one proves nothing for the others;
2. *Combination ordering*: NAS, pruning, quantization, and KD have been used at most pairwise, but *whether NAS-then-prune beats prune-then-NAS, or whether quantize-then-distill beats distill-then-quantize* has never been tested systematically. This is CTT's most critical claim: "ordering is not incidental, it's a key optimization factor";
3. *Missing budget perspective*: Existing work follows a "compress then see what happens" approach; there is no framework for "given hard constraints of latency ≤ X and memory ≤ Y, find the optimal compression combination";
4. *Absent economic framing*: Compression is fundamentally about "trading a bit of accuracy for engineering advantage," yet nobody has articulated this using the principled trade-off language of economics.

*** Its solution

CTT anchors its entire solution with the "carbon tax" metaphor:

#+begin_example
Tax (levy)                            Rebate
+-----------------------+              +-----------------+
| Stage 1: NAS + Pruning |              | Stage 3: KD     |
|   Tax base = arch.     | -----> ----> | Use teacher     |
|              redundancy|              | soft labels to  |
|   Tax rate = perf. loss|              | recover the     |
|                        |              | withheld        |
|                        |              | performance     |
+-----------------------+              +-----------------+
| Stage 2: Quantization  |
|   Tax base = numerical  |
|              precision  |
|   Tax rate = quant.     |
|              error      |
+-----------------------+
#+end_example

Concrete execution:

1. *Stage 1 — structural tax*: First use NAS to search a compact student skeleton across four dimensions (hidden size, FFN width, layer count, head count), then apply structured pruning to remove any remaining redundant layers/heads in that skeleton. NAS is too coarse-grained alone; pruning alone is too scattered; the combination is just right;
2. *Stage 2 — precision tax*: Perform quantization-aware initialization *before* KD — quantize the student to INT8 first, then distill under quantization noise. The ordering is critical: *quantize first, then distill* forces the student to learn to maintain performance under quantization noise from the very start, rather than distilling cleanly and then discovering a 5-point drop after post-hoc quantization;
3. *Stage 3 — rebate*: Use the teacher's soft labels to recover the performance lost in the two preceding stages.

The pipeline's central claim: *NAS → pruning → quantization → KD is the optimal ordering*. Ablation experiments show that any other ordering — KD first, quantization first, NAS last — degrades performance.

*** Why it works, and what trap it laid

The real reason it works is not novelty in any single component — all four are off-the-shelf — but that it is *the first work to treat "the ordering of a compression pipeline" as a first-class research problem*. This question had been lurking below the surface for years; CTT lifts it into plain view and provides an answer that holds across three architectures and three tasks.

The "tax + rebate" economic metaphor is not mere decoration: it genuinely explains why *levy-first, recover-later* outperforms *levy-and-recover simultaneously* — KD as a rebate needs a sufficiently well-defined "tax base" (a compact architecture + low precision) to target precisely; if you distill while compressing, the teacher cannot know what constraints the student will ultimately run under, so the soft-label signal is less informative.

The most striking experimental numbers:
- *Encoder-only (clone detection)*: 8–10× speedup, ~98% accuracy retained;
- *Encoder-decoder (summarization)*: 3× speedup, ~89% BLEU/ROUGE retained;
- *Decoder-only (generation)*: 4–7× speedup, 91% textual metric / 68% pass@1 retained;
- *Up to 49× memory compression, 81% CO2 reduction*;
- *CTT's compressed CodeT5 is 73× faster, uses 87× less memory, and emits 46× less CO2 than LLM.int8()-quantized CodeT5* — strong evidence that "quantization alone is not enough; the structure must be compressed too."

But CTT also explicitly acknowledges several unfilled gaps:

- *Capped at 1.5B*: Beyond that (10B+), fitting both student and teacher in VRAM simultaneously is infeasible; scaling up requires engineering breakthroughs;
- *Task-specific students*: No work on a "generalist" student that handles all three SE tasks simultaneously;
- *Fixed INT8 quantizer*: More modern quantizers like GPTQ/AWQ are claimed to be "plug-and-play replaceable," but were never actually swapped in;
- *Empirical optimality of ordering*: Ablations show NAS→pruning→quantization→KD is best in their tested settings, but there is no theoretical proof it is universally optimal.

* Frontier Extensions

CTT appeared in late April 2026, and as of today (2026-05-28) only one month has passed. *There is not yet any subsequent work that explicitly critiques and improves upon CTT.* However, several parallel, complementary, or contrasting directions are worth noting:

** Same Era | The Quantizer Camp: LLM.int8() / AWQ / GPTQ

CTT uses these as *counter-examples in the compression pipeline* — "if you only quantize without touching structure, it looks cheap but loses to a systematic pipeline." Specific evidence:

- *LLM.int8()-quantized CodeT5*: 73× slower than CTT's student, 87× more memory, 46× more CO2;
- *AWQ-quantized Qwen Coder 2.5 1.5B-instruct*: 5× larger than CTT's student, 4× more memory, 3× more CO2.

CTT's stance: *quantizers are "components," the pipeline is the "skeleton." Components can be swapped (GPTQ can replace INT8), but the paradigm of "structure first, precision second, then rebate" cannot change.*

** Same Era | The PEFT Camp: LoRA / Adapter / BitFit / Prefix-Tuning

CTT draws a clear boundary:

#+begin_quote
"PEFT methods still keep the entire base model in memory at inference time, so the overall latency, peak VRAM, and energy use remain essentially unchanged."
#+end_quote

In other words, PEFT addresses the *training cost* problem, not the *inference deployment* problem. CTT addresses the latter. The two are orthogonal.

** Likely Future Directions (Speculative)

Based on the limitations CTT itself lists, the most probable next steps are:

1. *Scale to 10B+*: How to perform KD when you cannot fit teacher and student in VRAM simultaneously? Distributed teacher inference or offline logit caching;
2. *Generalist student*: A single 1B student that handles clone detection + summarization + generation, moving CTT from task-specific to task-agnostic;
3. *Automated ordering search*: Make the fixed NAS→pruning→quantization→KD ordering learnable, so a model selects the optimal pipeline topology given task and deployment budget;
4. *Deep integration with modern quantizers*: Actually wire GPTQ/AWQ into Stage 2 rather than merely claiming they are plug-and-play;
5. *Carbon tax on the training side*: CTT currently taxes only inference; the enormous search cost of the NAS stage is unaccounted for. The natural next step is a *full lifecycle* carbon tax.

* Insight

On the surface this evolutionary line is "a history of code model compression technique progress." Look deeper and it is something else — *a field's journey from "technique stacking" to "technique orchestration."*

Examine the cognitive shift across five generations:

1. *DistilBERT* (2019): discovered that *KD can serve as the main road for compression* (previously it was pruning/quantization);
2. *Compressor* (2022): discovered that *student architecture should be searched* (previously hand-designed);
3. *Avatar* (2024): discovered that *energy/CO2 should be first-class metrics* (previously only accuracy mattered);
4. *ALPINE* (2025): discovered that *KD is not mandatory — pruning + quantization can get you there* (previously KD was assumed);
5. *CTT* (2026): discovered that *the combination ordering itself is an independent optimization variable* (previously any ordering was assumed to be fine).

Each generation broke the "obviously true" default assumption of the previous one. So where does the next step go? The most likely guess is that *the compression pipeline itself will be automated*. CTT hardcodes the "structure first, precision second, then rebate" ordering; the next generation will likely make it *learnable* — let a model choose the optimal pipeline topology given task, deployment budget, and hardware. This amounts to upgrading from "AutoML for student architecture" to "AutoML for compression pipeline."

Another clear direction is *making the carbon-tax framework end-to-end differentiable*: CTT's current "tax rates" (performance penalties) and "tax bases" (architectural redundancy) are human-specified. In principle, a meta-controller could learn to tune them, allowing the entire pipeline to automatically navigate the three-dimensional space of performance–carbon–latency.

* Inspiration

The implications for "how to find a research problem" are very concrete:

- *Everyone's shared default assumption is a gold mine.* For five years after DistilBERT, every compression paper silently assumed "when combining multiple techniques, ordering doesn't matter." CTT invented no new techniques; it simply put that default assumption under scrutiny and found it was wrong — and published in FSE. *A default assumption shared by a whole field is often the doorway to the next generation of work.*

- *Expanding the scope of the question contributes more than refining the answer.* The real progress in this line is not "I compressed it by a few more MB." It is a series of *scope expansions*: from general NLP to code; from binary classification to generation; from single techniques to combinations; from one architecture to three; from accuracy alone to carbon emissions. *Research progress often means "the questions we can ask have grown larger," not just "our numbers are better."*

- *Giving an old technique a new metaphor can open new territory.* NAS, pruning, quantization, and KD are none of them new, but CTT re-threaded them with the economic metaphor of "carbon tax + rebate" — *a metaphor transforms a messy engineering combination into a principled methodology.* This is both a writing technique and a research technique: find a single unifying analogy that holds all the components together, and the conceptual level of the paper rises.

- *Always ask "where is this field comfortable."* Five years of SE compression staying inside encoder-only is very suspicious — not because researchers are lazy, but because encoder-only is the easiest to evaluate: the most complete datasets, the clearest baselines. A field's comfort zone is the direction the next generation should expand into.

This suggests a concrete move: pick some step that the engineering community treats as "obviously fine" — pipeline ordering, initialization strategy, data ordering, retry logic — pull it out of the implicit and make it explicit, *and use ablation experiments to show it actually matters*. Once you prove "this is not a detail, it is a core variable," you have added a new optimization dimension to the entire field.

* Honesty Labels

- The citation chain CTT → ALPINE [43] / Avatar [49] / Compressor [50] is *directly evidenced in the paper's related work and introduction*, where the original text states: "Unlike prior pipelines [43, 49, 50], which are limited to single architectures and do not examine how ordering impacts results." This is direct evidence, not inference.
- The chain ALPINE → Avatar → Compressor is *inferred but reasonable*: all three are central SE code-model compression papers; Avatar and Compressor are two successive works from the same Jieke Shi team (an explicit progression), and ALPINE continues to compare against Avatar in its related work; however, CTT does not reconstruct this internal evolutionary causal chain — I assembled it from timeline and technical continuity.
- Compressor → DistilBERT is *inferred*: Compressor does not directly cite DistilBERT as a methodological basis, but the way it uses KD (student mimicking teacher's soft labels) is squarely in the DistilBERT tradition. Treating it as "the starting point of KD-based compression for code models" is a reasonable simplification, but not a strict "critique-and-improve" relationship.
- CTT has 71 references in total. This article selected the *most direct critique-and-improve chain* (ALPINE/Avatar/Compressor) as the main line, setting aside other branches (e.g., BERT-era quantization work like Q8BERT/DoReFa-Net, Lottery Ticket Hypothesis pruning theory, etc.).
- LLM.int8() / GPTQ / AWQ / LoRA are *same-era contrasts*, not successors; CTT uses them as ablation comparisons or boundary markers.
- *As of 2026-05-28, no subsequent work explicitly critiquing and improving CTT has been identified* — CTT was published only one month ago. The "Frontier Extensions" section is a reasoned extrapolation from CTT's self-declared limitations, not a description of research that has already happened.
