* The River of the Problem

How does a small model learn from a large one?

It sounds like tutoring: the teacher explains everything they know, and the student follows along. But in practice, problems surface immediately — some of the teacher's examples the student has already mastered, so hearing them again is wasted breath; others are so far beyond the student's reach that they learn nothing and instead scramble what they already knew. "Mimic every output the teacher produces" has been the default in LLM distillation for a decade, and it has been the pain point for just as long.

Every paper along this line is chasing the same question: *when you pour a teacher's knowledge into a student's head, what exactly should you pour, how, and when?* Early debates focused on the form of the signal (hard labels vs. soft distributions), then researchers realized the direction of fitting also mattered (should the student chase the teacher, or the teacher chase the student?), then came the insight that the student should generate its own practice problems rather than just study the teacher's reference answers — and finally we arrive at PACED: *which problems to train on should depend on where the student is actually stuck right now.*

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1978 | Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development: The Distant Ancestor

Strictly speaking this is not a paper but a concept from Soviet psychologist Vygotsky, proposed in the early twentieth century. Yet the soul of the entire PACED paper flows from here, so it deserves a mention.

Vygotsky observed that a child learns most efficiently not in the zone of "things they can already do independently" (too easy, no gain), nor in the zone of "things they cannot do regardless" (too hard, only frustration), but in the middle band of "things they can do with a little help from an adult" — a zone he called the *Zone of Proximal Development* (ZPD).

The concept became famous in educational psychology but lay dormant in machine learning for decades. It wasn't until 2026 that PACED formally brought it into neural-network training, embodying it in a single clean formula: =w(p) = p(1-p)=, which peaks at p = 0.5 and goes to zero at p = 0 and p = 1. A symmetric bell-shaped curve — surprisingly tidy for a 2026 AI paper.

Every paper discussed below is, in essence, searching for an engineering path that realizes this intuition in mathematics.

** 2015 | Hinton 2015: Dark Knowledge Lives in Soft Distributions

*** What Problem It Saw

A small model struggles to learn the "depth of knowledge" of a large one from hard labels alone. Labeling an image "cat" tells the model "this is a cat, everything else is not." But a large model actually knows "this is a cat, though it looks a bit like a dog, and definitely not a car" — that *relative confidence across wrong answers* is the most valuable thing the teacher possesses, and a hard label discards it entirely.

*** Its Solution

Have the student learn the teacher's *soft distribution*, not its argmax. In practice: raise the temperature of the teacher's softmax (flattening the distribution), then train the student to match it. With higher temperature, structure like "cat 80% / dog 15% / car 0.001%" is passed explicitly to the student.

This is what later became known as *dark knowledge* — the knowledge hidden in the shadows of the softmax.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Left)

The soft distribution is equivalent to telling the student "how similar the teacher considers these classes to be," effectively providing a free similarity matrix. The student uses it as a regularizer and learns more stably than from hard labels.

The pit it left: Hinton only considered *classification*, where each sample yields one softmax vector — a simple structure. Once the output is a *sequence* (machine translation, dialogue), things get complicated: a sequence has hundreds of tokens, each with its own softmax, all ordered and mutually dependent. Should every token be aligned? Align at the token level or the sentence level?

** 2016 | Kim & Rush: Sequence-Level Distillation

*** What Problem It Saw

Applying Hinton's framework to neural machine translation, the natural first attempt is token-level soft-distribution alignment (word-level KD). But translation quality gains were limited. Kim & Rush identified the issue: translation quality is a *whole-sentence* property; dragging each token's distribution closer does not guarantee a good overall translation — just as phoneme-by-phoneme mimicry of a native speaker doesn't produce natural speech.

*** Its Solution

Abandon token-level distribution alignment entirely. Instead, have the teacher *generate* complete translated sentences (beam-search best output), treat them as ground-truth labels, and train the student with ordinary cross-entropy. Token-level soft distributions are replaced by *sentence-level hard labels*.

This sounds like a regression to hard labels — but the crucial difference is "the labels are generated by the teacher": they represent the teacher's best answer *given its full understanding*, and that tends to fit the student's model capacity better than human-authored reference translations in the original data.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Left)

Teacher-generated sentences are "just digestible" for the student — lower complexity than real data, yet covering all of the teacher's common patterns. The student learns as if from a well-simplified textbook, with high efficiency.

The pit: the student now learns from *fixed sentences the teacher produced*. Whatever the teacher generates, the student is forced to imitate — if the teacher tends toward bland output in some regions (mode collapse), the student is locked in that blandness.

A subtler issue also emerged: *which direction of KL* to use for token-level alignment started to matter. Hinton implicitly used forward KL (student covers the teacher's full distribution), but in the LLM era this would cause serious problems.

** 2023 | Gu et al. — MiniLLM: Don't Make the Student Cover the Teacher's Long Tail

*** What Problem It Saw

LLM output distributions have a characteristic: at most positions the teacher's "correct-answer distribution" is *sharp* (a few tokens take 90% of the probability); but at some positions the teacher is genuinely uncertain and the distribution spreads across a long tail of low-probability tokens.

Training with forward KL (KL(teacher ‖ student)) forces the student to *cover every part of the teacher's distribution*, including the tails the teacher itself isn't confident about. Result: the student, trying not to miss those tails, spreads its probability mass and output quality degrades. This phenomenon is called *mode-covering* — trying to cover everything means excelling at nothing.

*** Its Solution

Flip the direction. Use *reverse KL* (KL(student ‖ teacher)). Intuitively, reverse KL penalizes the student only when it fails to match where *the teacher is confident*, and does not demand coverage of low-probability tails. This makes the student *mode-seeking* — it latches onto the teacher's few sharp peaks, and generation quality actually improves.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Left)

In real LLM deployment we want the student to *decisively* produce good answers, not waste effort on low-probability tails. Reverse KL aligns the training objective with this goal.

The pit: regardless of forward or reverse KL, *the student is still trained on sentences the teacher generated* (teacher-forcing mode). But at inference the student generates *its own sentences one by one*. This training-inference distribution mismatch is amplified in the LLM era — the student has never been trained on its own generation trajectories, so when it encounters a strange prefix it produced itself, it has no idea how to continue.

** 2024 | Agarwal et al. — GKD: Pull the Student's Own Rollouts Back for Training

*** What Problem It Saw

MiniLLM solved the "KL direction" problem but not the "source of training data" problem. The student always trains on inputs carefully constructed by the teacher and never sees what it itself generates. When deployed for free generation, errors snowball along the student's own trajectory (the LLM-era incarnation of exposure bias).

*** Its Solution

*On-policy distillation* — have the student first generate a response (rollout), then have the teacher perform *scoring-style alignment* against it (at each position of the student's rollout, tell the student "what the teacher would choose here"). Training data shifts from "teacher-generated" to "student-generated + teacher-annotated."

GKD also introduced a unification: forward KL, reverse KL, and Jensen-Shannon divergence are all subsumed into a single generalized KL framework (GKD = Generalized Knowledge Distillation), making them freely mixable.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Left)

The student is trained on its own trajectories, so the distribution it encounters at deployment is the same one seen during training — robustness improves dramatically. LinkedIn / DeepMind / Anthropic and a series of subsequent works all adopted on-policy distillation as the default configuration.

The pit — which is exactly what PACED addresses: on-policy lets the student generate rollouts, *but all problems are still weighted equally*. A problem the student solves correctly in all 8 rollouts (= already mastered), one it fails in all 8 (= completely beyond it), and one it gets right in 4 out of 8 (= actively learning) receive identical weight.

The first category wastes compute; the last category (all wrong) is worse — gradients point in nearly random directions, injecting noise into the optimizer and erasing knowledge already acquired (catastrophic forgetting).

Who decides *which problems should be practiced*?

** 2026 | Xu et al. — PACED: Let the Student's Own Pass Rate Decide What to Train On

*** What Problem It Saw

The PACED team did something no one had done systematically before: directly *measured* how useful the gradient signal from problems of different difficulty is during distillation training.

Their metric is the *cross-problem gradient SNR* — how consistent in direction the gradients from a batch of problems are. Consistency = signal; scatter = noise. The results (paper Figure 2) are striking:

- p ≈ 0 (student always wrong): large but chaotic gradients, averaging to near noise
- p ≈ 1 (student always right): each problem's gradient is "fine-tune a little more," but the fine-tuning directions differ across problems and cancel out
- p ≈ 0.5 (student half right, half wrong): gradients are highly aligned, SNR is maximized

A clean bell curve. Vygotsky's ZPD was directly observed in a neural network for the first time.

*** Its Solution

Simply multiply each problem's loss by the weight =w(p) = p(1-p)=. Here p is the student's pass rate on that problem, computed from K rollouts. When p = 0 or p = 1 the weight is zero (problem automatically skipped); the weight is maximized at p = 0.5.

*No hyperparameters.* No learning-rate schedule, no difficulty threshold, no curriculum schedule. A single closed-form function.

On the theory side, the authors prove that under the mild assumption of "boundary power-law decay," the Beta kernel =w(p) = p^α(1-p)^β= is the leading-order optimal weighting family, and is minimax-robust — even if you misestimate p, the worst-case efficiency loss is only O(δ²).

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Left)

This single change solves two things simultaneously:
1. *Plasticity* — concentrates compute on problems the student can actually learn from, maximizing return per unit of compute
2. *Stability* — automatically excludes problems the student cannot learn, preventing noisy gradients from overwriting already-acquired knowledge

Empirically: MMLU forgetting rate drops to 1.4% / 0.6% (distillation / self-distillation), while AIME 2024 / 2025 scores improve +3.9 / +8.2 over unweighted training and +3.6 over the strong baseline AKL. Achieving stability and plasticity simultaneously like this is rare.

Remaining pits:
- Student rollouts must be re-run periodically to recompute pass rates (non-zero compute cost)
- Only binary right/wrong signal is used; partial credit and reasoning-chain quality are ignored
- The Beta kernel's α and β still need to be chosen (though α = β = 1 already works well) — is a fully automatic solution possible?

* Frontier Extensions

PACED is a March 2026 preprint; the current version is v3 (April). Contemporaneous and subsequent work is pushing along several lines.

** 2026 | ACE (Xu et al.) — Fine-Grained Weighting at the Rollout Level

PACED weights at the *problem* level (all rollouts of a problem share one p). ACE goes further to the *rollout* level — different rollouts of the same problem can receive different weights based on each rollout's specific quality. The PACED authors explicitly note the two approaches are *orthogonal and composable*.

** 2026 | SDFT (Shenfeld et al.) — Formalizing Self-Distillation

SDFT formalizes the self-distillation configuration in which "teacher = an earlier version of the student." PACED's self-distillation experiments (Qwen2.5-Math-7B → itself) directly adopt this framing.

** 2026 | Sang et al. — Conciseness-Guided Self-Distillation

A collaborator of PACED, Sang, published a separate paper the same year focusing on "self-distillation that steers the student toward more concise outputs" — a different facet of the same direction from the same group.

** A New Direction

PACED pushes the observation that "pass rate is a freely available difficulty signal" to its limit. A predictable next step: using *richer* student signals (not just right/wrong, but also uncertainty, reasoning-chain length, confidence calibration) for finer-grained problem selection. Pass rate is v1; the future is likely a *competence vector* — a problem is characterized not just by "can the student do it" but also "how robustly" and "how efficiently."

* Insights

Stepping back and looking at this decade-long evolutionary arc, a hidden melody emerges: *authority is gradually transferred from the "teacher" to the "student."*

- Hinton era: teacher provides soft distributions, student accepts everything wholesale
- Kim & Rush: teacher generates full sentences, student hard-learns them
- MiniLLM: teacher still leads, but the student *admits it cannot cover everything* (reverse KL deliberately abandons long-tail coverage)
- GKD: training data shifts from teacher-generated to *student-generated*; teacher is demoted to "grader"
- PACED: even "which problems to practice" is determined by *the student's current ability*; the teacher is now entirely reactive

This is actually a major shift in *training paradigm* — from teacher-driven to student-signal-driven. The trajectory is nearly isomorphic to the RL era's shift from "reward designed by humans" to "reward from the environment": the student itself is an "environment" that feeds back its own capability boundary (pass rate), and training becomes a process of fitting the signal near that boundary.

Second observation: *simplicity beats complexity.* PACED invented no new loss function, no new architecture, no new training loop. It simply multiplies the existing GKD training loss by =p * (1 - p)=. That this works and outperforms far more complex baselines suggests that *a substantial portion of the complexity accumulated over the past decade was misaligned* — it didn't address the root problem, only added surface elaboration. A plain bell-shaped weighting curve outperforms many intricate schemes.

Third observation: *the most widely cited psychological intuition lay dormant in AI for fifty years before being engineered.* Vygotsky's ZPD from 1978 is effectively the founding concept of the entire curriculum-learning field. Yet it took until PACED for someone to express it as a clean mathematical training objective. This implies: *old concepts from other disciplines may still hide plenty of un-engineered gold* — not because the concepts are wrong, but because no one has yet found the corresponding "two-line code change."

Where does it go next? My bet:
1. *Richer student signals* — not just pass rate, but also confidence, reasoning-chain quality, efficiency
2. *Dynamic difficulty generation* — since problem difficulty is decided by the student, let the student *generate* problems at exactly the right difficulty
3. *PACED + RL merger* — pass rate is a weak reward signal; PACED is already doing implicit reward shaping

* Takeaways

If you do *research*:

- *Measure first, invent second.* The core of PACED is not "proposing the p(1-p) formula" but "being the first to *directly measure* gradient SNR across problems of different difficulty." Real progress in many fields comes from a simple measurement, not a clever method. Before trying to build something new, ask yourself: *What does the current situation actually look like? Has anyone truly measured it?*

- *Simple method + clean theory + strong experiments = high-citation paper template.* PACED has zero hyperparameters, a closed-form optimality proof via Beta kernels, and experiments spanning three model families against four baselines. This combination leaves reviewers with little to object to.

- *Find old concepts from outside your field + find an engineering path.* Vygotsky's ZPD is realized in PACED so naturally that you wonder "how did fifty years pass without anyone doing this?" Other disciplines — psychology, biology, education, cybernetics — still harbor many concepts waiting to be engineered.

If you do *engineering*:

- Any code that "treats all X identically" is suspect. Equal weights for all training samples looks like an obvious default, but PACED shows it is actually a massive source of waste. Does your codebase have loops like "do Y for every X"? Should those X actually *be treated differently*?

- *Student signals are more valuable than teacher signals.* In any system with a teacher/student configuration (in any form of collaborative setup), prioritize collecting the student's current state rather than working harder to optimize the teacher's output.

If you work in *product*:

- Product training is the same: paying users teach you "what kind of person is willing to pay," but *users on the verge of churning* teach you "what's blocking people at the door." Find the features with retention rates hovering around 50% and polish them — their ROI is far higher than features that are already stable or features that don't work at all. That's the product version of PACED.
