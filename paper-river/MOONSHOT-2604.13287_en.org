* The Story in One Line

A team wants to prune away the majority of parameters from a trained network without retraining it. Over thirty-plus years the field kept revising its answer to "how do you pick what to prune?"—first by weight magnitude, then by "how much would the training loss rise if this were removed?" (a global gradient signal), and finally by "does this layer's output still look like the original?" (a local reconstruction signal). Standing in 2026 and looking back, MOONSHOT argues: you have always been forced to pick one signal or the other, but each one only sees half the picture—just add them together.

* Layer 1: 1989 — On What Grounds Should a Weight Be Pruned?

*Protagonists*: LeCun, Denker, Solla — Optimal Brain Damage (OBD)

*The problem they saw*: At the time, the standard practice was to prune by the most naïve criterion imaginable—prune whichever weights have the smallest absolute value. LeCun argued this was far too crude. A weight with value =0.001= doesn't mean it is unimportant: if it sits at a point of very steep gradient, removing it could blow up the training loss. Conversely, a weight of =0.5= looks large but may sit in a flat region and be nearly harmless to remove. "Importance" cannot be read off the weight alone; it must be measured by *how much the loss function changes when the weight is removed*.

*Their solution*: A second-order Taylor expansion. Around the trained weight =w*=, the loss can be approximated as a quadratic—first-order terms vanish because the network has converged, so all the information lives in the Hessian. OBD further simplifies by assuming the Hessian is diagonal; each weight's importance score becomes =0.5 * H_ii * w_i^2=. Prune those with the lowest scores.

*What they left open*: The "diagonal Hessian" assumption is too crude. In real networks, weights are tightly coupled—removing one requires adjusting its neighbors to compensate. OBD has no mechanism for that compensation at all.

* Layer 2: 1992 — After Pruning, Can We "Patch Up" the Remaining Weights?

*Protagonists*: Hassibi & Stork — Optimal Brain Surgeon (OBS)

*What they saw wrong with OBD*: Exactly the above. Because OBD assumes a diagonal Hessian, pruning one weight has no effect on any other. OBS says: "We should not only decide which weight to prune; we should also compute exactly how the remaining weights need to shift to compensate."

*Their solution*: Drop the diagonal assumption and use the *full inverse Hessian*. The importance score for weight =i= becomes =w_i^2 / [H^{-1}]_ii=; after pruning, the remaining weights receive a closed-form compensation update. This is the celebrated "Optimal Brain Surgeon" formula. In theory, every single pruning step minimises the resulting increase in loss.

*What they left open*: The full inverse Hessian. For a layer with N parameters, the Hessian is N×N, and inverting it costs O(N^3). In 1992, networks had thousands of parameters—manageable. In 2017, tens of millions; in 2023, tens of billions. This formula was simply crushed by scale.

* Layer 3: 2017 — The Hessian Is Too Large; Can We Go Layer by Layer?

*Protagonists*: Dong, Chen, Pan — Layer-wise OBS (L-OBS)

*What they saw wrong with OBS*: The above. Computing the Hessian over the full network is infeasible. Dong et al. proposed a critically important strategic shift: *stop asking how removing a weight affects the training loss; instead ask how it affects that layer's output*. Each layer is handled independently, with its own small Hessian sized only to that layer's parameters.

*Why this shift matters*: From this point on, *the pruning objective itself changed*. Previously it was "how much does the training loss rise?" (a global signal). Now it is "does this layer's output still resemble the original?" (a local reconstruction signal). These two objectives are conceptually distinct: one draws on *gradient information* (what the network truly cares about); the other draws on *activation information* (what data actually passed through this layer). L-OBS is the first paper to make this move explicitly, though it still uses the OBS machinery.

*What they left open*: Even layer-by-layer, each layer's Hessian is still non-trivial, and the update formulas remain complicated. Applying this directly to a Transformer with tens of billions of parameters was still too slow to be practical.

* Layer 4: 2022 — Making "Layer Reconstruction Error" Actually Work

*Protagonists*: Frantar et al. — Optimal Brain Compression (OBC)

*What they saw wrong with L-OBS*: The right idea, but not engineered cleanly. OBC inherits the spirit of L-OBS—*focus solely on per-layer output reconstruction error, discard the training loss*—and then notices something beautiful: once the objective is reconstruction error, the Hessian for each row of weights depends only on the *input data*, not on the weights themselves, and every row's Hessian is *the same matrix*. This means the whole layer's Hessian is "block-diagonal with all blocks identical"—invert once and you're done for the entire layer.

*Their solution*: Exploiting this structure, OBC delivers an efficient weight-by-weight pruning algorithm with closed-form updates, making the OBS idea practically fast enough for ResNet-50 and BERT-scale models. "Layer reconstruction error" became the mainstream objective from this point.

*What they left open*: At the scale of hundreds-of-millions to billions of parameters it was fine, but at the GPT-3 / Llama level—tens of billions of parameters—weight-by-weight processing was still too slow.

* Layer 5: 2023 — Reaching the LLM Scale

*Protagonists*: Two teams emerged simultaneously, but from different starting points.

** SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh)

*Their improvement over OBC*: OBC prunes one weight at a time. SparseGPT prunes an entire column at once, coupled with a clever "column-by-column sweep with residual updates" procedure. The result: OPT-175B can be pruned to 50% sparsity in a matter of hours. This was the first time the "layer-reconstruction" line scaled to LLMs.

** Wanda (Sun et al.)

*Their stance is even more aggressive*: Wanda dispenses with the Hessian entirely. Importance is simply =|W| * ||X||_2=—weight magnitude times the activation norm of the corresponding input. No Hessian, no inverse-matrix updates. Surprisingly, performance is competitive with SparseGPT in many settings, at an order-of-magnitude lower computational cost.

** CAP (Kuznedelev et al.)

*A different path*: CAP brings the OBS philosophy back—it still uses a *second-order Taylor approximation to the training loss* (not layer reconstruction) but applies various Hessian approximations to make it scale to Vision Transformers. This amounts to an explicit choice to stay on the "global gradient signal" side.

*What they collectively left open*: The LLM pruning community had split into two camps—"layer reconstruction error" (SparseGPT / Wanda / OBC) versus "Taylor-approximated training loss" (CAP / OBS lineage). Each camp claimed its objective was more principled, but nobody had ever conducted a serious apples-to-apples comparison.

* Layer 6 (Final): 2026 — MOONSHOT: Stop Arguing; Both Are Right

*Protagonists*: Afriat, Meng, Ibrahim, Hazimeh, Mazumder — MOONSHOT

*What they saw wrong with all of the above*: They did something nobody had done carefully before—*paired each method with its "opposite" objective and ran the same experiments*. CAP (designed to consume the Taylor loss) was fed the layer reconstruction error; SparseGPT/Wanda (designed to consume layer reconstruction error) were fed the Taylor loss. The results were striking:

- In some settings, a method performed *better* with the objective it "wasn't supposed to" use;
- No single objective was optimal across all architectures × all sparsity levels;
- The two objectives *each saw blind spots the other missed*.

Layer reconstruction error uses local information (this layer's activations); it captures the shape of the data flow through the layer. The Taylor approximation uses global information (gradients/Hessian of the training loss); it captures how much a weight actually contributes to the final prediction. These are two orthogonal dimensions of "parameter importance," and neither should be used to the exclusion of the other.

*Their solution*: Jointly minimise both objectives—=L = (layer reconstruction error) + λ * (Taylor-approximated loss)=. MOONSHOT is a *wrapper*: it invents no new pruning algorithm but slots existing ones (SparseGPT, OBC, Wanda, CAP) into the multi-objective framework.

*Engineering challenge and how they solved it*: When the two Hessians are added together, the beautiful "block-diagonal with identical blocks" structure *breaks*—that is precisely the structure that makes OBC/SparseGPT efficient. MOONSHOT introduces a fast approximate inverse-Hessian procedure that keeps the combined Hessian invertible efficiently, preserving SparseGPT-class speed.

*Results*: On Llama-3.2 / Llama-2 at 2:4 sparsity, C4 perplexity drops by up to 32.6%; zero-shot classification accuracy improves by up to 4.9 points on average. Vision Transformers see over 5-point gains at 70% sparsity; ResNet-50 sees 4-point gains at 90% sparsity.

* The Core — What Was Really Happening Beneath the Surface

On the surface this looks like an engineering saga of "Hessians computed ever more precisely on ever larger networks." But the underlying conceptual shifts are just three:

1. *From "weight magnitude" to "cost of removing the weight"* (completed by OBD)
   Stop looking at the parameter directly; measure the penalty incurred by removing it. This step pulls pruning out of heuristics and into optimization.

2. *From "global training loss" to "local layer output"* (completed by L-OBS → OBC)
   The global signal is too expensive to compute; retreat to watching how each layer's output changes instead. This is a *compromise in exchange for scalability*—the objective function itself is replaced. It is the deepest fork in the entire story.

3. *From "pick one" to "use both"* (completed by MOONSHOT)
   The global signal (Taylor loss) and the local signal (layer reconstruction error) have actually been developing in parallel for over a decade, with the field assuming they are in conflict. MOONSHOT's real contribution is *demonstrating that they are complementary* and providing a feasible path to pack them into the same optimization problem.

The soul of the entire line is a single question: exactly *where* does a weight's importance reside? — and the answer has been acknowledged, progressively, to be not one-dimensional.

* Where This Line Might Go Next

- *Dynamic weighting*: MOONSHOT uses a fixed λ to balance the two objectives. The natural next step is to adapt λ per layer or per region. Different layers rely on the two signals to very different degrees.
- *A third signal*: Once "the two objectives are complementary" is firmly established, why stop at two? Candidates include activation-outlier information or quantization-aware signals. MOONSHOT has already opened the multi-objective framework; the marginal cost of plugging in additional terms is now lower.
- *Merging with quantization*: Pruning and quantization are increasingly a joint problem for LLM inference (GPTQ from the SparseGPT team is a case in point). Can MOONSHOT's dual-objective view extend to "sparsity + quantization" jointly optimised with two Hessians? That is a natural next step.
- *Multi-objective sparse training*: MOONSHOT operates in the post-training one-shot setting. Can the same "multi-objective" philosophy be brought into sparse training—maintaining sparsity throughout training? That is a distinct and challenging but meaningful direction.
