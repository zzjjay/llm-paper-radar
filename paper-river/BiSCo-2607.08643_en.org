* The Core Problem

How do you pack a stream of continuous numbers into as few bits as possible — without carrying around a "lookup manual" and without noticeably degrading quality?

The question sounds straightforward, but it sits on a seesaw: you can take the easiest route — round each number independently to the nearest representable value (scalar quantization). Simple, yes, but once the bit budget is squeezed to the extreme (say, just 2 bits per weight), the number of representable values collapses and quality falls off a cliff. Alternatively, you can encode a whole block of numbers together (vector quantization), which buys much more expressive power — but at the cost of maintaining a "codebook." Storing that codebook, querying it at inference time, and dealing with entries that are never selected are all real burdens.

This current first surfaced in image/video coding, then flowed into large-model weight compression. Both tributaries ran into the same wall, and they converge in this paper.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | VQ-VAE: Learn a Codebook, Turn Continuous Features into Discrete Symbols

*** What Problem It Saw

Early representation learning operated entirely in continuous space, with no natural way to feed discrete generative models — the kind that produce one token at a time. Getting discrete symbols required first "quantizing" continuous features into a finite set.

*** Its Solution

Maintain a codebook of learned vectors. Given an input feature block, find the nearest codebook entry and replace the feature with that entry's integer index.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Created)

The codebook effectively partitions the continuous space into finite "bins," and each bin's index is a discrete symbol — clean and effective. But the codebook must be learned, stored, and queried at inference time by comparing against every entry. The larger the codebook, the thicker this "dictionary," the slower each lookup. Worse, training easily produces "codebook collapse," where many entries are never selected and waste capacity.

** 2023 | MAGVIT-v2 / LFQ: Forget the Codebook — Binarize Each Dimension Independently

*** What Problem It Saw

VQ-VAE's codebook must be stored explicitly and queried explicitly. As the vocabulary scales up, lookup cost and training instability rise with it. For video — a data-heavy domain — a thicker dictionary becomes increasingly unaffordable.

*** Its Solution

Stop learning a concrete codebook. Instead, push each dimension independently to +1 or −1 (Lookup-Free Quantization). With d dimensions there are 2^d possible combinations, and those combinations implicitly form a codebook — one that never needs to be stored, because the rule is fixed and anyone can recompute it on the fly.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Created)

The explicit codebook disappears entirely, and the vocabulary can be made arbitrarily large at zero storage cost. The downside: each dimension is binarized in the original coordinate system independently, so scales differ across dimensions, quantization error has no clean theoretical guarantee, and error control becomes empirical rather than principled.

** 2024 | BSQ: Project onto the Sphere First, Then Binarize

*** What Problem It Saw

LFQ quantizes raw coordinates dimension by dimension without normalizing across dimensions, making quantization error hard to control and theoretically opaque — it is unclear how much information is lost at each step.

*** Its Solution

Before binarizing, project the entire vector onto a unit hypersphere (i.e., normalize it). All dimensions now live in a consistent range; then binarize dimension by dimension.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Created)

Coordinates on the sphere are naturally bounded, so quantization error can be given a mathematical upper bound, and entropy computations can be factored dimension by dimension — much easier to engineer. But BSQ was designed for visual encoders (image/video), and had never been validated for compressing LLM weights. Weight matrices have completely different distributions and sensitivity profiles compared to visual features.

** 2022 | GPTQ: LLM Weights Need Quantization Too — Start with the Simplest Approach

*** What Problem It Saw

LLM parameters are too numerous to store at 16-bit on a single GPU, and inference cost is high. Weights urgently need to be pushed to lower bit-widths.

*** Its Solution

Use second-order gradient information (approximate Hessian) layer by layer to guide rounding, quantizing each weight independently (or in small groups) to 4-bit or lower.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Created)

Scalar quantization is natively compatible with existing low-precision hardware operators — fast to run, simple to implement. But independent rounding leaves too few candidate values. Once the budget approaches 2-bit (only 4 representable values per weight), information loss is severe: in practice, GPTQ at 2-bit produces perplexity in the tens of thousands and the model is essentially unusable.

** 2024 | AQLM / QuIP# / QTIP: Borrow from Vector Quantization — Bring the Codebook Back

*** What Problem It Saw

Scalar quantization hits a hard ceiling at extreme low bit-widths — not from poor tuning but from the fundamental limitation of encoding each number independently. No rounding strategy can save it.

*** Its Solution

Pack multiple weights into a block and encode jointly. AQLM uses classical multi-codebook additive quantization; QuIP# uses a highly symmetric lattice (E8); QTIP uses a trellis structure with a "stateful decoder" that decouples codebook size from quantization dimensionality.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Created)

Encoding a whole block of weights together gives far more expressive power than independent rounding — 2-bit finally becomes viable. The price: whether it is additive codebooks, lattice codebooks, or trellis structures, all of them require some form of table lookup or a non-trivial decoding structure at inference time. The codebook storage and lookup overhead from the VQ-VAE era returns, unchanged, to the LLM compression world.

* Frontier Extension

BiSCo-LLM (this paper, 2026) is where the two tributaries converge. The image-coding world had already validated "zero-codebook, binary spherical coding" via BSQ; this paper is the first to systematically transplant that approach into LLM weight compression. Two adaptations are added for the new setting:

- A "residual BSQ" stage explicitly encodes the reconstruction error left by the base spherical coding, providing a clear rate-distortion knob without introducing any codebook.
- After updating each class of Transformer modules (e.g., all attention projection layers), a targeted distillation step is applied specifically to that class, mitigating the gap between "each block locally well-reconstructed" and "the assembled model behaves incorrectly."

The paper was published in July 2026. As of now, no follow-up work citing or extending it has been found — this is the latest link in this evolutionary chain, and it remains to be seen whether future work will push further on "can residual coding also be made codebook-free," "can the distillation step be replaced by a lighter calibration," or "can this spherical coding scheme be applied to KV cache or activation compression."

* Insight

What actually changes along this evolutionary line is not "how many more percentage points of compression ratio were gained" — it is the community's attitude toward the codebook itself. Initially the codebook was assumed to be a quantization given (VQ-VAE). Then its lookup, storage, and collapse problems were recognized as the root cause (LFQ). So people found ways to make the codebook "invisible" (BSQ uses spherical geometry to provide an implicit codebook at zero cost). The LLM compression tributary, starting from a different point (hardware-friendly scalar quantization first), retraced the full arc — "scalar is not enough → reinvent vector quantization → codebook problems resurface" — until someone realized the image-coding world had already solved this problem and simply imported the answer.

The next step will likely push "zero-codebook" further downstream: should residual coding also be made codebook-free? Can the distillation step be replaced by a lighter calibration method? And can this spherical coding scheme migrate to adjacent problems like KV cache compression or activation quantization?

* Takeaway

When a field is stuck, the answer does not have to come from within that field. BiSCo-LLM's key breakthrough was not a novel piece of mathematics invented inside LLM compression — it was noticing that image coding had solved the same abstract problem ("how to eliminate the codebook") two years earlier, and transplanting the solution wholesale.

The practical implication: when you are stuck on a seesaw between "expressive power vs. lookup cost," do not immediately grind away inside your own domain. Check whether signal processing, coding theory, or visual representation learning has already solved the same problem under a different name — importing a solution is almost always faster than reinventing it.
