* The River of the Problem

When pruning a large language model, how do you decide which structured units
(attention heads, FFN channel groups) are cheapest to remove?

On the surface this looks as simple as "score each unit, delete the low scorers."
But buried inside that procedure is a persistent assumption:
the total loss caused by removing a set of units equals the sum of the losses
each unit would cause if removed individually.
That assumption was already challenged in the era of weight-level pruning, then
quietly picked back up when structured pruning came back into fashion.
This river traces the life of that "additivity assumption" — how it was proposed,
validated, and repeatedly broken —
from the question of whether to account for second-order weight interactions,
to whether to account for unit-level interactions,
to the question this paper asks: should we account for interactions
*across modules* (attention and FFN)?

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1989/1993 | OBD / OBS: Should Weight Removal Account for Inter-Weight Correlations?

*** What Problem It Saw

Early pruning simply removed small weights — no theoretical justification,
and it frequently removed the wrong ones.

*** Its Solution

LeCun's Optimal Brain Damage argued: since the goal is to minimise the change
in loss, we should use the second-order Taylor expansion of the loss — the
Hessian — to compute each weight's true deletion cost, rather than looking at
the weight value itself. To keep things tractable, OBD assumed the Hessian is
diagonal: weights are mutually independent, and the total deletion cost is the
simple sum of individual costs.
Hassibi and Stork's Optimal Brain Surgeon pointed out that this assumption is
too crude — the true Hessian has large off-diagonal entries representing
inter-weight correlations; using the full inverse Hessian lets you compute, when
deleting one weight, exactly how to adjust all others to compensate.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left)

OBS worked because it was the first to mathematise inter-weight correlations and
exploit them for compensation. The trap was obvious: the full Hessian scales as
the square of the number of weights — even a moderately large network makes
matrix inversion infeasible.

** 2022 | SparseGPT / OBC (Frantar & Alistarh): Fitting OBS into a Billion-Parameter Model

*** What Problem It Saw

OBS's exact solution is completely impractical at LLM scale — Hessian inversion
costs explode.

*** Its Solution

SparseGPT restricts the problem to a layer-wise, output-channel-wise
approximation of the Hessian, transplanting OBS ideas to multi-billion-parameter
models via efficient iterative weight updates — pruning plus reconstruction
finishes in a few hours.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left)

It worked by confining OBS's correlation modelling to a computationally feasible
local scope (within a layer), trading accuracy of correlations for scalability.
But that confinement is itself the new trap: correlations are fragmented inside
each layer; cross-layer and cross-module correlations are approximated away.
And it is still weight-level (unstructured/semi-structured) pruning — a weight
update pass is needed after deletion. The hardware-friendly structured pruning
that industry actually wants — removing entire heads and channel groups — is not
directly addressed.

** 2023 | LLM-Pruner (Ma et al.): Switch to Structured Units, but Retreat to Independent Scoring

*** What Problem It Saw

Weight-level / semi-structured pruning produces irregular sparsity that cannot
be accelerated on real hardware. Industry needs structured pruning — removing
entire attention heads and entire FFN channel groups — to directly reduce memory
and increase throughput.

*** Its Solution

LLM-Pruner switches the pruning target to structured units, uses a first-order
Taylor expansion to estimate each unit's impact on the loss, scores each unit
independently, ranks by score, and deletes. Accuracy is recovered afterwards
with lightweight LoRA fine-tuning.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left)

It worked by finally shifting to the granularity that hardware actually cares
about. But the new trap: SparseGPT at least preserved some within-layer
correlation; LLM-Pruner, having moved to the unit level, reverts to the most
naive possible assumption — each unit is scored independently, losses are
additive — and moreover still depends on fine-tuning.

** 2024 | Wanda-sp / FLAP: Fully Training-Free, at the Cost of Fully Independent Scoring

*** What Problem It Saw

LLM-Pruner still needs fine-tuning to recover accuracy; the calibration data and
compute cost are non-trivial — it is not a truly one-shot, label-free pruning
method.

*** Its Solution

Wanda-sp (Sun et al.) scores each unit by weight magnitude × activation
magnitude; FLAP (An et al.) uses an activation "fluctuation index." Both discard
gradients entirely, relying purely on statistics computed from a single forward
pass — completely training-free, no fine-tuning required.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left)

It worked by pushing cost to the absolute minimum: one forward pass, no labels,
no gradients, no fine-tuning. But the trap was compressed to the same extreme:
every unit's score is a purely local statistic with no interaction modelling
whatsoever. The "additivity assumption," which in the OBD era was at least a
named simplification requiring justification, became here an unspoken default —
never mentioned, simply taken for granted.

** 2024–2025 | SlimGPT / SlimLLM / 2SSP: Recovering Some Refinement, but Still No Cross-Module View

*** What Problem It Saw

Pure local heuristics lose accuracy fast at high sparsity rates; and how to
allocate the pruning budget across different layers and modules is essentially
arbitrary in Wanda/FLAP-style methods.

*** Its Solution

SlimGPT reintroduces OBS-style grouped Cholesky decomposition for head-level /
FFN-group-level scoring, combined with progressively increasing cross-layer
sparsity rates to control error accumulation. SlimLLM replaces "score individual
weights then aggregate" with a holistic importance statistic computed directly
over the entire head or channel group, and allocates sparsity ratios per layer.
2SSP works in two stages: first prune FFN width by activation magnitude, then
iteratively evaluate perplexity to prune attention modules, with a mechanism to
balance the two-stage budget.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left)

All three work because they are more refined at the *node* level than Wanda/FLAP:
better head/group statistics, more principled cross-layer budget allocation,
staged width-then-depth pruning. But the trap never changed: no matter how
sophisticated the scoring algorithm or how carefully the budget is apportioned,
the object being evaluated is always a *single unit* or a *single layer*. None of
them ever put "this attention head" and "that FFN channel group" side by side and
ask "is deleting both together more damaging than deleting each alone?" The
coupling that can exist between attention and FFN sub-layers — which share the
same residual stream — is structurally excluded from all three frameworks.

** 2026 | CoCurve (Gong et al.): Lifting OBD/OBS "Off-Diagonal" to the Cross-Module Unit Level

*** What Problem It Saw

The authors identify a concrete, lethal scenario: a Transformer's sub-layers are
coupled through a shared residual stream. Two units that each appear unimportant
in isolation can be jointly indispensable — remove one and the model copes;
remove both together and the loss far exceeds the sum of the individual damages.
Independent scoring — however sophisticated the scoring algorithm — is
constitutionally blind to this dependency: it will flag both low-scoring,
mutually dependent units as "safe to remove," delete them together, and produce
unexpectedly severe degradation. This is precisely the point where the
additivity assumption — simplified and carried forward from OBD through
Wanda/FLAP through SlimGPT/SlimLLM/2SSP — has never been genuinely challenged
at the structured, cross-module level. The only method that ever took the
challenge seriously, OBS, operated at the weight level and was subsequently
layer-wise approximated away by everyone who followed for the sake of scale.

*** Its Solution

CoCurve lifts the OBD/OBS "diagonal = independent saliency, off-diagonal =
correlation" second-order geometry up one level of granularity: not correlations
between weights, but correlations between structured units (attention heads, FFN
channel groups), and specifically across modules.
Concretely: define pruning risk as the token-level KL divergence between the
full model and the masked model; take a second-order Taylor expansion at the
zero-pruning point; this naturally yields a Fisher matrix H. The diagonal entry
H_uu is the classical (OBD-style) unit saliency; the off-diagonal entry H_uv is
the "co-pruning curvature edge" — the extra damage incurred by simultaneously
removing u and v beyond removing them separately.
To avoid computing O(M²) pairwise ablations, the authors prove that under a
first-order approximation this matrix is the Gram product of single-unit ablation
feature vectors — only M single-unit ablations are needed (one per unit), no
gradients, no Hessian-vector products, to reconstruct the full M×M interaction
matrix. The final step — selecting a subset of units under a budget constraint
to minimise total second-order risk — is cast as a cost-constrained quadratic
program and solved in one shot. Entirely label-free, fine-tuning-free,
weight-update-free.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left)

The core logic is: model *nodes* and *edges* separately, rather than — like OBD —
assuming edges do not exist, or — like SparseGPT — confining edges to within-layer
computation. Instead, edges exist explicitly in a global, cross-module unit graph,
and a clever Gram product trick brings the computation back to the same order as
node scoring (M ablations, not M²). Experiments show that at aggressive sparsity
ratios the cross-module edge term delivers gains (up to 14.5× lower perplexity on
a 24B model at 40% sparsity) that dwarf what any improvement to node scoring alone
could achieve — and ablations confirm the gains come from the edge term, not from
improvements to the node saliency itself.

The traps the paper leaves behind are visible from within the paper itself: the
edge-matrix reconstruction is a "first-order additivity" substitute approximation
(finite ablations replacing infinitesimal derivatives). As the sparsity ratio
grows and more units are removed, third- and higher-order interactions among three
or more units (entirely unmodelled) begin to matter — which is why the authors
introduce a trust-region damping coefficient λ to partially discount the edge
term's contribution at high sparsity. This is essentially an acknowledgement that
a second-order approximation is always only a local model: it breaks down in
aggressive regimes, just later than the purely diagonal baseline does.

* Frontier Extension

This paper was submitted on 2026-07-20, barely one day before this writing
(2026-07-21). No public downstream work citing, critiquing, or improving upon it
has been found. Honestly, the river ends here for now. The authors themselves do
not explicitly spell out "next steps" in the body of the paper, but reading the
remaining traps — higher-order interactions unmodelled, λ requiring
manual/rule-based tuning as a function of sparsity — the next paper will most
likely go in one of two directions: explicitly modelling third-order and higher
unit interactions, or making λ fully adaptive and eliminating the need for a
trust-region heuristic. There is no evidence for either direction yet.

* Insight

On the surface this lineage looks like "scoring algorithms becoming ever more
refined" (Hessian diagonal → weight magnitude → first-order Taylor → activation
statistics → holistic statistics + per-layer budget → second-order cross-module
interaction matrix). But what is really shifting is a deeper cognitive pattern:
*every time the granularity of the pruning target is upgraded (weights →
structured units), the additivity assumption is implicitly re-accepted, because
challenging it at the new granularity looks computationally prohibitive all over
again — until someone finds a cheap enough method to bring correlation modelling
back.*

- OBS seriously challenged the additivity assumption at the weight level, but the
  cost of full Hessian inversion forced SparseGPT to confine correlations to
  within-layer approximations.
- Once the target was upgraded to structured units, LLM-Pruner, Wanda, FLAP, and
  the SlimGPT family — three generations of methods — all restarted from
  "independent scoring" at this new granularity. Not because they were unaware
  that correlations matter, but because "unit-level full Hessian" looked just as
  intractable as "weight-level full Hessian" once had.
- CoCurve's key insight is not "discovering a new problem" but finding a shortcut
  everyone else had missed: the unit-level interaction matrix can be reconstructed
  from the Gram product of M single-unit ablations — no O(M²) pairwise ablations,
  no Hessian-vector products required. The act of "re-pricing the idea and
  discovering it was never that expensive" is the true turning point of this river.

This suggests a pattern: *a theoretically correct idea that was abandoned (OBS's
correlation modelling) is usually not obsolete — it has simply been sentenced to
death as "too costly" at a new problem granularity without anyone actually
computing what it would really cost at that new granularity.*
The most likely next directions are extending this "reconstruct the full
interaction matrix from a handful of unit-level ablations" technique to third-order
and higher unit interactions, or transplanting it to other model compression
scenarios — quantisation, distillation — that likewise default to independent,
additive per-layer or per-channel decisions.

* Takeaway

This evolutionary line most resembles a recurring cycle: "correlation modelling
is shown to matter → abandoned due to compute constraints → revived at the next
scale." Each time the problem is enlarged by one level of abstraction (weights to
units), the correlation-modelling difficulty that was already solved at the
previous level reappears, looking equally daunting.

Implications for how to do research and find problems:

- *When upgrading problem granularity, look back at ideas abandoned for
  "computational reasons" at the previous level.* OBS's core idea (diagonal =
  independent term, off-diagonal = correlation term) has not aged in thirty-plus
  years; it is just re-sentenced as "too expensive" each time the pruning target's
  granularity is upgraded. CoCurve's contribution is essentially re-running that
  cost calculation and finding that the Gram product trick brings O(M²) down to
  O(M).
- *Look for dimensions that have never been crossed, not for another few percentage
  points on the same dimension.* SlimGPT, SlimLLM, and 2SSP all compete on the
  same axis (node scoring accuracy), where marginal returns are already thin.
  CoCurve switches to a dimension nobody had touched (cross-module edges) and
  gains an order of magnitude (14.5×), not a few points. When choosing a research
  direction, "has this dimension ever been seriously challenged?" may be a more
  valuable question than "can we still squeeze more performance out of this one?"
- *Let feasibility drive your theoretical choices, not the other way around.* The
  paper does not use the theoretically more complete O(M²) pairwise ablations; it
  finds a "first-order additivity" approximation (Gram product) that is feasible,
  then works backwards to determine which mathematical formulation to adopt. This
  is the same decision pattern SparseGPT used when restricting OBS to within-layer
  approximations — only here the restriction becomes "single-ablation additivity,"
  the acknowledged price is "higher-order interactions unmodelled," and the trust-
  region damping coefficient λ makes that price explicit rather than pretending it
  does not exist.
