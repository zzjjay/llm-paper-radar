* One-liner

Compress an LLM's weights to an average of 1.35 bits and still beat FP16 —
not by inventing cleverer quantization tricks, but by reframing an old
problem: extreme-low-bit training causes "which parameters matter" to vanish
entirely, so the separation of "important parameters take the high-precision
lane, ordinary parameters take the 1-bit highway" must be *welded in at the
architecture level*.

* Evolutionary Narrative (from the beginning)

** Starting point: replacing neural-network multiplication with XOR (BNN, 2016)

The story begins not with LLMs but in the era of image recognition.

The question then was blunt: does a neural network really need 32-bit
floating point? A multiply-accumulate is slow and power-hungry; if weights
can only take +1 or −1, matrix multiplication degrades to addition and
subtraction — or even directly to XNOR + popcount (counting ones), a
hardware instruction tens of times faster than floating-point, with
dramatically lower power.

BNN (Binarized Neural Networks, Hubara 2016) was the first to make this work
end-to-end: during the forward pass, both weights and activations are
binarized to ±1; during back-propagation, a full-precision "shadow weight" is
secretly retained for updates. On small tasks like MNIST and CIFAR-10, the
accuracy drop was not catastrophic.

What BNN saw: *precision is not free, but the lower bound is much lower than
people imagined.*

What BNN left unsolved: the moment you move to ImageNet, everything collapses.
Multiplying raw ±1 values produces wildly wrong magnitudes across channels,
and the model's representational capacity is effectively locked.

** Step two: adding a knob to binary multiplication (XNOR-Net, 2016)

XNOR-Net's insight was simple: you cannot demand that every individual weight
in a convolution kernel be exact, but you can allow *the entire kernel to
share a single floating-point scale factor* α. The forward pass computes
sign(W) × X × α — cheap, yet magnitude is restored.

What XNOR-Net saw: BNN discards all degrees of freedom, including the
"magnitude" degree that is actually cheap to keep.

What XNOR-Net left unsolved: this mechanism works for CNNs because their
kernels are small and inter-channel structure is strong. Scale it to an LLM
where a single matrix is thousands × thousands, and one shared α is nowhere
near enough.

(This line of work then went quiet for years, because the Transformer arrived
and 16-bit gave everyone more than enough to do.)

** Step three: LLMs push the contradiction to the extreme — a counter-intuitive discovery (PB-LLM, 2023)

Fast-forward to 2023. Once LLaMA appeared, extreme-low-bit compression
suddenly mattered again: a 70B model that goes from 16-bit to 4-bit already
saves half the memory; below 2-bit it fits on a consumer GPU.

Among the first serious attempts at binarization on LLMs was PB-LLM
(Partially-Binarized LLM, Shang 2023). When they tried to compress weights to
1-bit, they encountered a phenomenon that *violated the intuition of
"compress everything"*:

- If you compress the entire matrix to 1-bit, accuracy collapses irreparably.
- But if you keep *the most salient 10% of weights* at 4-bit or 8-bit and
  compress the remaining 90% to 1-bit, accuracy almost fully recovers.

This gave rise to the concept of "salient weights" — a small cluster of
weights that carries the overwhelming majority of the model's representational
capacity and must be protected.

The key difference from XNOR-Net: XNOR-Net treats all weights as equivalent
and concedes only on *scale*; PB-LLM admits that weights play *unequal roles*
and concedes on *precision*. This is the cognitive foundation for everything
that follows.

What PB-LLM left unsolved: how do you automatically identify that 10%?
Without an ad-hoc threshold? And this is a PTQ scheme — the model is already
trained, you are only post-hoc compressing it, so the expressivity ceiling is
baked in.

** Step four: refining salient selection (BiLLM / OneBit, 2024)

Two improvement lines appeared in parallel after PB-LLM:

- *BiLLM (Huang 2024)*: instead of naively picking top-10%, use Hessian
  information to compute which weights are most sensitive and set them aside;
  then apply a "bell-shaped residual approximation" to the remaining
  non-salient weights for extra precision recovery. Two stages.

- *OneBit (Xu 2024)*: a mathematical decomposition route. Each weight matrix
  W is written as W ≈ sign(W) × (a × b^T) — a 1-bit sign matrix plus two
  high-precision column/row scale vectors. This is essentially extending
  XNOR-Net's "single α" to "two vectors α".

What they saw: PB-LLM's salient selection is crude; and every PTQ scheme's
ceiling is still the original FP16-trained weight.

What they left unsolved: no matter how accurately you select salient weights,
you are still compressing after the fact. The fine-grained correlations that
exist in a FP16 model cannot be recovered once quantized. To break through the
ceiling, the model must *adapt to the 1-bit world during training*.

** Step five: just train 1-bit from scratch (BitNet, 2023)

BitNet (Wang 2023, appearing almost simultaneously with PB-LLM but taking the
exact opposite path) proposed: stop training in FP16 and then compressing;
start from random initialization and train a natively 1-bit LLM directly.

Technically simple — replace every Linear with a BitLinear (weights ±1,
full-precision shadow weights retained during training), add standard
LayerNorm and initialization adjustments, then pretrain normally.

What BitNet saw: PTQ routes (PB-LLM, BiLLM, OneBit) are all fighting against
the "geometric structure of an already-formed FP16 model"; but the model is
fully capable of *learning a geometry suited to 1-bit representation* — it
just needs to learn it from scratch.

Result: at matched size, it significantly outperforms all PTQ binarization
schemes, making "end-to-end 1-bit LLM" seem viable for the first time.

What BitNet left unsolved: there is still a non-trivial accuracy gap versus
FP16, and *the gap shrinks only slowly with scale* — a 3B model still lags
clearly behind a FP16 3B.

** Step six: one extra state, nearly matching FP16 (BitNet b1.58, 2024)

The immediately following BitNet b1.58 ("The Era of 1-bit LLMs", Ma 2024) did
exactly one thing: expand {−1, +1} to {−1, 0, +1}. The extra "0" brings the
average bit-count to log₂(3) ≈ 1.58.

Why does this matter? Because "0" lets the model learn "no connection",
effectively embedding sparsity into the weight representation — equivalent to
automatic pruning. At 3B scale, b1.58 nearly matches FP16 LLaMA. The paper
title boldly declares "Era of 1-bit LLMs."

What b1.58 saw: BitNet's ±1 forces every weight to *always connect*, which is
unnecessary waste.

What b1.58 left unsolved — and this is pQuant's entry point: b1.58 looks
beautiful at 3B, but *scaling further yields diminishing returns*. The
1-bit/1.58-bit scaling curve is noticeably flatter than FP16. Why? Nobody had
an answer.

** Step seven: pQuant's diagnosis — "parameter democratization" (this paper, 2026)

The pQuant team did something nobody else had done: rather than inventing a
new quantization operator, they *opened up a BitNet-trained model and looked
at what had happened inside*.

The procedure was simple — score each weight with a standard "sensitivity"
metric (approximately the squared perturbation error under the Optimal Brain
Surgeon framework), then plot heat maps.

Findings:

- *16-bit LLaMA-3*: sensitivity is extremely uneven; a small cluster of
  weights (a few percent) has sensitivity orders of magnitude higher than the
  rest. Fully consistent with the "salient weights" found by PB-LLM and BiLLM.
- *1-bit BitNet at the same size*: sensitivity is flattened to near-uniform.
  *All weights appear equally important — which is to say, equally
  unimportant.*

They named this phenomenon: *parameter democratization*.

This is a pointed diagnosis. The "sensitive-weight structure" that PTQ-era
researchers painstakingly discovered is *erased by the training process itself*
in the QAT-from-scratch world. To fit the hard 1-bit constraint, the model
learns a solution in which every weight contributes uniformly — a "mediocre
optimum." It works, but it cannot scale further, because there is no
"priority" left to strengthen.

The key difference from BitNet b1.58: b1.58 tries to expand expressivity by
adding a "0" state; pQuant argues the real problem is not the number of
states but that *training erases structural differentiation capacity along the
way*.

** Step eight: pQuant's prescription — weld the differentiated structure into the architecture

With the diagnosis in hand, the logic of the fix becomes clear: if training
will flatten "which parameters matter," then *don't give it the opportunity*
— separate "important parameters" from "ordinary parameters" into distinct
physical channels at the architecture level, before training begins.

The concrete mechanism (decoupled linear layer):

#+begin_src
  Input x
    |
    +---------+----------+
    |                    |
    v                    v
  1-bit main branch   High-precision (8-bit) small branch
  (high rank, all 1-bit)  (very low rank, only a few columns/rows)
    |                    |
    +---------+----------+
              v
            Output
#+end_src

Every Linear layer in every FFN is replaced by this dual-path structure: a
wide 1-bit backbone plus a narrow 8-bit bypass. Two important details:

1. *Feature scaling guidance*: a learnable scale vector is inserted before
   each branch. During training, features with large scale naturally flow more
   through the 8-bit branch; features with small scale flow through 1-bit.
   This gives the model an explicit "how important is this feature" switch,
   letting it *learn* to push sensitive content toward the high-precision path.
2. *MoE-ifying the 8-bit branch*: this is the real capacity multiplier.
   Replace the single 8-bit bypass with *a group of sparsely activated small
   experts*, each token activating only 1 via a lightweight router. This
   effectively multiplies the high-precision capacity without increasing
   inference cost.

The key difference from everyone before: PB-LLM/BiLLM distinguish sensitive
weights *at compression time* (post-hoc); BitNet makes no distinction at all
(one-size-fits-all); pQuant *enforces* the distinction *in the architecture*,
so training cannot circumvent it.

Results: on a 1.3B model, at an average of 1.35-bit weights, WikiText2
perplexity is 17.2 — surpassing OmniQuant 2-bit (42.4), PTQ1.61 1.61-bit
(39.6), and pure BitNet 1-bit (21.8). At scale, inference throughput exceeds
FP16 by more than 2×, while accuracy is on par with FP16.

* Insights

** What is actually changing along this line

On the surface it looks like "bit-count marching from 32 down to 1.35." But
the deeper shift is: *where "parameter importance" gets expressed*.

- In the BNN/XNOR-Net era: importance is not expressed at all; all weights are
  equal.
- In the PB-LLM/BiLLM/OneBit era: importance is *read out after the fact* —
  train in FP16, then identify which weights matter.
- In the BitNet/b1.58 era: importance *disappears* — to fit the 1-bit
  constraint, training automatically grinds it away (only later discovered and
  named by pQuant).
- In the pQuant era: importance is *predefined in the architecture* — you
  carve out "important parameters live here" rooms in the network upfront, and
  training naturally moves the important ones in.

The whole line is really a *battle between explicit and implicit*. The
conventional deep-learning wisdom is "let the model learn for itself"; pQuant's
counter-thesis is "under extreme constraints, the model cannot learn the
structure you want on its own — you must *weld the structure in*, then let it
learn." This is the reverse direction of the "reduce inductive bias" trend that
drove early CNN → Transformer progress.

** Where things are most likely to go next

Three directions:

1. *Carry the decoupling idea into attention*: pQuant uses the decoupled
   linear layer only in FFN blocks; MHA remains pure 1-bit. Does the attention
   matrix's sensitivity structure also get democratized? Almost certainly. This
   is the most direct extension.
2. *Adaptive rank allocation*: pQuant's high-precision branch rank is set
   manually. Different layers and different heads surely have different appetites
   for high-precision capacity. Letting the model *learn* how much high-precision
   capacity each layer needs is the natural next step.
3. *Hardware co-design*: the mixed dataflow of 1-bit backbone + 8-bit bypass +
   sparse MoE is not necessarily optimal on a general-purpose GPU. Dedicated
   ASICs will follow, just as a wave of binary accelerators appeared in the wake
   of XNOR-Net.

A more interesting meta-level question: if "democratization" is a general
pathology of QAT-from-scratch, does it also exist to varying degrees in
*2-bit* and *4-bit* QAT? Researchers may have been unknowingly living with
suboptimal solutions all along.
