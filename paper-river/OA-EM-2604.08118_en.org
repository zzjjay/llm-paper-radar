* The Problem River

Take a 3B–8B parameter model, crush it down to 2 bits — only four possible values per weight — and run it on a phone or edge device. What, exactly, is the bottleneck?

The intuitive answer: not enough precision, not enough search, not enough fine-tuning. So generation after generation of papers widened the search (ever-larger beam widths) and deepened the fine-tuning (PV-tuning with end-to-end backpropagation). But right at the 2-bit threshold, that playbook suddenly stops working — a beam of 16 performs worse than a beam of 8, and 4 rounds of PV-tuning cannot rescue a model that started on the wrong foot.

OA-EM punctures something the field collectively failed to see: at extreme compression, how a codebook is *initialised* — the very first step — matters more than how long you search or fine-tune afterwards. Once initialisation lands in a poor optimisation basin, every subsequent effort merely circles within that basin. The entire research agenda is thereby rewritten: from "how to search more cleverly" to "how to start in the right place."

These are all "structured codebook" approaches that sidestep the free-form initialisation problem, but at the cost of requiring MAC operations at inference time. OA-EM sits firmly on the "free-form must enable LUT inference (zero MAC)" branch, which is precisely why it cannot afford to get initialisation wrong.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2011 | PQ: Slice High-Dimensional Vectors into Segments, Look Up Each Independently

*** What Problem It Saw

Databases need to store hundreds of millions of high-dimensional vectors for nearest-neighbour retrieval. Raw vectors consume too much space; compressing them with k-means over the whole vector is intractable — covering a D-dimensional space needs an exponential number of cluster centres.

*** Its Solution

Deceptively simple: split a D-dimensional vector into M segments (each D/M-dimensional), run k-means independently on each segment to learn a small codebook. A vector is then encoded as M codeword IDs, each requiring only log K bits. At query time, retrieve the partial distances from M tables and sum them — hence "Product Quantization."

*** Why It Worked (and What It Left Behind)

It decomposes exponential codebook complexity into M independent small codebooks whose product restores expressive power. The price is that sub-spaces are hard-cut: if segment 1 and segment 2 are strongly correlated, PQ cannot see it because their codebooks are learned independently. Important information often straddles exactly the cut boundary.

This left the first opening for successors: can we avoid cutting at all?

** 2014 | AQ: Stop Cutting — Every Codebook Sees All Dimensions, Sum Them Up

*** What Problem It Saw

PQ's partitioning is an engineering compromise, not a mathematical optimum. Discarding cross-dimensional correlations is wasteful — especially for image and text vectors, where adjacent dimensions are often strongly correlated.

*** Its Solution

Make every codebook full-dimensional (rather than covering only D/M dimensions), and represent each vector as "the sum of M full-dimensional codewords." The number of possible representations remains K^M — but now each representation makes full use of all D dimensions, yielding a much denser coverage of the space.

*** Why It Worked (and What It Left Behind)

The price is that the assignment problem becomes NP-hard. Given a vector, selecting one codeword from each of M codebooks so their sum is closest is a combinatorial optimisation problem. AQ approximates it with beam search: maintain a candidate set of width B, greedily adding one codeword at a time. Codebooks are trained with "greedy sequential initialisation" — k-means fits the first codebook to the original vectors, the second codebook to the residual after subtracting the first, and so on.

This greedy initialisation caused no serious trouble in the 2014 retrieval setting, because K^M was typically far larger than the number of data points — the system was overcomplete. The trap it set would not spring for another ten years, until LLM quantization pulled the trigger.

** 2024 | AQLM: Port AQ to LLMs, Enable Zero-MAC LUT Inference for Edge Deployment

*** What Problem It Saw

By 2024, 4-bit LLM quantization was largely solved (GPTQ / AWQ / SmoothQuant), but below 3 bits the wheels came off — scalar quantization's 16 bins simply cannot capture the fine structure of weight distributions. A new paradigm was needed.

*** Its Solution

Port Babenko & Lempitsky's additive quantization directly to LLM weight compression. Per-layer weights are divided column-wise into groups of size g (e.g. g=8); each group is encoded with M codebooks (e.g. M=2 or M=3), each containing K=256 entries.

Why AQ rather than PQ or GLVQ? The inference path is decisive: each group requires only M table lookups and additions — no multiply-accumulate (MAC) operations at all. For ARM CPUs, phones, and microcontrollers — devices that lack tensor cores and are bottlenecked by MAC — memory accesses (LUT) are fast and arithmetic is expensive.

*** Why It Worked (and What It Left Behind)

At 3 bpp (M=3) AQLM works beautifully. At 2 bpp (M=2) it is unstable, requiring beam widths up to 32 and additional PV-tuning epochs to barely contain the perplexity. The AQLM paper attributed this to "compression being too aggressive" and did not pursue the true root cause.

It directly inherited AQ's 2014 greedy sequential initialisation unchanged. In the 2014 retrieval setting K^M vastly exceeded the dataset size, so initialisation was inconsequential. Ported to LLMs at 2 bpp, K^M = 65,536 faces N ~ 1 million weight groups — the ratio is completely inverted, and there are nowhere near enough slots to go around. Nobody looked at this under a microscope at the time.

** 2024 | PV-tuning: End-to-End Backpropagation to the Rescue

*** What Problem It Saw

AQLM quantizes layer by layer, locking each layer before moving to the next. Errors accumulate across layers and can explode by the final layers. Moreover, optimising layer-wise reconstruction MSE ignores what the downstream next-token prediction actually cares about.

*** Its Solution

PV-tuning introduces end-to-end fine-tuning: straight-through estimators (STE) allow gradients to flow through both continuous codeword entries (P) and discrete indices (V). The loss function is directly next-token cross-entropy or KL divergence, not weight MSE. The quantized model is trained as if it were a "hard-quantized but differentiable" network.

This pushed AQLM at 2 bits to state-of-the-art — perplexity dropped from barely usable to practically usable.

*** Why It Worked (and What It Left Behind)

It implicitly encodes the assumption: *given enough fine-tuning, any defect in initialisation can be smoothed away.* At 3 bpp (overcomplete) this is true — PV-tuning can pull a poor starting point back near the optimum.

At 2 bpp it fails. The paper's central finding is precisely this: a greedy-initialised Llama 3.2 3B with beam=8 reaches perplexity 60.61 after quantization (vs. 7.28 for FP16), and after PV-tuning still sits at 11.76. An OA-EM initialisation may start at only ~18, and after PV-tuning reaches 11.53. The gap is numerically modest (0.23 perplexity) but it holds across all beam widths, all epoch budgets, and all models — this is "basin persistence": the basin chosen by initialisation cannot be escaped by any amount of subsequent optimisation.

PV-tuning is a powerful tool, but it is aimed at the wrong target — treating the symptom (pulling back accuracy via fine-tuning) rather than the disease (starting on the wrong path to begin with).

** 2026 | OA-EM (This Paper): Shift the Investment from "Search" to "Starting Point"

*** What Problem It Saw

Every successive paper in the AQLM / PV-tuning pipeline added more muscle: wider beams, more epochs, larger calibration sets. Yet at 2 bpp, adding muscle increasingly fails — going from beam 8 to beam 16, AQLM-greedy actually gets *worse* post-PV (11.76 → 12.01). This is anomalous. If "insufficient search" were the real problem, more search should always help.

OA-EM's insight: *if more search makes things worse, the problem is not search — it is the starting point of the search.* Searching harder from a bad initialisation only digs deeper into the wrong basin.

*** Its Solution

Introduce the representation ratio rho = N / K^M. Intuition: N is the number of weight groups to be assigned (demand), K^M is the total number of representable points in the codebook (supply).

- rho < 1 (overcomplete, e.g. 3 bpp): more slots than groups; even a rough start can find a suitable slot through later refinement
- rho > 1 (undercomplete, e.g. 2 bpp): more groups than slots; each slot must be shared by multiple groups — the positions of the initial few slots directly determine which groups get "well represented" and which get "lumped into the same drawer"

The 256× capacity collapse from 3 bpp to 2 bpp (K^M drops from 16.8M to 65K) is not a gradual drift — it is a phase transition. On either side of it the algorithm operates in a qualitatively different regime.

How is the starting point changed? Replace the greedy sequential k-means with *OA-EM (Output-Aware EM)*:

1. *Stop pursuing nearest neighbours in weight space* — use Hessian-weighted Mahalanobis distance instead
2. *The Hessian comes from calibration activations*: H ≈ X Xᵀ (X = layer inputs), directly encoding "how much does an error in this weight direction affect the output?"
3. *EM iteration*: not a single pass, but repeated E-steps (reassign weight groups to codebook entries) and M-steps (recompute codebook centres as Hessian-weighted means)
4. *Output-aware*: the objective measures reconstruction error in *activation space*, not Euclidean distance in weight space — the latter is only loosely correlated with model quality

This mechanism ensures that the initial codebook is spread according to output sensitivity rather than weight-value density. Directions with large Hessian eigenvalues (high impact on outputs) receive densely packed codewords; directions with small Hessian eigenvalues receive sparse coverage. At 2 bpp, where every slot counts, this bias is the difference between success and failure.

*** Why It Worked (and What It Left Behind)

Three bodies of evidence:

1. *Basin persistence*: OA-EM's initialisation advantage is stably preserved after PV-tuning, across all beam widths, all epoch counts, all models (Llama 3.2 3B / 3.1 8B / Qwen 2.5 3B), and both bitrates.
2. *Asymmetric search scaling*: going from beam 8 to beam 16, OA-EM post-PV improves from 11.53 to 11.49; AQLM-greedy post-PV *degrades* from 11.76 to 12.01. This is decisive — additional search helps only good starting points; it actively harms bad ones.
3. *Pareto dominance*: on the quality-vs-compute Pareto frontier, OA-EM comprehensively dominates.

Open questions left behind:

- OA-EM is validated specifically in the LLM weight compression setting. Whether the representation ratio rho generalises to other learned-codebook VQ applications (speech, retrieval, vector databases) remains unclear.
- Hessian-weighted distance is still a second-order approximation of Euclidean distance. If the output sensitivity landscape is itself highly non-Gaussian, the paper does not characterise where this approximation breaks down.
- The comparison with structured approaches like GLVQ remains an open engineering trade-off: structured methods sidestep initialisation entirely but lose zero-MAC LUT inference. OA-EM rescues the free-form branch, but "free-form vs. structured" remains an unresolved question.

* Frontier Extensions

OA-EM is an April 2026 paper; no papers explicitly building on or critiquing it have yet appeared. But several concurrent fronts are worth noting:

- *GLVQ (Zhang et al., 2025)* is the complementary exit from the same problem — it uses a lattice + Babai rounding where the initial point is a closed-form solution, entirely sidestepping "greedy initialisation." The cost is per-group matrix–vector multiplication at inference (O(d) MAC per group), sacrificing zero-MAC LUT inference. OA-EM and GLVQ are really two answers to the same question: "structured bypass vs. free-form repair."
- *QuIP# (Tseng et al., 2024a) / QTIP (Tseng et al., 2024b)* also follow the structured route (Hadamard + E8 lattice or trellis code) and similarly require no free-form initialisation.
- Likely next steps: OA-EM is the first paper to *formalise the initialisation problem*. Once the concept of rho is established, it should catalyse rho-adaptive algorithms — ones that automatically decide whether to use OA-EM, GLVQ, or fall back to scalar quantization based on the current ratio. It may also inspire new codebook structures that mathematically prevent rho from exploding (e.g., hierarchical codebooks).
- Another probable extension: applying OA-EM's insight in reverse — since initialisation exerts such a deep influence over the optimisation basin, could one design *training* (not quantization) to produce weight geometries that are inherently quantization-friendly? This would push the research frontier from "post-hoc compression" to "quantization-aware pretraining."

As of this writing (2026-05), this is the latest development on the "free-form additive quantization for LLM" line.

* Insights

Having traced this entire line, the deepest observation is: *the community was collectively steered by a mislocated bottleneck for years.*

When the AQLM paper was written, "2-bit instability" was naturally read as "compression too aggressive, too little information" — so the response was bigger beams and more fine-tuning. PV-tuning reinforced this narrative: tune long enough and you can fix anything. The narrative was correct at 3 bpp and wrong at 2 bpp, but because nobody wrote down the ratio rho, the two regimes were indistinguishable, and the wrong lesson became received wisdom.

OA-EM's real contribution is not its algorithm — EM dates to 1977, Hessian-weighting is a direct descendant of the OBS/OBQ second-order analysis tradition, and Mahalanobis distance is a statistics textbook staple. The genuine contribution is *re-attributing a misdiagnosed phenomenon to its true root cause.* The value of such work is that once the concept is in place (rho > 1 is a phase transition), every subsequent methodological choice in the field gets recalibrated.

More generally, this illustrates a pattern that recurs across science: *a field applies ever-increasing effort in one direction, but returns diminish and eventually reverse* — the correct interpretation is rarely "we haven't pushed hard enough yet" but rather "we have been pressing on the wrong bottleneck." The diagnostic signal OA-EM provides — beam size increasing while performance degrades — is a classic symptom: whenever a resource that "should always help more" instead hurts when increased, something upstream is almost certainly the binding constraint.

One level deeper: *optimisation geometry is severely distorted in compressed spaces.* The smooth loss landscape of FP16 space becomes a jagged terrain of deep basins in the 2-bit discrete space. In such a space, both "search" and "fine-tuning" are local operations — they can only move within the current basin. To change basins, the only lever is initialisation. This observation likely generalises well beyond quantization: any "highly discrete / highly constrained" learning problem — neural architecture search, sparsification, pruning — may exhibit an analogous phase transition.

The most probable next directions:

1. Make rho a first-class parameter in algorithm design — automatically select init strategy and search budget based on rho
2. Carry "initialisation determines the basin" into sparsification and pruning — also discrete structural choices, likely subject to similar phase transitions
3. Training-quantization co-design: since initialisation matters so much to quantization, pretraining should already account for "quantization-friendly geometry"

* Implications

Three practical takeaways for doing research:

1. *The most visible bottleneck is not necessarily the real one.* When you see a field piling on resources (wider beams, more epochs, larger calibration sets) with ever-diminishing returns, stop and ask: *is something else actually the binding constraint?* OA-EM's diagnostic signal — "adding beam width makes things worse" — is an "anti-scaling" resource effect, almost always a strong signal that another bottleneck is being ignored. Learn to recognise this pattern.

2. *Give phenomena a ratio.* OA-EM's core contribution is writing down rho = N / K^M and using it to partition 3 bpp and 2 bpp into two distinct regimes. When you find a method that works in setting A but fails in setting B, don't be satisfied with "empirically, that's just how it is" — find the ratio that predicts *when* it works. That ratio is itself the central contribution.

3. *When porting a method, audit its hidden premises.* AQ's greedy sequential initialisation was carried from the 2014 retrieval setting to 2024 LLM weight compression without any modification. But the rho values differ by a factor of 250 — the two settings are not in the same regime. Whenever you transplant a method across domains, enumerate every implicit assumption it relies on. What was an "inconsequential implementation detail" in the original setting may be load-bearing in the new one.

What this means in practice: the next time you work on compression, sparsification, pruning, or any other "highly discrete" learning task, resist the urge to immediately modify algorithms or add resources. First ask yourself: *what is this task's representation supply-demand ratio?* Is the system currently overcomplete or undercomplete? If undercomplete, is the initialisation scheme an unexamined inheritance from the overcomplete era? Answering those two questions correctly may be all you need for a core contribution.
