* The River of Problems

To squeeze a model onto a phone or a laptop, the first cut is quantization—slashing FP16 down to INT4, shrinking size by four and doubling throughput. But not every layer in a model is created equal: some can take a heavy cut without flinching, while others fall apart the moment you touch them. The question becomes: *how do you know which layers can't be touched?*

This looks like an engineering problem, but the deeper you dig, the more fundamental it becomes. You need a "sensitivity" metric—a score for each layer, where high-scoring layers keep high precision and low-scoring ones get aggressively quantized. Early approaches used Hessian matrices (HAWQ), which were too slow; later, signal-to-noise ratio (SQNR) worked cheaply but only for CNNs. When large language models arrived, and then SSM architectures like Mamba, the old metrics failed one by one. Each generation thought it had found the "right ruler," only for the next generation to point out that the ruler was measuring the wrong thing.

This paper, *KL Lens*, is the latest handoff: it argues that measuring per-layer sensitivity for language models should use KL divergence—not SQNR, not MSE. The reason is a mathematical fact, not an empirical intuition.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2018 | Jacob et al. (INT8 for CNNs): Quantization works—but no per-layer distinction yet

*** What problem it saw

At the time, models were deployed in FP32, and mobile chips couldn't keep up. Could they be compressed? The most direct idea was to replace floating point with 8-bit integers—4× smaller storage, an order-of-magnitude reduction in multiplier area. But how do you know the lost precision is acceptable?

*** Its solution

Calibration: run a small batch of data through the model, record each layer's activation range, then linearly map to [-128, 127]. Google demonstrated on MobileNet and similar vision models that this approach drops accuracy by only a point or two.

*** Why it worked (and where it buried new problems)

Vision model activations are relatively well-behaved, with no extreme outliers. But the method treated all layers identically—*"every layer gets 8 bits."* Once models had large per-layer sensitivity differences (like the attention outputs in later Transformers), uniform quantization started to fail. The problem the next generation needed to solve became clear: *different layers should use different bit-widths.*

** 2019 | HAWQ (Dong et al.): Using the Hessian to find the layers you must not touch

*** What problem it saw

In CNNs, some layers' weights cause large output changes under even tiny perturbations; others can be aggressively cut without any effect. Treating them identically means letting the weakest link drag down the whole model.

*** Its solution

Borrow from classical optimization: the larger the eigenvalues of the Hessian (second-order derivatives of the loss with respect to weights), the higher the cost of perturbation in that direction. Therefore: large Hessian trace → sensitive layer → keep at high precision (e.g., 8-bit); the rest can be pushed down to 2-bit.

The intuition: "if I add a tiny bit of noise in this layer, how much does the loss increase?" More increase means more sensitive.

*** Why it worked (and where it buried new problems)

The theory is elegant, but two engineering headaches appeared:

1. *Hessian requires second-order gradients.* Even computing this once for a large model is painful, let alone per layer.
2. *It requires labeled data for backpropagation.* Many deployment scenarios have the model weights in hand but no in-domain data (privacy, copyright).

This pushed people to look for "cheap proxies"—and that gave the next generation its opening.

** 2022–2024 | SQNR-guided mixed precision (Pandey et al. and others): Replacing gradients with signal-to-noise ratio

*** What problem it saw

HAWQ is unrunnable on LLMs—computing the Hessian for billions of parameters is a non-starter. And in data-sensitive deployments there is simply no labeled set to backpropagate through. What's needed is a sensitivity estimate that is *forward-only, requires no labels.*

*** Its solution

Borrow from digital signal processing's old friend—the *signal-to-quantization-noise ratio* (SQNR):

#+begin_example
SQNR(layer) = 10 * log10( ||original activation||^2 / ||original activation - quantized activation||^2 )
#+end_example

In other words: how much does this layer's output change after quantization? Small change = high SQNR = robust layer, cut aggressively; large change = low SQNR = sensitive, keep at high precision.

It only requires two forward passes (one original, one simulated quantization), then a subtraction—very cheap. Pandey et al. showed that on Transformer LLMs, ranking layers by SQNR and assigning 4/8 bits accordingly outperforms uniform INT8 on GLUE and SQuAD.

*** Why it worked (and where it buried new problems)

It works because SQNR roughly correlates with "how much this layer's output has been disrupted," and small disruptions tend to have small downstream effects.

But the pit: *SQNR cares about whether the signal still resembles the original signal—but a language model's final output is not a signal, it's a token distribution.* Two distributions can be very close in SQNR terms while being completely different as probability distributions (you just need to shift all logits uniformly; SQNR won't notice, but the softmax output changes completely).

This mismatch isn't very visible with Transformers, whose activations are relatively well-behaved. But switch to the next generation of architecture…

** 2023 | Mamba (Gu & Dao): Cutting Transformer's quadratic complexity down to linear

*** What problem it saw

Transformer attention is O(n^2) in sequence length. Processing 32k-context sequences runs out of memory; 64k is simply impossible. RNNs are O(n), but their slow training and poor long-range memory had gotten them replaced by Transformers a decade earlier.

*** Its solution

Resurrect the RNN—but as a *Selective State Space Model* (Selective SSM). The core is a continuous-time linear dynamical system, discretized into an RNN-like form, but with carefully designed parameterization (making the state-transition matrix A depend on the input) that recovers expressive power.

Engineering wins: long sequences drop from O(n^2) to O(n); memory drops from O(n) to O(1) (fixed-size recurrent state).

*** Why it worked (and where it buried new problems)

Mamba's core is *state transition*: at each step h_t = A_t * h_{t-1} + B_t * x_t. The internal tensors A_t, h_t, x_proj and friends have activation distributions that look nothing like Transformer attention outputs—extreme outliers, numerical ranges spanning several orders of magnitude.

This planted a landmine: *all quantization experience carefully calibrated for Transformers had to be redone from scratch for Mamba.*

** 2024 | Pierro & Abreu (Mamba PTQ): Systematic quantization of SSMs reveals entirely different rules

*** What problem it saw

After Mamba gained traction, the first instinct was "just deploy it INT4, same recipe as Transformers." Empirical results told a different story: uniform INT4 sent perplexity through the roof; even INT8 was unstable.

*** Its solution

No new sensitivity metric—instead, careful systematic experiments, layer by layer. Conclusions:

1. *State-transition components (A, B, x_proj) are abnormally sensitive* and must stay at INT8 or even FP16.
2. *SSM outputs have large-magnitude outliers* that are easily clipped away during quantization.
3. *Mamba's sensitivity profile is completely different from Transformers'*—all prior intuition about which layers can be cut is void.

*** Why it worked (and where it buried new problems)

This work was the "lid-lifter"—it told the community SSMs can't be treated as Transformers. But its method is *case-by-case trial and error* with no general, cheap, portable sensitivity metric. Switch to a different model (say, Hymba, an SSM-Transformer hybrid) and the whole process has to be repeated.

What was needed next: *a universal, purely forward, SSM-compatible sensitivity metric.*

** 2026 | KL Lens (this paper): Replacing the ruler with the right one

*** What problem it saw

Stack up the previous three pitfalls:

1. The HAWQ route (gradient-based): expensive, requires data, never applied to Mamba.
2. The SQNR route (forward-based): cheap, but *not designed for language modeling*—it measures numerical distortion, not token-distribution distortion.
3. SSM-Transformer hybrids like Hymba: contain both attention and SSM components, each with distinct outlier behavior, requiring differentiated treatment.

*The authors' key insight*: what you actually want is not "do the activations still look like the original activations," but "does the output token distribution still look like the original model's?" The latter directly connects to perplexity—the gold standard for whether a quantized model is usable. So why take the detour through SQNR? Why not measure distributional distance directly?

*** Its solution

Quantize each layer ℓ individually (keeping all other layers at FP16) to obtain a *student* model; the original model is the *teacher*. Run both forward on calibration data, collect the two logit distributions, and compute:

#+begin_example
KL_ℓ = KL( student_ℓ || teacher )
#+end_example

This is the sensitivity score for layer ℓ. Large KL = this layer's quantization strongly perturbs the output distribution = sensitive = keep at high precision; small KL = cut aggressively.

Rank all layers by KL, assign the top-k most sensitive layers to 8-bit, the rest to 4-bit. A complete mixed-precision plan emerges in one pass.

*How it differs from SQNR*: SQNR computes the geometric distance between activation vectors; KL computes the information-theoretic distance between output distributions. The latter *directly* upper-bounds the change in perplexity—the paper provides a formal proof: the gap in log-perplexity is bounded by the KL term, but cannot be bounded by SQNR.

*Engineering advantages*:

- Purely forward: two forward passes per layer, no gradients.
- No in-domain data required: any batch from wikitext suffices, because KL measures "the difference in how two models respond to the same input."
- Tested on Intel Lunar Lake: KL-guided mixed precision achieves perplexity close to FP16 while matching uniform INT4 in model size and throughput.

*** Why it works (and where it buries new problems)

It works because it redefines "sensitivity" in terms of a metric *aligned with the final task*. SQNR gives you a by-product of intermediate layers; KL gives you the ultimate fact at the output layer.

The new pitfalls are clear:

1. KL is a ranking signal, not an absolute quantity. Switch models or data and the absolute values shift—does the ranking need recalibration?
2. The current approach perturbs one layer at a time (one-at-a-time), *ignoring inter-layer coupling*—the effect of quantizing two layers simultaneously may not be the sum of their individual KL scores.
3. It only tells you "which layers are sensitive"; bit allocation is still a manual top-k strategy without joint optimization.

These are the starting points for the next handoff.

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026, KL Lens is the latest advance along this line. The foreseeable next steps:

- *Joint bit allocation*: feed the KL signal into an integer program and optimally assign bits under hardware latency constraints.
- *KL × hardware-aware*: different bit-widths deliver different actual speedups on different ISAs (AVX-512, NPU, GPU tensor cores); sensitivity rankings need to be weighted by "latency saved."
- *Extension to non-language tasks*: KL assumes a softmax output. How does this generalize to vision, speech, and multimodal tasks?
- *Extension to quantization-aware training* (QAT): dynamically use KL signals during training to decide when to raise or lower precision.

* Insights

Looking back along this line, *what has truly been evolving is not the technology but the understanding of what constitutes a correct sensitivity ruler.*

- HAWQ's ruler: curvature of the loss surface (expensive but close to the truth).
- SQNR's ruler: do activations still resemble the original? (cheap but far from the truth).
- KL Lens's ruler: does the output distribution still resemble the original? (cheap and close to the truth).

Every generation has been seeking a balance between cost and fidelity to the truth. SQNR sacrificed fidelity for cheapness, betting that "similar activations ≈ similar outputs"—a bet that barely holds for CNNs, wobbles for LLMs, and collapses for Mamba. KL Lens wins by discovering that *the distance between output distributions is itself cheap to compute* (one KL after softmax), so there is no need to compromise the physical meaning of the metric just to save computation.

This is a recurring pattern: *when a field adopts a new output form (from classification logits to token distributions), every intermediate-layer proxy metric must be re-examined.* Carrying CNN-era engineering intuition directly into LLMs, then into SSMs, was always going to land people in trouble.

* Takeaways

- *For research*: if everyone in a new setting is using a "second-generation proxy metric" inherited from an older setting, step back and ask "what is the original objective? Can it be computed directly?"—often it can, and inertia is the only reason no one tried.
- *For engineering*: when quantizing SSM-Transformer hybrid models, *do not* import Transformer mixed-precision recipes wholesale. The state-transition family (x_proj, A, dt_proj) will almost certainly need to stay at high precision; FFN layers and attention projections can be cut aggressively. For exactly which layers are sensitive, run one pass of the KL Lens algorithm (open-sourced at github.com/jasonkongie/kl-ssm-quant).
- *For thinking*: choosing the right metric matters more than choosing the right algorithm. If the metric is wrong, even the cleverest optimization is just climbing the wrong hill.
