* Target Paper

"Unveiling the Potential of Quantization with MXFP4: Strategies for Quantization Error Reduction" (Chhugani et al., 2026, arXiv:2603.08713).

One sentence: MXFP4 is a 4-bit format that is very efficient in hardware, but its accuracy trails NVIDIA's NVFP4 by roughly 10%. This paper closes that gap to within 1% using two purely software techniques (OAS + MBS), while preserving all of MXFP4's hardware advantages.

* The Single-Line Story

To make LLM inference cheaper, weights must be stuffed into fewer bits — but fewer bits means outliers can destroy the precision of an entire tensor. For a decade the field has been racing to answer: how do you make one shared "ruler" accommodate the vast majority of ordinary values without being wrecked by a handful of extreme ones? The line starts with "one ruler per tensor," then progressively shrinks the ruler's coverage until the MX standard lands on 32 elements per ruler; then comes a forced choice between ruler precision and hardware cost — NVFP4 chose precision, MXFP4 chose hardware friendliness — and what this paper does is let MXFP4 catch up to NVFP4's accuracy without touching the hardware.

* Reading Backwards: What Is This Paper Arguing Against?

The direct target is *NVFP4*: MXFP4 is already standardized and already being fabricated in silicon (saving ~12% tensor core area), yet its accuracy is crushed by NVFP4 by ~10% — and "a standard that gets outperformed so badly won't get adopted."

The indirect target is *vanilla MXFP4 / the OCP MX standard itself*: the standard fixes the block scale to E8M0 (powers of two only) for hardware simplicity. That choice is reasonable, but the accompanying convention for *how to pick that power of two* — align the block maximum to the FP4 full-scale value — causes large values to overflow and be clipped, and is one of the root causes of the accuracy gap.

* Forward Narrative: How Did the Problem Evolve to This Point?

** Starting Point: One Ruler Per Tensor, and a Few Numbers Ruin the Whole Batch

The earliest approach to LLM quantization was straightforward: take a tensor (say, a 4096×4096 weight matrix), find the maximum absolute value, and scale every element into the INT8 range [−127, 127] using that value. One tensor, one scale factor.

The problem surfaced quickly — Dettmers 2022 (LLM.int8()) showed that in models with 6.7B parameters or more, activations develop *systematic outlier features*: certain dimensions are more than 20× larger than the average. If you set the scale by the maximum, 99% of ordinary values get crammed into the lowest few INT8 bins and lose all precision; if you set it by the median, the outliers saturate and clip. No matter how you choose, a single ruler cannot win.

LLM.int8()'s fix was "mixed precision": detect the outlier channels and compute them in FP16, route the rest through INT8. It works, but it is engineering-ugly — it adds a second data path.

** Second Step: Instead of Two Paths, Move the Outliers

SmoothQuant (Xiao 2023) had a more elegant insight: outliers are troublesome because they appear in *activations* — activations are dynamic and cannot be calibrated in advance. Weights are static and easy to quantize. So why not *migrate* the activation spikes into the weights?

The math is simple: Y = (X / s) × (W × s). Divide activations by a per-channel factor s and multiply s back into the weights. Activations become smoother; weights acquire a slightly larger dynamic range — but weights can be handled offline. This is the "smoothing" trick.

This step looks clean, but accuracy loss remains. GPTQ (Frantar 2023) then picks up: instead of quantizing the entire weight matrix in one shot, *quantize column by column, and after each column compensate the resulting error into the remaining unquantized columns*. The idea treats quantization as lossy compression and eats away at the loss iteratively using an approximate Newton step. This paradigm became the ancestor of essentially all PTQ (post-training quantization) methods that followed.

By this point INT8 was largely solved. The frontier shifted — the field wanted *INT4*, wanted *FP4*. Fewer bits cause the single-ruler problem to blow up exponentially.

** Third Step: Make the Ruler Finer — The Revival of Block Floating Point

If one ruler per tensor is too coarse, what about *one ruler per 32 elements*?

That is the idea behind block floating point (BFP) — an old idea that had appeared in Nervana and the MICRO 2019 STC hardware. Its revival for the LLM era came from Darvish Rouhani et al. 2023 in "Shared Microexponents": every 32 consecutive elements share one exponent (scale), while the data itself is stored at very low bit width (4 or 8 bits). Within a small block the dynamic range is tight enough to cover all the values; an outlier can at most corrupt its own block and cannot pollute the entire tensor.

Microsoft, together with AMD, NVIDIA, Intel, and Meta, standardized this as the *OCP MX standard*, defining MXFP4, MXFP6, MXFP8, and MXINT8: each format uses 32 elements per block with an 8-bit power-of-two scale (E8M0). The power-of-two choice is critical — applying the scale in hardware requires only a bit-shift, no multiplier, saving area and power.

** Fourth Step: NVIDIA Disagrees and Builds Its Own Thing — NVFP4

NVIDIA introduced NVFP4 starting with Blackwell: also a block format, but with *block size reduced to 16 and scale represented in FP8 (E4M3)*.

The cost of these two changes is clear:
- Block size 32 → 16: metadata doubles, more storage and bandwidth
- Scale E8M0 → E4M3: applying the scale now requires a genuine FP8 multiply, not a bit-shift

But the benefit is *accuracy*: the scale is no longer constrained to powers of two and can align precisely with the block maximum; the smaller block has a tighter internal dynamic range. The result is that NVFP4 beats MXFP4 on LLM benchmarks by ~10% on average.

A format war ensued: MXFP4 is hardware-friendly but less accurate; NVFP4 is accurate but hardware-expensive (~12% more area). Chip designers face a genuine dilemma: which side to pick?

** Fifth Step: This Paper's Question — Do We Have to Choose?

Chhugani et al. stare at that 10% gap and ask the reverse question: *Is the gap really caused by hardware limitations, or has MXFP4 simply been used poorly in software?*

They decompose the error and find two concrete pain points.

*Pain Point A: The "greedy" scale selection wastes dynamic range.* The standard approach: find the block's maximum absolute value amax, then pick the smallest 2^k such that amax / 2^k does not exceed FP4's maximum representable value (6.0). This guarantees no overflow, but the cost is that all small values get squeezed into the lowest few FP4 bins, producing large quantization noise.

*OAS (Overflow-Aware Scaling) does the opposite*: deliberately choose a *smaller* 2^k, allowing the largest few values to overflow and be clipped to ±6. The cost is that a few outliers lose precision; the gain is that *the vast majority of ordinary values receive more effective bits*. In mean-squared-error terms this is a mathematically clean optimization problem with an analytic solution: find the k that minimizes the sum of clipping loss and quantization loss.

An intuitive analogy: you have a ruler with 16 tick marks, and 32 sticks in the block. The traditional approach says "align the longest stick exactly to tick 16" — but if that stick is an anomaly, the other 31 sticks all cluster between ticks 1 and 2, producing large error. OAS says: "Let the longest stick overshoot and get trimmed, shift the ruler's center of mass toward where most sticks actually are, and total error goes down."

*Pain Point B: Outliers are still hurt by the power-of-two scale.* Even after OAS optimizes which 2^k to pick, the set {2^k} is still sparse. In some blocks the ratio of outlier to median value falls squarely between two adjacent powers of two, and OAS cannot help.

*MBS (Macro Block Scaling) fixes this*: above the 32-element micro block, add a second layer — a *macro block* spanning (e.g.) 4–16 micro blocks — each equipped with a *high-precision FP32 scale*. The full pipeline becomes: FP4 data × E8M0 micro scale × FP32 macro scale. Two layers of scale: the first handles the hardware-friendly bit-shift; the second contributes a fine-grained correction pass.

The overhead of the macro layer is small — its granularity is coarse so metadata is negligible; the FP32 multiply occurs only once per macro block boundary, while all GEMM arithmetic inside the macro block still runs on the original MXFP4 path. The net GEMM overhead is ~6%, and the hardware area advantage (~12%) is preserved.

Together: OAS optimizes scale selection to the analytic optimum; MBS adds a coarse correction layer for blocks where OAS still falls short. The accuracy gap shrinks from ~10% to <1%.

** Present State and What Comes Next

Among concurrent work:
- *MXPlus* (Lee 2025) — also attacks MXFP4 accuracy, via rearrangement of elements within blocks
- *MicroScopiQ* — uses learned scale selection, more aggressive than OAS's analytic solution

This paper's advantage over both is "zero hardware changes + closed-form solution," making it easier for industry to adopt directly.

* The Deeper Cognitive Shifts Behind the Evolution

On the surface this looks like "blocks get smaller and rulers get finer." At a deeper level there are three cognitive transitions.

1. *Accepting that outliers are signal, not noise.* Early approaches treated outliers as noise to be clipped or filtered, which damaged model capability. Starting with LLM.int8(), the entire field acknowledged that outliers are a fundamental property of LLMs and must be *explicitly represented by the format*.

2. *Using hierarchical granularity to fight dynamic-range explosion.* A single granularity — whether full tensor or single element — always fails. What wins is a hierarchy of coarse and fine blocks, each level of scale addressing a different scale of variation. This paper's MBS pushes that idea to its logical conclusion: micro × macro, two layers of scale working in concert.

3. *Hardware constraints driving software innovation in reverse.* The MXFP4 vs. NVFP4 contest is really a tension between "hardware simplicity" and "software laziness." This paper's stance is: accept the hardware constraint (power-of-two scales stay fixed) and squeeze all innovation into the software layer. That is actually a healthier paradigm — hardware changes on a decade timescale; software changes every six months.

* Most Likely Directions Forward

Near term (within one year):
- OAS thinking applied to INT4, FP6, and other low-bit formats
- MBS granularity selection moving from heuristic to learned (different macro block sizes per layer)
- Joint quantization-aware training (QAT) + OAS

Medium to long term:
- Hardware support for truly dynamic scale selection — baking OAS logic into tensor cores
- Cross-head / cross-layer shared macro scales to further reduce metadata overhead
- Convergence with KV cache quantization — currently weight quantization and KV quantization are developed independently

Longer horizon: if the "hierarchical rulers" path reaches its limits, the next step may be *learned formats* — not having humans define E8M0/E4M3, but letting the model learn an optimal bit distribution. Quartet FP4 already hints in this direction.
