* The Problem River

How do you slim a hundred-billion-parameter language model down to something deployable—and produce several different slimmed versions at once, without running a separate, prohibitively expensive compression pipeline for each one?

The difficulty has three layers. Layer one: naively deleting weights makes the model dumb; you need a sufficiently principled method to know which weights can go. Layer two: LLMs are so large that the classical principled methods are mathematically too expensive to run. Layer three: in practice you don't need just one compressed version—you need a 50%-sparse version, a 60% version, a 70% version, and re-running the full pipeline for each one stacks costs to the point where nobody bothers. Generation after generation of researchers has stormed these three gates; each time one falls, the next generation inherits a new wall.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1989 | OBD: Mathematics Tells You Which Weight to Delete for the First Time

*** What Problem It Saw

The dominant approach in early neural-network pruning was *magnitude pruning*—simply delete the weights with the smallest absolute values. The logic sounds reasonable: small weights, small effect. But the logic has a fatal flaw: a weight's importance is not determined by how large it is, but by *how much the error increases when it is removed*.

Imagine a neuron with two input weights, 0.01 and 100. Magnitude pruning deletes 0.01. But if that 0.01 sits on an extremely steep direction of the error surface, removing it causes the error to explode; meanwhile the 100 sits on a flat direction and can be removed harmlessly. Magnitude pruning is completely blind to this.

*** Its Solution

LeCun, Denker, and Solla proposed scoring weight importance using the *second derivative of the error function*. The central tool is the Hessian matrix—it tells you how curved the error surface is along each weight direction. The more curved a direction, the more expensive it is to delete the weight there; the flatter the curvature, the safer the deletion.

OBD uses only the *diagonal* of the Hessian (cheap to compute), scores each weight independently, and removes the lowest-scoring batch.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Hole It Left)

It is smarter than magnitude pruning because it genuinely asks "how costly is removing this weight?" rather than "how large is this weight?" Results improved noticeably on the small networks of the day.

But the flaw is: the Hessian *diagonal* captures only each weight's own second derivative, completely ignoring interactions between weights. In reality, removing weight A changes the optimal value of weight B—they are not independent. The diagonal approximation throws that information away entirely.

** 1992 | OBS: Account for Interactions — Delete One, Compensate All the Rest

*** What Problem It Saw

Hassibi and Stork ran a direct comparison that called out OBD's diagonal assumption. Under the same network and the same pruning ratio, OBS needed only 38% as many weights as OBD to reach the same accuracy. The problem was not the theory—it was that OBD was using the wrong mathematical tool.

*** Its Solution

OBS uses the *full inverse Hessian* to do two things:

First, choose which weight to delete—not the one with the lowest score in isolation, but the one for which the increase in error, *after optimally compensating all remaining weights*, is minimized.

Second, immediately upon deletion, *automatically adjust all remaining weights*—the adjustment is computed exactly from the inverse Hessian so the error recovers as much as possible.

An analogy: OBD is like dismantling a stone bridge by picking up whichever stone looks lightest. OBS is like an engineering crew—before removing any stone they calculate exactly how to reinforce with steel, and they reinforce immediately after; overall structural integrity is maximized.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Hole It Left)

On small networks OBS is far more precise than OBD, and it is the theoretically correct solution. But computing the full inverse Hessian costs O(n^3), where n is the number of weights. In 1992, networks had tens of thousands of weights—barely feasible. In the GPT-3 era, a single model has tens of billions of weights; that computation would not finish before the heat death of the universe. OBS's mathematics is right; its engineering is a dead end.

** 2023 | SparseGPT: Retrofit OBS Into Something LLMs Can Actually Use

*** What Problem It Saw

By 2023 demand for LLM compression was urgent, yet two difficulties pressed simultaneously:

1. Existing "high-quality" pruning methods (those in the OBS tradition) were computationally infeasible at LLM scale.
2. Existing "tractable" methods (magnitude pruning and the like) suffered too much accuracy loss, or required multiple passes of full-data retraining whose cost rivaled pretraining.

The result: no method could prune a GPT-3-class model to a usable sparse version without retraining.

*** Its Solution

Frantar and Alistarh's key insight: OBS is expensive because it treats all weights across all layers as one giant matrix. But *weights between layers are inherently decoupled*—you can run OBS independently on each layer, and the per-layer problem is far smaller.

Concretely: each layer's pruning is cast as a *sparse regression problem*—given input activations, find the sparsest weight matrix that minimizes output error. A lazy Hessian update trick (columns are processed in batches; the Hessian is recomputed only every 128 columns) brings the total computation down to something that finishes within hours for OPT-175B.

Result: without any retraining, OPT-175B at 50% sparsity suffers almost no perplexity increase.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Hole It Left)

The cleverness is in decomposing "global OBS" into "layer-wise sparse regression"—mathematically equivalent but engineeringly feasible.

Two new holes open. First, even greatly accelerated, Hessian computation and storage still stress memory. Second—and this is the wall EPTS must break—SparseGPT produces *one fixed-sparsity model per run*. Wanting a 50% version and a 60% version means running the full procedure twice: twice the time, twice the memory, zero reuse.

** 2023 | Wanda: Can Activation Statistics Alone Replace the Hessian?

*** What Problem It Saw

SparseGPT's results were already strong, but an intuition nagged: its edge comes from second-order Hessian information, and Hessian estimation itself depends on calibration data and is not cheap. How much would be lost by removing it?

Wanda starts from an empirical observation in large-model research: LLM activations contain *emergent large-magnitude features*—certain input dimensions have anomalously large activation values that dominate the output. This means weights that multiply those large activations are more important regardless of their own magnitude.

*** Its Solution

The scoring formula is strikingly simple: importance of a weight = |weight value| × |L2 norm of the corresponding input activation|. Scores are compared locally within each row (each output neuron), and the lowest-scoring weights are pruned.

No Hessian, no weight updates, no retraining. Computation is so fast it is practically negligible.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Hole It Left)

The surprising finding: at 50% unstructured sparsity, Wanda matches SparseGPT and even surpasses it on some large models (LLaMA-30B, 70B). Activation magnitude turns out to be an effective proxy for weight importance, and the extra precision of the Hessian is not always worth it in this regime.

But Wanda shares the same fundamental limitation as SparseGPT: *each run produces exactly one fixed-sparsity model*. Wanting a different ratio means starting over from scratch. In academic benchmarks that is no problem; in production, it is a concrete wall.

** 2024 | FCPTS / UniPTS: Smarter Per-Layer Allocation, but Still One Version per Run

*** What Problem It Saw

SparseGPT and Wanda default to the same sparsity ratio for every layer (say, 50% everywhere). But different layers tolerate pruning very differently—some lose almost nothing at half their weights gone, while others degrade noticeably at 20% pruning. Uniform allocation is wasteful.

FCPTS and UniPTS each propose a way to find the "optimal per-layer sparsity allocation." FCPTS uses differentiable bridge functions to turn the discrete sparsity decision into a gradient-optimizable problem; UniPTS uses a reducing-regrowing dynamic search over sparsity structure. Both improve model quality at the same global sparsity by distributing it non-uniformly across layers.

*** Their Solutions and Limitations

The contributions are real: non-uniform per-layer allocation genuinely raises accuracy at the same overall sparsity. But neither framework escapes the "single-sparsity optimization" paradigm—you feed in "global sparsity 60%," you get out "a 60%-sparse model"; changing the target means rerunning.

Consider a deployment scenario: heavy daytime traffic requires a 50%-sparse version; ample overnight resources justify 30%-sparse (more accurate); peak hours call for a temporary switch to 70%. Under this framework, that means storing three separately trained models and loading different checkpoints on switch. The cost is simply too high.

** 2026 | EPTS: One Optimization, Elastic Coverage of All Sparsity Levels

*** What Problem It Saw

EPTS names the shared limitation of all prior methods: *Single-Sparsity Optimization*. Each optimization targets one sparsity ratio; covering N ratios requires N runs; computational cost scales linearly with the number of deployment configurations needed.

This is more than a speed problem—it also prevents *knowledge sharing* across sparsity levels. The information gained while optimizing at 50% sparsity (which weights matter, which combinations are redundant) is completely discarded when the 60% run starts fresh, with no reuse whatsoever.

*** Its Solution

EPTS introduces two core components:

*MS-HiLoRA* (Multi-Sparsity Hierarchical LoRA): models at different sparsity levels are not fully independent—a low-sparsity model (more weights retained) naturally contains knowledge that a high-sparsity model (more weights removed) needs to learn. MS-HiLoRA designs a hierarchical low-rank adaptation structure so that low-sparsity reconstruction results can serve as "teachers" that transfer knowledge to higher-sparsity versions. Intermediate results from one optimization flow across all sparsity levels rather than being learned in isolation.

*MSFM* (Multi-Sparsity Feature Mixer): at inference time, given a target sparsity, MSFM dynamically blends feature representations from different sparsity granularities instead of simply selecting the output of one fixed sparsity pattern. This gives the elastic model reasonable feature quality at any sparsity level, not just at the few fixed points seen during training.

The result: *one optimization run* produces an elastic model that can operate at any (or multiple predefined) sparsity levels.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Hole It Left)

The key insight: versions at different sparsity levels actually stand in a "parent–child" relationship—the sparser the version, the more it needs to borrow from the less-sparse version. MS-HiLoRA formalizes this intuition into a structure that can be optimized end-to-end.

Two new holes may open. First, EPTS is still a post-training method that depends on a small calibration set, and it has some sensitivity to the quality and distribution of that data. Second, how the elastic model efficiently switches between sparsity levels at deployment time involves sparse hardware accelerator support—not every inference engine handles dynamic sparsity seamlessly.

* Frontier Extensions

** 2026 | Elastic Attention: Carrying the Elastic Idea Into Attention Heads

Concurrent work Elastic Attention (arxiv 2601.17367) applies "one training run supporting multiple sparsity configurations" to attention-head sparsity. Traditional sparse-attention methods fix the sparsity ratio at deployment; Elastic Attention introduces a lightweight Attention Router (a gating mechanism similar to MoE) that at inference time dynamically assigns each attention head to either full-attention or sparse-attention mode, so the overall sparsity rate adapts to the input.

This work and EPTS point in the same direction—both answer the question "can one model cover all efficiency configurations?"—with EPTS focusing on weight sparsity and Elastic Attention focusing on activation/attention sparsity. The two lines are likely to converge soon.

As of June 2026, EPTS represents the most recent advance in elastic multi-configuration compression on the weight-sparsity track.

* Insight

Reading this lineage, a pattern recurs: *each generation's "right answer" becomes the next generation's bottleneck*.

OBS is mathematically complete, but its very correctness—the need for a full inverse Hessian—becomes the wall the next generation must route around. SparseGPT routes around that wall, but the design decision of "one run, one sparsity model" becomes a new wall once multi-version deployment demands appear. Each paper resolves the most pressing constraint of its moment while bequeathing the cost of its own solution to the next generation.

At a deeper level, the core question is quietly shifting. In 1989 it was "which weight can be deleted?" (correctness); by 2023 it had become "how do we make it tractable on LLMs?" (feasibility); by 2026 it is "how do we serve multiple deployment scenarios?" (usability). The migration from model quality toward deployment economics is becoming the main axis of LLM compression research.

The most likely next step: EPTS and Elastic Attention merge, yielding a unified framework that simultaneously supports elastic weight sparsity and elastic attention sparsity; alongside that, how elastic sparse models combine with quantization (elastic quantization) to pull joint "sparse + low-bit" compressed variants from a single model.

* Takeaway

The lesson this evolutionary arc offers for *how to find research problems* is: *re-examine the premises your solution takes for granted*.

SparseGPT's premise was "one optimization produces one model." That was a reasonable constraint while it was solving the feasibility problem, but it was never questioned. EPTS's contribution is not inventing entirely new technology; it is noticing an assumption that went unchallenged for three years and then asking a simple question: "Why does it have to be one model per run?"

This suggests a practical move: in any active research direction, surface the assumptions that everyone silently accepts without ever stating them aloud, then list them and ask one by one, "Why can't this be broken?" Collectively overlooked constraints are often the most valuable entry points for new research.
