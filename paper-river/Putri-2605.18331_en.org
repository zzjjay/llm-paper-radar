* The River of Problems

Can a 70-billion-parameter language model survive losing 95% of its parameters?

That sounds absurd, but it is exactly what a community of researchers has been chasing for thirty years. The source of the problem is simple: neural networks are too large. Too large to fit on a phone, too large for the chip inside a car, too large for anything that is not a data center. So someone had an idea: with that many parameters there must be redundancy — just throw away the useless ones.

But every time someone took "throw away the useless parameters" seriously, they fell into a new layer of trouble:
- How do you know which parameters are useless? (LeCun was already asking this 30 years ago.)
- After cutting, do the remaining parameters still work together? (Not without updating them — but updating is expensive.)
- Removing individual parameters is pointless if hardware still computes in full blocks — so what granularity of removal actually helps? (This pressure pushed the entire field toward *structured pruning*.)
- Once you try to remove more than 90%, every prior approach collapses — but why?

Putri's May 2026 paper sits at the newest bend in this river. Its contribution is not a new principle; it fills three potholes that the river has always flowed around. To understand it, you need to walk the river backwards.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1989 | OBD: Treating Pruning as a Mathematical Problem for the First Time

*** What Problem It Saw

1980s neural networks were small, but training was expensive. LeCun asked: after training a network, can we remove some weights to make it faster without losing accuracy?

The most naïve approach is to look at weight magnitude — delete the small ones. But he found that wrong: a small weight does not mean an unimportant one. Some small weights carry critical discriminative information, and removing them causes collapse.

*** Its Solution

Instead of looking at the weight itself, look at "how much would the loss increase if this weight were removed?" This quantity can be approximated by the second-order derivative of the loss with respect to the weight — the diagonal of the Hessian. Rank all weights by this score and remove the least painful ones first.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Left)

This was the first time pruning moved from "gut feeling" to "compute a number, then decide." Essentially all work in the field over the next thirty years answers variants of the same question: how do you compute this importance score?

The pit left behind: the Hessian is intractable for large models. OBD assumes a diagonal Hessian; the follow-up OBS (Optimal Brain Surgeon) dropped that assumption and the computational cost exploded. This remained unsolved until the 2020s.

** 2023 | SparseGPT: Forcing OBD's Framework into LLMs

*** What Problem It Saw

By 2023, models had grown from millions to tens of billions of parameters. The OBD/OBS line had been stuck for 30 years — the Hessian is intractable, inter-weight correlations are impossible to estimate accurately. The result: large models could only be trained in full; even quantization stopped at 4 bits.

*** Its Solution

Frantar and Alistarh did something clever: they accepted that the full Hessian is intractable, but noticed that LLMs are stacked layer by layer, and each layer can be handled independently. Within a single layer the input dimension is manageable (a few thousand), so the Hessian is computable.

They used an iterative trick: prune one column, absorb the error into the remaining columns, prune the next column. Layer by layer, a 70B model reaches 50% sparsity in about 4 hours.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Left)

SparseGPT made unstructured pruning of large models feasible for the first time. But it broke down in real deployments: a 50%-sparse weight matrix does not run fast on GPUs.

The reason is simple: GPUs are designed for dense matrices. Zeros scattered throughout a matrix cannot be skipped by the hardware. Specialized sparse kernels exist, but at the small batch sizes typical of LLM inference they are painfully slow.

The question therefore changed: pruning can no longer aim for "how many zeros," but for "does the matrix actually get smaller after pruning?" This was the starting point for the rise of structured pruning.

** 2025 | ShortGPT: If We Need "Actually Smaller," Delete Whole Layers

*** What Problem It Saw

Unstructured pruning was a dead end. For the GPU to actually run faster, what is removed must be a contiguous block — full columns, full rows, or entire transformer layers.

Men et al. proposed an extreme version: delete entire transformer layers outright. Their observation: after training, many LLM layers have inputs and outputs that are nearly identical (the residual connection has learned to be close to zero). Such a layer can be removed as if it were never there.

*** Its Solution

Define a "Block Influence" score: measure the cosine similarity between a layer's input x and its output x + f(x). If they are nearly identical (cosine close to 1), f(x) contributes almost nothing, and the layer can be dropped. Rank by score and delete from the bottom up.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Left)

Brutal but effective. Remove 25% of layers and the model barely loses quality. Fast to apply, immediate GPU speedup.

But the granularity is too coarse. A transformer layer contains both an FFN and an Attention block; the FFN might be dispensable while the Attention is critical, or vice versa — ShortGPT cuts both with one stroke.

More seriously: once the target sparsity exceeds ~50%, there are not enough removable layers, and model quality collapses.

** 2025 | EvoPress: FFN and Attention Should Be Judged Separately

*** What Problem It Saw

Sieberling et al. pushed ShortGPT's logic further: if FFN and Attention inside the same transformer layer have different importances, why bind their fate together?

Judging them independently should, in theory, preserve more useful structure at the same sparsity level.

*** Its Solution

Treat every FFN sub-block and every Attention sub-block as an independent "removable unit." Use evolutionary search to find which combination of removals yields the lowest perplexity.

They also found a key empirical shortcut: evaluating perplexity on a single sequence gives nearly the same ranking as using a large corpus, dramatically reducing search cost.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Left)

EvoPress is one notch finer than ShortGPT and achieves better results. But it has a fundamental issue: it only decides *what to remove*; it never updates the surviving weights.

At low sparsity (below ~30%) this barely matters — little is removed, errors are small. Above 50%, each removal perturbs the subsequent layers. EvoPress looks the other way.

A second problem: removing an entire FFN sub-block is still too coarse. An FFN has thousands of intermediate neurons; perhaps only a subset are expendable, while the FFN itself should remain. Neuron-level pruning is needed.

** 2025 | 2SSP: Two Steps — Prune FFN by Neuron

*** What Problem It Saw

Sandri et al. identified two concrete flaws in EvoPress: (1) FFN treatment is too coarse — individual neurons should be pruned; (2) FFN and Attention should be handled by different procedures.

*** Its Solution

Two stages:
- **Stage 1 (width pruning):** For each FFN layer, rank intermediate neurons by output norm and prune those with the smallest norms.
- **Stage 2 (depth pruning):** Apply EvoPress-style search, but only to Attention sub-blocks — use perplexity to decide which ones to drop.

They also derived a closed-form allocation formula: given a target overall sparsity s, the formula dictates how many Attention layers to drop and how much of each FFN to trim.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Left)

2SSP was the 2025 state of the art. It solved "FFN should be pruned by neuron" and outperformed all predecessors at 50% sparsity.

But the authors of Putri stared at 2SSP long enough to see three new pits:

1. **Weights are not updated after FFN pruning.** The remaining neurons were co-trained with their now-removed partners. With half the partners gone but weights unchanged, errors accumulate layer by layer — worse the deeper you go.

2. **FFN layers are pruned in parallel.** Each layer independently decides which neurons to drop, but once layer N is pruned its output distribution changes. Layer N+1 still makes its pruning decision based on the original (pre-pruning) distribution — it is reasoning from stale information.

3. **Attention is still removed as full sub-blocks.** An Attention sub-block contains multiple heads (and in GQA, groups of KV-heads). Dropping the whole block wastes fine-grained information. Pruning should operate at the head level — and existing head-level methods only support MHA, not GQA, which modern LLMs universally use.

** 2026 | Putri: Filling the Three Pits One by One

*** What Problem It Saw

Exactly the three above. There is also a hidden ambition: push structured pruning past 90% sparsity.

Every prior method stops around 50%; quality degrades badly at 70% and collapses near 90%. Putri's authors argue the reason for collapse is precisely those three pits — the higher the sparsity, the worse the accumulated error from not updating weights; the worse the layer-to-layer perturbation from parallel pruning; and the more wasteful it is to remove Attention in full-block increments.

*** Its Solution

Three patches, one per pit:

1. **Prune then repair.** After pruning a batch of neurons from an FFN layer, immediately re-solve for the surviving neurons' weights via least squares: find the weights that make the pruned layer's output as close as possible to what the unpruned layer would have produced. This has a closed-form solution — no gradient descent needed.

2. **Sequential FFN pruning.** Only after layer N has been pruned *and* repaired does layer N+1 begin. When layer N+1 makes its pruning decision, the input it sees is the true output of the already-pruned layer N. Error cannot snowball across layers.

3. **Head-level Attention pruning with GQA support.** The removal granularity is a KV-head: selecting one KV-head for removal also removes all Q-heads that are grouped with it. Remove a batch, re-evaluate perplexity, iterate. The GQA grouping structure is handled directly for the first time.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pits It Left)

The effect shows up directly in the numbers:
- 50% sparsity: matches or slightly surpasses 2SSP
- 75% sparsity: perplexity roughly half that of 2SSP
- 90% sparsity: every baseline either collapses or returns NaN; Putri still runs
- 95% sparsity: Putri is the only method that completes on most models with a finite perplexity

The deeper significance: Putri is the first demonstration that structured pruning can reach 95% sparsity usably. That means structured pruning can now compete head-to-head with quantization and distillation — previously, stopping at 50% put it in a different league entirely.

Pits that remain:
- At 95% sparsity, zero-shot task performance approaches random guessing; perplexity is no longer a meaningful metric. New evaluation methods are needed.
- For small models (0.6B–1.7B), Putri still cannot reach 99%. Extreme structured pruning of tiny models remains a dead end.
- The full method is layer-wise local optimization; no global cross-layer joint optimization is performed. The obvious next step leads there.

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026, Putri represents the latest advance on this line (high-sparsity structured pruning). The paper was uploaded less than two weeks ago; no public follow-up work exists yet.

Several natural extension directions are visible, all hinted at in Putri's own discussion of limitations:

1. **Global rather than layer-wise pruning.** Putri decides independently per layer, with no cross-layer joint optimization. Someone will do this.

2. **Joint pruning + quantization.** Pushing structured pruning to 95% puts it on the same stage as quantization (e.g., INT4 / FP4). Combining both could compress further.

3. **Pruning + brief fine-tuning.** Putri is a post-training method (no gradient updates). Allowing even a small amount of fine-tuning (e.g., a few hundred steps) should recover significantly more quality at high sparsity.

4. **New benchmarks.** Perplexity fails at 95% sparsity. For this line of research to continue, someone needs to propose new evaluation protocols.

* Insight

Compressing thirty years of this river reveals one thing: *this is not a story of pruning algorithms getting smarter — it is a story of pruning algorithms being shaped backwards by hardware.*

Every generation does the same thing: identify the least important parts and remove them. But the answer to "what counts as important" is not determined by mathematics; it is determined by what the GPU is willing to skip.

In the OBD era, hardware was indifferent, so the finest granularity (individual weights) was worth pursuing. SparseGPT took that to its limit, and hardware refused to cooperate. The entire field was then forced coarser: weights → neurons → Attention heads → FFN sub-blocks → full transformer layers. The coarser the granularity, the more practically useful — and the further from pruning's original elegance.

Putri occupies an interesting middle position. It sidesteps the two extremes and settles on a middle tier: FFN by neuron (fine), Attention by head (medium), skipping the FFN-sub-block / full-layer tier (coarse). It picks the finest granularity that hardware respects, executes the algorithm correctly at each tier (weight updates, sequential processing), and then pushes sparsity to 95%.

This hints at a larger trend: *the algorithmic ceiling of model compression is actually set by the hardware interface.* To surpass Putri, someone must either invent new hardware interfaces (making sparse matrices genuinely fast), further refine granularity (by channel, by token, by time step, dynamically), or simply merge with quantization — the last option being the most realistic.

One more thing worth noting: *none of Putri's three improvements is a new principle.* Updating weights after pruning is what SparseGPT already does; sequential processing to avoid error accumulation is standard wisdom for any layer-wise method; head-level Attention pruning existed in the MHA era.

Putri's contribution is doing all three things that were "obviously right but nobody bothered doing inside the 2SSP framework," and discovering that together they break through to 95%. That is an engineering victory, not a scientific breakthrough. But it reveals a more general law: *in a mature field, the next SOTA often comes not from a new idea, but from getting right every detail that predecessors considered not worth the trouble.* 2SSP left three pits because each looked non-fatal in isolation; only when Putri filled all three simultaneously did it become clear they were three faces of the same pit.

* Takeaways

For researchers, this river offers three concrete lessons:

1. **Target what predecessors marked "omitted for simplicity."** 2SSP openly admitted "we do not update weights because it is simpler" — that kind of sentence is a vein of ore. The next SOTA is often buried in what the prior paper found too troublesome to do. All three of Putri's improvements correspond to a "we didn't do this" in 2SSP.

2. **Combine advantages that different papers each hold separately.** Updating weights after pruning came from SparseGPT; sequential layer processing came from layer-wise methods; head-level pruning came from the MHA era. Putri invented none of them individually — its exclusive contribution is the combination.

3. **Pick an extreme value that everyone avoids, and go straight there.** Everyone stopped at 50% sparsity; Putri simply asked "does 95% work?" Pushing the target to an extreme forces all the hidden failure modes of prior methods into the open — many pits only become visible in extreme regimes.

Practical takeaways:

- If you are deploying LLMs and need to fit models onto small hardware: Putri is the current best choice for structured pruning, especially when you require 70%+ compression.
- If you are doing research and looking for the next SOTA: look toward global optimization, joint pruning + quantization, and brief fine-tuning after pruning.
- If you are doing evaluation: start thinking about metrics beyond perplexity at 95% sparsity — this is a clearly open problem that needs new tools.
