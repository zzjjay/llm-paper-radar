* The River of the Problem

Give the same large model the same input, the same hardware, the same weights, turn off all sampling randomness (greedy decoding) — will it produce the exact same token sequence across two separate inference runs?

Intuitive answer: yes. This is mathematics. a*W is a*W; nothing should wobble.

Actual answer: no. Change just one thing — toggle the KV cache on versus off — and the same prompt, same model, same GPU produces token sequences that are 100% different. Across LLaMA-2, Mistral, and Gemma, on GSM8K, under all sampling strategies: 100% divergence. And it is not random divergence — it is systematic: the cache-ON variant achieves higher accuracy in 8 out of 9 conditions.

How did the river reach this point? Because the entire LLM inference stack, from the day mixed-precision training in 2017 nailed FP16 as the default precision, through FlashAttention in 2022, PagedAttention and GQA in 2023 each arriving to "optimize the KV cache," carried a single unexamined assumption: that cache-ON and cache-OFF are mathematically equivalent, and therefore numerically equivalent.

The trouble is that FP16 addition is not associative. (a+b)+c ≠ a+(b+c). Any two execution paths that differ only in *accumulation order* will diverge in their outputs — initially just a few bits at the tail, but those bits are written into the KV cache, read back out on the next step to participate in new computation, and the error compounds like interest. GQA then broadcasts this error from a single KV head to every query head that shares it, turning many small independent errors into correlated large ones.

What this paper actually does, then, is excavate an assumption everyone has been standing on without looking down — "the KV cache is a free optimization" — and nail a counterexample to it.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Gupta et al.: "16 bits is enough"

*** What problem it saw

Training neural networks in 2015 defaulted to FP32. GPUs could handle it, but fitting models into FPGAs or ASICs and reducing power made 32 bits too costly. Could fewer bits work?

*** Its solution

Train with 16-bit fixed-point arithmetic. On MNIST and CIFAR, accuracy barely dropped.

*** Where the trap was set

It only validated that *training loss* was stable, on small models and small datasets. Nobody asked: during inference, if the same weights are run along two different computational paths, will the outputs agree bit-for-bit? In 2015 that question did not exist — there was no KV cache, no FlashAttention, no distributed inference. But "16-bit precision is sufficient" became the field's default belief from that day forward.

** 2017 | Micikevicius et al.: FP16 + loss scaling, "safety" declared permanent

*** The problem it saw

Gupta's fixed-point was impractical — NVIDIA Volta shipped native FP16 floating-point, and industry wanted that. But FP16's dynamic range is too narrow; small gradient values underflow to zero and training stalls.

*** Its solution

Mixed-precision training: compute in FP16, keep FP32 master weights, multiply the loss by a large constant (loss scaling) to push gradients into FP16's representable range. Validated on ImageNet, machine translation, and language modeling: training speed roughly doubles; final accuracy is unchanged.

*** How it shaped everything that followed

After this paper, FP16 shifted from "experimental optimization" to "industry standard." Everyone accepted the claim: neural networks are robust to FP16 precision.

But the proposition that was actually proved was very narrow: after training converges, validation-set loss does not significantly degrade. What was never proved — and never asked — was: do two different FP16 implementations of the same model produce bit-for-bit identical outputs during inference?

These two things are worlds apart. The first requires only that the noise in the last few bits averages out without bias. The second requires every single bit to match. That gap in belief is the floor on which the entire KV-cache optimization lineage was built.

** 2017 | Vaswani et al.: Transformer, making the problem much larger

*** The new constraint it introduced

Transformers replaced RNN-style recurrent state with global attention — great for parallel training, but costly at inference: generating each new token requires recomputing K and V for the entire prefix. By the time the 1000th token is generated, the K/V for the first 999 tokens have been computed 999 times over.

This is the origin of the KV cache — a seemingly pure engineering optimization: save what has been computed to avoid redundant work. Everyone assumed this optimization left model behavior unchanged.

** 2019 | Shazeer: MQA, the first structural change to KV

*** The problem it saw

KV cache memory explodes at long sequence lengths. 32 heads × sequence length × hidden dimension × 2 (K and V), for a 7B model generating several thousand tokens, already consumes multiple gigabytes.

*** Its solution

Let all query heads share a single KV head. Memory is divided by the head count — a 32× saving.

*** The trap it set

Notice a subtle thing that happened here: previously each head had its own K/V, so rounding errors were independent; now all heads share one K/V, so rounding errors are shared too. What were 32 small independent errors, partly canceling each other when combined, became 32 fully correlated errors pointing in the same direction, amplifying rather than averaging.

But in 2019 nobody cared — Shazeer only validated that accuracy did not drop much. FP16 rounding behavior? Nobody asked.

** 2022-2023 | FlashAttention: changed accumulation order, then called it "mathematically equivalent"

*** The problem it saw

Attention on a GPU is not compute-bound but memory-bandwidth-bound: the standard implementation writes the entire N×N attention matrix back to HBM and then reads it again, which is catastrophically slow.

*** Its solution

Tiling: split Q, K, V into blocks; compute a local softmax for each block inside SRAM using the online-softmax trick and merge; never materialize the full N×N matrix in HBM. The paper claims "exact attention" — mathematically equivalent to standard attention.

*** The precise tension with this paper

"Mathematically equivalent" ≠ "numerically equivalent." FlashAttention changes the accumulation order from "scan by row" to "merge by tile." In real arithmetic the results are identical; in FP16, because of non-associativity, they are not. The FlashAttention paper does not mention this — it assumes FP16 precision is sufficient to absorb the difference.

That is exactly the assumption this paper, four years later, dismantles: the difference is not absorbed — it is *written into the KV cache*, then read back out at every decode step to participate in new computation, then written back again. The error does not dissipate; it accumulates.

** 2023 | GQA: making "error sharing" the mainstream architecture

*** The problem it saw

MQA is too aggressive and noticeably hurts accuracy; vanilla MHA is too expensive. Where is the middle ground?

*** Its solution

Every R query heads share one KV head (R=8 means 8 queries share one KV). LLaMA-2-70B, Mistral, and nearly all modern open-weight models use GQA.

*** Its role in this paper

This paper runs a particularly elegant experiment: layer-wise drift profiling. The result: GQA models (LLaMA-2, Mistral) show cache-ON vs. cache-OFF divergence that *erupts at layer 1*; models with behavior closer to vanilla MHA (Gemma, due to its large head dimension and sliding-window attention) accumulate divergence uniformly across layers.

Why does GQA explode at layer 1? Because the FP16 rounding error in a single KV head is simultaneously broadcast to all R query heads that share it. The attention output errors across those R heads are fully correlated, so they do not cancel — they amplify by a factor of R. This is a failure mode that did not exist in the MHA era.

GQA's designers did not see this — they only measured accuracy, which did not drop. But accuracy not dropping does not imply cache-ON and cache-OFF produce consistent outputs. This paper shows: cache-ON and cache-OFF yield 100% divergent token sequences.

** 2023 | PagedAttention / vLLM: elevating the assumption to the top of the stack

*** The problem it saw

Hundreds of concurrent requests in production have variable KV cache sizes; statically allocating for the maximum length wastes 60-80% of GPU memory.

*** Its solution

Page-manage the KV cache like virtual memory: fixed-size pages, allocated on demand. GPU utilization roughly doubles.

*** The assumption it relied on

Paged and unpaged cache values must be *numerically identical* — otherwise users would notice "the same request gives different output today than yesterday." The PagedAttention paper treats this as a self-evident premise throughout; it is never verified.

By the end of 2023, the production LLM inference stack (FlashAttention + GQA + PagedAttention + FP16 + various batching schedulers) had accumulated five layers of abstraction, each assuming the layer below it produces "deterministic and equivalent output." Nobody had verified the equivalence at any layer.

** 2026 | This paper: pulling up the floor everyone was standing on

*** The problem it saw

Every link in the chain above assumed cache-ON ≡ cache-OFF, but nobody had systematically tested it. If the assumption is wrong, several serious consequences follow:
- Academic benchmarks are not reproducible (the same model gives different output in different inference engines)
- Safety evaluations are invalidated (alignment failures found by red-teaming may not reproduce in user-facing deployments)
- All numerical ablation experiments need to be redone

*** Its solution (and a precise diagnostic procedure)

Five experiments chained into a causal argument:

1. *Behavior*: 3 models × 3 sampling strategies, cache-ON vs. cache-OFF token divergence rate = 100%. Greedy decoding is also 100%, ruling out sampling randomness.

2. *Direction*: cache-ON achieves higher accuracy in 8 of 9 conditions (McNemar test, Bonferroni-corrected p<0.05). Divergence is systematic, not noise.

3. *Root cause*: replace all FP16 accumulations with FP32 (everything else unchanged) and divergence drops 8 orders of magnitude, flip rate falls to zero. FP16 non-associativity is the sole root cause.

4. *Propagation*: layer-wise drift profiling — GQA models explode at layer 1; Gemma accumulates uniformly. Architectural differences predict error propagation profiles.

5. *Localization*: activation patching of the entire residual stream (forcibly injecting the cache-OFF residual stream into the cache-ON execution path) cannot recover the cache-OFF trajectory. The lesion is not in the residual stream but in the stateful KV cache itself — once incorrect FP16 values have been written into the cache, no downstream intervention can undo them.

*** The new questions it leaves open

If cache-ON and cache-OFF are not equivalent, which one is "correct"? The conventional assumption is that cache-OFF is ground truth (because it matches the fully parallel forward pass used during training) — but this paper finds cache-ON achieves *higher* accuracy. This means we may have been benchmarking with one version while *deploying* the other, all along.

** 2025+ | Downstream: from "pretend equivalence" to "actively engineer determinism"

This paper is a diagnostic report. Work following it has begun prescribing treatments:

- *Batch-invariant kernels* (Thinking Machines et al.): redesign GEMM and attention kernels so that the same request, regardless of batch size or KV-cache paging, produces bit-for-bit identical output. At a modest performance cost.
- *Deterministic inference modes* (vLLM, SGLang, and similar engines): offer a "deterministic mode" so users can choose between "fast but non-deterministic" and "slower but reproducible."
- *FP16 → BF16 → selective FP32*: the industry is migrating toward BF16 (larger dynamic range, more predictable rounding behavior) and selectively retaining FP32 accumulation on stateful paths such as the KV cache.

* What Is Actually Happening Along This River

On the surface this is a paper about FP16 numerical precision. Underneath, it marks a cognitive turning point for the LLM systems community:

*From "operator equivalence is free" to "equivalence must be verified."* The past decade of optimizations rested on an implicit contract: "I changed the implementation but the math is correct, so the output is unchanged." This paper shows that contract breaks systematically under FP16 + stateful caching. Once it breaks, everything downstream that depends on determinism — evaluation, reproducibility, alignment, safety — must be audited.

*From "check loss/accuracy" to "check bit-level behavior."* The traditional validation paradigm in deep learning is statistical: if accuracy does not drop, the change is "equivalent." This paper raises the verification bar to the per-bit, per-token level — a standard that is more engineering-oriented and closer to classical systems software. As LLMs have matured they increasingly resemble *distributed systems* rather than *statistical models*, and distributed-systems engineers do not accept "outputs are approximately the same."

*Where the next steps most likely lie:*
1. *Cache-aware training*: since deployed models always run cache-ON, should training also use the same accumulation order? Currently training runs cache-OFF (fully parallel), deployment runs cache-ON — a train-test mismatch.
2. *Bit-deterministic inference engines as the standard*: engines like vLLM and SGLang will begin treating determinism as a first-class performance metric.
3. *Hardware-level intervention*: next-generation GPUs or TPUs may provide dedicated instructions for attention with a *fixed accumulation order*, eliminating the FP16 non-associativity exposure surface.
