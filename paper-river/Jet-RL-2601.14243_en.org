* The Problem River

When using reinforcement learning to teach large models to reason, more than 70% of the time is spent on "letting the model speak" (rollout) —
the model generates answers token by token while a reward signal waits on the side.
The most natural speedup is to let the model speak in low precision (FP8) during rollout, since it's just generation;
but training still needs high precision (BF16) to keep gradients stable.

This sounds completely sensible. And so the whole industry ran with it for a year — and everyone knew that sooner or later training would collapse.
The collapse was also peculiar: fine for the first 20 steps, then sudden catastrophic failure at step 21.
Nobody could explain why.

The question this river sets out to answer is: when training and inference use different numerical precisions, exactly what goes wrong?
And can it be fixed? If not, we need to make them speak the same language.

This is hard precisely because it straddles two communities:
people who design RL algorithms don't understand how FP8 rounding works on a GPU,
and people who do low-level quantization don't know how sensitive PPO's importance-sampling ratio is to the sixth decimal place.
Both sides kept moving forward independently, and the crack between them widened for two years before anyone tried to close it.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | PPO: Turning Policy Gradient into Something That Actually Works

*** What problem it saw

Before PPO, stabilizing policy gradient required TRPO — an algorithm with second-order KL constraints.
Every step required solving a constrained optimization problem: slow and impractical to scale.
More fundamentally, TRPO assumed you always update with data generated by the *current* policy;
once the data aged (i.e., the policy had moved on), it could not be reused — enormous waste.

*** Its solution

PPO folds the constraint directly into the loss function via a simple clip operation:
if the policy update exceeds a threshold, cut it off.
A first-order optimizer can handle this, lowering the engineering barrier by an order of magnitude.

*** Why it works (and what it buries)

The clip turns "new and old policies can't diverge too far" into a soft constraint:
close enough, let it through; too far, cut it off. Simple and direct.

What it buries: PPO's entire theoretical framework assumes the "training policy" and the "sampling policy"
are the same thing — same kernel, same numerical precision, same machine.
*Nobody imagined these two could diverge due to differences in underlying implementation.*
This implicit assumption would not be broken for eight years.

** 2022 | InstructGPT: Scaling PPO to Language Models, Birthing the Prototype of ChatGPT

*** What problem it saw

PPO worked beautifully in games, but the reward function there was hand-written (game score).
Language models have no hand-writable reward — what counts as a "good answer"?
Christiano 2017 proposed training a reward model from human preferences, but it had never been systematically applied to LLMs.

*** Its solution

A three-stage pipeline: SFT → train reward model → optimize with PPO.
A 1.3B RLHF model outperformed the original 175B GPT-3 on preference evaluations.

*** Why it works (and what it buries)

Human preferences capture the multi-dimensional character of "good answers" better than hard rules.
PPO efficiently distills that preference signal into model weights.

What it buries: the critic network must be as large as the policy (~7B+), doubling memory.
And rollout still ran synchronously on the same machine as training — nobody realized this was a bottleneck.

** 2024 | GRPO: Dropping the Critic and Pushing Reasoning RL into the R1 Era

*** What problem it saw

PPO's critic is notoriously unreliable for LLMs —
the reward only appears at the final token, yet the critic must predict the value of every token.
The signal is too sparse; prediction is bound to be inaccurate.
And every additional critic costs more memory and compute.

*** Its solution

Drop the critic entirely. Sample G responses per prompt (e.g., 16),
score them all, then use the group's mean and standard deviation as the baseline:
advantage = (r_i − mean) / std.
In-group relative ranking replaces a learned value function.

*** Why it works (and what it buries)

Mathematically, within-group normalization is a "parameter-free critic" —
you no longer need to learn a network to estimate the baseline,
because the samples themselves provide an unbiased estimate.
DeepSeek-R1 used GRPO to train reasoning ability to 79.8 on AIME.

What it buries: each prompt now requires G times as many samples, multiplying rollout cost severalfold.
Generation has formally shifted from "one stage in the training pipeline" to "the dominant part of the training pipeline."

** 2024 | veRL / HybridFlow: Measuring the 70%, Splitting the Two Engines

*** What problem it saw

After GRPO, everyone was doing reasoning RL, and rollouts grew longer and longer (chain-of-thought easily reaching tens of thousands of tokens).
But every framework still used a single engine for both training and rollout —
running generation through FSDP/Megatron kernels optimized for training,
where single-token decoding was agonizingly slow.

*** Its solution

Split rollout and training into two independent worker pools:
rollout uses vLLM/SGLang (engines purpose-built for inference),
training uses FSDP/Megatron. A 3D-HybridEngine handles weight resharding between them.

For the first time, the 70% was quantified: rollout 70%, reward 5–13%, training 21–23%.

*** Why it works (and what it buries)

vLLM's continuous batching and PagedAttention were built to serve large numbers of requests at once —
5–10× faster than FSDP kernels in generation scenarios. Splitting is the right call.

What it buries — and this is the river's pivotal turn — *splitting the two engines means they use different GPU kernels.*
Same weights, same prompt: the token probabilities vLLM computes
and those FSDP recomputes are *not identical.*
Nobody cared at the time, because the gap was invisible to the naked eye.
Two years later, it would turn out that this tiny gap gets amplified exponentially.

** 2023 | FP8-LM (Side Branch): First Large-Model FP8 Training That Actually Runs

*** What problem it saw

NVIDIA proposed FP8 formats in 2022 (E4M3/E5M2 encodings),
but Transformer Engine only used FP8 inside GEMMs;
gradient communication, optimizer states, and master weights all stayed in FP16/FP32.
FP8 should theoretically save 4× bandwidth, but in practice saved less than half that.

*** Its solution

Push FP8 into three new places:
(1) gradient all-reduce with a single globally shared scaling factor;
(2) Adam first moment in FP8, second moment in FP16;
(3) activations crossing tensor-parallel boundaries also transported in FP8.

GPT-175B training: 75% faster, 39% less memory.

*** Why it works (and what it buries)

FP8-LM proved one thing: FP8 is not just for computation — it can also store and communicate.
The key is choosing the right encoding for each tensor (E4M3 for high precision but narrow range; E5M2 the reverse).

What it buries: only 40B tokens were trained; nobody knew what would happen at a trillion tokens.
Adam's second moment diverges under FP8 — a clue not picked up until Smooth-SwiGLU a year later.
Also, FP8-LM was purely about pre-training; nobody thought about which precision to use during RL.

** 2025 | BF16 Train + FP8 Rollout: The Lazy Default That Ruled for a Year

*** What problem it saw

After veRL split the engines, rollout ran on vLLM.
vLLM already supported FP8 inference — just cast the weights to FP8 and speed doubles immediately.
Why not? Keep training in BF16 (FP8 training is notoriously hard), and run rollout in FP8.

SLIME, NeMo-RL, OpenRLHF, and veRL all shipped this configuration as their default.

*** Its solution

Blunt and simple: after each training step, cast BF16 weights to FP8 and hand them to vLLM for the next rollout round.
No calibration, no compensation.

*** Why it works (and what it buries)

Fine on short sequences and small models. But on long CoT (>8K tokens) and large models,
*training would suddenly collapse* — AIME accuracy dropping from 70% to single digits.
Nobody could explain it. The only recourse was a grab-bag of hacks: cap rollout length, lower the learning rate, add KL penalties.
The problem was suppressed, not solved.

PPO's eight-year-old implicit assumption — "sampling policy == training policy" — was broken by reality for the first time.
But everyone assumed the culprit was FP8 quantization error, and missed the deeper issue.

** 2025 (Oct) | Rollout-Training Mismatch: Formalizing the Crack

*** What problem it saw

Yao, Liu et al. ran an experiment: BF16 training + BF16 inference, *perfectly matched precision* — and there was still a mismatch.
This ruled out the hypothesis that "the problem is FP8."

The real culprit was GPU kernel selection.
vLLM decodes one token at a time using small-batch, specific-tile-size kernels;
FSDP processes full sequences using large-batch kernels.
These two kernel types have different internal reduction orders — floating-point addition is not associative,
so a different order yields a different result.

Quantified finding: under BF16, the log-prob discrepancy between rollout and train is 24× larger than under FP16.

*** Its solution

Truncated Importance Sampling (TIS):
during rollout, additionally record the log-prob of each token;
during training, use the true ratio π_train / π_rollout (rather than comparing to the previous-step policy),
and truncate when it exceeds a threshold.

*** Why it works (and what it buries)

TIS statistically corrects the distributional bias back toward zero.

What it buries:
(1) TIS costs 25% extra compute (rollout must store an extra log-prob per token);
(2) it is only a patch — the distribution is still wrong; the loss function is merely discounted;
(3) FP8 rollout still underperforms BF16 unified; TIS narrows but does not close the gap.
The deeper question: *why not just make both sides use the same kernel and the same precision?*

** 2025 (Oct) | Defeating TIM via FP16: Unified Precision Is the Real Fix

*** What problem it saw

Qi et al. stared at that 24× discrepancy for a long time and asked a counter-intuitive question:
*Is BF16 actually better than FP16?*

BF16 has 8 exponent bits and 7 mantissa bits (wide dynamic range, lower precision);
FP16 has 5 exponent bits and 10 mantissa bits (narrower range, higher precision).
LLM training defaulted to BF16 over the past few years to avoid loss scaling.
But RL rollout involves tens of thousands of autoregressive multiplications — *mantissa error accumulates exponentially.*

*** Its solution

Switch both rollout and training back to FP16, with conventional loss scaling.
No TIS, no ACR, no patches of any kind.

Result: BF16 + GRPO peaks at 73–84% accuracy then collapses;
FP16 unified runs stably to 99% training accuracy, and AIME 2024 improves from 34% to 39%.

*** Why it works (and what it buries)

This is the river's cognitive turning point —
*the problem is not in the RL algorithm, not in the quantization algorithm, but in the fact that both sides use different numerical representations.*
Unify them, and the problem disappears naturally.

What it buries: FP16 is slower than BF16, and much slower than FP8.
The paper's final sentence: what about FP8? Left as future work.
That future work arrived three months later, as Jet-RL.

** 2026 (Jan) | Jet-RL: Pushing Unified Precision Down to FP8

*** What problem it saw

The Defeating TIM paper proved that "unified precision is correct,"
but stopped at FP16 — a format that runs even slower than BF16 on NVIDIA hardware.
Not a single percentage point of the 70% rollout cost was saved.

Meanwhile, every existing framework defaulted to BF16 train + FP8 rollout,
and the collapse pattern was industry-wide attributed to "FP8 not being accurate enough."
Nobody asked: *what if training also used FP8, so both sides matched again?*

The prevailing assumption was: FP8 training is already difficult; adding RL makes it impossible.

*** Its solution

Push FP8 simultaneously to both the training and rollout sides of RL, letting them share the same FP8 weights.
Key design choices:
- Training uses DeepSeek-V3's fine-grained block quantization (1×128 activations, 128×128 weights),
  ensuring training stability
- Rollout reuses the same quantization scheme with no secondary calibration
- No inter-step calibration (because there is no longer any need to bridge two different precisions)

Results: rollout 33% faster, training 41% faster, end-to-end 16% faster, negligible accuracy loss.
BF16 train + FP8 rollout collapses on Qwen3-8B within 20 steps; Jet-RL runs all the way to the end.

*** Why it works (and what it buries)

Two insights combined:
DeepSeek-V3 showed that FP8 training can be stable;
Defeating TIM showed that both sides must be consistent.
Jet-RL = these two things fused together for the RL setting.

What it buries:
(1) FP8 backward pass has its own numerical noise, which may corrupt small gradient updates —
  this is the point QaRL sets out to address;
(2) even with unified precision, PPO's clip ratio can still explode at low precision —
  this is what QuRL fixes;
(3) unified precision locks mismatch to zero, but sacrifices the safety margin of "high-precision training" —
  the AIS paper will argue this is overcorrection.

* Frontier Extensions

Within three months after Jet-RL, three papers carried the work forward from different angles:

** 2026 (Feb) | QuRL: Adaptive Clipping + Quantization-Aware Updates

Identifies two hidden failure modes still present under unified FP8:
the PPO clip ratio blows up to 10^5 by step 1000, and KL divergence spikes from 0.002 to 0.025;
worse, per-step weight updates (10^{-7} to 10^{-6}) are smaller than FP8 quantization error,
meaning *the model is effectively frozen.*
Solution: dynamically adjust the clip threshold, and add one-time Q/K/V re-quantization to amplify the gradient signal.

** 2026 (Apr) | QaRL: Align the Forward Pass, Protect the Backward Pass

Disagrees with Jet-RL's choice to lower the backward pass to FP8 as well —
FP8 backward degrades gradient quality, producing "erroneous tokens" on long responses.
Solution: the training *forward pass* uses FP8 to align with rollout (eliminating mismatch),
but the *backward pass* stays in high precision (protecting gradients).
Paired with sequence-level dual clipping. On Qwen3-30B MoE, outperforms the pure-quantization baseline by 5.5 points.

** 2026 (May) | AIS: Bringing Importance Sampling Back

Explicitly criticizes Jet-RL: "unified FP8 sacrifices the trainer's numerical integrity."
Their argument is that mismatch is non-stationary — the correction strength needed differs between early and late training,
and unified precision applies a static solution to a dynamic problem.
Solution: three per-batch diagnostics (effective sample size ratio, log-prob discrepancy, variance amplification)
fused into an adaptive mixing coefficient α,
interpolating dynamically between "no correction" and "full IS correction."
On Qwen3-8B, AIME25 rises from 26.81% to 43.33%, surpassing even the BF16 baseline (36.7%).

* Insights

On the surface this river traces the technical evolution of "FP8 + RL."
Underneath, what is really happening is *the forced merger of three previously independent disciplines: RL algorithms, training systems, and numerical precision.*

The PPO generation had no idea they would one day face vLLM and FSDP kernel discrepancies —
they were just writing mathematical equations.
The FP8-LM generation had no idea that RL rollout is more sensitive to precision than pre-training —
they were just measuring pre-training loss.
The veRL generation had no idea that splitting the engines would introduce a latent off-policy bias —
they were just optimizing throughput.

Each generation solved the problem they could see, while simultaneously digging a hole they couldn't,
waiting for the next generation to arrive from a different angle and spot it.

The deeper pattern is: *once an abstraction boundary is broken, it can never be fully restored.*
RL algorithms and low-level numerical representation were once completely decoupled —
PPO's paper didn't need to mention a single floating-point format.
That is no longer true.
After Jet-RL, writing an RL paper requires thinking about GEMM kernel selection,
and writing an FP8 paper requires thinking about importance-sampling ratios.
The two disciplines are now welded together.

Where does this go next?
(1) MXFP4/FP4 + RL — precision can be halved again, but quantization error will be larger,
  inevitably introducing a new round of mismatch and remediation;
(2) Combining async training with quantization — AReaL decouples rollout and training in time,
  Jet-RL aligns them in precision; nobody has yet done both together;
(3) Precision challenges in multimodal RL — the numerical distributions of vision/audio tokens
  differ from text tokens, and FP8 design may need to be rethought entirely.

* Takeaways

For researchers, the most memorable lessons from this river:

*Don't trust "obviously sensible engineering combinations."*
"BF16 train + FP8 rollout" sounds perfectly reasonable — be careful where it matters, be fast where it doesn't.
The whole industry ran with it for a year without question. But it was wrong at a fundamental level.
*An engineering choice universally accepted as common sense is often the most productive proposition to attack.*
The payoff for that attack is a paper that defines a sub-field.

*The seams between disciplines are gold mines.*
Both the RL and FP8 communities had top talent, yet the mismatch problem
sat unseen in both communities' blind spots for two years.
The author list of Jet-RL — quantization expert Song Han and Han Cai, plus inference-engine expert Ligeng Zhu —
spans exactly those two communities. That is not a coincidence.
*The best coordinates for finding problems: the joint between two mature fields.*

*Diagnosis is more valuable than the fix.*
The real cognitive breakthrough was not Jet-RL; it was the NeurIPS Workshop paper on Rollout-Training Mismatch —
which was the first to *name* the problem.
Once the problem had an accurate name, three solutions appeared within three months.
*Papers that name a problem are rarer, and more important, than papers that solve one.*

What this means in practice: find an engineering practice in your own domain that "everyone does but nobody can clearly explain" —
write down its implicit assumptions, then attack the weakest one.
If nobody in the field has attacked it within a year, that is your question to answer.
