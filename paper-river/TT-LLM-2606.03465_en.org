* The River of Problems

A large model has tens of billions of parameters — far too many to fit on a consumer GPU. Can we, after training is done, "flatten" these weights and pack roughly the same capability into fewer numbers?

Where does the difficulty lie? Compression always means losing something; the key is to lose what hurts least. For decades one road looked especially tempting: mathematics had long proved that a "low-rank approximation" of a matrix — keeping its most important directions and discarding the rest — is "optimal" in a certain sense. Generation after generation walked that road: first compressing one matrix, then a group of matrices, then stacking weights into higher-dimensional "tensors" and compressing them together. Each step was more aggressive, more "clever," theoretically saving more.

But one question was never answered head-on: when you actually bring these fancy tensor methods to real large models at real deployment scale and push hard, do they genuinely outperform the plain matrix approach? This 2026 paper arrives to pour cold water — it runs a systematic benchmark and finds the answer is "no," and it explains why.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1936/1960 | Eckart-Young-Mirsky: The Mathematical Stamp of Approval for "Keep the Big, Drop the Small"

*** What problem did it see?

This is not a paper the river is critiquing — it is the source of the entire river. The question is disarmingly simple: given a large matrix and only a low-rank matrix with which to approximate it, how do you minimize the approximation error?

*** Its solution

Decompose the matrix via SVD, yielding a sequence of singular values arranged from largest to smallest, each reflecting the importance of one direction. The Eckart-Young-Mirsky theorem proves: retain only the largest few, zero out the rest, and the resulting approximation is globally optimal under any unitarily invariant norm.

*** Why it works — and what pit it digs

It works because it delivers a clean, ironclad promise: "cut by magnitude and you cannot go wrong." That promise was so attractive that everyone who came after used it as a foundation for model compression.

But the pit is buried right there: the theorem only guarantees optimality for *reconstructing this particular matrix*. It never claimed that *the model built from such truncated weights would also be optimal on downstream tasks*. The gap between matrix reconstruction error and final model performance is the seam that every subsequent paper has tried to patch.

** 2023 | LASER: Dropping Weight "Noise" Actually Strengthens Reasoning

*** What problem did it see?

The founding theorem treated low-rank approximation purely as compression. LASER noticed a counter-intuitive phenomenon: in certain layers of a large model, the directions corresponding to small singular values are not merely "secondary information" — they are outright noise that actively drags performance down.

*** Its solution

LASER's intervention is almost impossibly minimal: pick one layer, one matrix (typically a late-stage FFN/MLP input matrix), apply SVD, slash the rank to as little as one percent of the original, and stop there. No new parameters, no calibration data, no further training.

*** Why it works — and what pit it digs

The intuition: training stuffs weights with high-order noise that serves no purpose; removing it is denoising, and the model "sees" more clearly — on certain QA tasks performance can jump twenty to thirty percentage points.

But LASER traps itself inside "one matrix at a time." It exploits only intra-matrix redundancy, leaving an entire gold mine untouched: are the many attention heads within a layer — all structurally similar — also redundant with one another? And its choice of which layer to cut, and by how much, is entirely manual, with no theoretical compass. These two gaps are picked up by the two papers that follow.

** 2024 | SVD-LLM: Making "How Much to Cut" Finally Match "How Much Performance Drops"

*** What problem did it see?

Both LASER and the earlier ASVD faced the same chronic problem: when you remove a singular value, how much does the model's final loss actually increase? Nobody could say. ASVD applied activation normalization but "did not establish a direct relationship between singular values and compression loss." The result was that rank truncation was a blindfolded knife swing — wrong cuts, undetected.

*** Its solution

Transform LASER's single-matrix SVD one step at a time. Before SVD, use calibration data to compute input-activation correlations, construct a "whitening matrix" via Cholesky decomposition, multiply the weight by it, and then apply SVD. This whitening is theoretically proven to make singular value magnitude correspond strictly to model compression loss — so cutting the smallest singular values causes the smallest loss. After truncation, a closed-form layer-wise update recovers accuracy.

*** Why it works — and what pit it digs

The elegance of whitening: it makes each input channel independent, turning "which directions can be safely cut" into a question with a precise answer rather than a guess. This is orders of magnitude faster than ASVD (15 minutes vs. 5.5 hours at 20% compression).

But SVD-LLM is still "matrix thinking" at heart — it processes one weight matrix at a time and treats the entire Transformer as a bag of independent matrices. It never asks: the many attention heads within one layer look so structurally similar — can they be compressed together as a unit? The temptation to promote "matrix" to "tensor" now steps forward.

** 2025 | TensorLLM: Stack Multiple Attention Heads into a Tensor and Force a Shared Subspace

*** What problem did it see?

TensorLLM zeroes in on the shared blind spot of LASER and SVD-LLM: both search for redundancy *inside a single matrix*, while the Q/K/V/O matrices of multi-head attention, and the multiple heads within a layer, are also highly redundant with one another. Its diagnosis: earlier methods compress attention poorly because they "fail to exploit domain knowledge" — the multiple heads in a layer are meant to work in concert and should not be compressed as independent matrices.

*** Its solution

Promote SVD-LLM's "2-D matrix decomposition" by one dimension. Concretely: unfold each attention head's matrix into a 3-D tensor, then stack Q/K/V/O into a 4-D tensor, and apply Tucker decomposition (the high-dimensional generalization of SVD) — crucially, forcing all heads to share the same set of Tucker factor matrices.

*** Why it works — and what pit it digs

The core belief is captured in one sentence: "task-relevant information within the attention block of a single layer lives in a subspace shared across multiple heads." Forcing all heads into that shared subspace amounts to high-dimensional denoising; attention-weight compression ratios can reach 250×, without any training.

Remember this phrase — *shared subspace*. It is the entire premise of Tucker/tensor methods' high-compression capability: the matrices being jointly decomposed must genuinely share a common basis. On attention heads the assumption looked plausible, so extending it elsewhere seemed natural. The pit is buried in that "assumption" — whether it actually holds had never been directly tested.

** 2026 | TD-MoE: Stack an Entire Layer's Experts into a Tensor and Compress Jointly

*** What problem did it see?

TD-MoE saw TensorLLM apply tensor methods to attention while passing over a far more abundant target: a MoE model has dozens or even hundreds of "experts" per layer, each a set of FFN weights, with massive cross-expert redundancy. Existing low-rank compression "typically operates at the granularity of individual experts," thus "overlooking structural redundancy shared across experts."

*** Its solution

Adopt TensorLLM's recipe wholesale, substituting "experts" for "attention heads": stack all experts' weights in a layer into a 3-D tensor, apply multilinear whitening (inheriting SVD-LLM's whitening idea), Tucker-decompose, and use an adaptive three-dimensional rank allocation. In short — "cross-expert tensorization + joint 3-D decomposition."

*** Why it works — and what pit it digs

On Qwen2-57B and Mixtral-8x7B, 20% compression is nearly lossless; at 40%/60% compression it outperforms comparable decomposition baselines by 11%/14%. Tensor methods appear to have claimed yet another territory.

Note, however: TD-MoE still feeds on TensorLLM's unverified assumption — that experts genuinely share a low-rank subspace. And like every paper before it, it reports wins under self-selected settings against self-selected baselines. By this point the entire river has traveled far down the same belief without anyone turning back to ask: does the shared-subspace assumption actually hold for real large models at real deployment scale?

** 2026 | This Paper (TT-LLM): A Systematic Benchmark Reveals Tensor Methods Cannot Beat Matrix Methods

*** What problem did it see?

This paper immediately punctures the comfort zone of the entire river: "existing work evaluates these methods only in narrow settings," and tensor methods "report positive results under evaluation protocols that do not reflect" real deployment. In other words — the wins reported by LASER, SVD-LLM, TensorLLM, and TD-MoE may be an artifact of permissive evaluation setups.

*** Its solution

Rather than inventing a new method, it conducts an honest, large-scale systematic evaluation. Spanning both dense models and MoE architectures, under unified conditions with matched compression rates, it pits tensor methods (TensorLLM, TD-MoE, etc.) head-to-head against matrix methods (SVD-LLM, SliceGPT, Dobi-SVD, FLAT-LLM, HASSLE-free, etc.), alongside quantization (RTN), pruning, and lightweight LoRA fine-tuning.

The conclusion in one sentence: "across all settings, tensor formats fail to outperform their matrix counterparts at matched compression rates."

*** Why it works — and what pit it leaves

More importantly, the paper explains *why*. Tensor decomposition's high-compression gains depend entirely on an assumption passed down through the entire lineage — that the jointly decomposed weights share a low-rank subspace. But the paper shows that "the shared subspace assumed by tensor decompositions is fundamentally misaligned with the heterogeneous representations learned by modern LLMs." Real large models learn *heterogeneous* representations at every layer, every head, every expert — each component manages its own; none shares a common basis. Forcing them into a shared subspace wastes more than it saves compared to straightforwardly applying matrix methods.

The new pit it opens is constructive: if pure tensor decomposition is a dead end, does tensor structure retain any value at all? Perhaps it should not serve as a standalone compressor but as one component in a pipeline alongside quantization and pruning.

* Frontier Extensions

This paper is a "diagnostic report." After it, the line branches in two directions.

The first accepts the ceiling of tensor decomposition as a "standalone compressor" and pivots toward engineering it and combining it with other techniques. *Minima* (arXiv:2602.01613, 2026) takes this direction — it no longer agonizes over whether tensor methods can beat matrix methods in isolation, but instead builds a "production-grade tensor-network compression pipeline," returning tensor decomposition to its appropriate role: a component in a pipeline, not a universal remedy.

The second direction returns to the root cause this paper exposes — "heterogeneous representations." Since each layer and each head learns its own thing, the natural next question is: can we abandon the "globally shared subspace" and instead use "locally/group-shared" or "data-adaptive subspace allocation"? This line is still unfolding; this paper functions more as a brake pedal and a road sign for the entire tensor-compression trajectory.

* Insight

On the surface this river is a technical progression: SVD → whitened SVD → Tucker tensor → MoE tensor. But what is really happening underneath is the story of *an untested assumption being stacked higher and higher until a single honest evaluation punctures it*.

The founding promise of Eckart-Young-Mirsky was "optimal matrix reconstruction." Starting from LASER, people quietly substituted a stronger and far less guaranteed claim: "low-rank structure is also optimal for downstream tasks." TensorLLM added another layer: "different matrices/heads share the same low-rank subspace." TD-MoE extended it to experts. Each step was more aggressive; each step silently accepted the previous step's assumption — until this paper carried the whole stack to real scale and weighed it, finding the most critical piece, "shared subspace," hollow. Real large models learn *heterogeneous representations*: each component manages its own, sharing no common basis.

The genuine cognitive shift this lineage traces is therefore: *from "the more elegant the structure, the higher the dimension, the stronger the compression" to "a prior structure must match the data's true structure; otherwise elegance is a burden."* Tensor decomposition is not inherently bad — it is that the "sharing" prior it imposes clashes with the "heterogeneity" of LLM reality.

Where does the field most likely go next? Two roads: either abandon global sharing in favor of data-adaptive local subspaces (letting structure fit heterogeneity rather than suppress it), or frankly accept that tensor methods should not play the lead role and demote them to a component in a quantization/pruning pipeline — a pragmatic path Minima is already walking.

* Takeaways

The biggest takeaway from this river is about *how dangerous inherited assumptions can be*.

Every paper stands on the shoulders of its predecessors, but in doing so it silently inherits the unstated premises those predecessors never wrote down. LASER → SVD-LLM → TensorLLM → TD-MoE: not one of these papers is wrong, yet together they promoted an assumption that "looked valid in small settings" (shared subspace) to the status of an axiom, piling ever higher, with no one stopping to go back and check the foundation. Until someone was willing to do the unglamorous thing — not invent a new method, just honestly re-run the benchmark at scale.

The practical landing point: this means that *one of the most valuable moves you can make in research may not be adding yet another layer, but going back to verify the assumption everyone takes for granted and nobody has directly tested*. When every paper in a field reports wins under self-selected settings, the unified evaluation — the bucket of cold water — is often weightier than one more state-of-the-art result. When hunting for a problem, fix your eyes on "the sentence everyone believes but no one has tested" — that is usually where a good paper is hiding.
