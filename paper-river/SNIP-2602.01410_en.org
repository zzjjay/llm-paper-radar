* What This River Is About

LLM training keeps getting more expensive. Over the eight years from 2018 to 2026, the entire field has been doing one thing: *pushing the numeric precision of training lower*. From 32 bits down to 16, then to 8, and now attempting 4.

Every step down sharpens the same tension: *save money, but don't let the model collapse*. Every landmark paper on this line answers a different variant of the same question — "at this precision level, how do we keep the model alive?"

Reading the line in reverse, you find a beautiful pattern: solutions have steadily moved from "one global setting fits all" toward "careful per-component accounting." SNIP is the current end of this evolutionary arc; it turns the question of "which layer should use which precision" into a solvable math problem.

* Section 1: From 32 Bits to 16 Bits (2018)

The story begins with Micikevicius's 2018 paper, *Mixed Precision Training*.

Before that, neural network training defaulted to 32-bit floating point (FP32). One number occupies 4 bytes; at GPT-3 scale, weights and activations alone would blow out GPU memory. The natural question: *can we halve the bit width and use FP16?*

A direct swap doesn't work. FP16 has a much narrower representable range; once gradients get small they underflow to zero and learning stops.

Micikevicius introduced three patches:

1. *FP32 master-weight copy*: keep a 32-bit "original" of each parameter, compute gradients in FP16, but write updates back to the FP32 copy
2. *Loss scaling*: multiply the loss by a large constant (e.g., 1024) before the backward pass so gradients are amplified into FP16's representable range, then scale back before the update
3. *Operator-level precision selection*: matrix multiplications use FP16, but accumulation uses FP32

This approach established an implicit paradigm — *"mixed precision" is not a uniform reduction but a targeted one: keep critical spots high, compress the rest*. That is the seed of everything that follows.

But Micikevicius left two gaps open. First, the FP32 master-weight copy still consumes memory, discounting the claimed "2× savings." Second, he said nothing about lower precisions (8-bit, 4-bit) — he only solved the 16-bit tier.

* Section 2: From 16 Bits to 8 Bits (2022–2023)

Four years later, hardware caught up. The NVIDIA H100 natively supports FP8. A new question emerged: *can 16 bits be pushed further down to 8?*

Again, a direct swap fails. FP8 has an even narrower range than FP16; the old "FP32 master weights + loss scaling" patches are no longer sufficient — gradients blow up more easily under FP8.

Two key papers define this period. Micikevicius's 2022 *FP8 Formats for Deep Learning* first defined *two FP8 variants*: E4M3 (higher precision, suited for the forward pass) and E5M2 (wider range, suited for backward gradients). The implicit message: "8 bits is too narrow to be one-size-fits-all; we need two flavors."

Then Peng's 2023 FP8-LM landed that idea in full LLM training. They did something aggressive — *extending FP8 from forward matrix multiplications to gradients, optimizer states, and cross-device communication*. The result is a tiered framework:

- O1: compress only gradient all-reduce communication to FP8
- O2: also compress optimizer states to FP8 (Adam's m and v)
- O3: also compress distributed weight broadcasts to FP8

Measured on GPT-175B: memory reduced 39%, speed increased 75%. This was effectively a proclamation: "FP8 is no longer just a GEMM precision — it is the precision of the entire training pipeline."

DeepSeek-V3 (2024) carried this to production-scale trillion-parameter models, proving the approach holds at the largest real-world scale.

Yet the FP8-LM family left one conspicuous gap — *it assumes all GEMMs run at FP8*. This is a "compress everything uniformly" strategy: every layer, every matrix multiplication, treated at the same precision tier.

* Section 3: Attempting 4 Bits (2025)

The hardware roadmap kept descending. The Blackwell architecture began supporting FP4. The problem repeated itself: *can 8 bits be compressed to 4?*

The difficulty jumped by an order of magnitude. FP4 has only 16 representable values (2^4 = 16); the moment an outlier arrives it warps the entire quantization grid. The "add a bit of loss scaling, be careful about range" fixes from the FP8 era are nowhere near sufficient.

Wang et al.'s 2025 paper *Optimizing LLM Training Using FP4 Quantization* is the first serious FP4 training work. Its two core ideas:

1. *Differentiable quantization estimator*: the quantization function is not differentiable, so back-propagated gradients are wrong. They designed a smooth approximation so weight update directions are not misled by quantization noise
2. *Outlier clamping and compensation*: detect extreme activation values, handle them separately, and prevent them from blowing up the entire quantization grid

This approach held quality loss to "nearly negligible" on 13B models. But the cost is visible — *gradient computation becomes more complex, and outliers need a separate sparse GEMM pass*.

More importantly, Wang et al. still followed the "compress everything" philosophy: all layers use FP4, and carefully engineered numerical tricks absorb the error.

At this point, a central contradiction was fully exposed:

#+begin_src
  Reality: different layers vary enormously in sensitivity to precision loss
  - Attention projection layers: small precision drops are tolerable
  - Layers adjacent to LayerNorm: even a small perturbation can cause collapse
  - First and last layers: typically more sensitive than middle layers
  - Early vs. late training: the sensitivity distribution shifts over time

  Practice: uniform treatment — either all FP4 or all FP8

  This wastes budget. We can do better.
#+end_src

* Section 4: Per-Layer Precision Selection (2026, This Paper: SNIP)

SNIP sees this contradiction and asks a new question: *can we independently select the precision for each layer — leaving sensitive layers at FP8 and compressing insensitive ones to FP4?*

The idea itself is not original to SNIP — *the inference community has been doing it for years*. HAQ (2019) uses reinforcement learning to pick bit widths per layer; OBC (2022) uses the Hessian to measure sensitivity; BBS (2024) uses KL divergence. But SNIP's critique of these methods is sharp:

#+begin_quote
"They only measure local quantization error, without accounting for training dynamics."
#+end_quote

What does that mean? At inference time you only care about "how much does this layer's output differ from the full-precision version." Training is different — *quantization error flows through gradients into the optimizer, is amplified by AdamW's momentum, and ultimately deflects the weight update trajectory*. A scheme with small local error can send the entire model off course after 1000 training steps.

SNIP therefore designs two new metrics:

** Metric 1: Loss Divergence (Forward Path)

If layer i is quantized, *by how much does the training loss rise?* Computing this exactly requires a fresh forward pass — too expensive. SNIP uses Theorem 4.1 for approximation: treat quantization as adding a small perturbation δ to the input, so the change in loss is approximately *gradient · perturbation magnitude*. The gradient is already computed during back-propagation, so this estimate is essentially free.

** Metric 2: Weight Divergence (Backward Path)

Looking at the forward loss alone is insufficient because the optimizer has memory. SNIP also asks: *if layer i is quantized and one optimizer step is taken, how much do the weights diverge from the full-precision version?* This involves second-order cross-derivatives (of the loss with respect to both weights and inputs), which are never computed during training. The solution is clever: *inject a small Gaussian noise into the input, run one extra forward–backward pass, and observe how the gradients change*. This probes the otherwise uncomputed second-order terms at the cost of two extra passes every few steps — far cheaper than evaluating each layer independently.

** Connecting Both Metrics to an Integer Program

With *(loss divergence + weight divergence) = cost of choosing precision j for layer i*, the rest is a math problem:

#+begin_src
  Minimize   sum( q_{i,j} * x_{i,j} )       // minimize total quality loss
  Subject to sum( e_{i,j} * x_{i,j} ) >= E_t // total speedup meets target
             sum_j x_{i,j} = 1               // exactly one precision per layer
             constraint 1 satisfied within each pipeline stage  // keep pipeline balanced
#+end_src

This is a classic *knapsack variant*, solved with scipy's milp solver (HiGHS backend) in about 30 seconds. The output is a globally optimal assignment — a list specifying "FP8 here, FP4 there" for every layer.

Measured on Llama at scales of 1B / 3B / 7B / 70B, SNIP saves up to 80% of FLOPs with negligible quality degradation.

* What Is Really Happening Beneath This Line

On the surface, bit widths are falling — 32, 16, 8, 4. But the deeper cognitive shift is something else entirely.

*First shift: from "precision is a numerical problem" to "precision is an allocation problem."* Early work asked "how do we keep FP16 from blowing up" — a question of numerical technique. In SNIP's era the question becomes "given a fixed precision budget, how do we allocate it optimally" — a *resource scheduling problem*.

*Second shift: from "precision strategy is model-agnostic" to "precision strategy is coupled to the model itself."* FP8-LM's strategy is identical for every LLM; SNIP's strategy differs per model and per training phase. Mixed precision has evolved from a "framework feature" into a "model-specific hyperparameter."

*Third shift: from "use whatever the hardware supports" to "hardware / algorithm / compiler co-optimization."* SNIP explicitly encodes pipeline-parallelism constraints into the ILP — you cannot concentrate FP4 in certain pipeline stages without unbalancing the pipeline. Precision selection is no longer an isolated decision; it is coupled to the distributed execution strategy.

* Where This Might Go Next

Following the evolutionary trajectory:

1. *Dynamic precision*: SNIP currently re-solves periodically (gather statistics, then solve once). But training dynamics change. The next step is *reallocating precision at every training step*, which will require even cheaper sensitivity estimates.
2. *Hardware co-design*: once per-layer precision is algorithmically determined, can the most frequently ILP-selected precision combinations inform chip design? "Which combinations does the ILP pick most often" is exactly the data chip architects want.
3. *Cross-task transfer*: the same model architecture may have a different sensitivity distribution under different training regimes (pre-training / SFT / RLHF). Can we learn a *"meta-model of sensitivity"* rather than re-profiling from scratch each time?
4. *Down to 1–2 bits*: beyond FP4, 1.58-bit quantization (BitNet) is already underway — but that is a different line: *redesigning the architecture to suit low precision*, rather than *allocating precision within a fixed architecture*. The two lines may eventually converge.
