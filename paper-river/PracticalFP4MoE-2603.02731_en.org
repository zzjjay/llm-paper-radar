* TL;DR

One problem thread: MoE models grow too large to train → use fewer bits to represent numbers → FP8 works on Hopper → FP4 works on Blackwell → but 95% of the world's GPUs are Hopper, and the hardware provides no FP4 compute. What then?

This paper's answer: *don't let FP4 participate in computation — only in storage and transmission*. Computation stays at FP8, but bytes in memory and bytes crossing card-to-card links are cut in half. Hopper users can still harvest FP4's memory savings.

* Five Layers Back

** Layer 1 -- The Direct Targets Being Critiqued or Improved

Where *this paper* stands and what it stands on:

- *DeepSeek-V3 (Zhao 2025a)*: trained a 671B MoE on Hopper using block-wise FP8. This paper inherits its 671B scale, Hopper platform, and blockwise FP8 philosophy — but criticizes it for leaving activations and all-to-all communication at FP8 when another cut is possible.
- *Tseng et al. 2025 (MXFP4 training, AISTATS 2025)*: demonstrated that MXFP4 + stochastic rounding + random Hadamard transform can run backward passes near-losslessly — but *only validated at 6.7B scale* and *depends on Blackwell's native FP4 compute*. This paper criticizes the limited scale and hardware lock-in.
- *NVFP4 training (NVIDIA 2025b) and Chmiel 2025 (FP4 All the Way)*: end-to-end full FP4 training is viable, but again *requires Blackwell*. Hopper users can only watch from the sidelines.
- *Wang et al. 2025 (FP8 flow for MoE)*: warned that repeated ~FP4 ↔ BF16 ↔ FP8~ format round-trips erase the benefits of low precision. This paper inherits that lesson and explicitly rejects the three-hop chain ~FP4 ↔ BF16 ↔ FP8~.

** Layer 2 -- What Those Targets Stood On

- *Peng et al. 2023 (FP8-LM)*: turned "train with FP8" from an idea into running code. It is the parent of DeepSeek-V3 and all subsequent FP8/FP4 work. The problem it solved: FP8's dynamic range is too narrow for per-tensor scaling. The solution: per-tensor + delayed scaling + dynamic range monitoring.
- *Mishra et al. 2025 (MXFP8 recipes)*: applied MX thinking to FP8, showing that block-wise power-of-two scaling with stochastic rounding can match BF16 convergence. It is the methodological template for MXFP4.
- *Open Compute Project MX specification 2023*: formally defined the MXFP8/6/4 family (one power-of-two scale per 32 elements). The shared "grammar" used by Tseng, Mishra, and this paper.

** Layer 3 -- One Level Further Back

- *Rouhani et al. 2023 (Microscaling data formats for deep learning, arXiv:2310.10537)*: the founding paper for microscaling. It answered: "per-tensor is too coarse, per-element is too expensive — where is the middle ground?" Answer: each small block of 32 elements shares one scale. Later MXFP8/6/4 formats and the OCP spec all grew from here.
- *Micikevicius et al. 2017/2018 (Mixed Precision Training, FP8 formats)*: pushed FP16/FP8 training from theory into the NVIDIA mainstream stack.

** Layer 4 -- One Level Further Back

- *Micikevicius 2017 (Mixed Precision Training, ICLR 2018)*: established the master-weight + loss-scaling paradigm — keep a FP32 master copy of weights, compute in FP16, cast back. Every paper about training with fewer bits sits in this chair.
- *The MoE lineage*: Shazeer et al. 2017 (Outrageously Large Neural Networks), the first large-scale sparsely-gated MoE. GShard, Mixtral, and DeepSeek-V3 are all its descendants.

** Layer 5 -- Foundations

- *IEEE 754 (1985)*: the bible of floating-point representation. All stories about sign + exponent + mantissa start here. Section 3 of this paper discusses subnormals, guard bits, sticky bits, and RTNE at length — all dialects of IEEE 754.
- *The concept of Mixture-of-Experts itself*: Jacobs & Jordan 1991 (Adaptive mixtures of local experts).

Recursion terminates here: going further leads into floating-point mathematics and neural network theory from 30 years ago.

* Forward Extensions -- What Came After This Paper

Progress publicly known as of early 2026 (possibly incomplete):

- *GPT-OSS-120B (OpenAI 2025)* is rumored to use FP4 training, but *no configuration has been published*. This paper is the first work to fully open-source 671B FP4-on-Hopper training.
- *Once Blackwell becomes widespread*: this paper's "software-simulated FP4" is a bridge for the Hopper transition period. When B200s proliferate, native MXFP4 GEMM will become available, and the bitwise conversion kernels here will become "historical scaffolding" — but the *asymmetric design philosophy of "FP4 for storage + FP8 for compute"* will persist. Even on Blackwell with sufficient FP4 compute, backward passes still need higher precision, making this paper's asymmetric approach broadly applicable.
- *Predictable but not yet published extensions*: pushing FP4 to optimizer states (Adam moments); extending "software simulation" to older A100/H800 GPUs; applying this paper's FP4 communication compression idea to pipeline parallelism.

* Evolutionary Narrative -- How the Problem Grew

The following traces the story forward chronologically, from 2017 to this paper.

** Starting Point: Bigger Models Are Better, but They Don't Fit

Around 2017, everyone noticed the same pattern: scale a neural network up and performance improves. But *"bigger" costs money* — more parameters overflow GPU memory, and backward passes grow slower.

The most direct way to save: *stop storing weights in FP32 (32-bit float) and use FP16 (16-bit) instead*. Immediately halves memory.

But switching directly to FP16 fails — the precision is too low, and gradient updates get swallowed. Micikevicius proposed *Mixed Precision Training* in 2017: keep a FP32 "master copy" of weights, cast to FP16 for computation, cast back for updates. Add loss scaling so small gradients aren't clipped to zero. Once this paradigm was established, all subsequent low-precision training work stood on its shoulders.

*Remaining problem*: FP16 is still somewhat expensive. Can we cut further?

** Cutting to FP8: Peng 2023 Makes It Work

By 2023, NVIDIA's H100 (Hopper) arrived with native *FP8* (8-bit float) support. In theory: half the memory of FP16 and twice the speed.

But FP8 is far harder than FP16 — *its dynamic range is too narrow*. FP16 spans roughly 1e-8 to 6e4; FP8 (E4M3) covers only about 1e-3 to 448. Activations and gradients overflow or underflow at the slightest extremes.

Peng et al.'s FP8-LM made it actually work via *per-tensor dynamic scaling* combined with delayed tracking of each tensor's maximum value. This was the first genuinely runnable FP8 training scheme.

*Remaining problem*: a single scale per tensor is still too coarse. A tensor containing both large and small values loses precision for the small values under a uniform scale.

** The Middle Ground: Microscaling (MX)

In 2023, Rouhani et al. proposed *Microscaling*: *instead of one scale per tensor or one scale per element, share one scale across every 32 elements*. The granularity is just right — 100× finer than per-tensor, yet with manageable metadata overhead (one 8-bit scale per 32 8-bit elements, roughly 3.1% overhead).

This approach was codified into a formal OCP specification, spawning a family: *MXFP8* (32 8-bit elements + scale), *MXFP6*, *MXFP4* (32 4-bit elements + scale). MX laid the road for all subsequent "cut another bit" work.

*Remaining problem*: MX is a standard, but *"can 4-bit actually train large models?"* No one knew.

** Cutting to FP4: But Only on Blackwell

In 2025, Tseng et al. (AISTATS) demonstrated: MXFP4 + stochastic rounding + random Hadamard transform can run *backward passes* successfully, with near-lossless quality at 6.7B scale. That same year, NVIDIA released *NVFP4* (smaller block size + E4M3 scale), and Chmiel et al. produced *FP4 All the Way* (fully end-to-end FP4 training).

Training a 12B model in FP4 to near-FP8 quality — it finally worked.

But *all of this depended on NVIDIA Blackwell (B200) GPUs with native FP4 Tensor Cores*. Hopper (H100/H800) has no FP4 compute.

An awkward situation emerged: *95% of GPUs running production training are Hopper, yet FP4's benefits are exclusively available to Blackwell users*.

*Remaining problem*: Can Hopper users capture any of FP4's gains?

** Meanwhile: MoE Amplifies the Memory and Communication Problem

A parallel thread. In 2017, Shazeer proposed *Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)*: each token activates only a subset of experts, making "large model, less compute" possible. In 2021, Google's *GShard* engineered MoE at scale; in 2024, Mistral's *Mixtral* and DeepSeek-V3 (Zhao 2025a) pushed MoE to 671B parameters.

But MoE carries a hidden cost: *activation memory and all-to-all communication explode*. Each token must be routed to the correct expert; each GPU must store intermediate activations and exchange expert inputs and outputs with other GPUs. The larger the MoE, the more this "routing and buffering" overhead becomes the bottleneck.

DeepSeek-V3 used *blockwise FP8 + DeepEP (FP8 communication library) + DeepGEMM (FP8 kernels)* to successfully train a 671B MoE on Hopper. This is the most complete FP8-on-Hopper success story to date.

*Remaining problem*: DeepSeek-V3's activations and communication run at FP8. Can another half be cut? FP4 could certainly do it — but Hopper has no FP4 compute.

** This Paper: Decoupling Compute from Storage and Transmission

All threads converge here. The problem inventory:

1. *Goal*: let a 671B MoE model on Hopper benefit from FP4's memory and communication savings.
2. *Constraint*: Hopper has no FP4 Tensor Cores.
3. *Trap*: Wang 2025 already warned that ~FP4 ↔ BF16 ↔ FP8~ round-trip casts erase the gains of low precision.

This paper's key innovation is not "how to do FP4 computation on Hopper" — that is impossible. Instead it *accepts the impossibility and routes around it*:

#+begin_src
   Desired FP4 benefit          | Feasible on Hopper | Approach
   ----------------------------+--------------------+-----------------
   Save memory (store acts)    | Yes                | Store acts as MXFP4
   Save communication bandwidth| Yes                | all-to-all sends MXFP4
   Save compute (run GEMM)     | No                 | GEMM stays at FP8
#+end_src

*Decouple "compute" from "store/transmit"*. Computation continues with Hopper's native FP8 Tensor Cores; storage and transmission are compressed to FP4. Hopper users get *half* of FP4's benefits (memory + bandwidth) while forgoing the other half (compute throughput) — and that is already attractive.

To make this design actually run, the paper's two core technical contributions are:

1. *Direct FP8 ↔ FP4 bitwise conversion kernels*. No BF16 intermediate. Hand-crafted bit manipulation (handling implicit leading bits, subnormals, guard bits, sticky bits, RTNE rounding) remaps FP8 (E4M3) directly to FP4 (E2M1) and back.
2. *Scaling-aware row/column layout conversion*. MXFP4's per-32-element block scales must be transposed between row-major and column-major layouts; the paper designs a "compute scales first → asynchronous write-back" pipeline that overlaps scale computation with subsequent compute kernels, hiding the overhead.

Plus one engineering finding: *FP4 in the forward pass, retreat to FP8 in the backward pass*. This is an empirical conclusion — the authors found that the extra FP4→FP8 dequantization overhead during the backward pass outweighs the communication savings from transmitting FP4 gradients. Hence an asymmetry: *aggressive forward + conservative backward*.

Results: 671B MoE on Hopper, activation memory −14.8% (11.8 GB), throughput +12.5% (1157 → 1302 tokens/GPU/s), convergence on par with the FP8 baseline.

* Insights -- What Is Really Changing Along This Evolutionary Thread

On the surface: the bit count is shrinking (32 → 16 → 8 → 4).

But beneath the surface, several deeper transitions are underway.

** 1. From "Uniform Precision" to "Structured Precision"

Early mixed precision had just two tiers: master FP32 + compute FP16. The FP8 era began *allocating precision by position* — one tier for weights, one for activations, one for gradients. With MX, precision is allocated *per 32-element block*. In this paper, precision is allocated *by forward vs. backward pass* and *by compute vs. store vs. transmit*.

Precision has moved from being a scalar hyperparameter to being a design space. This is a paradigm shift: future low-precision training design is fundamentally a *precision routing problem* — deciding how many bits to assign to each tensor, each phase, and each purpose.

** 2. From "Hardware Leads" to "Software Asserts Control"

Along the FP16 → FP8 → FP4 trajectory, the pattern has been: NVIDIA releases a new GPU → academia adopts the new format → industry follows. Blackwell's FP4 follows this script.

This paper reverses it: *the hardware gave us no FP4 compute, so software simulates FP4 storage and transmission*. This is a shift from "wait for hardware" to "route around hardware." The same logic applies to "software-simulated MXFP4" on ROCm/MI300 or "software-simulated MXFP8" on A100. Once "low-precision training" is decomposed into three independent design variables — store, transmit, compute — "software can patch hardware gaps" becomes a general strategy.

** 3. From "Vertically Integrated" to "Interface as King"

DeepSeek-V3 wrote its own DeepGEMM and its own DeepEP — a fully custom, vertically integrated stack. This paper does the opposite: *plug into Transformer Engine's blockwise FP8 recipe*, define three well-specified conversion kernels (~BF16ToFP4Row~, ~FP4RowToFP8Row~, ~FP4RowToFP8Col~) as the interface, and reuse the existing stack for everything else.

This signals that low-precision training is evolving from "large vertically integrated engineering projects" toward "a standardized precision-conversion layer plugged into an existing training stack." Whoever defines the de facto standard for precision-conversion interfaces will own the ecosystem position.

** 4. Where This Thread Is Most Likely to Go Next

- *Precision routers*: automatically deciding how many bits each tensor uses at each phase. Moving from hand-crafted rules (FP4 forward, FP8 backward) toward learned or searched policies.
- *Software-simulated FP4 spreading to all non-Blackwell GPUs*: H800, A100, MI300, and Huawei 910 will all develop their own "soft FP4" implementations.
- *Pushing FP4 into optimizer states*: Adam's first and second moments are still FP32. The next cut will fall here (8-bit Adam already exists; 4-bit Adam is inevitable).
- *Communication precision becoming an independent design variable*: this paper is already doing it — FP4 for communication, FP8 for compute. In the future, communication precision will be pushed more aggressively than compute precision, because bandwidth is always more expensive than compute.

* Explaining the Whole Thread to a Non-Expert -- One Paragraph

People want to train larger models, but GPUs can't hold them, so they started cutting the bit count: from 32 down to 16, then to 8, then to 4. Each cut introduces a new problem of "not enough precision, dynamic range too narrow," and each solution is "make the scaling factor more fine-grained — from one scale for the whole tensor, to one scale per 32 numbers." But cutting to 4 bits hits a fork in the road: you need Blackwell GPUs for 4-bit compute, and most of the world is still running Hopper. *This paper's clever move is to split "compute" from "store and transmit"* — computation stays at FP8, but what sits in memory and crosses card-to-card links is compressed to FP4, so Hopper users can capture half the gain. The cost is writing a set of bitwise conversion kernels that move data directly between FP4 and FP8 without touching an intermediate format. The result: on a 671B MoE model, 14.8% less memory, 12.5% faster throughput, convergence unchanged.
