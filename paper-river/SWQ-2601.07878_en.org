* The River of the Problem

Can you squeeze a model with tens of billions of parameters onto your laptop—where every weight gets only 2 bits—and still have it speak coherently?

This has been the hardest wall in the neural-network compression lineage for the past decade. 4-bit is nearly lossless; 3-bit starts to hiccup; 2-bit descends into incoherence. The difficulty is not in deciding what to discard, but in figuring out how to rescue the remaining weights so that the model's overall output stays as close as possible to the original.

Each generation of papers is answering the same question: when you are forced to throw away information, how do you use the remaining capacity to minimise the damage? The sticking point is that every generation believed it had found the right "loss function"—only for the next generation to discover that the loss function was blind along certain dimensions.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

Starting from 1992, let us trace how this line advanced step by step to 2026.

The focus of each section is not "what did this paper do" but "what did it see that the previous paper could not."

** 1992 | OBS: Prune One Weight, Surgically Compensate All the Others Immediately

*** What Problem It Saw

In 1989, LeCun proposed Optimal Brain Damage to determine which weights could be pruned. His criterion was a second-order Taylor expansion of the loss around the current weights, coupled with the assumption that the Hessian is diagonal—meaning weights are independent of one another.

This assumption is mathematically convenient but physically wrong. When two weights are strongly correlated, pruning one of them should shift the other toward a new optimal value. A diagonal Hessian erases that interaction entirely, so the resulting "importance" scores are misleading.

*** Its Fix

Hassibi used the full inverse Hessian. For every weight he computed not only how much removing it would raise the loss (the saliency) but also exactly how the remaining weights should shift to drive the loss back down—a quadratic problem whose Lagrange multiplier yields a closed-form solution.

Analogy: OBD asks "if I remove this beam, how much does the building sag?" OBS asks "if I remove this beam, how should I tighten the other beams so the building does not sag at all?"

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because it acknowledged that weights are not independent. But the Hessian of a large network is astronomically large—a d-dimensional layer has a d×d Hessian, and inverting it costs O(d^3). 1992 networks were small enough to handle; 2022 LLMs were not.

This line was frozen for thirty years at that point.

** 2022 | OBQ: Thirty Years Later, Someone Finally Noticed OBS Could Quantize Too

*** What Problem It Saw

Pruning and quantization look like different problems—pruning sets a weight to 0, quantization snaps it to the nearest grid point. But both are fundamentally "force a weight to a particular value, then measure how much model loss is disturbed."

The OBS framework applies naturally, yet for three decades everyone treated it as a pruning-only tool and never extended it to quantization. The result: pruning enjoyed OBS's elegant second-order path, while quantization was stuck with brute-force round-to-nearest.

*** Its Fix

OBQ transplanted the full OBS machinery into quantization. At each step it picks one weight, "rounds" it to the nearest grid point (the disturbance), then uses the Hessian formula to push the compensation error into the remaining weights. It proceeds row by row, weight by weight.

The transformation: replace "set to 0" in OBS with "set to q(w)"; every other formula stays unchanged.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because the compensation is not a post-hoc patch—it is optimal at every step. On the scale of ResNet-50 the results were striking, finishing in under an hour.

But the cost is O(d^3) per row. GPT-175B has hidden dimension 12288; running a cubic operation on every row would take an estimated 6 months.

*This is the starting point for GPTQ: a provably correct algorithm that is completely infeasible at LLM scale.*

** 2022 | GPTQ: Give Up the Perfect Order, Gain a Thousand-Fold Speedup

*** What Problem It Saw

OBQ is slow because at every step it selects "the weight that introduces the smallest error next"—a greedy strategy that requires a full rerank on each iteration. At large dimensionality this is the primary performance killer.

But the Frantar team realised something: when thousands of columns need to be quantized, the difference in final loss between "the perfect ordering" and "any fixed ordering" is far smaller than the computational cost of insisting on perfection.

*** Its Fix

Three moves:

1. Fix the column order — process strictly left to right, no reranking.
2. Block-wise processing — handle 128 columns at a time, update within the block, lazily synchronise across blocks.
3. Rewrite the Hessian inverse via Cholesky decomposition — numerically stable, avoids blow-up at large dimensionality.

Result: OPT-175B completes on 4 GPUs in 4 hours, with accuracy almost indistinguishable from OBQ. A three-thousand-fold speedup.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because it acknowledged that "the perfect algorithm at a certain scale is simply infeasible—engineering trade-offs are necessary." The payoff of that trade-off was turning the impossible into the practical.

But GPTQ left three openings: first, it cares only about weights and ignores activations entirely, while LLM activations contain extreme outlier channels; second, its rounding is one-shot with no room for iterative refinement; third, it uses a plain element-wise MSE loss, assuming "the closer each layer's activations are to the original, the better."

The next three generations would each plug one of these gaps in turn.

** 2023 | OmniQuant: Since Activations Have Outliers, Learn to Work Around Them

*** What Problem It Saw

GPTQ holds up at INT4 but starts to collapse at INT3. The cause is not weight quantization error but activations. In certain channels of an LLM, activations can be 100× larger than in other channels. These "outlier channels" stretch the quantization grid so wide that most normal activations are crammed into only a handful of central grid points, wasting resolution.

SmoothQuant and AWQ had already identified this, but their remedy was hand-crafted: fix a smoothing factor and multiply the activation scale into the weights. This hardcoded approach works at 4-bit but loses its grip at 2–3 bits.

*** Its Fix

OmniQuant introduces two learnable modules:

1. *LWC (Learnable Weight Clipping)*: learn a per-channel clipping threshold—determining the range to which weights are clamped before quantization.
2. *LET (Learnable Equivalent Transformation)*: learn a per-channel scale and shift that equivalently migrates activation outliers into the weights—a slightly larger weight is fine because LWC can control its quantization precision independently.

The transformation: replace SmoothQuant's "hardcoded smoothing factor" with "learnable parameters," then train them with block-wise SGD on calibration data while the LLM itself stays frozen.

Key: after training, LET's scales and shifts are folded back into the weights and LayerNorm, leaving zero inference overhead.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because it acknowledged that "outliers are not noise—they are structure, and should be learned rather than avoided." OmniQuant beats GPTQ substantially at W3.

But OmniQuant still uses block-wise MSE loss. And a subtler pit: its "rounding" is actually soft rounding—continuously differentiable during training, then hard-cut to integers at deployment. The soft-to-hard transition was never truly optimised.

** 2024 | TesseraQ: The Jump from Soft Rounding to Hard Rounding Is the Final Precision Cliff

*** What Problem It Saw

Li and Panda examined OmniQuant's training process closely and found something: during training, the continuously relaxed "soft" weights yield a low loss; but the moment deployment snaps them to hard integers, the loss jumps. At W3 this jump is tolerable; at W2 it eats all the training gains.

The underlying issue they identified: OmniQuant optimises the loss under soft weights, but the user receives a model with hard weights. Nobody was managing the gap between the two.

*** Its Fix

*PAR (Progressive Adaptive Rounding)*: turn "soft-to-hard" into a gradual annealing process rather than a single final switch. During each block's reconstruction, the rounding variables are pushed from soft toward hard incrementally, and the model simultaneously adjusts its weights to accommodate the progressively harder rounding.

Analogy: OmniQuant teaches you to walk on grass, then suddenly places you on ice; TesseraQ transitions you gradually from grass to ice, letting you adapt to the changing friction at every step.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because it acknowledged that "the gap between training objective and deployment objective itself needs to be optimised." TesseraQ cuts OmniQuant's 37.37 PPL on W2 down to 8.05.

But TesseraQ still uses an MSE block-reconstruction loss. It optimised "MSE under hard rounding," but never questioned MSE itself.

*This is the starting point for the 2026 SW-Loss paper: by this point, every player in the lineage was using MSE—but MSE itself was the blind spot.*

** 2026 | SW-Loss: Perplexity Looks Fine, but the Distribution Has Already Drifted

*** What Problem It Saw

The authors ran an experiment: a model quantized with OmniQuant showed only a tiny perplexity gap relative to the original. Looks good.

But when they examined the shape of each layer's activation distribution, it had shifted. Mean and variance were roughly preserved, but the shape—skewness, kurtosis, tail weight—had changed. Under certain prompts this shape drift could amplify suddenly, causing the model to produce wrong answers. Perplexity, being an average metric, masked this localised drift entirely.

At a deeper level: MSE loss only measures "how close are two numbers at the same position"—it is completely blind to distributional shape. Two distributions can have identical MSE yet one is Gaussian and the other bimodal; MSE cannot tell the difference. An element-wise loss is structurally incapable of capturing distribution-level discrepancies.

What about KL divergence? The authors tried it; the results were unstable. KL is extremely sensitive to low-probability events, producing wildly varying signals under ultra-low-bit settings. It also applies only to the final softmax output—it offers no purchase on the geometric alignment of intermediate hidden layers.

*** Its Fix

Introduce *Sliced-Wasserstein distance* to align block-output distributions.

Wasserstein distance can compare the shape difference of any two distributions, but in high dimensions it is computationally prohibitive. Sliced-Wasserstein is an elegant compromise: randomly project the high-dimensional distribution onto many one-dimensional directions, and compute the Wasserstein distance along each one-dimensional slice—a 1-D Wasserstein is just a sorting problem and extremely fast. Average these 1-D distances; theoretically this approximates the true Wasserstein at rate O(1/sqrt(L)).

Overall loss: MSE handles point-wise location alignment; SW handles overall distributional shape alignment; the two are combined as a weighted sum. SW computation occurs only during the calibration phase—zero additional inference overhead.

The transformation: replace TesseraQ's loss L = MSE with L = MSE + lambda * SW; leave the rest of the training pipeline unchanged.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked for two reasons: first, it squarely addressed the long-ignored problem that "element-wise losses are blind to distributions"; second, it found a computationally feasible distribution-alignment tool for high dimensions.

Empirically, it recovers 4–20% of the accuracy loss on LLaMA-2-7B over OmniQuant, and 3–7% over TesseraQ.

Remaining pits: SW loss requires choosing the number of projection directions and the lambda weight—these hyperparameters still need tuning. SW is also only an approximation of Wasserstein, leaving some distance from the optimal distributional metric. At a deeper level, this paper challenges "which loss to use" while remaining within the same overarching framework—block-wise calibration. The next generation may ask "why must it be block-wise at all?"

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026, no second PTQ paper has been observed using SW loss for activation alignment. This "loss-function" branch is currently a lone path.

But a parallel evolutionary line has been active over the same period, answering the same problem with a different kind of answer:

* Insight

Flattening the whole lineage into a single view, what is truly evolving is not the "algorithm" but *"the definition of what counts as good"*:

1. *1992 → 2022*: Good = minimise local quadratic loss (OBS/OBQ framework)
2. *2022*: Good = minimise quadratic loss that is actually computable at scale (GPTQ makes the definition realistic)
3. *2023*: Good = minimise quadratic loss that also accounts for activation distribution scale (OmniQuant)
4. *2024*: Good = minimise quadratic loss that holds under true hard rounding, not under soft relaxation (TesseraQ)
5. *2026*: Good = not only do individual numbers match up, the shape of the entire distribution must match as well (SW-Loss)

Each generation questioned "the 'good' that the previous generation thought it had pinned down." This is the most easily overlooked aspect of research—everyone assumes scientific progress is about "finding a better solution," but more often it is about "redefining the problem."

A second observation: from 2023 onward, the PTQ community split into two parallel tracks. One track does "smarter calibration inside transformer blocks" (OmniQuant → TesseraQ → SW-Loss); the other "replaces the weight representation itself" (QuIP → QTIP → AQLM). The former is fine-grained local refinement; the latter is replacing the foundation. Both tracks are advancing independently, and nobody has yet combined them systematically—this is a hybridisation direction that could plausibly appear in 2026.

A third observation: the true contribution of the SW-Loss paper is not the SW tool itself but the fact that it exposes a hidden assumption: "low perplexity means the model is still fine." When an entire community evaluates with the same metric, that metric quietly gets overfit.

Where is the next step most likely to go? Two directions:

- *Make distribution alignment a first-class citizen during pre-training*: today it is a one-shot post-PTQ alignment; in future it could run continuously inside QAT (Quantization-Aware Training).
- *Upgrade from per-layer alignment to cross-layer alignment*: both MSE and SW are layer-wise, but quantization errors accumulate and propagate across layers. A cross-layer distributional alignment objective may be needed.

* Takeaways

This evolutionary line offers three directly actionable lessons for "how to do research":

*First, do not trust the tool everyone uses by default.* From OBQ to TesseraQ, MSE was in use for over a decade without anyone asking "is MSE even appropriate?" It took SW-Loss to mount a direct challenge. Once a tool becomes the default, it becomes the most deserving target for scrutiny.

*Second, first make the computationally infeasible feasible, then optimise it.* The step from OBQ to GPTQ is not "algorithmically cleverer"—it is "accepting that the perfect algorithm is infeasible at 175B scale and making engineering compromises." Many breakthroughs lie not in algorithmic elegance but in turning "theoretically sound but practically unrunnable" into something that actually runs.

*Third, every paper should explicitly name the pits left by the previous one.* OmniQuant states directly that GPTQ ignores activations; TesseraQ states directly that OmniQuant never optimises hard rounding; SW-Loss states directly that everyone has been misusing MSE. This "honestly name the prior blind spot" style of writing lets readers grasp in a single glance why a new method is needed—far more effectively than a comprehensive survey.

*What this means for you*: next time you read the latest paper in a field, do not go straight to the method section. First extract what its introduction's third paragraph says—specifically "what concrete deficiencies do prior methods have?" That is the leading edge of the problem's evolutionary line. Then dig backwards five layers, and what you hold is not the knowledge of one paper but the full longitudinal depth of the field.
