* The River of the Problem

In a MoE (Mixture-of-Experts) large model, each forward pass activates only a
handful of experts, yet the entire expert pool must sit in GPU memory at all
times — you pay for the memory of every expert while benefiting from the compute
of only a few. How do you actually remove the experts that are never used,
without making the model dumber?

The hard part is "how to judge whether a given expert matters." Experts cannot
be scored in isolation — how tokens flow from layer to layer, how choosing a
particular expert in one layer cascades into which expert catches the token in
the next, these cross-layer causal relationships are completely invisible to any
local scoring scheme. Generation after generation of methods has bounced back
and forth against this wall: some borrow the old playbook from dense models,
some peek at the routing signal of the current layer only, and none has ever
taken seriously the fact that "a routing decision is an entire trajectory."

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2016-2023 | The Dense-Pruning Trinity (Wanda / SparseGPT / LLM-Pruner): Every Parameter Is Present, So Score by Average Signal

*** What Problem It Saw

Large language model parameter counts exploded, but not all parameters matter
equally. In a dense Transformer every parameter participates in every forward
pass, so you can safely use signals like "how large is this weight," "how strong
is its activation," or "how much does the gradient change if you remove it" —
global, per-token-averaged signals — to decide whether a weight, an attention
head, or an entire layer is redundant.

*** Its Solution

Wanda scores by "weight magnitude × input activation norm"; SparseGPT applies
second-order information for one-shot pruning; LLM-Pruner uses gradient
information to remove entire structural blocks. Their strategies differ, but
they share one premise: the model is dense, every parameter is present in every
forward pass, so you can "keep a running tab" on each parameter by averaging
over time.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Digs)

The premise holds — parameters really do participate every time, and average
statistics really do reflect their contribution. But this premise collapses
entirely in MoE architectures: a given expert may be skipped by the vast
majority of tokens. Using an "average" signal to evaluate a module that is
"frequently bypassed" is structurally misaligned from the ground up. That
collapse of the premise is exactly the pit the next paper must face.

** 2024 | MoE-Pruner: Force-Fitting the Dense Formula with a Routing Weight

*** What Problem It Saw

Xie et al. recognized that the dense pruning "average-signal" scoring formula
cannot be applied directly to MoE — because experts are selectively activated
by a router, not universally present. Rather than discarding the old formula,
however, they tried to "stuff routing information into it."

*** Its Solution

MoE-Pruner takes Wanda's "weight magnitude × activation" formula and multiplies
it by an additional routing-weight term — effectively patching the old formula
with an "MoE-specific correction" so that scoring at least knows "how likely
the router is to select this expert."

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Digs)

This step does make scoring "aware of routing," which is a real step forward
over blindly copying the dense formula. But the resulting quantity is still
something you can compute from a single layer in a single forward pass — it
only looks at "how strongly this expert was selected at this layer this time,"
with no consideration of "after selecting this expert, which experts get
chain-selected in the next layer." The structural cross-layer routing
correlations that truly characterize expert-centric architectures are completely
sidestepped by this patch. This half-measure — seeing routing but not
cross-layer routing — forced the two branches that followed.

** 2024-2026 | MoE-Specific Pruning Branch (Lu et al. / MoP / REAP): Better Local Signals That Understand Experts

*** What Problem It Saw

The "force-fit formula" of MoE-Pruner was too crude. This wave of work
recognized that MoE pruning needs importance signals designed specifically
around the "expert" as a unit, not a formula inherited from dense pruning with
one extra multiplication term.

*** Its Solution

Lu et al. measure an expert's importance by "how much performance drops when a
subset containing it is removed"; Mosaic Pruning (MoP) uses activation variance
of experts; REAP uses router-weighted expert norms. Each signal has its own
ingenuity, but all of them share one trait: they still assign an independent
score to each expert at each layer separately, then concatenate the scores
across layers.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Digs)

These signals do track the true usage patterns of experts more faithfully than
MoE-Pruner, and they deliver better empirical results (REAP is the strongest
baseline in the paper). But the paper's own description is "local,
layer-isolated heuristics" — each layer and each expert is treated as an
independent individual, with no modeling of "does choosing a particular expert
at this layer tend to pull certain experts into play at the next layer?" And
MAESTRO will later show that it is precisely this cross-layer joint distribution
that constitutes a complete picture of an expert's true contribution.

** 2023-2025 | Expert-Merging Branch (MEO / HC-SMoE): Don't Throw Them Away — Merge Them

*** What Problem It Saw

This parallel branch approached the problem from a different angle: pruning is
hard deletion that permanently discards what experts have learned. Since many
experts functionally overlap, why not "knead" redundant experts together,
preserving their shared knowledge, instead of simply throwing them away?

*** Its Solution

MEO performs weighted averaging of multiple experts' weights according to
routing similarity, merging them into one; HC-SMoE goes finer-grained — it
first clusters functionally similar experts via hierarchical clustering, then
merges experts within each cluster.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Digs)

Merging preserves more information than hard deletion, and intuitively feels
safer. But the REAP paper (Lasby et al., 2026) demonstrated something
counterintuitive in experiments: merging loses fine-grained routing control and
introduces an "irreversible error" — because the merged expert no longer
corresponds to the precise decision boundary the router originally learned. This
finding actually bolstered the case for continuing with pruning rather than
merging, and it also shows that regardless of which branch you take — pruning
or merging — both are stuck on the same shared dead end: local signals are not
enough.

** 2026-07 | MAESTRO: Treating the Entire Routing Trajectory as a Markov Chain

*** What Problem It Saw

All the methods in both preceding branches are fundamentally asking "does this
expert, at this layer, matter on its own?" But the authors counter: could an
expert be "moderately active" at layer ℓ precisely because the tokens routed to
it will almost all land on a "rarely-used" expert at layer ℓ+1? If so, the
expert's true value depends on its position in the entire routing trajectory,
not on its isolated appearance frequency at a single layer. Local signals are
structurally incapable of capturing this kind of relationship.

*** Its Solution

The (layer, expert) state sequence that a token traverses through the network
is directly modeled as a Markov chain: which expert is selected at layer ℓ+1
depends only on which expert was selected at layer ℓ (the Markov assumption),
and transition probabilities are estimated from autoregressive rollouts on a
small calibration dataset. Connecting the last layer back to the first forms a
cycle; adding a small self-loop smoothing term ensures the chain is ergodic
(irreducible + aperiodic), so it possesses a unique stationary distribution π —
the probability that the router, in the long run of infinite generation, rests
on each expert state. The experts with the smallest π values are the ones that
are most expendable in the global sense.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Digs)

The stationary distribution π is a global quantity "simmered out" of the entire
autoregressive trajectory; it naturally encodes cross-layer dependencies. It
does not ask "how many times were you selected at this layer?" but rather "if
we follow the model's own generation distribution indefinitely, how large a
long-run share of the whole network do you occupy?" Empirically, at 50%
compression, MAESTRO improves average performance by up to 3.6% relative to the
strongest baseline REAP, with noticeably lower cross-task variance — confirming
that capturing global routing structure is more robust than any local signal.

Yet the authors themselves flag two new pits in their Limitations section: first,
the Markov assumption is only first-order — layer ℓ+1 only sees layer ℓ, not
any earlier history, so if an expert's importance depends on longer routing
histories, the chain will underestimate it; second, MAESTRO makes only binary
keep-or-remove decisions at the whole-expert granularity, and cannot be combined
with width-pruning that thins out the internals of individual experts. These two
points are the currently unfilled pits left on this problem's evolutionary line.

* Insight

What truly changed along this line is not "how many times the scoring formula
was swapped," but rather the domain of the concept "importance" itself kept
expanding: from "this parameter," to "this expert at this layer," to "this
expert's long-run standing in the entire routing trajectory." Every
methodological upgrade took whatever context the previous generation "couldn't
see" and brought it inside the scoring input — from ignoring routing entirely,
to seeing single-layer routing, to seeing the joint distribution of cross-layer
routing.

The most likely next directions are the two pits MAESTRO itself points out:
replacing the first-order Markov chain with a higher-order one (capturing longer
routing-history dependencies), and combining "whole-expert binary pruning" with
"intra-expert width/depth pruning" for joint optimization — both are essentially
continuations of the same parent theme: let the importance signal see a little
more context.

* Takeaway

This line reveals a general pattern for finding new problems: when a field's
methods are all "scoring locally" and the system itself contains cross-unit
dependency structure, "explicitly modeling that dependency" is usually the next
breakthrough — regardless of whether the dependency is cross-layer routing in
MoE, cross-module call chains in some other system, or cross-time-step state
transitions.

In practice this means: whenever you encounter an existing method that uses
"local scoring + independent evaluation," ask yourself first — "among these
units being scored, is there a temporal or structural dependency I am quietly
ignoring?" If there is, modeling that dependency as an explicit graph or chain
(even something as simple as a Markov chain) is very likely the lever that will
let you surpass the existing approach.
