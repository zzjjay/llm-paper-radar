* The River of the Problem

If you compress a large model to one-quarter of its size, what happens to its ability to write code?

This looks like an engineering question, but the deeper you dig the more it becomes an ontological one: what does a model actually "know" about code? Is the model's internal representation of a ~for~ keyword, an indentation, or a semicolon written into its most prominent "trunk knowledge," or is it written into those marginal, rare, easily-discarded neurons in the tail?

The question is hard precisely because nobody has ever measured it properly. Everyone knows compression causes accuracy loss, but which part of the capability is lost remains as murky as a fog. Run HumanEval, watch pass rate drop two points, and people say "slight degradation, acceptable" — yet what is happening at the token level? Which characters are being compressed away? Which are actually amplified? Nobody looks.

The headwaters of this river lie in a problem ignored for ten years: *what kind of fragments does a tokenizer cut code into*. Its downstream is a phenomenon only recently discovered: *compression does not harm the model uniformly — it specifically harms rare tokens*. In between, generation after generation of researchers collided with this problem from different angles, each collision opening a new crater.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

The real protagonist of this line of work is not any single paper but a phenomenon that slowly came into focus: *when compressing a model, what gets dropped first is not "ordinary capability" but "capability living in the tail."* Code keywords, special symbols, and rare identifiers sit exactly in that tail.

Nobody thought in those terms in 2015.

** 2015 | Hinton Distillation: Pouring a Large Model's Knowledge into a Small One

*** What problem it saw

Deploying a 175B-parameter model is too expensive. But the knowledge acquired during training is clearly not confined to one-hot labels — an image classifier that tells you "0.9 cat, 0.08 dog, 0.0001 airplane" carries information in those relative probabilities themselves. Throwing away that soft distribution is wasteful.

*** Its solution

Make the small model (student) fit not hard labels but the *soft probability distribution* output by the large model (teacher). Raise the temperature a little so the small-tailed probabilities also participate in training. This is "distillation" — the name comes from chemistry: extracting the essence from raw material.

*** Why it worked (and where a new pit was buried)

The soft distribution contains "dark knowledge": the teacher's probability of mistaking a cat for a dog is orders of magnitude higher than mistaking it for an airplane, and that relative relationship tells the student "cats and dogs look alike." A student seeing only one-hot labels can never learn this.

The buried pit: everyone assumed this transfer was "lossless." For ten years nobody seriously asked: if you distill repeatedly, does that soft distribution grow progressively narrower? Does the tail get shaved off generation by generation? That question would not receive serious attention until 2023.

** 2020 | Bostrom & Durrett: BPE Is Actually Suboptimal

*** What problem it saw

GPT-2 used BPE tokenization, and everyone followed. But BPE produces peculiar subwords — "Completely preposterous suggestions" becomes "Comple-t-ely prep-ost-erous suggest-ions," violating any linguistic intuition. Yet nobody stopped to ask: what if we swapped in a different tokenizer? Five years passed this way.

*** Its solution

The most elementary scientific method: control the variable. Same Transformer architecture, same training data — only swap the tokenizer from BPE to Unigram-LM. The latter segments the same word as "Complete-ly pre-post-er-ous suggestion-s," which aligns with morpheme boundaries.

The result: across English and Japanese, across multiple downstream tasks, Unigram matched or beat BPE across the board.

*** Why it worked (and where a new pit was buried)

BPE is greedy merging by frequency, with no regard for semantic boundaries. Unigram-LM uses a probabilistic model to select segmentation points, naturally converging toward morpheme structure.

The buried pit: it proved "the tokenizer is a first-class hyperparameter, not a preprocessing afterthought" — but gave no answer on how to fix things, and even less on "what specific trouble does the tokenizer cause in a special-symbol-dense domain like code?" Is ~for~ a single complete token or split into ~f~ + ~or~? Nobody had looked systematically.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Even a 175B Model Can Be Quantized in One Shot

*** What problem it saw

Previously, compressing a model from 16-bit to 4-bit required either quantization-aware training (QAT) — re-running the full training loop, astronomically expensive for 175B — or earlier PTQ methods like ZeroQuant that took days at 175B scale. In practice, nobody could afford to compress.

*** Its solution

Use approximate second-order information (layer-wise Optimal Brain Quantization + Cholesky reparameterization) to compress weights to 3–4 bits in one shot, without retraining. A 175B model done in 4 hours. Reported perplexity is nearly unchanged; inference is 3–4× faster.

*** Why it worked (and where a new pit was buried)

Second-order information tells you which direction to nudge each weight and by how much to minimize damage. Computed once, no iterative refinement needed.

The buried pit — and it is the pit that every subsequent paper in this river attacks: *"near-lossless perplexity" is an extremely coarse aggregate metric*. A 1% average drop could mean "99% of common tokens are completely unchanged, 100% of rare tokens are destroyed." Perplexity cannot reveal this distribution. At this point, nobody had any incentive to look deeper. That incentive would come once model-collapse research made everyone alert to the rule that "the rare things die first."

** 2023/24 | Shumailov "Model Collapse": Training on a Model's Own Output Is Dangerous

*** What problem it saw

An accidental discovery: if you use GPT-3 to generate text, train a new model on it, use that model to generate more, train again, and repeat — after a few generations the model "forgets" rare content from the original data. Eventually only high-frequency boilerplate remains.

Prior work assumed "high-quality synthetic data is equivalent to real data." Shumailov et al. found: not equivalent, and the gap is cumulative and irreversible.

*** Its solution

More precisely, they named the phenomenon — "model collapse" — and identified two phases:
- *Early collapse*: the tails of the distribution vanish first
- *Late collapse*: the remaining distribution collapses to a narrow low-variance peak

Three mechanisms were identified: statistical approximation error, functional expressivity, and functional approximation error.

*** Why it worked (and where a new pit was buried)

Intuitively correct — any sampling process loses low-probability tail events; training on those samples means the new model doesn't even know those events existed.

The buried pit: purely empirical observation, no quantitative theory. "When will collapse happen?" "Can you predict after how many generations it breaks?" "How much does bias contribute vs. variance?" — none of these questions could be answered. That left a clear opening for the next paper.

** 2024 | Dohmatob et al.: Model Collapse Done in Math

*** What problem it saw

Shumailov gave a name and intuition, but no predictive or quantitative power. A researcher asking "how much capability do I lose per distillation round?" got no answer from Shumailov.

*** Its solution

In the clean toy setting of high-dimensional regression, derive a *closed-form solution*:
- Test error grows *linearly* with recursive generation count
- Error decomposed into bias and variance components
- In kernel regression, a clean phase transition between "model still holds" and "model collapses" is identified

The paper also carefully distinguishes "model collapse" from "self-distillation is actually beneficial" (the Mobahi line) — the former uses the student's output to train the student; the latter distills using the teacher's soft distribution. Mathematically distinct.

*** Why it worked (and where a new pit was buried)

Regression is the simplest statistical model: it admits closed-form solutions and lets you see directly *why the tail dies first* — because the tail corresponds to directions with small eigenvalues, which are the first to be swamped by noise. This intuition transfers to neural networks.

The buried pit: theory built on regression, not validated on real LLMs. What does "code-edition collapse" under distillation look like? Do keywords die first or do variable names? That would have to wait for Siniaev.

** 2024 | Ouyang et al.: GPTQ's "Lossless" Is an Illusion — Because the Model Was Undertrained

*** What problem it saw

GPTQ and AWQ papers both claim "low-bit quantization barely hurts accuracy." But Ouyang noticed a peculiar split: results look fine on small, undertrained models, yet once the model trains to full convergence, 2–3 bit quantization starts to collapse.

In other words, "lossless" holds precisely because the model hasn't yet learned what it's supposed to learn — and dropping what was never learned doesn't hurt.

*** Its solution

Run more than 1,500 Pythia checkpoints, quantize each with GPTQ/AWQ/bitsandbytes, and plot "quantization damage vs. training token count."

A clean scaling law emerges: quantization damage grows exponentially with training token count. Extrapolating: a model trained on 100T tokens would be completely destroyed by low-bit PTQ.

*** Why it worked (and where a new pit was buried)

The insight is deep: the longer training runs, the more the model absorbs fine-grained, rare, long-tail knowledge; this knowledge is encoded in the low-frequency, small-magnitude directions of the weights; and quantization destroys those directions first.

The buried pit: proved "compression disproportionately harms rare directions," but what exactly is "rare"? For natural language, code, and math, is the damage landing on the same things? Nobody looked. That is exactly what Siniaev set out to do.

** 2024 | Pagnoni et al. BLT: Just Throw Away the Tokenizer

*** What problem it saw

Returning to the pit Bostrom & Durrett left — given all the tokenizer's problems, could we solve them at the root? BPE bakes in domain bias, is brittle to noise, has no character-shape awareness, is unfair across languages, and violates incrementality (how a prefix is segmented depends on subsequent bytes).

Deeper still: a fixed vocabulary allocates compute uniformly — ~the~ and some rare identifier get the same FLOPs.

*** Its solution

Read raw bytes; use a small byte-level LM to predict "is the next byte's entropy high?" and cut patches at high-entropy points. A large Transformer operates on patches; small encoder/decoder modules handle the byte ↔ patch conversion.

Scaling experiments reach 8B parameters / 4T bytes, demonstrating that byte-level models match BPE models at equal FLOPs while being more robust to long tails and noise.

*** Why it worked (and where a new pit was buried)

The fixed preprocessing step of "tokenization" becomes "learned dynamic chunking." Hard-to-predict places get more compute; easy places get less — a more rational allocation.

The buried pit: this is a "bypass" solution. BLT is a new model requiring training from scratch. But in the real world, hundreds of billions of tokens have already been tokenized with BPE, trillion-parameter models are already running, and 73% of production deployments use compressed models — what do you do about the models that already exist? BLT can't help.

That is the question Siniaev's paper sets out to answer.

** 2026 | Siniaev et al. (This Paper): Watching What Compression Actually Does at the Code Token Level

*** What problem it saw

Assembling everyone's perspectives:
- Tokenizers critically affect code (Bostrom & Durrett)
- Quantization disproportionately harms rare directions (Ouyang)
- Distillation causes soft-distribution collapse (Dohmatob, Shumailov)
- But *what these harms look like specifically at the token level* — nobody had ever measured it

Code is an especially sensitive domain: programming keywords ~for~ ~while~ ~return~ are rare tokens (compared with ~the~ ~and~ ~of~) yet indispensable for writing code; indentation, semicolons, and brackets are special-symbol tokens on which structural correctness entirely depends. If compression disproportionately harms rare tokens, code generation should be the worst-hit area.

And benchmarks like HumanEval and MBPP only measure pass rate — they cannot detect "did the model render ~for~ as ~fo~ + ~r~?" A new measurement tool was needed.

*** Its solution

*Cold-start probability analysis* — no prompt; inspect the model's probability distribution over the very first token it would generate. This isolates "what token distributions look like in the model's head," free from any prompt influence.

Four metrics are defined:
- PKP: Programming Keyword Probability
- STP: Special Token Probability
- KAP: Keyword Average Probability
- NLP: Natural Language Probability

Systematically run quantization (Q2_K through Q8_0) and size/variant comparisons across the Qwen2.5 family.

*** Why it worked (and where a new pit was buried)

Cold-start bypasses prompt interference and directly reads the token distribution; the four metrics precisely capture "which class of tokens had its probability suppressed or amplified."

Two counter-intuitive findings:
1. *Moderate quantization actually improves the balance between keywords and special-symbol tokens* — possibly because special-symbol tokens were over-represented in the original model, and quantization's "blurring" acts as a regularizer.
2. *Distillation causes keyword probability to drop by 97%* — this is the concrete form of Shumailov-Dohmatob model collapse in the code domain. The student model's tail is shaved almost flat, and code keywords live precisely in that tail.

An additional intriguing observation: the model's preference for "4-space indent vs. tab" is 60:1, perfectly consistent with PEP 8 — proving that token probability distributions encode real coding-convention knowledge.

The buried pit: the phenomena are identified, but no engineering fix is provided. If you are using a distilled model to write code, should you fall back to the base model, or is there some fine-tuning that could rescue it? That is what Fang, Dong, and others in 2025–26 are chasing.

* Frontier Extensions

Siniaev's paper was just published (v3 on 2026-02-08), so direct follow-up citations are still sparse. But empirical work in the same vein has already begun to sprout.

** 2025-26 | Fang et al.: Quantized Models Are Actually More Robust to Noise

Across LLaMA / DeepSeek / CodeGen / StarCoder (350M–33B), systematically measuring how quantized models hold up under noisy weights and adversarial prompts. Quantized models achieve 51.59% accuracy under adversarial conditions; full-precision models reach only 42.86%.

This result validates Siniaev's finding that "moderate suppression of special tokens is actually beneficial" — quantization is, in some sense, a regularizer.

** 2025-26 | Dong et al.: It Is Not the Bit Count — It Is Activation Quantization

"8-bit quantization destroys HumanEval pass rate by 92%, while 4-bit only drops it by 22%" — apparently contradictory, but closer inspection reveals that their 8-bit uses W8A8-INT (weights and activations both quantized to int8), while their 4-bit uses W8A16-INT (weights only).

This advances Siniaev's "what got broken?" diagnosis all the way to "which compression knob broke it?" — activation quantization is the true code killer.

* Insight

Reading this entire river, what comes into view is not "a handful of compression papers" but a *generational refinement of measurement granularity*.

In 2015 people asked whether a model "works or not" — binary.

In 2022 GPTQ asked whether perplexity dropped — a single global average.

In 2023–24 Shumailov and Dohmatob asked whether the shape of the distribution had changed — beginning to care about higher moments, about the tail.

In 2024 Ouyang asked which directional capabilities were compressed away — decomposing down to directions in weight space.

In 2026 Siniaev asks which specific token probabilities were altered — decomposing all the way to individual entries in the vocabulary.

This is a progressive deepening of understanding, *from average to distribution, from distribution to each individual item*. Every generation of research digs the measurement granularity one layer deeper. Every layer deeper, it discovers that the previous generation's "lossless" was an illusion.

This also explains why this river will keep flowing: as long as there is a finer granularity left to dig, someone will find a new form of loss. The next step is likely to be *watching how token probabilities evolve during the generation process* (not just the cold-start moment — how does keyword probability change dynamically across the entire generation trajectory), or *token-loss maps across programming languages* (Python keywords, Rust keywords, Go keywords — who loses the most and why?).

A deeper-layer insight: *compression is never uniform*. It has its own preferences, systematically consuming knowledge that lives "at the margin, in the tail, among the rare." And real-world critical capabilities — code keywords, technical terminology, rare but important facts — live exactly at that margin. This means every engineering mindset that equates "compression" with "uniform degradation" needs to be revised: compression is *structured damage*, not uniform thinning.

* Takeaways

The most instructive aspect of this whole river for how to find problems: *never trust the phrase "lossless."* Any method that calls itself "lossless" is lossless only with respect to some particular measurement. Switch to a finer measurement, and "lossless" immediately becomes "severely damaged."

Concretely actionable:

- If you are doing model compression, *don't rely solely on HumanEval pass rate*. Run a cold-start probability analysis specifically to see how much your code keyword probability has dropped. If it drops more than 30%, start worrying.
- If you are using a distilled model to generate code, *know that it has blind spots in the tail*. Pack extra code examples into the system prompt to force those eroded keywords back into context.
- If you are doing research, *there is always a finer measurement granularity than the one you are currently using*. Dig one layer deeper and you will very likely discover a phenomenon the previous generation missed — that is how every paper in this river was born.
- If you are selecting a production model, *activation quantization (W8A8-INT) is a minefield for code generation*. Prefer weight-only quantization (W4A16 / W8A16); the cost is lower.

Perhaps the largest takeaway: *a field's genuine progress often lies not in inventing new methods but in inventing new ways to measure*. Siniaev did not invent a new compression algorithm; he invented the "cold-start probability analysis" observation tool, and through it saw things others could not see. The next breakthrough in this field is likely hiding in exactly the same place — the dimension nobody has yet measured.
