* The River of the Problem

One sentence: *A large model has hundreds of layers — how many bits should each one get? And who decides?*

This sounds like an accounting question, but it has been the most nagging thorn in eight years of quantization research. You have a total budget — say, "4.5 bits per weight on average" — but how do you divide it? Some layers are fragile: shave off one bit and they collapse. Others are robust: cut them to 3 bits and nothing breaks. Apply the same bit-width to every layer and you inevitably "under-spend where it matters and over-spend where it doesn't."

The real difficulty is that layers are not independent. If you reduce precision in layer 10, the error propagates and amplifies through the network; by layer 50, no one can predict the damage just by staring at layer 10 in isolation. The question "how sensitive is this layer?" is therefore fundamentally unanswerable in isolation — yet almost every existing method answers it in exactly that way.

The headwaters of this river is "let the machine search all assignments"; the middle stretch is "use mathematics to measure each layer's sensitivity"; further downstream comes "turn bit-width into a continuous variable and gradient-descend it." dMX stands at the latest link in this chain and asks a question everyone else skipped: *all these clever methods were designed for integer (INT) formats — but today's hardware runs floating-point (MXFP). Who handles bit allocation for floats?*

* Evolutionary Narrative

From start to finish, one question drives this entire line: *in a network with hundreds of layers, how many bits should each layer get?* Watch how the form of the answer shifts — from "let the machine try everything," to "use mathematics to measure sensitivity," to "let gradients learn it," and finally to "floats must be learnable too."

** 2019 | HAQ: Let Reinforcement Learning Try Every Allocation

*** What problem it saw

Before HAQ, choosing bit-widths for different layers in a network was essentially manual. Engineers would look at the model and guess: "this layer is important, give it 8 bits; that one is fine at 4." The trouble is that modern hardware already supports mixed precision from 1 to 8 bits, creating a search space far too large for any human to explore. Worse, the automated methods that did exist optimized proxy metrics like FLOPs or model size — numbers that simply do not track real on-chip latency or energy.

*** Its solution

HAQ's approach is straightforward: if the space is too large for humans, bring in reinforcement learning and let an agent search it automatically. The key innovation is the reward signal — instead of a proxy metric, HAQ plugs in a hardware simulator. Every time the agent proposes a bit-allocation scheme, the simulator returns real latency and energy as feedback. The policy the agent learns is therefore genuinely hardware-aware and can be specialized to different networks and chips.

*** Why it worked — and what new gap it left

It worked because it was the first to replace "expert intuition" with "machine search anchored to actual hardware." But the cost hides in the word "search": the number of possible allocations is exponential in the layer count. For a network with dozens of layers, the search space is astronomical; training an RL agent is painfully slow. And the result is not transferable — change the network or the chip, and the entire search restarts from scratch. That pain of "search is too expensive" directly motivated the next paper.

** 2019 | HAWQ: Stop Searching; Compute Each Layer's Sensitivity from Second-Order Information

*** What problem it saw

HAWQ immediately identifies HAQ's fundamental weakness: "the mixed-precision search space is exponential in the number of layers, making brute-force approaches infeasible for deep networks." RL is smarter than exhaustive search, but it is still trial-and-error inside an exponential space — the expense is not solved. HAWQ's insight is that you should not *search* at all; you should *compute*.

*** Its solution

To transform approach A (search) into approach B (compute), you discard the trial-and-error agent and pick up a mathematical ruler. That ruler is the Hessian — the second derivative of the loss. The intuition is: the larger a layer's Hessian spectrum (curvature), the more sensitive the loss is to perturbations in that layer, so that layer deserves more bits. HAWQ uses each layer's Hessian spectrum to rank sensitivity automatically, assigns higher precision to higher-curvature layers, and uses the second-order information to determine a principled fine-tuning order as well. No exponential search is needed.

*** Why it worked — and what new gap it left

Replacing trial-and-error with curvature computation turns exponential search into a single forward/backward pass, giving orders-of-magnitude speedups. But the gap HAWQ leaves is pinpointed precisely by dMX: the Hessian is a *local* sensitivity measure — it only quantifies how much the loss rises when one layer is perturbed in place. It still does not capture how errors accumulate non-linearly across layers as they propagate through the network. In other words, every layer is still evaluated as an island. Moreover, the entire decision hinges on the chosen sensitivity metric: swap the metric and the conclusions can change. This "two-stage pipeline — estimate sensitivity, then allocate" structure is exactly what the next generation sets out to dissolve.

** 2020 | Bayesian Bits / FracBits: Make the Bit-Width Itself a Gradient-Differentiable Variable

*** What problem it saw

Methods like HAWQ — whether using Hessian or RL — are at heart two-stage pipelines: first estimate each layer's sensitivity, then allocate bits accordingly. This means the quality of the final allocation is entirely hostage to how well the sensitivity estimate captures what actually matters — and the sensitivity is itself only a proxy. This generation asked: can we skip the "estimate sensitivity" step entirely, and instead let the bit-width be part of the training objective itself, optimized jointly with the weights?

*** Its solution

The core transformation is to change "bit-width" from a discrete label decided after training into a continuous parameter written into the computation graph, differentiable with respect to the loss. FracBits lets each layer's bit-width occupy a "fractional" state between two adjacent integers — for example, 4.3 bits, meaning an interpolation between 4-bit and 5-bit formats — and this fraction slides continuously under gradient updates during training. A differentiable regularizer simultaneously pulls the total budget (sum of bit-widths) toward the user-specified constraint. Bayesian Bits takes an equivalent but different path: learnable stochastic gates decide, level by level, whether to double the bit-width; opening or closing each gate corresponds to different precisions. Both methods converge on the same conclusion — bit allocation is end-to-end learnable for the first time.

*** Why it worked — and what new gap it left

It worked because "allocation" and "training" are finally unified: instead of estimating sensitivity then allocating, the final loss directly tells each layer — through gradients — how many bits it needs. Sensitivity is learned implicitly, with no external proxy required. But this generation left two gaps that become dMX's starting point. First, virtually all of these methods are designed for *integer* formats (FracBits, SDQ, BSQ, Q-ViT — without exception). Second, when the continuous bit-width must ultimately be snapped to a legal discrete format, these methods tend to oscillate with abrupt jumps between neighboring formats late in training. dMX specifically calls out SDQ for having "no continuous transition between values" and FracBits for requiring "a two-stage pipeline to actually deploy."

** 2023 | MXFP / Microscaling: Not an Allocation Method — the Target Format dMX Must Serve

*** What problem it saw

The first three generations all wrestled with "how to allocate bits," but the "bits" in their minds were almost universally integers. Meanwhile, something significant happened on the hardware side: the Open Compute Project (OCP), together with a consortium of vendors, standardized the Microscaling format. The problem it solved was different — in ordinary FP8 or INT8, the entire tensor shares a single scale factor, making the dynamic range too rigid; a single outlier can ruin the precision of an entire tensor's representation.

*** Its solution

MXFP pushes the scale downward: every 32 elements form a block, and each block carries its own shared scale; the elements themselves are represented in very narrow floating-point. This yields three formats — MXFP4, MXFP6, MXFP8 — where the number in the name is the per-element bit-width. Block-level shared scaling gives fine-grained dynamic range control while remaining hardware-efficient because one scale is amortized over 32 elements.

*** Why it worked — and what new gap it left

It worked by finding the sweet spot between "one scale per tensor" (too coarse) and "one scale per element" (too expensive). But it left a vast blank that becomes dMX's thesis: the standard provides three floating-point formats (4 / 6 / 8 bit) and says *nothing* about which layer should use which one. And all the accumulated wisdom from the previous three generations — search, Hessian, differentiable learning — was built for integers; none of it can be handed off to handle floating-point allocation.

** 2026 | dMX: Compress the Floating-Point Format Choice into a Single Learnable Offset and Let Gradients Decide

*** What problem it saw

dMX brings together two streams of this river. On one side, differentiable mixed-precision (the FracBits generation) established that "letting gradients learn bit-widths" outperforms "first estimate sensitivity, then allocate." On the other side, MXFP has become the actual target format in hardware. Yet dMX observes that *these two streams have never merged*. In its own words: "no prior work learns floating-point format selection through a gradient-based method." Every differentiable method stops at integers; every floating-point method (e.g., MicroMix) falls back on heuristics for precision assignment.

*** Its solution

If you morph FracBits's "fractional bit-width" into the floating-point world, you arrive at dMX. The method proceeds in three steps. First, compress each layer's MXFP format choice into a *single learnable offset* — not learning a discrete label, but learning a continuous scalar that slides smoothly among MXFP4, MXFP6, and MXFP8. Second, to address the oscillation problem inherited from the previous generation, apply a *temperature annealing* schedule: the offset starts fully continuous and gradually solidifies, smoothly and stably, into a hardware-legal MXFP format over the course of training, avoiding abrupt jumps. Third, add a *budget-aware regularizer* that gently steers the network's average bit-width toward the user-specified target.

*** Why it worked — and what new gap it left

It worked because it turns allocation into a fully continuous, differentiable optimization problem — and does so for floating-point formats for the first time. Compared head-to-head with heuristics such as KL-divergence-based approaches that "isolate each layer, measure sensitivity, and pre-assign formats," dMX achieves Pareto dominance. The advantage is especially pronounced in the mid-range bit-width regime, where KL-based sensitivity approximations become unreliable and the heuristic curve flattens, while dMX continues to improve. These results are validated on Llama, Qwen3, and SmolLM2 using WikiText-2 perplexity and four zero-shot reasoning benchmarks. The remaining gaps — which mark the next step along this line — are: the continuous offset relaxation currently covers only "which MXFP format to select"; block size, rotational preprocessing, and other configuration dimensions have not yet been pulled into the differentiable framework; and the temperature annealing schedule itself remains a hyperparameter that must be tuned.

* Frontier Extensions

As of this writing (June 2026), dMX is the latest link in the "mixed-precision bit allocation" line to connect *differentiable learning* with *floating-point MXFP formats*. It cites several contemporaneous works as baselines — MicroMix (Liu et al. 2025, heuristic float allocation), InfoQ (Akbulut et al. 2026), MixQ-SAM (Ranjan et al. 2025), MixQuant (Sanjeet et al. 2026), and a gradient-free method (Cohen et al. 2025) — but these are either heuristic/search-based or not targeted at MXFP floating-point.

The next steps one can anticipate: extending "differentiable offset" from "format selection" to "block-size selection" and "whether to apply rotational preprocessing," so the entire MXFP configuration becomes end-to-end learnable. Alternatively, someone may challenge the stability of temperature annealing and propose an annealing-free discretization scheme. This line of work is not closed — it has simply turned into the new channel of floating-point formats.

* Insights

Reading this river from source to sea, the surface story is "bit-allocation techniques iterating." But the deeper event is a *continuous transfer of decision authority*: from human experience, to RL agent trial-and-error, to Hessian mathematics, to gradients themselves. Each generation strips away another layer of "human-defined proxy metrics." HAQ still requires humans to design the reward; HAWQ requires humans to select Hessian as the sensitivity metric; by the FracBits/dMX generation, even "sensitivity" as an intermediate concept is gone — the final loss speaks directly through gradients to tell each layer how many bits it needs.

dMX's real insight is not "yet another differentiable method." It is the discovery of a *paradigm–hardware misalignment*: academia had refined differentiable mixed-precision to a mature state, but entirely on integers; industry hardware had already moved to MXFP floating-point, yet was still using heuristics for allocation. The sharpest contributions along this line often come not from inventing a new algorithm, but from noticing that "two mature things have never been connected" — dMX is that connector.

Where the line most likely goes next: the boundary of differentiability will keep expanding. Today we can learn "which floating-point format to pick"; tomorrow someone will learn "how large a block should be," "whether to pre-rotate," "how many bits the scale itself needs" — until the entire quantization configuration becomes a single end-to-end differentiable object. The day proxy metrics reach zero is the day this river meets the sea.

* Takeaways

The biggest lesson this evolutionary line offers for "how to find a good research problem" is: *the best problems often hide in the seam between two mature fields, not at the frontier of either one*. dMX did not invent a new differentiable technique, nor a new floating-point format. It simply noticed that "differentiable mixed-precision is all integers" and "hardware has moved to floats" — two widely known facts that had never been placed side by side.

The practical implication: when looking for a research topic, rather than grinding out incremental gains at the frontier of a single line, scan for *pairs of mature lines that obviously should have intersected but haven't*. Put "the established method from field A" and "the actual need in field B" on the same sheet of paper; the undrawn line between them is often a clean, Pareto-dominating piece of work with a legible causal story.
