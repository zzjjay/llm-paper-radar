* The River of Problems

Take every weight in a billion-parameter large language model and compress it from a floating-point number to one of three choices — {-1, 0, +1} — while keeping the model sharp enough to hold a conversation.

Where is the difficulty? Ternarization slashes weight precision by more than 90%, leaving the model as though most of its neurons had been severed — it suddenly "becomes dumb." The standard remedy is retraining, but retraining a 7B model demands hundreds of billions of tokens, a cost roughly equivalent to training a new model from scratch. The problem stalls here: *Can we ternarize an existing model without retraining it — and without letting it get dumber?*

This thread stretches from CNN ternarization in 2016 all the way to LLM post-training quantization in 2026, crossing the entire LLM era in between.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2016 | TWN: Giving Weights an "Abstain" Vote for the First Time

*** What Problem It Saw

In 2015, BinaryConnect and XNOR-Net demonstrated that neural network weights could be binarized to {-1, +1}, yielding large gains in speed and memory. But the accuracy loss was painful — a convolutional kernel where every weight could only "support" or "oppose" had very weak expressive power.

Li et al. did a simple calculation: a 3×3 convolutional kernel has 2^9 = 512 distinct patterns if binary, but 3^9 = 19,683 patterns if ternary (with an "abstain" zero added) — 38× more expressive power in one step.

The question was: how do you efficiently assign a floating-point weight W to one of three values {-1, 0, +1}?

*** Its Solution

TWN's approach was straightforward: partition weights by absolute magnitude into three segments — those with absolute value above a threshold Δ take ±1 according to their sign; those below Δ take 0. The threshold Δ is set to 0.7 times the layer-wise mean absolute value of the weights. To reduce quantization error, each layer uses a learnable scaling factor α to "stretch" the ternary weights.

Formally: W_T = α * t, where t ∈ {-1, 0, +1}, and α is found by minimizing ||W - α * t||².

During training, a full-precision copy of the weights is retained; ternary weights are used only in the forward and backward passes, while parameter updates are performed in full precision.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Pit It Dug

Zero weights require no multiply-accumulate operations (they are simply skipped), so the hardware cost equals that of binary while expressive power multiplies tenfold. 

The remaining problem: weight distributions vary greatly across layers, yet TWN assigns only a single global α per layer. Positive and negative weights are forced to share the same scaling factor, systematically introducing error in layers with asymmetric weight distributions.

** 2017 | TTQ: Letting Positive and Negative Weights Speak Separately

*** What Problem It Saw

Zhu et al. observed that in many neural network layers, the weight distribution is asymmetric — positive weights cluster around one value, negative weights around another. TWN's forced use of a single α for both sides is like weighing two piles of material with different densities on the same scale: the error is structural.

*** Its Solution

TTQ (Trained Ternary Quantization) introduces two independent learnable scaling factors per layer: W_p (scaling for positive weights) and W_n (scaling for negative weights), plus a learnable threshold. All three parameters are jointly optimized with SGD during training.

This lets positive and negative weights be "tuned" independently, no longer squeezed onto the same scale.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Pit It Dug

Experiments showed that asymmetric scaling genuinely recovers substantial accuracy, approaching full-precision performance on ImageNet.

However, both TWN and TTQ only experimented in the CNN and ResNet era, at scales of at most a few dozen layers. More critically, both methods require training from scratch or nearly from scratch — unacceptable in the LLM era with billions of parameters and hundreds of billions of training tokens.

**(The 2017–2023 Jump)**

Over the following years, LLM research focused on GPT, BERT, and the Llama family — parameter counts growing from hundreds of millions to hundreds of billions. Works like GPTQ and AWQ demonstrated that post-training quantization (PTQ) with a small calibration set could compress FP16 models to INT8 or INT4 with minimal accuracy loss. PTQ as a framework became accepted. But no one pushed PTQ to the extreme of ternary precision — the prevailing belief was that ternary was too aggressive and required QAT.

** 2023 | BitNet: Eliminating Multiplication from the Transformer

*** What Problem It Saw

The bottleneck in LLM inference is not arithmetic throughput but memory bandwidth. Generating each token requires reading all weights from GPU memory to perform matrix multiplication. More weights means more bandwidth pressure and slower inference.

Wang et al. asked a radical question: if weights are only {-1, +1}, then matrix "multiplication" becomes addition and subtraction. Multiplications can be completely eliminated. For specialized hardware and low-power devices, this is a qualitative leap.

*** Its Solution

BitNet replaces standard linear layers with BitLinear layers — weights are constrained to {-1, +1} and participate in forward and backward passes directly in 1-bit form during training (using STE to handle non-differentiability). It is trained from scratch, not quantized from an existing model.

Experiments show that BitNet exhibits a scaling law similar to full-precision Transformers: larger models and more data yield better performance.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Pit It Dug

It proved that a "multiplication-free Transformer" was a viable path.

The remaining problem: pure binary {-1, +1} still has limited expressive power, and perplexity remains significantly higher than same-scale full-precision models. *Zero* matters — it allows a weight to "sit out" a computation, which is a natural expression of attention mechanisms and sparsity.

** 2024 | BitNet b1.58: Add the Zero Back, and Everything Changes

*** What Problem It Saw

Ma et al.'s intuition: in modern LLMs, weight distributions are heavily concentrated near the mean. Forcing near-zero weights into ±1 introduces large noise. Keeping these weights as 0 — silent, not noisy — is better for the model.

*** Its Solution

Building on BitNet, zero is restored to the weight set: {-1, +1} becomes {-1, 0, +1}. Each layer is normalized by its absolute mean to determine the scaling factor α, then weights are quantized via round(W / α) clipped to {-1, 0, +1}. Zero-valued weights are skipped in multiply-accumulate operations, adding no computation.

The result: at equivalent scale, BitNet b1.58's perplexity matches same-scale full-precision Llama — the first evidence that a ternary LLM can fully align with a full-precision LLM in performance.

Concurrently, Kaushal et al.'s *Spectra/TriLM* (2407.12327) independently conducted more systematic scaling experiments, pushing ternary LLMs to 3.9B parameters and 300B training tokens, validating the scalability of ternary quantization.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Pit It Dug

"Adding zero back" changed the statistical properties of the quantizer, allowing the central mass of the weight distribution to be faithfully represented rather than forcibly displaced.

But this buried the largest engineering problem along this thread: BitNet b1.58 and TriLM must *be trained from scratch*, requiring 100B–300B tokens. You cannot take an already-trained Llama-3 or Qwen and ternarize it; you must rerun pretraining. This cost is roughly equivalent to training a full-precision model.

The more fundamental question: why does directly applying ternary PTQ to a pretrained FP model work so poorly?

** 2025 | Tequila: Identifying the Root Cause of "Getting Stuck"

*** What Problem It Saw

Huang et al. shifted attention to QAT (quantization-aware training) rather than from-scratch training: can you take an already-trained full-precision model, fine-tune it with a moderate amount of data, and make it ternary?

They identified a concrete failure mechanism called *deadzone trapping*.

Ternary quantization has a "dead zone": when a weight's absolute value falls below the threshold Δ, it is mapped to 0, does not participate in computation, and gradients can only be carried crudely through STE (straight-through estimator). The problem is that STE provides extremely noisy gradients inside the dead zone — with no clear direction — so weights have no idea which way to move to "escape." They get trapped near the boundary, oscillating back and forth, unable to converge.

This is like placing a ball on the rim of a flat-bottomed pan — gentle perturbations send it rolling back and forth, but it can never settle into the center or roll out cleanly.

*** Its Solution

Tequila's approach: since weights trapped in the dead zone cannot get useful gradients, let them change roles — instead of being "silent zeros," they become *dynamic bias terms* that continue to participate in computation.

The "rescued" weights provide a continuous signal in the forward pass and receive meaningful gradients in the backward pass without relying on noisy STE. At inference time, these bias terms can be precomputed and packed into a lookup table, adding virtually zero inference overhead.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Pit It Dug

Tequila achieves 4% higher than the previous SOTA on the ARC benchmark, nearly matching full precision, while delivering 3× inference speedup.

But the fundamental cost problem remained: Tequila *still requires 10B tokens of QAT retraining*, and was only validated on Llama3 1B and 3B. For a 70B model, 10B tokens of QAT is still a substantial compute bill.

Moreover, even though Tequila identifies and fixes deadzone trapping, it is fundamentally a *QAT* method — it improves gradient flow but does not address the root question of why ternary PTQ cannot be done with a small calibration set.

That root question has two components:
1. *Distributional misalignment*: forcibly mapping FP weights into {-1,0,+1} causes a sudden shift in statistical properties — mean, variance, and threshold selection that are reasonable in FP space may be entirely unreasonable in ternary space.
2. *Non-differentiability*: the hard ternarization function is a discrete jump; gradient descent over a calibration set is simply not applicable.

** 2026 | CAT-Q: 512 Samples, Saving 100,000× in Training Cost

*** What Problem It Saw

Wang et al. looked back across the entire thread and noticed: Tequila compressed "from-scratch training" to "QAT on 10B tokens," but in essence it was still "using large amounts of data to re-teach the model to be ternary."

Is it possible to bypass this "re-teaching" — finding the highest-quality ternary representation *within the neighborhood of the existing weights*, using only a tiny calibration set?

The reasons ternary PTQ failed were clear: (1) distributional misalignment and (2) non-differentiability. The former means the initial ternary weights start with large errors; the latter means no gradient-based optimization can take hold.

*** Its Solution

CAT-Q addresses these two problems with two separate modules:

*Module One: Learnable Modulation (LM)*

Idea: rather than directly ternarizing W, first apply a "learnable coordinate transform" that adjusts W's distribution into the most quantization-friendly position before ternarization.

LM introduces three extremely small learnable parameters:
- δ_μ: adjusts the center of the weight distribution (slight shift)
- δ_α: adjusts the spread of the weight distribution (slight stretch)
- δ_Δ: adjusts the width of the ternarization threshold

These three parameters are initialized to "do nothing" (identity transform) and make only small adjustments during calibration — analogous to slightly tweaking the coordinate system around the full-precision weights to minimize ternarization error.

Key design choice: the mean shift μ is used only for distribution re-alignment and is *not* included in the reconstruction formula (W ≈ α * T, not α * T + μ), so hardware friendliness is preserved (pure addition and subtraction, no multiplication).

*Module Two: Softened Ternarization (ST)*

Idea: replace the non-differentiable "hard jump" with a differentiable "soft transition," then revert to the hard jump at the end of training.

ST replaces the hard ternarization function with a tanh-based smooth function:

  f(W; s, Delta) = [tanh(s*(W-Delta)) + tanh(s*(W+Delta))] / (2 * tanh(s))

When s → 0, this function approaches the identity map; when s → ∞, it converges to hard ternarization.

Training proceeds in two stages:
- Stage One (first 80% of epochs): use the soft function with s increasing gradually, allowing gradients to flow smoothly and enabling LM's three parameters to converge effectively.
- Stage Two (final 20% of epochs): switch back to hard ternarization, starting from the Stage One convergence point to finalize the weights.

*** Why It Works

The LM + ST combination eliminates the two obstacles of ternary PTQ from two orthogonal directions: LM addresses "where" (distributional position), ST addresses "how to get there" (a differentiable path).

Results: on Llama and Qwen family models (from 1B to 32B parameters), CAT-Q matches or surpasses BitNet b1.58 v1 and v2 using only 512 calibration samples and 60 epochs (a matter of hours) — while the BitNet family requires 100B tokens of from-scratch training. This translates to roughly a 100,000× reduction in training cost.

* Frontier Extensions

CAT-Q was submitted on June 25, 2026 and is extremely new work; no citations exist yet.

However, several parallel threads are active in the same space at the same time:

*Parallel Thread One: Hardware Acceleration*
TOM (Ternary Read-only Memory Accelerator, 2602.20662) explores dedicated hardware for ternary LLMs, storing {-1,0,+1} weights in read-only memory and completely eliminating multiplications at inference time. CAT-Q solves "where ternary models come from"; TOM solves "how to run them fast."

*Parallel Thread Two: Concurrent Ternary PTQ Work*
PT²-LLM (OpenReview 2026) also follows a PTQ route, using an asymmetric ternary quantizer plus a two-stage refinement process. Its approach is similar in spirit to CAT-Q but differs in technical details; the two appeared almost simultaneously.

*Parallel Thread Three: The Spectra Family Keeps Pushing*
Spectra 1.1 (2506.23025, June 2025) pushes ternary LLM from-scratch pretraining to 1.2T tokens, catching up to Llama-1 7B performance and releasing the TriRun GPU kernel for end-to-end 5× speedup. This "strongest ternary model from scratch" direction and CAT-Q's "low-cost ternarization of existing models" direction are complementary goals.

As of now, CAT-Q is the most recent and impactful work in the "ternary PTQ" thread.

* Insight

Reading through this thread, one pattern recurs: *each generation solved the gradient/optimization problem but left the data cost for the next generation.*

TWN→TTQ solved the asymmetry of the scaling factor but still required full training. The BitNet family solved "can a Transformer be ternary" but bound that answer to from-scratch training. Tequila solved the gradient noise in the STE dead zone but 10B tokens was still too heavy.

CAT-Q is the first work on this thread to genuinely *bring data cost down to the PTQ scale*. Its core insight is that distributional misalignment and non-differentiability are two independent obstacles that can be broken separately — one adjusts "where," the other solves "how to get there."

This means ternary quantization is, for the first time, as *lightweight* as AWQ/GPTQ — you do not need a training cluster, just one GPU and a few hundred calibration samples to ternarize any open-source LLM.

*Where will the field most likely go next?*

1. *Scale Validation*: CAT-Q has been tested up to 32B, but 70B and 100B remain untested. Is ternarization still stable at those scales?

2. *Ternary × Sparsity*: Sherry (1.25-bit, 2601.07892) is already exploring 3:4 sparse ternary quantization, pushing average bit rate below 1.25-bit. Can the PTQ framework transfer?

3. *Activation Quantization in Tandem*: BitNet v2 is already addressing low-bit activation quantization (using Hadamard transforms to suppress outliers). If weight-ternary + activation-4-bit can be achieved via PTQ, the result would be the most thorough end-to-end quantization scheme.

4. *Dedicated Hardware Deployment*: Work like TOM is waiting for a "standard ternary model." CAT-Q provides a low-cost path to ternarize existing models; if these two connect, deploying ternary LLMs on edge devices becomes practically feasible.

* Inspiration

This thread offers one concrete methodological lesson: *first name the failure mode's mechanism, then design a targeted solution.*

Tequila advanced precisely because Huang et al. did not stop at "QAT doesn't work well" — they named the failure "deadzone trapping" and drew a mechanistic diagram of why weights get stuck. CAT-Q's advance follows the same logic: "distributional misalignment" and "non-differentiability" are two separately named problems corresponding to two separately designed solution modules.

Naming the failure mode = finding a target you can actually aim at.

What this means for practice: whenever you see a method "not working well," start by asking where exactly it breaks down — is it a problem of gradient flow, statistical distribution, or the optimization landscape? Translate "not working" into a mechanistic description, and the solution often follows.
