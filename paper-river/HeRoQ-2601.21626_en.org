* What This River Is About

The most direct way to run a hundred-billion-parameter model on a single GPU is to compress its weights from 16-bit down to 4-bit or even 3-bit. The catch: after compression, models often get dumber — and the more aggressive the compression, the dumber they get.

For the past five years, people have been wrestling with this "compress-and-degrade" problem. Early on, the working assumption was that if each quantized weight stayed close to its original value, the model would survive more or less intact — "small error = small loss." But empirical runs kept surfacing a peculiar split: the measured quantization error looked tiny, yet the model's inference quality completely collapsed. Low error, high loss.

This river is chasing exactly that one thing: why doesn't "quantizing accurately" equal "keeping the model good"? And how do you make it so that it actually does?

The target paper, HeRo-Q, is the river's current leading edge. Its answer: stop fixating on error. Look instead at *the shape of the loss landscape*.

* Digging Backward: Tracing HeRo-Q All the Way to OBS

** Who HeRo-Q Is Arguing Against

HeRo-Q explicitly targets three methods: GPTQ, AWQ, and SpinQuant. It argues that all three minimize "quantization error" — the distance between W and Q(W). But it points to an uncomfortable fact: a quantization scheme with very small error can still cause the model's loss to explode. The culprit, it says, is the *Hessian matrix*: a few directions in the loss landscape are extraordinarily steep, and once a quantization perturbation lands on those directions, even a tiny error gets amplified into a catastrophic loss increase.

So the most immediate predecessor is SpinQuant — which also applies a learned rotation matrix to the weights. The difference is the objective: SpinQuant learns to eliminate outliers so quantization becomes more accurate; HeRo-Q learns to flatten the loss landscape. Same tool, different goal.

** Who SpinQuant Is Arguing Against

SpinQuant's predecessor is QuaRot. QuaRot had already established a beautiful fact: you can insert a rotation matrix R into the weights and activations simultaneously — W · X = (W · R^T) · (R · X) — leaving the forward pass unchanged while making the distribution of R · X far smoother than X. Outliers get "spread out," and quantization becomes tractable. QuaRot uses a *random* Hadamard rotation.

SpinQuant identified QuaRot's pain point: random rotations vary enormously in quality — the best random rotation can outperform the worst by 13 percentage points. In other words, QuaRot handed its fate to the random-number generator. SpinQuant's response was direct: if the choice of rotation matters that much, stop sampling randomly and learn one.

** Who QuaRot Is Arguing Against

Before QuaRot, the mainstream approach was AWQ and SmoothQuant — "non-rotation" methods. Both shared the assumption that activations contain a few "important" outlier channels that need special treatment: either keep them at high precision (mixed precision), or migrate scaling factors into the weights (SmoothQuant), or selectively protect the top 1% of weights (AWQ).

QuaRot's critique was sharp: all of these methods treat certain channels differently, which complicates hardware implementations. What if a transformation could homogenize the entire distribution so that *every* channel is easy to quantize? Rotation is the answer. After rotation there are no special channels; everything goes through at 4-bit, and the hardware pipeline runs without branches.

** Who AWQ Is Arguing Against

AWQ's predecessor is GPTQ. GPTQ does layer-wise weight reconstruction: using second-order Hessian information, it adjusts weights one by one to minimize the objective Q(W)·X ≈ W·X.

AWQ identified two problems. First, GPTQ depends on reconstruction and can overfit the calibration dataset — performance drops on out-of-domain tasks like code or math. Second, more fundamentally: GPTQ treats every weight equally, but AWQ's experiments showed that protecting just 1% of "important" weights dramatically reduces quantization error. How do you find that 1%? Not by looking at weight magnitudes, but at activation magnitudes — the weights corresponding to channels with large activations are the ones that matter.

** Who GPTQ Is Arguing Against

GPTQ's ancestors are the 1990s-era OBS (Optimal Brain Surgeon) and OBD (Optimal Brain Damage). Those methods addressed the question of which weights to delete with minimal impact on loss, using second-order Taylor expansions of the loss — with the Hessian as the central tool.

GPTQ's problem with OBS was computational: it simply cannot scale to 100B-parameter models — the Hessian matrix is far too large. GPTQ's contribution was engineering: decompose the problem layer by layer and column by column, use an approximate Hessian to guide each rounding step, and apply Cholesky decomposition to avoid numerical collapse. The result: a 175B model fully quantized in 4 GPU-hours.

This is the bottom of the dig. OBS/OBD are the foundational works; going further upstream leads into classical second-order optimization theory, which belongs to a different river.

* Forward Narrative: How Each Problem Forced the Next Solution

** Stop 1: The Seed OBS Planted (1990s)

LeCun and colleagues wanted to compress trained neural networks. The question: "Given a trained network, which weights can I delete without degrading loss?" They expanded the loss as a quadratic function near the current weights using a Taylor series, which naturally brought in the Hessian matrix — it tells you which directions are steep and which are flat. Deleting weights along flat directions is painless; deleting along steep ones is catastrophic.

This intuition was planted: *the curvature of the loss landscape determines how much perturbation you can tolerate.* At the time, networks had only millions of parameters, and no one anticipated that this intuition would become a lifeline in the trillion-parameter era.

** Stop 2: GPTQ Transplants the Old Tool to Large Models (2022)

Twenty years later, GPT arrived and models grew to 175B parameters. The task shifted from pruning to compression: 16-bit floats → 4-bit integers. The task changed, but the underlying question was the same: how do you perturb weights without destroying the model?

GPTQ picked up OBS's second-order tools but had to solve two new problems: (1) the Hessian is too large to compute directly; (2) quantization is fundamentally different from pruning — the former is a discrete rounding, not a zeroing-out. GPTQ's answer: work layer by layer, column by column, and use an approximate Hessian to guide each rounding decision.

This was the first time "minimizing quantization error" was made practically usable at scale. But it buried a landmine: *it only watched the error in weight space, never what was happening in loss space.*

** Stop 3: AWQ Discovers That Not All Weights Are Equal (2023)

GPTQ held up reasonably at 4-bit but started collapsing at 3-bit. The AWQ team ran a series of experiments and found something counterintuitive: if, after GPTQ quantization, you restore just 1% of the "important" weights to 16-bit, the model nearly fully recovers. This meant that most quantization error is harmless — a small fraction of weights absorbs almost all the damage.

How do you identify that 1%? Weight magnitude doesn't work, because magnitude doesn't directly correspond to influence in the forward pass. AWQ's key insight: *use activation magnitudes to locate important weights.* If a given input channel's activation is consistently large, then the weight column for that channel is important — its perturbation gets amplified by the large activation.

Rather than mixed precision (which is hardware-unfriendly), AWQ applies a per-channel scaling factor s: W' = W · s, X' = X / s, leaving the product unchanged, but W' is spread out so its relative quantization loss shrinks.

At this point the problem sharpened: *quantization difficulty is not uniformly distributed; the key is how to handle the "hard" channels.*

** Stop 4: QuaRot Tries to Eliminate "Hard Channels" Once and For All (2024)

AWQ and SmoothQuant both involved special-casing the hard channels. The QuaRot team asked a different question: can we make all channels equally hard — or equally easy?

There is a mathematically elegant fact: for an orthogonal matrix R, W · X = (W · R^T) · (R · X). You can insert a rotation in the middle without changing the forward pass, yet R · X can have a dramatically different distribution — especially with Hadamard-style rotations, which spread outlier energy uniformly across all channels.

Once spread, there are no hard channels. Everything looks like a mild, roughly Gaussian distribution; 4-bit quantization works in one pass. Whole model, all 4-bit, hardware pipeline runs without branches.

But QuaRot used a *fixed random Hadamard*. It was fortunate: they happened to sample a rotation that worked well.

** Stop 5: SpinQuant Discovers That Rotations Can Be Learned (2024)

The SpinQuant team ran a simple experiment: repeat QuaRot 100 times, each time with a different random rotation. The downstream accuracy varied by up to 13 percentage points.

What does this mean? There are "good rotations" and "bad rotations" in rotation space, and QuaRot just happened to sample a decent one. What if you could search for the best one?

SpinQuant parameterizes the rotation matrix and runs gradient descent on a small calibration set — with the objective of making the quantized model's output match the original as closely as possible. After learning, performance substantially exceeds QuaRot.

Notice, however, that SpinQuant's learning objective is still *output alignment*, i.e., minimizing quantization error. It is still following the old signpost left by GPTQ.

** Stop 6: HeRo-Q Changes the Learning Objective (2026)

During their experiments, the HeRo-Q team encountered the peculiar split: sometimes the rotation learned by SpinQuant reduced quantization error yet the model performed worse on downstream tasks. That awkward "low error, high loss."

They looked at the loss landscape and saw: *small quantization error does not imply small loss.* The loss around a weight is approximately quadratic: L(W + δW) ≈ L(W) + δW^T · H · δW, where δW is the quantization perturbation. If H has a very large maximum eigenvalue, even a small δW can produce a huge δW^T · H · δW.

So the problem is not "make δW small" — it is "make H's steepest directions less steep."

HeRo-Q's approach: still learn a rotation matrix, but the learning objective is not output alignment — it is *minimizing the maximum eigenvalue of the Hessian*. After rotation, the equivalent loss landscape has been flattened: the steep directions have been rotated away, and quantization perturbations landing anywhere no longer cause disasters.

This is a return to the intuition that ran all the way from OBS: *curvature determines tolerance.* After thirty years of detours — from pruning to quantization, from error minimization to landscape flattening — the problem has been peeled down to its root.

* Distilled Insights

** On the Surface It's "Increasingly Accurate Quantization"; Underneath It's "Increasingly Correct Objective Functions"

Viewed from the outside, this river looks like tool iteration: error minimization → channel weighting → random rotation → learned rotation. But look closer and *the real change is the optimization objective itself.*

- GPTQ: minimize |W − Q(W)| (error in weight space)
- AWQ: minimize |W·X − Q(W)·X| (error in output space)
- QuaRot / SpinQuant: minimize output error after rotation
- HeRo-Q: minimize the maximum eigenvalue of H (loss landscape curvature)

Each step moves one increment closer to "what we actually care about." The final step is the most decisive: it abandons "error" as a proxy target entirely and optimizes the loss landscape directly.

** "Tool" and "Objective" Are Two Different Things

SpinQuant and HeRo-Q use nearly identical tools — both prepend a learned rotation matrix to the weights. But their learning objectives are completely different. This tells us something about the field: *tools are converging (everyone uses rotation)* while *objectives are diverging (loss landscape vs. error)*. The differentiator over the next decade will likely be precisely "what are we actually minimizing."

** Where Does the River Flow Next

Three directions stand out:

1. *Jointly optimizing higher-order spectral information*: HeRo-Q only suppresses the maximum eigenvalue. What about the full spectrum? The trace? The condition number? HAWQ-V2 has already taken a few steps along this line.
2. *Baking curvature awareness into pretraining*: HeRo-Q is a PTQ method. If the loss landscape were shaped to be "quantization-friendly" during pretraining, the effect would be more fundamental.
3. *Layer-adaptive strategies*: Each layer has a different Hessian spectrum; a uniform policy may waste capacity. CALM (2026) is already doing per-layer modularization, and this thread is worth following.
