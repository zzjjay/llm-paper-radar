* The River of Problems

Ask an LLM to read a 500,000-word document and it must pack all 500,000 tokens' Keys and Values into GPU memory — because generating the next word theoretically requires looking back at any one of them. Every time the sequence doubles in length, the KV cache doubles in size, soon outweighing the model weights themselves. The entire research line is wrestling with one simple intuition: *most of those 500,000 historical records are never used — can we store and compute fewer of them?*

The difficulty is not in "cutting" but in "cutting precisely." How do you know which token is useless now and will remain useless later? For seven or eight years, successive generations of methods have been answering this question, and they all share an increasingly suspect hidden assumption: *importance can be determined in advance by some unified rule* — either by position (keep the beginning, keep the recent window), or by a global score (keep what gets attended to repeatedly), or by handing every attention head the same budget.

The sharp contribution of this paper (EntropyInfer) is to put that hidden assumption squarely on the table and knock it down with experiments. It finds that attention heads are simply not one kind of thing. Some heads (*Rigid Heads*) have extremely concentrated attention, with entropy near zero, fixating on a fixed set of positions regardless of the input; other heads (*Dynamic Heads*) have attention that fluctuates wildly with context, carrying the true semantic structure. More critically, *the same head can be rigid on one input and dynamic on another* — meaning "which heads matter" cannot be determined offline in advance; it must be judged *online, per input*.

The question therefore shifts from "which tokens to keep" to: *why use a single ruler to measure all heads and all contexts?*

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2023 | H2O: Attention Is Sparse, So Keep Only the "Heavy Hitters"

*** What Problem It Saw

During long-text generation, the KV cache grows linearly with sequence length and quickly exhausts GPU memory. H2O's starting point was an empirical observation: in many pretrained LLMs, *more than 95% of attention is sparse* — meaning only ~5% of historical tokens actually contribute at any given step. Could we keep just that 5% and cut the cache to 1/20th?

*** Its Solution

H2O introduced the concept of "Heavy Hitters": a small number of tokens absorb the vast majority of attention. It maintains a *cumulative attention score* for each token (the sum of all historical queries' attention to it), keeps the cache at a fixed size — half for the most recent tokens, half for the top-scoring heavy hitters — and greedily evicts the current lowest-scoring token at each step.

An analogy: a fixed-capacity VIP club that always admits whoever just walked in (recent window) plus the historically most popular guests (heavy hitters), and asks whoever is currently least popular to leave.

*** Why It Works (and What Hole It Dug)

It is cheap, training-free, and plug-and-play, compressing memory to a constant. But H2O itself acknowledged a hard flaw: *eviction is permanent and greedy* — "evicted KVs are never seen in future steps; losing critical KVs causes severe performance collapse." A token that looks useless now but becomes critical later is gone forever. Moreover, scoring by *cumulative* attention naturally favors "long-lived" old tokens (they have accumulated more steps), conflating "frequently attended" with "important in the future."

This "gone forever" problem is what the entire subsequent line keeps trying to patch.

** 2023 | StreamingLLM: Don't Discard by Content — First Understand Why You Can't Keep Only a Recent Window

*** What Problem It Saw

H2O still requires maintaining scores and evicting step by step — somewhat cumbersome. The most naive idea is: just keep the most recent N tokens (a sliding window), and you have constant memory. StreamingLLM found this approach *collapses* — once the sequence exceeds the window size and the initial tokens are pushed out, perplexity explodes immediately.

*** Its Solution

It identified the reason: the first few tokens are "attention sinks." Softmax requires attention weights to sum to 1; when a query finds no strong match anywhere, the surplus attention must *go somewhere* — and since the first tokens are visible to every subsequent position, training turns them into this "spare-change jar." Remove the jar (the initial tokens) and the arithmetic breaks down.

The fix is elegantly simple: permanently retain ~4 initial sink tokens plus a sliding recent window, with no fine-tuning required, enabling stable streaming over 4 million tokens.

*** Why It Works (and What Hole It Dug)

It revealed the counterintuitive fact that "importance is not just about recency — it also depends on being a sink." But its limitation is the same destination reached by a different road: StreamingLLM itself states it "does not extend the context window or enhance long-term memory" — any mid-range information that falls outside the sink + recent window *is gone forever*, and retrieval accuracy "decays with distance, dropping to zero beyond cache capacity."

At this point, both naive paths (discard by content, discard by position) have hit the same wall: *any permanent trade-off made by a fixed rule will discard the wrong things for some tasks.* The next paper approached this from a different angle — could the trade-off be made smarter and later?

** 2024 | SnapKV: The Model Already Knows What It Will Look at Before Generating

*** What Problem It Saw

SnapKV focused on the *timing* of methods like H2O: they accumulate and evict token by token throughout the decoding loop, maintaining bookkeeping to the end — unnecessarily expensive. SnapKV's judgment was that this is unnecessary: the model's attention distribution over the prompt *is essentially settled before generation begins*. If that pattern can be read from the prompt itself, the cache can be compressed *once* at the end of prefill, with no ongoing maintenance. The paper's title captures this claim: "LLM Knows What You are Looking for Before Generation."

*** Its Solution

SnapKV focuses on an *observation window* — a small segment at the end of the prompt (32 tokens by default on LongBench). The intuition: the last few tokens of the prompt are the query's most recent "voice"; wherever they attend is a strong predictor of where the upcoming generated tokens will attend.

1. *Voting*: take each query in the observation window's attention over all preceding keys, sum along the query dimension to get an importance score for each preceding position.
2. *Selection*: each head independently keeps its top-k preceding positions, then appends the observation window.
3. *Pooling*: apply a 1D max-pool (kernel size 7) before selection to preserve *neighbors* of important tokens — without this, you get a fragmented result akin to "keeping only the country code of a phone number and filling in the rest from thin air."

Analogy: near the end of a meeting, you note which documents the last speaker kept pointing to, keep only those (plus the surrounding pages), and bet that the subsequent discussion will reference the same batch.

*** Why It Works (and What Holes It Dug)

Replacing "incremental maintenance" with "one-shot snapshot" is both fast and memory-efficient while preserving quality. But it introduced two holes that this paper directly inherits and addresses:

1. *The prefill blind spot*: the selection is entirely driven by attention observed *during prefill*. SnapKV assumes this pattern remains stable after generation begins; but if generation causes the relevant context to shift (e.g., multi-step reasoning surfaces new dependencies), the prefill snapshot becomes stale — and the compression decision is *final, with no revisiting*. This paper calls it a "fundamental blind spot": tokens that look important based on prefill signals may not be the ones truly driving decoding.
2. *Uniform budget across all heads*: each head independently selects *which* positions to keep, but *how many* (the budget) is a globally fixed constant. Heads with concentrated attention and heads with diffuse attention are forced to keep the same number of entries — the former wastes capacity, the latter goes hungry. This is precisely the gap Ada-KV was built to close.

** 2024 | Ada-KV: Same Selection Rule, But Redistribute the Budget Across Heads

*** What Problem It Saw

Ada-KV agrees with SnapKV's *selection rule* but targets its *budget allocation*: giving each head the same number of slots "ignores each head's unique attention pattern" and is not theoretically optimal in terms of minimizing approximation error.

*** Its Solution

Ada-KV is a *budget redistribution layer* mounted on top of an existing eviction method (hence "Ada-SnapKV"). Instead of letting each head take its own top-k within a fixed budget, it:

1. Pools all heads' candidate attention weights into a single collection;
2. Takes the global top-B (B = total budget);
3. Each head's budget = how many of its weights made the global cut.

Concentrated heads naturally receive fewer slots; diffuse heads receive more. Theoretically (Theorem 3.1/3.3) this minimizes the upper bound on the L1 distance between attention outputs before and after eviction — equivalent to *maximizing the retained attention quality across all heads* under a fixed total budget, and proven superior to uniform allocation. A safety floor (α=0.2) mixes in a uniform allocation to prevent any head from being starved to zero.

Analogy: SnapKV gives every department the same budget regardless of need; Ada-KV piles all requests into one pool, globally approves the highest-priority ones first, and each department's final allocation equals how many of its requests cleared the bar — with a floor so no one is completely defunded.

*** Why It Works (and What Hole It Dug)

Budget allocation became more precise, preserving more useful attention under the same total expenditure. But the hole is clear: Ada-KV optimizes *how to allocate the budget*, while *the basis for allocation is still the same prefill blind-spot signal inherited from SnapKV*. It performs smarter redistribution from a snapshot that may already be stale — if the snapshot itself misjudges what generation actually needs, even an optimal allocation is just rearranging deck chairs on a wrong signal. Moreover, the budget is distributed across *heads* in a one-shot fashion, with no adjustment as generation proceeds and no per-segment adaptation.

** 2024 | CritiPrefill: Move Sparsity from Decoding into Prefill

*** What Problem It Saw

The previous papers all saved *decoding* memory. CritiPrefill pointed to a neglected bottleneck: in long-context settings, *prefill itself* is the dominant latency cost — attention is O(L²), and prefilling 1M tokens can take half an hour. Saving decoding memory alone does not solve this.

*** Its Solution

The key observation is query-side locality: adjacent query tokens tend to attend to similar historical KVs. Computing criticality per token is too expensive for compute-intensive prefill, so it operates at *segment* granularity:

1. Partition queries into fixed-size segments (512) and KVs into blocks (32);
2. A segment-level algorithm estimates the "criticality" of each KV block for each query segment;
3. An inter-layer fusion step refines the scores; each segment selects its top-k blocks within a budget (1024), pruning unimportant (segment, block) pairs.

On a single A100 with 128K context on Llama3-8B, this achieves a 2.7× speedup with negligible quality loss.

*** Why It Works (and What Hole It Dug)

It was the first work to cleanly apply sparsity to the prefill stage, demonstrating that prefill can be substantially accelerated without hurting quality. But it left the opening this paper exploits: the budget is a *single global value* applied per segment, so *all heads within a segment share the same compute envelope*. A head doing dense retrieval and a head doing local streaming have completely different geometries, yet they are stuffed into the same k. In other words, CritiPrefill promoted sparsity from "per token" to "per segment," but not yet to "per head."

** 2026 | EntropyInfer: Heads Are Not One Kind of Thing — Stop Using a Single Ruler

*** What Problem It Saw

This paper distills the shared root cause of all prior methods into one sentence: *they all assume some form of "uniformity" — across positions (StreamingLLM), across heads (SnapKV's uniform budget, CritiPrefill's uniform k), or across contexts (offline pre-determination of head roles) — and this uniformity simply does not hold in practice.*

It quantifies "head heterogeneity" via attention entropy: some heads are *Rigid Heads*, whose entropy is "below 10⁻⁵ regardless of input," fixating on fixed positions with near-deterministic certainty; others are *Dynamic Heads*, whose entropy "fluctuates dramatically with query position, carrying genuinely context-dependent semantic structure." And *the same head can be rigid on one input and dynamic on another* — making it impossible to determine offline which heads are "important" (this directly refutes the approach taken by DuoAttention, which uses a learned classifier, and RazorAttention, which uses static analysis to pre-assign head roles). The conclusion: classification *must happen online*.

Along the way, it folds SnapKV/Ada-KV's prefill blind spot into the same framework: those methods assume "prefill attention reliably predicts decoding importance" and "head behavior can be characterized before seeing the input" — both assumptions are falsified by the entropy analysis.

*** Its Solution

EntropyInfer is a training-free, plug-and-play "attention replacement module" that modifies both ends:

1. *Prefill side — per-head, per-segment compute allocation guided by entropy.* The row-wise attention entropy of each head is measured online. Rigid heads receive a fixed minimal budget (they are fixating on a few positions anyway — extra budget is waste); dynamic heads receive a budget that *scales with the entropy fluctuation between adjacent segments* — "concentrating compute where attention is most uncertain." This simultaneously breaks both uniformity assumptions: CritiPrefill's "same k across heads within a segment" and SnapKV's "same budget across heads" — achieving *per-head × per-segment* adaptive allocation.

2. *Decoding side — latent KV compression to fix the blind spot.* Rather than locking in which tokens to keep the moment prefill ends, the method *waits for a small number of output tokens to be generated first*, folds those *actual output* tokens into the observation window, and uses them to re-rank the KV cache entries. This directly addresses SnapKV's "prefill snapshot fails to predict true decoding needs" — since patterns shift once generation begins, let the genuine first few generated tokens vote rather than relying solely on the prompt tail as a proxy.

Tracing the continuous transformation: SnapKV uses the last 32 prompt tokens as a "proxy for the future"; EntropyInfer argues the proxy is not accurate enough, so *wait for the near-future to show a small slice of its face before deciding* — the observation window shifts from "the tail of the prompt" to "the tail of the prompt plus a few already-generated real tokens."

*** Why It Works (and What Open Problems Remain)

The core shift: this line moves from "find a better unified ruler" (smarter global scores, more optimal uniform-budget replacements) to *accepting that there is no unified ruler* — importance varies per head, per segment, per context; the tool for measuring it is *entropy*; and it can only be measured online. The paper reports that EntropyInfer consistently outperforms SnapKV, AdaKV, and CritiPrefill baselines on Llama, Qwen, and openPangu.

Open questions it leaves (also the natural next stops on this line): online entropy measurement and per-segment budget scaling carry runtime overhead — whether the adaptive savings can outweigh the measurement cost, and whether this remains worthwhile at even longer sequences and larger head counts, requires verification beyond the paper. "Latent compression" waits for output tokens, pushing the compression point later — the first few decoding steps still carry a larger cache, a trade-off to weigh in memory-critical scenarios.

* Frontier Extensions

As of June 2026, EntropyInfer represents the latest publicly available step along the specific path of "using entropy to distinguish heads online and adapt per head per segment." Several adjacent lines are advancing in parallel around the same core problem — *prefill-to-decoding attention mismatch* and *per-head adaptivity* — though these are related works rather than confirmed follow-ups citing this paper:

- *DuoAttention* (2024-10, ICLR 2025): partitions heads into "retrieval heads" (full KV retained) and "streaming heads" (only sink + recent window), identified via lightweight optimization on synthetic data. Point of contrast with this paper: head classification is a *pre-learned static assignment*, whereas this paper insists head type varies online with the input.
- *RazorAttention* (2024-07, ICLR 2025): training-free static analysis assigns heads a "retrieval / non-retrieval" role; non-retrieval heads discard distant tokens and fold the dropped information into a compensation token. Again *pre-inference static analysis* — precisely the class of assumption this paper falsifies with entropy evidence.
- *LoopServe* (2025-07): a two-phase scheme for multi-turn dialogue that does online prefill sparsification plus progressive KV compression during decoding, directly targeting the prefill→decode attention mismatch — the same motivation as this paper's latent compression.
- *LouisKV* (2025-10): treats KV as a *retrievable* store, exploiting temporal locality to trigger retrieval only at semantic boundaries, and decouples KV management for input vs. output. It points toward a more radical direction: rather than repeatedly tuning budgets within the attention mechanism, treat history as external storage and retrieve on demand.

The larger open question: now that this paper has established "importance must be measured online, per head," the next step is likely to either drive down the *cost* of online measurement (making entropy computation and budget scheduling nearly free), or follow LouisKV's direction and stack "per-head adaptive sparsity" together with "KV external retrieval" along two independent axes.

* Insight

Reading through this line, the surface story is "compress KV ever more precisely," but what is actually happening underneath is that *the very concept of "importance" is being interrogated step by step, retreating from "can be predetermined" all the way to "must be measured online, per head"*:

- *H2O / StreamingLLM era*: importance = a *global rule* (cumulative score / position). One ruler for all tokens.
- *SnapKV / Ada-KV era*: importance = a *snapshot* voted out by the observation window at the end of prefill. The ruler is refined to "use the tail of the prompt to predict the future," and it begins to acknowledge that different heads deserve different budgets — but the measuring instrument is still the same snapshot.
- *CritiPrefill era*: importance is pushed down to *segment* granularity, yet within a segment all heads still share the same k.
- *EntropyInfer era*: importance is a quantity that *varies per head, per segment, per context*; no offline rule can predetermine it; the instrument for measuring it is *entropy*; and it can only be measured online.

Each leap is not about pressing the curve a little lower within the previous generation's framework — it is about *shrinking the range of what can be assumed in advance*. H2O assumes a global score suffices; SnapKV assumes the prefill snapshot suffices; Ada-KV relaxes "uniform budget across heads"; CritiPrefill relaxes "uniform rule across positions" — but all still grip the assumption that "importance can be characterized by some offline or prefill-time rule." EntropyInfer's lever is to pull out *the constraint everyone had silently accepted and no one had questioned*, and break it: entropy demonstrates that head behavior is neither uniform nor predictable, so the classification is moved entirely online.

This is the same theme as the "prefill blind spot" critique, seen from the other side: SnapKV assumes "prefill can predict decoding"; this paper says it cannot — so *let the real decoding show a small slice of its face before deciding*. Both changes (online entropy measurement, latent compression) share one cognitive shift: *from "guess ahead of time" to "measure while running."*

The next hidden constraint waiting to be lifted is, I suspect, *"online measurement itself must be cheap enough to ignore."* EntropyInfer moves the judgment online, gaining accuracy but also taking on the runtime cost of online entropy computation and dynamic budget scheduling. If someday someone can make "per-head, per-segment importance measurement" itself nearly free — say, by having the model learn a lightweight entropy predictor during pretraining — or follows LouisKV's path and externalizes history into retrievable storage triggered only at semantic boundaries, this line may undergo another categorical leap: from "measure importance online" to "importance measurement is amortized into the architecture itself."

* Lessons

1. *When a field's improvement curve flattens, it is often not because the optimum is near, but because everyone shares an unquestioned assumption.* Five generations of work in this line built ever-finer "unified importance rulers," until EntropyInfer used entropy to show that no ruler, however fine, is correct — because there is no unified ruler; heads are heterogeneous and vary with input. The concrete posture for finding a breakthrough is therefore: *list what everyone assumes can be decided in advance; whatever is implicitly treated as a constant is the next lever.*

2. *"Falsifying a hidden assumption" is often more valuable than "optimizing a metric."* Ada-KV achieved a more optimal budget allocation within SnapKV's framework (metric optimization); EntropyInfer demonstrated that the ground beneath that framework — prefill signal is reliable, heads can be characterized offline — is wrong (assumption falsification). The former pushes the curve down a little; the latter changes what is being optimized. Asking "is there a premise in this field that has been repeatedly inherited but never empirically tested?" pays off one order of magnitude more than tuning hyperparameters.

3. *A good diagnostic quantity can pry open an entire research line.* EntropyInfer's real fulcrum is not any particular new algorithm — it is treating "attention entropy" as a diagnostic metric for distinguishing rigid from dynamic heads. Once that measure is established, "must classify online," "allocate per head per segment," and the other design choices grow out naturally. When looking for a research problem, *the ability to find a simple, measurable quantity that reveals heterogeneity where everyone assumed homogeneity* is itself a rare skill.

4. *Fixing a "proxy signal's blind spot" sometimes requires only shifting the observation point slightly later.* SnapKV uses the prompt tail as a proxy for the future; this paper simply extends the observation window to include "the few real tokens already generated" — a small temporal shift that patches the prefill blind spot the entire line had carried for two years. The change need not be complex; the key is recognizing "what exactly are we using as a proxy for the future, and why does that proxy introduce bias?"

This suggests a transferable heuristic: *take any system that "makes trade-offs based on a score or rule computed in advance" and ask two questions — was this score computed before the moment when the decision actually matters? Does it assume all the objects being scored are the same kind of thing?* If the answers are "computed earlier" and "assumes uniformity," then moving the measurement online and measuring each genuine subtype separately is usually the next step forward.
