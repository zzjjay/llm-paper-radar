* The River of Problems

One core question has sustained an entire field for over three decades: *How do you take a trained neural network, cut it down to size so it runs faster and uses less memory — without retraining it?*

What makes this so hard? Every channel, every attention head, every weight in a neural network is entangled with the others. Remove one, and the remaining partners suddenly find that a teammate is gone — they were collaborating to produce some feature, and now the collaboration chain is broken. The signal the downstream layer receives is completely transformed, and accuracy collapses.

There are two routes out. If you can afford it, fine-tune: retrain the compressed network to realign. If you cannot — data is gone, a client won't show you theirs, compute budget is exhausted — then after the cut you must find a way to *compensate*. This entire river is about that second path: *after the cut, how do you patch things back up?*

From 1990 to 2026, every generation has answered the same question, but with ever-finer technique: from "look at the second derivative of the loss" to "look at activation statistics," from "compensate one weight at a time" to "compensate an entire block of the network at once," from "only works for convolutional networks" to "also works for large language models."

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1990 | OBD: Using Second Derivatives to Judge "Which Weights Matter Least"

*** What problem it saw

In LeCun's era, neural networks were already growing uncomfortably large. The instinct was to prune them — both for speed and for regularization. But which weights? The most naive idea is "prune the small ones" — small absolute value means low importance, right? This intuition fails regularly: a weight with a tiny absolute value might sit on an extremely steep slope of the loss surface, and the moment you touch it, the loss explodes.

*** Its solution

LeCun said: forget the magnitude, look at the *sensitivity* to the loss. Concretely, take a Taylor expansion of the loss L around the current weights. If weight w_i is pruned (set to zero), the loss increases by approximately (1/2) * w_i^2 * H_ii, where H_ii is the diagonal entry of the Hessian.

This quantity H_ii * w_i^2 is called the "saliency." Prune the weights with the smallest saliency.

*** Why it worked (and what hole it left)

Second-order information is genuinely more reliable than first-order (gradients) or zeroth-order (weight magnitudes) — it tells you not how large a weight currently is, but how much the loss would hurt if you removed it.

But it carries two buried problems. First, it assumes the Hessian is diagonal — that is, weights do not interact. In reality they are strongly coupled: remove one, and the optimal values of the others shift. Second, there is no compensation after pruning — surviving weights keep their original values, even though their optimal values have drifted.

Hassibi would pick up both problems three years later.

** 1993 | OBS: After Pruning, All Surviving Weights Should Be Recomputed

*** What problem it saw

OBD assumes a diagonal Hessian — every neuron is its own island. Hassibi saw through this immediately: the real Hessian is dense with off-diagonal terms everywhere, reflecting the coupling among weights. Remove one weight, and the optimal values of all the others shift.

Pruning without compensating is like pulling a gear out of a machine and expecting it to keep running unchanged — it simply cannot.

*** Its solution

Hassibi used the full Hessian (not just the diagonal). Pruning weight w_q is equivalent to the constraint e_q^T * w + w_q = 0. Minimizing the loss increase subject to this constraint via Lagrange multipliers yields two results:

1. The increase in loss caused by removing w_q — a saliency score that is strictly more accurate than OBD's.
2. The correction every remaining weight should receive — an analytical update formula: Δw = -(w_q / [H^-1]_qq) * H^-1 * e_q

In other words, the moment one weight is pruned, *all* surviving weights are analytically adjusted to minimize the increase in loss.

*** Why it worked (and what hole it left)

OBS was the first method to give a closed-form, analytical "prune + compensate" pair. The intuition is elegant: if you pull out a gear, the teeth of the remaining gears should realign — and this realignment can be computed exactly.

But OBS assumes: (1) you are pruning individual scalar weights, not entire columns or channels; and (2) you can afford to invert the Hessian — for a network with one million parameters, the Hessian is a one-million by one-million matrix, impossible even to store.

OBS ran beautifully on the small networks of the 1990s and simply could not be lifted into the CNN era of the 2010s.

** 2017 | He et al.: Hardware Hates Scattered Zeros — Prune Entire Channels

*** What problem it saw

OBD/OBS prune individual weights, leaving a matrix peppered with zeros — "unstructured sparsity." Theoretically elegant, practically useless: a GPU or a convolution kernel does not care how many zeros you have. It still computes the full row-by-column product, and zeros save no time.

Real acceleration requires pruning entire columns or entire channels — structured compression.

But the moment you do that, OBS's analytical solution breaks down: it was derived for a single scalar, not a vector.

*** Its solution

He et al. shifted perspective: instead of using second-order information to estimate how much the loss rises when you prune, look directly at activations.

Record the activations X for a layer (using a small calibration batch). Use LASSO to select which channels to retain, then solve a least-squares problem: make the downstream output after pruning match the original downstream output as closely as possible. Concretely, min ||Y - X_kept * W'||^2, and solve for the new W'.

Selection is handled by LASSO; compensation is handled by least squares — two clean steps.

*** Why it worked (and what hole it left)

Shifting from "parameter space" to "activation space" was a conceptual leap — you no longer need to compute the Hessian; a single forward pass is enough to record the activations. This trick would be borrowed repeatedly.

Remaining problems: (1) it processes one layer at a time, propagating forward, so errors accumulate; (2) selection (LASSO) and compensation (least squares) are tightly coupled — if LASSO doesn't converge, the whole procedure fails; (3) it cannot handle the producer-consumer coupling structure of Transformer layers. Most critically: by the LLM era of 2023, with tens of billions of parameters and hidden dimensions of tens of thousands, LASSO's iterative solver is completely unaffordable.

** 2023 | SparseGPT: Decompose OBS into Independent Per-Layer Problems to Make It Work on LLMs

*** What problem it saw

For a model like OPT-175B, full OBS is not even worth considering — the complete Hessian would be 175 billion by 175 billion. But He et al.'s "process one layer at a time" intuition is right: the input and output activations of each layer can be recorded independently. So why not apply the OBS philosophy inside each layer, while decoupling layers from each other?

That is exactly what Frantar and Alistarh seized on: decompose the global OBS problem into one independent OBS sub-problem per layer.

*** Its solution

Each layer is optimized independently: minimize ||W * X - W_pruned * X||^2, where X is the calibration activation matrix.

The OBS update formula is adapted: approximate the Hessian as H ≈ 2 * X * X^T (twice the activation Gram matrix). This Hessian is only d × d (d is the hidden dimension, on the order of thousands) — tractable. Pruning and updating then proceed column-by-column in OBS style. A clever algorithmic trick lets the Hessian-inverse computation be shared across columns, keeping the total complexity manageable.

The result: OPT-175B pruned to 50% sparsity in a few hours, with almost no drop on zero-shot tasks.

*** Why it worked (and what hole it left)

SparseGPT genuinely solved the problem of post-training compression for large language models — no fine-tuning required, done in hours. It dragged the spirit of OBS (analytical second-order compensation) into the LLM era.

But it left two uncomfortable residues:
1. *Selection and updating are tightly coupled.* The OBS-style algorithm selects and updates simultaneously; the pruning criterion is embedded in the OBS solve and cannot be swapped out.
2. *It is fundamentally unstructured.* Although a "semi-structured" (2:4 sparsity) mode exists, the algorithm does not naturally support pruning entire channels or entire attention heads. Variants like ZipLM are required.
3. *The implementation is complex.* Hessian inversion, column update scheduling, blocked processing — the engineering bar is high.

The next generation could not resist asking: can it be simpler?

** 2024 | Wanda: Maybe You Don't Need Matrix Inversion at All — the Simplest Criterion Is Enough

*** What problem it saw

Sun et al. looked at SparseGPT's elaborate Hessian inversion, column scheduling, and blocked updates and asked: is all of this truly necessary? They suspected not.

They ran an experiment of almost disarming simplicity: can a criterion that requires no inversion whatsoever, and no weight updates whatsoever, come close to SparseGPT's accuracy?

*** Its solution

Wanda's importance score is a single formula:

  importance(w_ij) = |w_ij| * ||X_j||_2

Each weight's importance equals its absolute value multiplied by the L2 norm of the corresponding column of input activations.

Prune the weights with the smallest scores. *No weight updates at all* — zero compensation.

Comparison is done within groups defined by the output dimension (each output neuron ranks its own incoming weights independently), keeping local sparsity uniform.

*** Why it worked (and what hole it left)

Wanda's core observation is both awkward and profound: in the "mild compression" regime around 50% sparsity, *the difference between updating weights and not updating them is surprisingly small*. SparseGPT's elaborate second-order compensation, relative to Wanda's bare "select and freeze," is far less decisive than anyone expected.

This implies that in the LLM era, *activation statistics* may carry more signal than *second-order parameter information* — consulting the L2 norm of activations alongside the weight magnitude already smuggles in a fraction of the second-order information.

But the gap Wanda leaves is obvious: *no compensation*. Push toward more aggressive compression — 60%, 70%, whole-channel structural pruning — and Wanda collapses. The numbers in Table 1 of the paper are blunt: Wanda at 50% sparsity gives a WikiText2 perplexity of 171, at 60% it is 272, at 70% it is 1839 — a complete blowup.

So between 2024 and 2025, a wave of papers added compensation back.

** 2024–25 | Wanda++ / SlimGPT / FLAP: Everyone Adds Their Own Compensation, But Compensation Stays Glued to Selection

*** What problem it saw

Wanda is simple but compensation-free; aggressive compression collapses. SparseGPT has compensation but is algorithmically complex and locked to unstructured pruning. What to do?

Each group made a different choice:
- *Wanda++* added "local gradient refinement" — after pruning, run a short gradient update on the retained weights within a local region.
- *SlimGPT* added "curvature reconstruction" — update weights using a Newton-style approximation.
- *FLAP* added "head-level bias compensation" — a bias correction term designed specifically for attention-head pruning in LLMs.

Different solutions, but all sharing one property: the compensation mechanism is *tightly bound* to its own pruning strategy.

*** Its solution

Each group patched a Wanda-style pruner with its own version of a correction. Accuracy recovered somewhat, but at a price:

1. Want to swap the pruning criterion? You cannot; the compensation was designed for this specific criterion.
2. Want to move from language models to vision models? You cannot; FLAP's bias compensation is exclusive to LLM attention heads.
3. Want to do folding (cluster-and-merge channels) instead of pruning (discard channels)? You cannot; all these compensations are designed for "retaining a subset," not for "merging into centroids."

*** Why it worked (and what hole it left)

This generation proved the point: *add any compensation at all, and aggressive compression can recover a large fraction of the lost accuracy*. FLAP plus bias correction is noticeably better than bare Wanda at 60% sparsity.

But all these compensations are "bound-form" — coupled to a specific pruning algorithm and a specific architecture. If you want a *general, decoupled, architecture-agnostic* compensation component — a true plug-and-play compensation module — it does not yet exist.

GRAIL was built to fill that gap.

** 2026 | GRAIL: Fully Decouple Selection from Compensation — One Ridge Regression Covers Everything

*** What problem it saw

Tang et al. re-read the thirty-plus-year story and spotted a structural problem that had been overlooked throughout:

Every generation had coupled "which channels to prune" with "how to compensate after pruning" — OBS selects and computes compensation simultaneously, SparseGPT embeds the selection criterion inside the OBS solve, and Wanda++/SlimGPT/FLAP each weld their compensation to their own selection strategy.

But these two concerns are *fundamentally independent*: selection is "decide who survives"; compensation is "given the survivors, realign the rest of the network." Welding them together means every new selection strategy demands a redesigned compensator; every new architecture (CNN/ViT/LLM) demands a redesigned compensator; even switching from pruning to folding demands a redesigned compensator.

*Can we build a compensation module that is completely indifferent to how selection was done?*

*** Its solution

GRAIL's core is a single move:

1. However you chose your retained subset P — magnitude, Wanda, Gram-based, folding — after pruning you have a reduced hidden activation h_P.
2. Run a small calibration dataset (128 images, or a few thousand tokens) through the original uncompressed model and record the downstream consumer layer's input activations h.
3. Compute the activation Gram matrix G = H^T * H (no gradients, no labels needed).
4. Solve a ridge regression: B = G_PH * (G_PP + λI)^-1, finding a linear map such that h ≈ B * h_P.
5. Absorb B directly into the downstream consumer layer's weights: W' = W * B.

The most elegant stroke: for *folding* (merging channels into centroids), the formula extends naturally — simply replace the Gram sub-matrix with M_fold^T * G * M_fold. The entire mathematical structure is preserved. Pruning and folding are unified in a single formula.

For LLM attention-head pruning, a special treatment is introduced: heads are viewed as a block Kronecker structure, letting GRAIL operate at the head level. GQA is also compatible.

*** Why it worked (and what holes remain)

The intuition: when you remove some channels, the downstream layer receives a "lame" hidden state. The Gram matrix encodes *inter-channel correlations* — which retained channels are strongly correlated with the pruned ones. The ridge regression uses these "informed proxies" to linearly reconstruct the signal carried by the pruned channels.

Why ridge instead of ordinary least squares? Because G_PP is frequently ill-conditioned under aggressive compression — tiny singular values make inversion explosive. The λ term keeps the numerics stable; it is, in essence, regularization.

Why a linear map? Because it has an analytical closed-form solution (one matrix inversion); because it can be *absorbed into the downstream weights*, adding zero inference cost; because it reconstructs the post-activation, nonlinear outputs, sidestepping the hard problem of "passing reconstruction through a nonlinearity."

The results in Table 1 of the paper: SlimGPT at 70% sparsity gives a WikiText2 perplexity of 16764 (blown up); GRAIL brings that down to 43.14. Wanda at 50% gives 171; GRAIL brings it to 18.85. Even FLAP, which already carries its own compensation, drops further when GRAIL is added on top.

Remaining limitations:
1. The calibration Gram matrix G is H × H; on very large models this can be memory-intensive.
2. Linear reconstruction is both GRAIL's strength and its ceiling — at extreme compression ratios (70%+), the signal carried by pruned channels cannot always be recovered from a linear combination of the survivors.
3. The method assumes producer-consumer pairs are locally adjacent; more complex network topologies (multi-branch, skip connections) require additional handling.

* Frontier Extensions

GRAIL is a March 2026 preprint and represents the current frontier of the "post-training structured-compression compensation" line. It is the most recent known node in this river.

Foreseeable next steps (the paper itself does not state these; they are the natural continuation of the river):
- Replace linear reconstruction with a low-rank nonlinear reconstruction (a small adapter, LoRA-style) to cover more aggressive compression ratios.
- Global cross-layer reconstruction, rather than only local producer-consumer pairs.
- Carry the "selection-compensation decoupling" mindset from compression into quantization — methods like AdaRound/BRECQ are already moving in this direction, but the connection has not been fully bridged.

* Insights

This 36-year evolutionary arc looks on the surface like "compression algorithms getting smarter," but underneath, three fundamental shifts are taking place.

*Shift one: from parameter space to activation space.* OBD/OBS look at the second derivative of the loss with respect to parameters — a "parameter-space" instrument. He et al. pivoted to an "activation-space" view: forget what the Hessian looks like, just inspect the activations. SparseGPT nominally uses OBS, but its Hessian has already been replaced by the activation Gram matrix X*X^T. Wanda looks at nothing but the L2 norm of activations. GRAIL places the activation Gram matrix at the very center.

This is not coincidence. A neural network's "importance" is fundamentally data-driven — how much a weight affects the output on a specific piece of data is more actionable than its saliency in some idealized second-order expansion. In the deep-learning era, *data is a better approximation to the Hessian than the Hessian itself*.

*Shift two: from coupling to decoupling.* OBS welds selection and compensation together; SparseGPT does the same; Wanda++/SlimGPT/FLAP all say "our compensation for our selection." GRAIL is the first clean separation — and this is an "engineering aesthetics" victory: when two responsibilities can be decoupled, decouple them. The whole ecosystem gains exponential flexibility. Selection strategies and compensation modules can now evolve independently and combine freely.

*Shift three: from single-architecture solutions to universal modules.* He et al. in the CNN era did not handle Transformers; SparseGPT ignored ViTs; FLAP was exclusive to LLM attention heads. GRAIL is the first method to claim "one mathematical framework, three architectures." The field is beginning to develop *infrastructure* — where previously each architecture was its own island, a general bridging component has appeared.

*Where does the field go next?* Two bets: (1) Linear reconstruction will be supplemented by "nonlinear but low-rank" reconstruction (small LoRA-style adapters), covering the 70%+ aggressive compression regime. (2) The "activation statistics plus analytical compensation" pattern pioneered by GRAIL will spill out of compression into quantization, knowledge distillation, and model merging — everywhere you modify a model after training — because it is the same underlying mathematical skeleton.

* Takeaways

If you are doing research: when a field is in a phase where every new paper "adds something" — adds compensation, adds regularization, adds a strategy — but always welds the new thing tightly to the existing thing, *resist the urge to also add something*. Step back and ask: can the existing pieces be decoupled? Decoupling itself is a paper. GRAIL did not invent a new selection strategy; it did not invent new compensation mathematics (ridge regression is textbook material). It only pulled "selection" and "compensation" apart — that is its entire contribution, and it was enough to publish.

If you are doing engineering: post-training compression is now mature enough to industrialize. If you have a large model to deploy and cannot afford fine-tuning, *today's toolchain already supports "prune 50% from a bare model with almost no accuracy drop."* Concrete recipe: use Wanda for channel selection, use GRAIL for compensation, calibration data is a few hundred samples. In 2023 this required a PhD team doing manual tuning; by 2026 it is close to a configuration checkbox.

If you are learning this field: do not start with the GRAIL paper. Start with OBD in 1990 and read forward. Each paper is a direct reaction to the one before — follow the thread of "what exactly is this paper complaining about in its predecessor," and you will spend a hundred times less energy than memorizing "what each paper does," and remember it a hundred times longer. This river is the best textbook there is.
