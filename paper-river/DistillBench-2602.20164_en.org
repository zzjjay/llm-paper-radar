* The River of Problems

Can an 8B small model really hold its own against 70B or 235B giants? And if so, how much does it cost to train one?

Floating on this river are everyone who cannot afford a GPU cluster — startups, university labs, enterprises that want to run models on their own servers. Frontier models keep getting larger, training costs keep growing more terrifying: GPT-4o is estimated to have consumed 27 million H100-hours and over 9,000 tonnes of CO₂. This game locks out the vast majority of players.

The only way out: can a small model inherit the "soul" of a large one without being trained from scratch? People proposed this idea ten years ago, but the road since has been full of detours — some proved it impossible, others proved it possible, so whom do you believe? This 2026 benchmark paper aims to settle the entire ledger.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Hinton's Trio: Distillation Is Invented

*** What Problem It Saw

LLMs did not yet exist in 2015, but the same tension had already appeared: the best-performing networks on ImageNet were huge ensembles that were too slow to serve in production, while smaller replacement models suffered a noticeable accuracy drop. The standard approach to training a small model was to use hard labels — "this image is a cat" — but one-hot labels are information-starved: they say nothing about "0.001% probability of being a dog, 0.0001% fox," and so on.

*** Its Solution

Have the large model emit its full post-softmax probability distribution (called soft labels), then have the small model learn that distribution rather than hard labels. Hinton's intuition: those 0.001% probabilities are not noise — they are the teacher's implicit encoding of inter-class similarity: cats are closer to dogs than to airplanes. Teaching the student this is equivalent to transferring the teacher's "dark knowledge."

To make those small probabilities visible, they introduced a *temperature parameter T*: divide the logits by T before softmax; the larger T is, the flatter the distribution and the more salient the dark knowledge. The student trains with the same T and reverts to T=1 at inference time.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfalls It Buried)

The intuition was sound: small models did learn the teacher's implicit classification boundaries. But — it was validated only on classification tasks, and "model" back then meant CNN, not a language model with billions of parameters. A decade later, when researchers tried to port it to LLMs, they found that soft labels are not so straightforward on generative tasks: at each step an LLM must choose among tens of thousands of tokens, the distribution is extremely sparse, and a host of new questions arise — how to tune T, how to compute KL divergence, how to stabilize training. These are the gaps the subsequent papers set out to fill.

** 2022 | Chinchilla: Data Matters More Than Parameters

*** What Problem It Saw

Between 2020 and 2022, the field fell into a "parameter race" — GPT-3 at 175B, Gopher at 280B, MT-NLG at 530B, bigger always better. But the Hoffmann team suspected these models were data-starved "undernourished giants." Given a fixed compute budget, should you spend it on more parameters or more data?

*** Its Solution

They ran over 400 experiments spanning different model sizes and dataset sizes, then fitted a clean formula: *model size and training token count should scale in equal proportion.* When Gopher was trained at 280B parameters, it used 300B tokens; by the Chinchilla formula it should have used 5.6T — nearly 20× more data. They trained Chinchilla at 70B parameters with 1.4T tokens and it beat 280B Gopher.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfalls It Buried)

It told the community: "small model + enough data > large model + insufficient data." This directly paved the road for the distillation revival — because it proved that model size is not the ceiling; data quality is. But Chinchilla addressed the optimal allocation for *training from scratch*; it did not answer "if you have access to only 0.1% of a GPU cluster, can you borrow someone else's compute?" That is the gap distillation was meant to fill.

** 2023 | Gudibande et al.: "Imitation Is an Illusion" — Cold Water on Distillation

*** What Problem It Saw

After ChatGPT's breakout in 2023, the open-source community flooded with models fine-tuned on ChatGPT outputs — Alpaca, Vicuna, and many others. They appeared impressive in conversation, and people assumed they had caught up with GPT-3.5. The Berkeley group (Gudibande et al.) stepped in to puncture that assumption.

*** Its Solution

They conducted a systematic comparison: small models were trained extensively to imitate ChatGPT outputs, then rigorously evaluated on standard NLP benchmarks (MMLU, HumanEval, BBH). The verdict was blunt: *imitation models learned style, not capability.* They replied in ChatGPT's confident, well-structured register, but showed no improvement whatsoever on factual accuracy or reasoning tasks — human evaluators thought they were impressive only because they were fooled by surface fluency.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfalls It Buried)

The paper's impact was severe: it wrote off "distilling LLMs" as a dead end. From 2023 through mid-2024, the consensus was broadly that "if you want a genuinely capable small model, train it from scratch — distillation is cosmetic at best." The 2026 Lenovo paper cites it explicitly [11] as evidence of "the inherent limitations of fine-tuning." That consensus had to be broken before the DeepSeek-R1 story could unfold — and breaking it required either changing *how* you distill, or changing *what* you distill.

** 2024 | MiniLLM: The KL Direction Was Backwards

*** What Problem It Saw

Why does LLM distillation fail to transfer capability? MiniLLM (Gu et al.) offered a technical diagnosis: standard distillation uses *forward KL divergence* (KL(teacher ‖ student)), which forces the student to cover every mode of the teacher's distribution — including the teacher's low-probability long tail that the student has no hope of learning. To accommodate that tail, the student distorts its representation of the main modes as well.

*** Its Solution

Switch to *reverse KL* (KL(student ‖ teacher)). This direction only penalizes the student for saying things the teacher would not say; it does not require the student to cover all of the teacher's possibilities. The intuition: let the student learn only what it can learn well, and not overreach. Technically this requires policy-gradient optimization, which is somewhat involved, but the results were significant — small models could closely approach the teacher on instruction-following tasks.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfalls It Buried)

It repaired distillation at the *methodological level*, but it still did not answer Gudibande's core challenge: can distillation transfer *reasoning ability*, not just instruction following? Instruction following is ultimately still "learning style" — learning how to respond politely, how to structure an answer. Real reasoning (math competitions, graduate-level physics) requires a *thinking process*, not an output format. Crossing that barrier was left to the next paper.

** 2025-01 | DeepSeek-R1: Distill the Thinking, Not the Answer

*** What Problem It Saw

The DeepSeek-R1 team's insight was remarkably simple: prior distillation was transferring the distribution of *final outputs.* But a reasoning model (like o1) generates a long chain of thought before answering — "let me check the conditions, suppose x=3, substitute in…". The real capability lives in that chain. If you only let the student learn the final answer "x=5," the student never learns how to get there.

*** Its Solution

They used R1 (685B) as the teacher and generated 800,000 samples: 600,000 reasoning trajectories with Chain-of-Thought plus 200,000 ordinary dialogues. They then used these *complete trajectories* (not soft labels) to directly SFT a series of small models — Qwen 7B/14B/32B, LLaMA 8B/70B. The results were astonishing: R1-Distill-Qwen-7B achieved 55.5% pass@1 on the AIME mathematics competition, surpassing GPT-4o (9.3%) and Claude 3.5 Sonnet.

Note the hidden twist: they actually reverted to the most straightforward SFT, without MiniLLM's reverse-KL machinery. The *form* of distillation regressed, but the *content* was upgraded — from probability distributions to reasoning trajectories. The "dark knowledge" is not in the softmax; it is in the chain of thought.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfalls It Buried)

DeepSeek-R1 pulled distillation back from "cosmetic procedure" to "genuine capability transfer" in one move — directly refuting Gudibande 2023. But it left two questions open:

1. Is this a one-off or a general principle? Does it only work with R1 as the teacher, or does it work with any sufficiently strong teacher?
2. Is it economically sound? Everyone heard "distillation is cheap," but how cheap? How much better than fine-tuning? How many orders of magnitude cheaper than training from scratch?

The second question is exactly what the present paper (Lenovo 2026) set out to answer.

** 2026-01 | This Paper (Lenovo): Accounting for Distillation Economics

*** What Problem It Saw

The DeepSeek-R1 paper focused on proving that distillation can transfer reasoning, but offered no systematic *cost-efficiency* data. The field was missing a report you could put on a CTO's desk: how much does training a vanilla 8B cost? A distilled 8B? What are their benchmark scores? How do they compare cost-per-performance against GPT-4o, Claude, and Grok?

At a deeper level: Chinchilla taught everyone how to optimally allocate "data vs. parameters" for training from scratch. But if you have a powerful teacher whose compute you can "borrow," does the entire compute-optimal framework need to be rewritten?

*** Its Solution

Rather than inventing new techniques, they conducted a *meta-benchmark*. They assembled training FLOPs, H100 GPU-hours, CO₂ emissions, and GPQA/AIME scores for 17 models (vanilla + distilled + proprietary) onto a single table. Key figures:

- Training vanilla Llama 3.1 8B: 1.46 million GPU-hours, 420 tonnes CO₂, AIME 10%
- Distilling DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B: 610 GPU-hours, 0.2 tonnes CO₂, AIME 50.4%
- *2,000× reduction in compute, yet performance increases 5×*

The starker comparison: the distilled 8B (86%) outscores the vanilla 235B (85.7%) on AIME, while requiring 60,000× less training compute.

Their FLOPs model was also refined: distillation was decomposed into "teacher inference cost (2·N_teacher·D)" plus "student training cost (6·N_student·D)," revealing that the teacher inference term dominates — which means *the same distillation dataset can be reused across multiple student models*, with ever-decreasing marginal cost.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfalls It Buried)

It elevated DeepSeek-R1's single data point into a *general principle*: across multiple vanilla/distilled pairs the cost-efficiency ratios are consistent. "Distillation is the economically optimal strategy" now has quantitative evidence, not just one exemplar.

But the pitfalls it left behind:
1. Every number assumes the teacher is already trained — *where does the teacher's training cost get allocated?* The 9,000 tonnes of CO₂ burned for GPT-4o — who pays? The true full-lifecycle accounting has not been done.
2. Only static SFT distillation was benchmarked; on-policy distillation (MiniLLM, reference [13]), RL-based distillation, and teacher-free self-distillation are not covered.
3. The study relies entirely on two reasoning benchmarks (GPQA, AIME). Whether findings generalize to code, agent tasks, or long-context settings is left open.

* Frontier Extensions

As of early 2026, this line of research is still evolving rapidly:

- *On-policy distillation (Agarwal et al. 2024)*: The student generates its own responses; the teacher provides corrections only where the student goes wrong — avoiding the distribution mismatch problem of off-policy distillation. This paper cites it as [13] but does not benchmark it.
- *Sequence-level RL distillation*: Merging distillation with RLHF/DPO. The success of OpenAI o1 and DeepSeek-R1 is fundamentally a combination of RL and distillation.
- *Multi-teacher distillation / teacher ensembles*: A single student learns from multiple teachers, mitigating any single teacher's biases.
- *Teacher-free self-distillation*: The student uses a previous version of itself as the teacher, improving iteratively — no 685B teacher required.

The question to watch over the next year or two: once the Chinchilla "train-from-scratch" formula is comprehensively displaced by "distillation + RL," what does the *new scaling law* look like? Is there some joint optimum over parameters, data, and teacher capability?

* Insights

Reading this river from source to present, one thing stands out: *the progress of distillation as a technique was not driven by cleverer algorithms — it was driven by a continual expansion of what could be distilled.*

- 2015: distill the *softmax distribution* — carrying implicit inter-class similarity
- 2024: distill a *corrected KL direction* — avoiding the tail the student cannot learn
- 2025: distill *Chain-of-Thought trajectories* — transferring process rather than outcome
- Next: likely distilling *search paths*, *tool-call sequences*, *failure-and-correction trajectories* — any "process" that can be tokenized

Behind this lies a deeper shift: *the carrier of intelligence is migrating from weights to sequences.* Early on, everyone assumed knowledge lived in the weights and therefore had to be trained into them directly. We then discovered that knowledge can equally live in token sequences — given rich enough sequences, a student SFT pass can inherit that knowledge. This in turn makes "data synthesis" a more central competitive advantage than "model architecture."

Gudibande's 2023 verdict — "imitation is an illusion" — was not actually wrong. The imitation they observed really was hollow. What was wrong was their assumption that this represented *the ceiling of distillation.* The truth was that what people were distilling at the time was too shallow. Switch the object of distillation from "how ChatGPT speaks" to "how ChatGPT thinks," and the whole enterprise becomes sound.

* Takeaways

For researchers: when an authoritative paper sentences a line of work to death, do not accept the verdict uncritically. Think one level deeper — *are they condemning the methodology itself, or one specific instantiation of it?* Gudibande rejected "distilling ChatGPT outputs," not "distilling large models" — those are different things, and the difference lies entirely in *what* is being distilled. Separate the variables, and the death sentence may be commuted.

For engineers: if you are building LLM applications today, there is no reason to train from scratch. The Lenovo accounting is clear — for the same compute budget, distillation is more economical than fine-tuning and three orders of magnitude cheaper than training from scratch. If you cannot access a closed-source teacher like GPT-4o, the open-source ecosystem already offers public teachers such as DeepSeek-R1 (685B). The remaining work is: find the "high-quality trajectory data" for your domain — perhaps an expert's workflow, a historical case library, or synthesized reasoning chains — and distill it into a 7B–14B student.

For those watching trends: notice that the frontier of "what can be distilled" is still expanding. The next wave of innovation may not lie in model architecture but in *how to construct and compress the teacher's process.* Whoever can translate more forms of intelligence into token sequences will be able to extend distillation's reach across more tasks. This means "data engineers" and "trajectory designers" will become scarcer and more valuable than "model architects."
