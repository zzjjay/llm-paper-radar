* The River of the Problem

One deeply practical question: when compressing a large model's weights from 16 bits down to 4 bits — across tens of billions of numbers — *which values are worth spending a few extra bits to protect, and which can safely be discarded?*

The question is hard because the magnitude of a weight is deceptive. A numerically small weight that is always multiplied by a large activation during inference will break the model if quantized carelessly. A numerically large weight whose input channel almost never fires can be quantized aggressively without harm. So "importance" has never been just about the weight itself.

For thirty years — from neural-network pruning to today's LLM quantization — successive generations have answered the same question, each with a different definition of "importance." Some looked at second derivatives (the Hessian), some at activation magnitudes, some at gradients, some at covariances. Every method claimed to have captured what matters, yet none could clearly articulate how it differed from the others.

This 2026 paper steps back and says: you are all computing the same thing — you have merely made different simplifying assumptions along the way.

* Evolutionary Narrative

The protagonist of this story is not any single quantization algorithm but the concept of "importance" itself — how it evolved from "second derivative" in 1990 to "gradient-weighted activation" in 2026.

** 1990 | OBD: Scoring Weights with Second Derivatives

*** What problem did it see?

Early pruning researchers followed intuition: prune the smallest weights. LeCun recognized immediately that this was wrong — a small weight might be critically sensitive to the loss. To honestly answer "how much does the loss rise if I remove this weight?" you need a Taylor expansion.

*** Its solution

Perform a second-order Taylor expansion of the loss with respect to the weights. The first-order term vanishes at a converged minimum, so importance is dominated by the second-order term — the diagonal Hessian. Each weight's "importance score" is approximately =w_i^2 * H_ii=: it looks at both weight magnitude and loss curvature.

*** Why it worked (and what hole it left)

The intuition is solid: if you want to know the cost of changing a value, compute mathematically how much the loss changes. 

But OBD took a shortcut: it used only the diagonal of the Hessian, assuming weights are independent of one another. The problem is that neural-network weights are highly coupled — pruning one shifts the optimal values of all the others. OBD cannot see this.

** 1993 | OBS: Accounting for Mutual Compensation Among Weights

*** What problem did it see?

Hassibi went straight at OBD's weak point: the diagonal assumption is badly wrong. After pruning weight A, weight B should have the opportunity to adjust and compensate for the loss — OBD denies it that chance, so the resulting importance scores deviate substantially from the true loss increase.

*** Its solution

Use the *full* Hessian (including all off-diagonal entries). When pruning weight i, not only compute the loss increase it causes, but simultaneously give every other weight a closed-form update so they automatically compensate. This is the origin of the name "Optimal Brain Surgeon": after every cut, immediately suture the wound.

*** Why it worked (and what hole it left)

Mathematically more honest. Every removal is globally optimal with compensation.

The new hole is computational cost: the full Hessian requires O(d^2) storage and O(d^3) inversion. Small 1993-era networks could handle this, but for modern models with hundreds of millions or billions of parameters it is completely intractable. That hole sat untouched for thirty years.

** 2022 | OBC / OBQ: Scaling OBS to Modern Models

*** What problem did it see?

Frantar revisited OBS. Prior PTQ work in the LLM era was either crude (direct rounding) or required retraining. OBS has beautiful mathematics — but no one had ever made it run on a modern network.

*** Its solution

The key observation: the whole-network compression problem can be decomposed into *layer-wise reconstruction* subproblems. As long as each layer's quantized output stays close to the original output, the global result is good. This shrinks the Hessian from O(total-parameters^2) to O(row-width^2) per layer, reducing O(d^4) to O(d_row * d_col^3). Combined with engineering refinements (ExactOBS), it becomes runnable on modern CNNs and Transformers.

The framework is also unified: replacing "prune" with "round to the nearest grid point" leaves the equations nearly unchanged. OBQ = OBS applied to quantization.

*** Why it worked (and what hole it left)

OBS mathematics are "fenced" inside a single layer — second-order information is preserved while complexity is brought under control.

The remaining hole: in a billion-parameter LLM, a single layer may be a matrix of thousands by tens of thousands. Quantizing one weight at a time and updating an entire column after each step is still too slow. Quantizing OPT-175B this way takes a week.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Making OBQ Usable on LLMs

*** What problem did it see?

OBQ runs on BERT and ResNet, but is completely impractical for LLMs. As Frantar himself wrote: OBQ takes one hour for ResNet50 — what would it take for GPT-3?

*** Its solution

Three engineering-level modifications:
1. *Fixed-order quantization*: OBQ selects the "cheapest" weight at each step; GPTQ simply sweeps columns left to right, eliminating the selection overhead.
2. *Lazy updates*: instead of immediately propagating compensation across the whole matrix, accumulate updates and apply them in blocks.
3. *Cholesky rewrite*: replace the Hessian inversion with a numerically more stable Cholesky decomposition.

Result: 1000× speedup. OPT-175B is done in hours.

*** Why it worked (and what hole it left)

GPTQ finally brought the decades-old OBS line into contact with large language models. But its importance measure is based on =X^T X=, the input covariance matrix — which treats every calibration sample equally, regardless of its actual effect on the loss.

Meanwhile, a completely different parallel line was growing — one that does not compute the Hessian at all, but looks at activations instead.

** 2023 | SmoothQuant: Pushing Outliers Out

*** What problem did it see?

GPTQ solves the W4 weight-only problem. But if you want to quantize activations too (W8A8, for inference throughput), you find that a small number of channels carry extremely large values (outliers) that simply do not fit in int8.

*** Its solution

A mathematically elegant trick: insert a pair of canceling diagonal scaling matrices =diag(s)= and =diag(s)^{-1}= around each linear layer. Divide the large activations by s to shrink them; multiply the corresponding weight columns by s to compensate. The activations become smooth; the weights become slightly harder to quantize — but the net gain is large.

*** Why it worked (and what hole it left)

This was a conceptual turning point in PTQ history: *difficulty can be transferred between weights and activations.*

But SmoothQuant is coarse — it applies uniform scaling at the channel level. It does not ask "which weights interact with large activations and are therefore more important?" The next paper made that question explicit.

** 2023 | AWQ: Find the Critical 1% by Looking at Activations

*** What problem did it see?

The Lin team discovered a counterintuitive phenomenon: in LLMs, protecting just 1% of "salient weights" from quantization recovers performance from catastrophic failure to near-lossless. The remaining 99% can be quantized as aggressively as you like.

The critical question then becomes: *how do you find that 1%?* Looking at weight magnitudes alone? No — empirically useless. Looking at the magnitudes of the *activation* channels those weights receive? Yes — the effect is immediate and dramatic.

*** Its solution

For each channel j, compute its average activation magnitude =E[||X_j||]= across the calibration set. Channels with large activations are marked as "salient," and the corresponding weight columns are protected via per-channel scaling (not by literally keeping them in FP16, but by mathematically boosting their relative precision in an equivalent way).

*** Why it worked (and what hole it left)

The intuition is correct: the importance of a weight = its own magnitude × the magnitude of its input activation. AWQ captures the second half.

But there is a theoretical awkwardness: GPTQ uses the diagonal of =X^T X=; AWQ uses =E[||X_j||^2]=. In certain corner cases the two metrics recommend completely different channels. Which is right?

The deeper issue: AWQ uses no loss signal at all. It assumes "large activation → important," but what about a channel with large activations yet near-zero gradients? No matter how large, it has no effect on the loss.

** 2026 | Activation Sensitivity: Unifying the Two Lines

*** What problem did it see?

By early 2026, AWQ and GPTQ had each established their own camp, trading blows in the literature. But *neither could articulate what it was approximating.* SmoothQuant used yet another vocabulary. The relationships among OBS, OBD, Fisher information, and the modern methods were murky.

What Xu identified was not that any one algorithm was wrong, but that the core concept of "importance" had splintered into several disconnected fragments across the literature.

*** Its solution

Return directly to the Taylor expansion — but this time expand simultaneously in *both weights and activations*. For a linear layer =Y = XW=, the expected effect on the loss of perturbing channel j is:

#+begin_example
   alpha_j = E[ || G^T * X_{:,j} ||^2 ]
#+end_example

where G is the gradient of the loss with respect to that layer's output. This quantity is called *activation sensitivity* — it simultaneously encodes "how large is this channel" (the X term) and "how much does a perturbation here propagate to the loss" (the G term).

Equivalently, =alpha_j = (X^T D X)_{jj}=, where =D = diag(||G_{i,:}||^2)=. That is: the diagonal of the *gradient-weighted input covariance matrix*.

Now the magic:
- Assume the gradient is isotropic (=D ≈ I=): sensitivity reduces to =E[||X_j||^2]= — that is precisely *AWQ*.
- Assume all samples contribute equally to the loss: sensitivity reduces to =(X^T X)_{jj}= — that is precisely *GPTQ*.

Two seemingly different methods are, under the higher-order "gradient-weighted activation norm" framework, two approximations of the same quantity — each discarding different pieces of information.

*** Why it worked (and what hole it left)

It elevates "importance" to a universal position: all these methods compute the same underlying quantity; the differences lie only in which simplifying assumptions you are willing to make.

This paper *proposes no new algorithm* and runs no experiments. Its value is diagnostic: it tells you that if you write a new PTQ method, you should first ask — which approximation of sensitivity does it correspond to? What information does it use that AWQ and GPTQ do not?

The obvious new hole: the sensitivity formula contains the gradient G. Part of the reason AWQ and GPTQ became popular is precisely that they require no backpropagation. Using sensitivity honestly means running gradients during calibration — an additional engineering cost.

* Frontier Extensions

Before this 2026 synthesis paper appeared, the quantization field had already branched down another road — *rather than carefully selecting which weights to protect, first make the structure of activations simpler.*

** 2024 | QuaRot / SpinQuant: Rotate the Outliers Away

The logic of this line is as follows: AWQ and GPTQ both accept the premise that "activation distributions are hard to quantize (outliers exist)" and try to minimize the damage. But what if outliers never appeared in the first place?

QuaRot inserts a fixed Hadamard rotation R and its inverse R^T before each linear layer. Mathematically this is an identity transformation, but the rotated activation distribution is "spread out" — outliers that were concentrated in a few channels are uniformly distributed across all channels. Quantization then becomes much more benign.

SpinQuant takes this further by making R a *learnable* orthogonal matrix, optimized on the Stiefel manifold to directly minimize the post-quantization loss. Meta incorporated it into the official quantized releases of Llama 3.2.

These two lines are complementary to the sensitivity framework of this paper: rotation makes the activation distribution friendlier, reducing variance in sensitivity across channels; this paper gives you the tools to reason about how to handle whatever differences remain.

* Insights

Compress the entire arc into one sentence: *thirty years of PTQ progress have consisted of continuously stuffing more signal into the concept of "importance."*

- OBD stuffed in second-order curvature
- OBS stuffed in weight coupling
- OBQ/GPTQ stuffed in engineering feasibility
- AWQ stuffed in activation magnitudes
- This paper stuffs in gradients (loss sensitivity)

Every step is not a revolution but an *addition of a term*. Early terms were local (curvature, coupling); later terms are data-dependent (activations, gradients). This trend toward increasing data-dependence is what distinguishes PTQ from classical pruning — in the large-model era, you cannot discuss importance without calibration data.

A deeper observation: the reason this 2026 paper could be written at all is that AWQ and GPTQ had both matured to the point where they *needed to explain each other*. When two parallel mature methods leave the community confused about "what are these things actually computing?", a unification paper is due. This is a characteristic rhythm of scientific development — a period of flourishing diversity, followed by consolidation.

Where does the field go next? Two directions:
1. *Use sensitivity explicitly*: replace the approximation terms in AWQ/GPTQ with the full =E[||G^T X_j||^2]=. This should yield visible gains at low bit-widths (W3, W2). The cost is that calibration must compute gradients.
2. *Merge with rotation methods*: first use QuaRot/SpinQuant to rotate activations into a smoother distribution, then apply sensitivity-based channel selection in the new coordinate system. Two complementary dimensions stacked together.

* Lessons

This line of work offers a few direct lessons for how to do research:

1. *Read the old papers first* — OBS is from 1993, yet in 2022 it was resurrected as the mathematical skeleton of OBQ and GPTQ. Many techniques that appear novel in the LLM era have roots in pruning papers from 30 years ago. Before building a new method, trace it back to its origin.

2. *Unification papers have real value* — this paper proposes no algorithm and runs no experiments; it contributes one formula and two derivations. Yet its value is comparable to an experimental paper, because it lets the community see the terrain clearly. When you observe a field with two or three methods that "everyone uses but no one can say which is more correct," that is an invitation to write a unification paper.

3. *Judging importance always requires looking downstream* — OBD taught the 1990 generation: "looking at weight magnitude alone is not enough; look at the weight's effect on the loss." Thirty-five years later, the same lesson replayed in LLM quantization: AWQ taught the 2023 generation: "looking at weight magnitude alone is not enough; look at the activation it receives." This paper teaches the 2026 generation: "looking at the activation alone is not enough; look at the activation multiplied by the gradient." Every generation relearns the same thing: *importance is an end-to-end concept that must be traced back from the loss.*

This means you can — in any "selection" problem you face (which samples matter, which features matter, which data to clean) — write down the end-to-end loss-sensitivity formula first, and then ask which term of it your heuristic is approximating. If you can write it down, you naturally know what you have discarded. If you cannot, you are probably assembling intuitions rather than reasoning from first principles.
