* The River of Problems

When an LLM tries to read a 170,000-word novel, an entire codebase, or a long meeting transcript, what stalls it is not compute — it is memory. Every token must store its Key and Value in GPU memory so later steps can look back; the longer the sequence, the more is stored, until the GPU simply runs out.

This gave rise to *KV cache pruning*: discard the "unimportant" tokens, keep only the critical ones. The hard question is — *how do you know which ones matter*?

Why is it hard?

- Aim for accuracy: let the target large model run through the sequence again and see which tokens actually receive attention. Cost: prefilling time doubles — you saved memory but paid it back in latency.
- Aim for speed: guess with heuristics (attention sink, recency window, cumulative scores). Cost: the guesses are often wrong, and performance collapses on long-context tasks.

For years, generation after generation has charged at this same question: *how do you estimate "which tokens matter" accurately enough, with as little compute as possible*? ProxyKV rewrites the question as: *can a small model guess on behalf of the large model, while both run concurrently, without blocking each other*?

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Transformer: Attention Leaves a Cache Behind

To avoid recomputing the attention of all previous tokens every time a new token is generated, the Transformer caches each layer's and each head's Key and Value during decoding. Typical sequence lengths at the time were a few hundred tokens; the cache was a few megabytes — no problem.

Nobody anticipated that eight years later people would ask the model to read a 170,000-token codebase.

*** The Hole It Left

KV cache memory grows *linearly* with sequence length. On a 128k-context Llama-3.1-8B, the KV cache alone consumes tens of gigabytes — exceeding the physical VRAM of a single H100. The problem was born: *something has to be discarded*.

** 2023 | StreamingLLM: Don't Keep Everything — Just the Start and the Recent

The first instinct was a sliding window: keep only the most recent N tokens. Performance collapsed — perplexity exploded.

Why? The Xiao team discovered *attention sinks* — the model inexplicably pours a large portion of its attention onto the very first tokens in the sequence (even semantically unrelated ones). This is a side effect of softmax normalization: scores must sum to 1, so when there is "nothing worth attending to," the probability mass gets dumped on the beginning. Once the beginning is discarded, the distribution collapses.

*** The Fix

Keep the initial 4 tokens + a recent sliding window. Both ends fixed; everything in between discarded.

*** The New Hole It Left

Tokens in the middle that genuinely carry semantic content are thrown away wholesale. Long-document QA, multi-hop reasoning — the critical information for these tasks is often buried in the middle. Position-based heuristics are too blunt.

** 2023–2024 | H2O / SnapKV: Pick by Attention Score

If the middle also contains important tokens, don't cut by position — cut by *importance*.

H2O (Zhang et al.) accumulates each token's total historical attention-received score and keeps the high-scorers ("Heavy Hitters"). SnapKV (Li et al.) is more refined: it uses the final segment of the prompt as an "observation window," identifies which earlier tokens that segment attends to, and selects those.

*** The Fix

Use *locally observed attention patterns* as a proxy for future importance. Cheap, because no extra computation is needed — attention scores are already produced during the forward pass.

*** The New Hole They Left

"Local observation" is the critical weak point. SnapKV's observation window is only the last few dozen tokens of the prompt; it cannot see what the subsequent generation will need. H2O's cumulative scores favor tokens that received attention early, leaving long-tail dependencies invisible. On long-context benchmarks (LongBench, RULER), the accuracy gap versus a true oracle is substantial.

** 2025 | KVZip: Let the Large Model Run Again, Get the True Scores

The accuracy ceiling imposed by heuristics is nailed in place — because they are guessing, while the oracle is right there: run the target model once more on the full sequence with teacher-forced prefill, record the true attention distribution, and prune by it.

*** The Fix

No guessing — compute the ground truth directly.

*** The New Hole It Left

The cost is so high it defeats itself. Pruning is meant to make long-context inference tractable; running prefill twice means the latency and memory savings from decode are erased — or worse, negative. The paper calls this the "*scoring-cost vs. accuracy gap*": the left end of the accuracy axis is heuristics, the right end is KVZip, and between them is a cliff.

** 2025 | IAM / SmallKV: Let a Small Model Guess for the Large One

Zhao et al. observed a key fact: within the *same model family* (e.g., Llama-3.1-8B and Llama-3.1-1B), the attention pattern of each layer and each head is highly correlated — whatever the small model attends to, the large model most likely attends to as well.

So why pay for the expensive large model to compute the oracle? Run the cheap small model for attention, then *transfer* the scores to the large model.

*** The Fix

A fixed head-to-head, layer-to-layer hard mapping: small model head_i score = large model head_i score.

*** The New Holes They Left

Two of them:

1. *Architecture-mismatch deadlock*: Llama-3.1-8B has 32 heads; the 1B version may have only 16. A hard mapping either wastes the small model's heads or fails to cover all of the large model's heads. Switch to a different model pair and the mapping rules must be redesigned from scratch.
2. *Still on the critical path*: Even though the small model is fast, its forward pass and mapper computation are still serialized before the large model's prefill — the large model must wait for scoring results before it can begin pruning and decoding. Speedup is limited.

** 2026 | ProxyKV: Soft Mapping + Asynchronous Concurrency

ProxyKV solves both outstanding problems at once.

*** The Fix

The "small model as stand-in" idea from IAM/SmallKV is retained, but with two fundamental redesigns:

1. *HybridAxialMapper*: no longer hard-binds head-to-head; instead learns a cross-axis mapping in three stages:
   - 1D Conv extracts temporal-axis features (each head's attention evolution along the sequence)
   - Transformer encoder adds context along the time axis
   - A set of learnable *target queries* pull and recombine features from the small model's head set into the shape of the large model's head set via cross-attention
   This way, regardless of how many heads the small or large model has, the mapping can be learned — hard binding becomes soft binding.
2. *Multi-Granularity Hybrid Loss*: prior alignment losses regressed the small model's scores onto the large model's scores (MSE-style). But the pruning task does not care about the *exact score values* — it cares about *who lands in the Top-K*. The loss is therefore reformulated as multi-granularity ranking consistency (Top-K binary, intra-Top-K rank, global mass rank, cosine, and others — five terms in total). The learning objective aligns with the downstream task.
3. *Asynchronous pipeline*: the small model and mapper run on an independent CUDA stream (single GPU) or a separate GPU (dual GPU), *concurrently* with the large model's prefill. The constraint: =t_score ≤ t_prefill= — as long as scoring finishes before the large model's prefill does, scoring costs nothing extra. The small model's KV cache is released immediately after use; the peak memory overhead is brief and confined to the prefill phase.

*** Why This Works

The three moves reinforce each other:

- Soft mapping makes "cross-architecture" a system-level capability rather than something hand-tuned per model pair.
- Ranking loss sidesteps the common trap of "scores are accurate but Top-K is wrong."
- Asynchronous concurrency turns scoring time from an *additive* cost into a *max* — this is the root cause of the qualitative shift in the performance curve. The 3.21× prefill speedup comes primarily from here, not from optimizing any single component.

*** The New Holes It Left

- *Intra-family constraint*: a same-family small model must be available. For a brand-new architecture with no smaller sibling, there is nothing to use.
- *Scoring-time budget*: if the target model is already very small (say 1B), there is little room to exploit the budget constraint.
- *Training cost*: a mapper must be trained for each (proxy, target) pair. Cheaper than KVZip, but still a deployment barrier.

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026, ProxyKV is freshly posted on arXiv — one of the most recent works on this line. Directions that may push further along the same thread:

- *Unsupervised cross-family alignment*: remove the "intra-family" constraint and pair any small model with any large model — requires more general representation alignment.
- *Dynamic budget*: the retention ratio ρ is currently a fixed hyperparameter; making it query-difficulty-adaptive could squeeze out further speed.
- *Orthogonal stacking with quantization / MoE*: ProxyKV answers "which tokens to keep"; quantization answers "how many bits per token." The two are composable. Quark/MXFP4 quantization + ProxyKV pruning is a natural combination.

A concurrent work worth watching: KVzap (Jegou & Jeblick, 2026) takes a different path — using a per-layer surrogate embedded inside the large model's hidden states to predict oracle scores directly. The ProxyKV paper explicitly excludes it as a baseline (training cost is high; protocols are incomparable), but it represents a clear architectural fork: "surrogate embedded inside the target" vs. ProxyKV's "surrogate externalized and async."

* Insights

On the surface this line of work is about "how to discard KV cache entries." At its core it is about *a more general phenomenon in inference systems*:

*When a computation is both expensive and necessary, the first instinct is to avoid computing it (heuristics); the second is to find a cheaper surrogate to compute it (proxy); the third is to pull it off the critical path and let it run concurrently elsewhere (async).*

KV pruning has evolved through almost exactly this sequence:
- StreamingLLM / H2O / SnapKV = don't compute (replace with heuristics)
- IAM / SmallKV = let someone else compute (small model as stand-in)
- ProxyKV = let someone else compute it somewhere else simultaneously (pulled off the critical path)

This "compute → offload → async" pattern is not unique to KV pruning. Speculative decoding (draft model asynchronously producing tokens), prefetching (asynchronously loading the next segment) — both share the same structure: *turning latency from addition into max*. Any inference bottleneck will eventually be cracked open this way.

Looking one step further: after the critical path is decoupled, the next frontier is *whether the surrogate itself can learn to generalize* — not retraining a mapper for every model pair, but a mapper that possesses cross-architecture generalization and can handle unseen (proxy, target) pairs out of the box. That pushes "system optimization" into "universal alignment."

* Takeaways

- *When looking for bottlenecks, don't only ask "how to compute this faster" — ask "can this be computed somewhere else entirely"*. The biggest leap across the whole KV pruning field was not any algorithm getting faster; it was ProxyKV pulling scoring off the critical path.
- *Misalignment between training objective and downstream task is easily overlooked*. MSE looks standard, but the downstream need is correct Top-K, so a ranking loss is the right choice. Always ask: is what I am training for the same thing as what I actually care about measuring?
- *Hard constraints (fixed head mapping) often mistake "implementation convenience" for "correct modeling"*. Replacing them with learnable soft alignment frequently unlocks an entirely new class of scenarios at once.
- *To identify a paper's true contribution, look at which couplings it breaks*. ProxyKV breaks three: the scoring–prefill time coupling, the proxy–target architecture coupling, and the loss–task coupling. Each decoupling is a source of order-of-magnitude improvement.

The practical landing point: the next time you design an LLM inference optimization scheme, start by drawing a "time vs. accuracy" scatter plot with existing methods plotted on it, find the cliff, and then ask whether "proxy + async" can fill it in. That is the genuinely transferable methodology ProxyKV leaves behind.
