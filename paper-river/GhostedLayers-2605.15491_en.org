* The River of the Problem

Once you carve out several layers from a large model, the remaining layers suddenly find themselves lost — they expected to receive features already processed by the removed layers, but instead get raw activations straight from the layer above, and the two simply do not match. This river has been circling the same question for two years: *how do you patch up that "misaligned interface" without retraining?*

The difficulty is real: removing whole layers is structurally clean (no sparse operators needed, no special kernels), but the cost is that the mismatch at the boundary propagates and amplifies through every layer that follows. If you rely on retraining to fix it, you lose the "training-free" advantage; if you do nothing, the model collapses into junk. Generation after generation has hunted for a middle path — something cheap enough to be practical, yet expressive enough to actually close the gap. The simpler the patch, the better — as long as it really works.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2024 | Gromov et al. (Unreasonable Ineffectiveness): Deep Layers Are More Redundant Than Anyone Thought

*** What Problem It Saw

Everyone had been trying to shrink LLMs, and the mainstream route was unstructured pruning (the SparseGPT / Wanda family) — score every individual weight, zero out the small ones. But that path had an awkward catch: zeroes are zeroes, yet at deployment you still need specialized sparse-matrix kernels to actually speed things up. A smaller model, still demanding bespoke hardware.

*** Its Solution

A completely different angle: instead of landing a thousand scattered blows at the weight level, land one clean cut at the layer level. Simply remove entire decoder blocks from the transformer; the remaining model has exactly the same architecture as before and runs on any standard inference stack. Their finding: many deep blocks have negligible impact on the final output — drop them and a small LoRA fine-tune recovers most of the performance.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Gap It Left

The intuition is that deep transformer blocks are already doing "fine polishing"; each one contributes only marginally at the margin, so removing a single block causes less disruption than expected. But two gaps remained: first, LoRA is still required — not truly training-free; second, the paper never fully explained "what happens to the next layer when it suddenly receives the unprepared original input" — that mismatch was glossed over by LoRA rather than addressed head-on. The next generation would first need to clarify *which* layers to cut.

** 2024 | ShortGPT: Quantifying "Which Layer to Prune" as a Score

*** What Problem It Saw

Gromov's work demonstrated that layer pruning was viable, but "which layers to prune" relied mostly on manual selection or exhaustive sweeps. For real deployments, you need a metric that automatically identifies unimportant layers.

*** Its Solution

It proposed *Block Influence (BI)*: compute the cosine similarity between each layer's input and output activations. High similarity means the layer changed almost nothing — dropping it hurts little. Prune the lowest-BI layers all at once, with no iteration and no training.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Gap It Left

Cosine similarity is a cheap, directly observable "redundancy signal" — if input and output look alike, the layer really did very little. But ShortGPT focused entirely on *selection*, doing almost nothing about *what happens after the cut*. Dropping the layers and deploying immediately produced painfully large accuracy drops. This forced the next question onto the table: someone had to take responsibility for *recovery*.

** 2024 | LLM-Streamline: The First Work to Treat Recovery as Its Own Problem

*** What Problem It Saw

ShortGPT pruned layers and walked away, leaving no one to handle the accuracy loss. LLM-Streamline was the first to treat "what transformation the pruned contiguous blocks actually performed" as something that needed to be modeled explicitly.

*** Its Solution

A two-step approach. Step one improved layer selection: find a contiguous stretch of blocks whose head and tail activations are highly similar (contiguous is better than scattered because it produces only one boundary). Step two was the key innovation — insert a lightweight substitute block at the pruned position and use distillation to learn the mapping from the pruned segment's input to its output.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Gap It Left

It was the first to clearly state "the pruned segment performs a non-trivial transformation that must be approximated by something." But the substitute block still required training and amounted to a full small network (a nonlinear mapping with activation functions). A wall still separated this from "truly training-free." The next generation would ask: can we replace this with an operator that needs no training at all?

** 2025 | Prune&Comp: The First Truly Training-Free Recovery

*** What Problem It Saw

LLM-Streamline's substitute block still needed distillation. Could recovery itself become training-free — compute something from a handful of calibration samples, insert it, and be done?

*** Its Solution

Reduce recovery to the absolute minimum: multiply the boundary weights by one scalar per channel. These *N* scalars are computed directly from a small calibration batch (no gradients needed), then inserted and left alone. The essence is "scale each channel independently."

*** Why It Worked — and What New Gap It Left

Cheap. But the expressive power is too weak — it can only adjust the magnitude of each channel and cannot handle mixing across channels. For example, if the pruned layers were doing "move information from channel 3 into channel 17," a per-channel scalar is completely incapable of expressing that kind of cross-channel interaction. The next generation would wonder: can we give the patch cross-channel capacity?

** 2026 | ReplaceMe: Absorb the Patch into Weights, Let a Linear Map Do the Work

*** What Problem It Saw

Prune&Comp's diagonal matrix was too thin; LLM-Streamline's small network was too heavy. Could a *full linear matrix* (C × C) approximate the pruned block's computation without adding any inference overhead?

*** Its Solution

Using a small calibration set, fit a linear map *L* to minimize the effect the pruned block had on the MLP output. After computing *L*, *absorb it into the weights of the adjacent MLP* — so at inference time no extra operator is visible at all. Zero overhead.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Gap It Left

The cleverness lay in "absorption"; performance improved substantially over Prune&Comp. But what it repaired was "what the pruned block did to the MLP output," *not a direct alignment of the boundary activations* — it took a detour. The next generation would ask: why not align the problematic boundary directly?

** 2026 | LinearPatch: Insert an Alignment Operator Directly at the Boundary

*** What Problem It Saw

ReplaceMe corrected the "pruned block's computation," but the root problem was really the *mismatch at the boundary activation* — the next layer's expected input and its actual received input did not match. Why not close that gap directly?

*** Its Solution

Insert a linear operator at the boundary whose objective is to minimize the difference between "the activations the next layer expected" and "the activations it actually receives." To keep the operator stable and interpretable, the authors chose a specific structure: *Hadamard rotation + diagonal scaling*. This construction is numerically stable and crosses channel boundaries (rotation mixes channels).

*** Why It Worked — and What New Gap It Left

It was the first to directly align boundary activations — the direction was right. But the "Hadamard + diagonal" construction carries a hard mathematical flaw: *the matrix it produces is inherently symmetric*. The entire C × C space is compressed into a "symmetric subspace." If the true optimal operator has antisymmetric components (quite likely, since residual-stream direction changes are not necessarily symmetric), LinearPatch can never reach that solution. The next generation would ask: why accept this symmetry constraint at all?

** 2026 | Ghosted Layers: Remove the Constraint, Solve for the Global Optimum in the Full Space

*** What Problem It Saw

LinearPatch pointed in the right direction — directly align boundary activations — but imposed an unnecessary straitjacket on itself: the operator had to be symmetric. The authors verified this empirically: on LLaMA-3.1-8B, the antisymmetric component of the optimal linear operator is *significant* — that missing half is precisely LinearPatch's ceiling.

*** Its Solution

Formulate boundary alignment as a least-squares problem: find a matrix *M* such that *X_pre · M* best approximates *X_post*. Solve over the full space R^{C × C}; the closed-form solution is the classic pseudoinverse *M* = (X_pre^T X_pre)^{-1} X_pre^T X_post. This solution decomposes naturally into a symmetric component plus an antisymmetric component — and the antisymmetric part is exactly where LinearPatch can never reach. Compute, insert via a forward hook (weights untouched), done.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Gap It Left

The core innovation is not inventing a new structure but *removing the constraint others had imposed on themselves* — the solution space expands from the "symmetric subspace" to the "full space," and the global optimum is naturally better. Remaining gaps (discussed by the authors): computing the closed-form solution requires inverting X_pre^T X_pre, so numerical conditioning deserves care with large calibration sets; and this is a "local" fix — only the boundary is patched, not the nonlinearity of the pruned block itself. If the next generation wants to push further, they may have to return to "nonlinear patches" — but nobody wants to give up the training-free advantage.

* Frontier Extensions

Ghosted Layers appeared in May 2026 (arXiv 2605.15491) and currently *represents the latest advance along this training-free boundary-alignment line*. Two plausible next directions:

- *Nonlinear patches, while remaining training-free*: use closed-form kernel methods or low-rank nonlinear approximations to extract another layer of expressiveness beyond the antisymmetric component.
- *Joint optimization over multiple boundaries*: each pruning boundary is currently solved independently; the next generation might treat all boundaries as a coupled system to avoid error accumulation across layers.

Neither direction has appeared in print yet — these are speculative.

* Insight

On the surface this line of work is about "compressing LLMs"; at its core it is *a continuous approximation of the question "how simple an operator suffices to patch a complex transformation?"* Every generation makes the same two-way trade-off:

1. *Training cost vs. expressiveness*: Gromov buys strong expressiveness with LoRA; LLM-Streamline buys lightness with distillation; from Prune&Comp onward training is abandoned entirely and calibration data alone drives closed-form computation — but expressiveness steadily falls from full-network fine-tuning down to a diagonal matrix.
2. *What is fixed vs. where it is fixed*: the first half of the timeline keeps modifying the pruned block or its surrounding weights; only LinearPatch pivots to directly repairing the boundary activations. Once the perspective shifts, the problem transforms from "how to approximate a segment of computation" to "how to align two distributions" — a clean least-squares problem.

What Ghosted Layers really tells you is: *predecessors did not miss this closed-form solution — their own parameterization blocked them from seeing it*. LinearPatch chose Hadamard + diagonal for numerical stability and interpretability, and in doing so trapped itself inside the symmetric subspace. When the authors simply solved for the pseudoinverse of X_pre^T X_pre, the constraint disappeared and performance rose. This is a textbook case of "analytical methodology polluted by engineering intuition" — everyone assumed that an unconstrained optimal solution would be numerically explosive, overfit, or uninterpretable; empirically it turned out to be simply better.

Where does the road lead next? Both directions are interesting: push toward nonlinearity (while keeping training-free), or accept that linear has reached its ceiling and shift attention back to *which layers to prune* — since the boundary can now be patched so well, can we prune more aggressively?

* Takeaways

- *First ask: "Am I trapped by my own parameterization?"* The entire novelty of Ghosted Layers is removing the symmetry constraint. Whenever your method searches a subspace and achieves "reasonable" performance, stop — ask what the solution in the full space looks like; you may be paying tuition for an unnecessary constraint.
- *Closed-form solutions deserve to be tried before SGD*: when a problem can be written as least squares, compute the closed-form solution first and use it as a baseline. It gives you two things: a performance ceiling that reflects true optimality, and a precise measure of the cost imposed by any additional constraint. What Ghosted Layers gained was not only a performance improvement but a quantifiable proof of prior methods' limitations (how large is the antisymmetric component?).
- *Breakthroughs = perspective shift + constraint removal*: the two genuine leaps in this river are LinearPatch (perspective shifts from "fix computation" to "fix activations") and Ghosted Layers (constraint drops from "symmetric subspace" to none). Future work that wants to leap forward will likely need another perspective shift — rather than continuing to tighten bolts within the linear-operator framework.
