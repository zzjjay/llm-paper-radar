* The Problem River

One core question runs through this entire river: *Can we store every weight of a large language model using only three values — {-1, 0, +1} — while losing almost no performance?*

Why bother being this frugal? Because models need to run on edge devices — phones, watches, cameras — where memory is scarce. A weight originally takes 16 bits (FP16); compressed to roughly 1.58 bits, the model shrinks by 3–4×, and multiplications can be eliminated, since multiplying by {-1, 0, +1} requires no actual multiply operation.

Where is the difficulty? *When you force a continuous, finely tuned weight into a three-slot bucket, information is inevitably lost.* How do you recover it? For a decade, answers have swung between two extremes:

- One extreme: *train from scratch*. Let the model know from day one that it can only use three values and learn to work within that constraint. Works well, but burns thousands of GPU-hours and hundreds of billions of tokens.
- The other extreme: *post-training quantization (PTQ)*. Cheap, nearly no retraining. But below 4 bits, the model collapses into random noise.

To make things harder, the river bends halfway through: the protagonist switches from Transformer to *Mamba (State Space Model, SSM)*. Mamba propagates information through recursion — each step's state depends on the previous step. Quantization error *snowballs* through that recursion, and all the remedies developed for Transformers fail completely.

What this river ultimately answers is: *how do we compress an already-trained Mamba down to ternary values at minimal cost, without letting the recursion amplify errors into a catastrophe?*

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2023.10 | BitNet: Instead of Compressing After Training, Give It Only 1 Bit From the Start

*** What Problem It Saw

Before BitNet, quantization was always "after the fact": train the model in FP16, then figure out how to compress the weights to 8 or 4 bits. The problem is that the model has no idea it will be compressed later. It distributes information with fine precision across 16 bits; cut that down in one stroke after the fact and those details are gone. 8-bit is tolerable; go lower and the model starts hallucinating.

*** Its Solution

BitNet's idea is direct: *don't wait until after training — let the model know from day one that it can only use 1 bit.* It replaces the standard linear layers in a Transformer with a layer called `BitLinear`. During the forward pass, weights are compressed to {-1, +1} by a `sign()` function (positive becomes +1, negative becomes -1), and the computation uses these binary weights.

But `sign()` is a step function — no slope, no gradient to back-propagate. BitNet uses a trick called the Straight-Through Estimator (STE): the forward pass uses binary weights, but the backward pass pretends the step is a straight line so gradients flow back normally. This lets the model gradually learn, under a "you only get 1 bit" constraint, how to pack information in.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It works because the model has a chance to *adapt* to the constraint rather than being passively cut. BitNet also finds that it follows scaling laws similar to full-precision models — meaning bigger is better.

The pit it digs: weights are limited to {-1, +1} — *there is no way to express "this weight is unimportant and should be zero."* Every weight is forced to take sides, either positive or negative. This ceiling on expressiveness is what the next paper breaks through.

** 2024.02 | BitNet b1.58: Add a Zero — From Binary to Ternary

*** What Problem It Saw

BitNet's binary {-1, +1} has an awkward issue: in practice many weights are naturally close to zero — they are nearly irrelevant. Forcing them to +1 or -1 is equivalent to injecting noise into the model. The model lacks the most basic form of expression: "this connection — just cut it."

*** Its Solution

The fix is so simple it makes you want to slap your forehead: *add a zero.* Weights go from {-1, +1} to {-1, 0, +1}. Three values; each weight carries log₂(3) ≈ 1.58 bits of information — that is where the name "1.58-bit LLM" comes from.

Concretely: a scaling value (absmean — the mean absolute value of the weights) acts as a threshold. Weights whose absolute value falls below the threshold are rounded to 0; the rest take ±1 according to their sign. Unimportant connections are automatically pruned; important ones retain their direction.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

That zero does two things at once: it gives the model the ability to prune, and it turns multiplications into pure additions (multiply by 0 and you skip the operation entirely). BitNet b1.58 *matches FP16 at the 3B scale*, proving ternary is not a toy.

The pit it digs: all of these results rest on "*training from scratch on 100B tokens.*" The ternary approach becomes nailed to the expensive prerequisite of training from zero. And it only applies to Transformers. Whether this can transfer to other architectures, and whether it can avoid training from scratch — both remain open.

** 2024.05 | Mamba-2: A New Protagonist — Recursion Instead of Attention

*** What Problem It Saw

Here the river bends; the protagonist shifts from Transformer to SSM. The attention mechanism in a Transformer doubles the computation every time sequence length doubles (quadratic complexity), and inference requires caching the KV pairs for every past token in memory — the conversation grows more memory-hungry with every turn. For edge devices, this is a disaster.

*** Its Solution

The Mamba family follows the "state space model" approach: instead of attending to all past tokens, it maintains *a fixed-size state* and advances it step by step like an RNN, updating the state with each new token. Complexity grows *linearly* with length; memory is constant.

Mamba-2's contribution is to theoretically prove that "Transformers are actually a special case of SSMs" (Structured State Space Duality, SSD), and to redesign the core layer accordingly — achieving 2–8× speedup over the original Mamba while holding its own against Transformers on benchmarks.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

Linear complexity plus constant memory — naturally suited for edge deployment. But precisely this "recursion" — the state being passed forward step by step — buries a pit that is deadly for quantization: *errors accumulate along the recursion.* The state at step T is the result of all previous states being filtered through the state dynamics layer by layer. Introduce quantization error into the weights and that error does not stay put; it is amplified by the recursion and stirred into the entire sequence history. This pit is not confronted head-on until Bi-Mamba, and especially until this paper.

** 2024.11 | Bi-Mamba: Porting 1-Bit to Mamba (at Staggering Cost)

*** What Problem It Saw

BitNet proved Transformers can be 1-bit; Mamba is more edge-friendly than Transformers — so combining the two into a 1-bit Mamba should be the ultimate answer for edge deployment. No one had successfully binarized Mamba before.

*** Its Solution

Bi-Mamba grafts BitNet's binarization onto Mamba-2: only the linear modules such as input and output projections are binarized (everything else stays at high precision), using `FBI-Linear` layers with learnable scale and bias, combined with STE and autoregressive distillation from a high-precision teacher like LLaMA2-7B. At the 1.3B scale it achieves 48.4% zero-shot average accuracy, approaching full-precision.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It proves that Mamba can indeed be compressed to extremely low bit-widths and remain usable — a genuine breakthrough. But it digs two pits: first, it is *binary* {-1, +1}, without that valuable zero, retreating to the expressiveness ceiling of the original BitNet. Second, and most painfully — it *trains from scratch on 1.26T tokens, burning 5,780 GPU-hours.* To turn an already-trained Mamba into low-bit form requires training from zero all over again — a cost that is prohibitive for most.

** 2025 | Slender-Mamba: Go Ternary — But Still Training From Scratch

*** What Problem It Saw

Bi-Mamba dropped back to binary and lost the zero. Slender-Mamba picks up the lesson from BitNet b1.58: *ternary is correct* — bring that pruning-capable zero back to Mamba.

*** Its Solution

Apply BitNet-style ternary {-1, 0, +1} to Mamba-2 with per-tensor scaling (one scaling value shared across the entire tensor), and train from scratch on 150B tokens.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It connects "ternary" and "Mamba." But it inherits the most stubborn assumption of the entire lineage — *still training from scratch*, with 150B tokens still expensive; and it is only validated on the small 170M-scale model, with coarse scaling (one value per tensor, unable to accommodate variation across channels). The root question — "why must we train from scratch at all?" — remains untouched.

** 2026.06 | Ternary Mamba: If a Trained Model Already Exists, Why Start Over?

*** What Problem It Saw

This paper sees at a glance the expensive assumption shared by all previous ternary/low-bit Mamba work: *they all train from scratch.* Yet the Mamba-2 pretrained checkpoint is sitting right there — why not just start from it, instead of burning hundreds of billions of tokens again? The prior work implicitly assumed "low-bit representations must be learned from zero." This paper bets that "the pretrained checkpoint is already good enough; what remains is only fine-tuning for adaptation."

It also clearly sees the fatal flaw of the PTQ side branch (Quamba, MambaQuant): cheap, yes, but *unable to go below 4 bits* — naïve W1.58 post-training quantization sends perplexity soaring to roughly 13 million, essentially equivalent to random output.

*** Its Solution

Chain the solution together through continuous transformation and you arrive at this paper: *take BitNet b1.58's ternary values + Slender-Mamba's "ternary on Mamba," but replace "train from scratch on 150B tokens" with "start from a pretrained checkpoint and run only 102M tokens of quantization-aware fine-tuning (QAT) + knowledge distillation."* Token budget drops by 1,000×; done in 4 GPU-hours. Three pieces assembled together:

1. *Grouped quantization (grouped QAT)*: each projection layer's weights are divided into blocks of 128, each block computing its own absmean scale independently. Finer-grained than Slender-Mamba's per-tensor (one value per tensor) or Bi-Mamba's per-column, capturing intra-group variation.
2. *Knowledge distillation*: freeze the original FP16 Mamba-2 as teacher; the ternary student learns from both real labels (cross-entropy) and the teacher's output distribution (KL divergence), each weighted equally (α=0.5). Experiments show pure cross-entropy is ~4× worse; pure distillation is ~1.75× worse; the 50/50 split is best.
3. *Fix a newly emergent bug — "zero-ratio collapse"*: see below.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

The key insight is: *starting from a pretrained checkpoint, the model does not need to "learn" a low-bit representation — it only needs to "adapt" to it.* Adaptation is far cheaper than learning from scratch, so the token budget collapses by 1,000×. The result at the 1.3B scale is 48.1% zero-shot accuracy — approaching Bi-Mamba's 48.4% (within the ±0.9pp confidence interval) — at a thousandth of the cost (4 vs. 5,780 GPU-hours).

But this new path of "starting from a pretrained checkpoint" grows its own pit — one that *simply does not appear when training from scratch* — namely *zero-ratio collapse*. If the quantization scaling value is made a learnable parameter, a positive-feedback death loop emerges: the optimizer discovers that increasing the scale reduces the distillation loss in the short term → a larger scale widens the zeroing threshold → more weights are pushed to zero → model capacity shrinks → the optimizer keeps increasing the scale to compensate … until 90.3% of all weights have collapsed to zero, perplexity hits 47.45, and the model is ruined.

The paper's fix is elegant: *don't make the scale learnable — recompute it as absmean on every forward pass.* This creates a self-regulating equilibrium: if the zero ratio rises, the absmean of the remaining nonzero weights falls, the threshold narrows, and fewer weights are zeroed out. The system stabilizes at roughly 26% sparsity and perplexity 11.25. A counterintuitive result: "non-learnable" turns out to be ten times better than "learnable."

The paper also explains why Transformer post-hoc remedies all fail when moved to Mamba: in a Transformer, attention is a weighted sum of value vectors, and quantization errors are approximately additive and independent, so linear corrections (Kalman gain, James-Stein shrinkage, LREC, QEP, etc.) can patch things up after the fact. But Mamba's recursion stirs errors into the entire history of the state; the error at step T depends on the full sequence history filtered through the state dynamics — it is "structural and history-dependent," not "additive and independent," and *no pointwise function can disentangle it.* The paper empirically confirms that these Transformer techniques monotonically worsen on Mamba, or are simply inapplicable (e.g., sigma-delta noise shaping requires intra-group smoothness TV<L1, but Mamba's measured TV/L1=1.421 exceeds the Gaussian baseline of 1.414 — the condition is not met).

* Frontier Extensions

As of this writing (2026-06), Ternary Mamba is itself the latest development along this "low-cost ternary SSM" line, published in June 2026. It shifts the river's focus from "can we achieve low-bit?" to "can we achieve low-bit *cheaply?*"

Two explicit gaps left open for future work:

- *Inference kernels have not caught up.* This paper proves that a ternary Mamba can be trained and that its accuracy holds, but the custom CUDA kernels — like those in TernaryLLM that achieve 5–8× GEMM speedup — have not yet been adapted to the projection layer shapes of Mamba-2. In other words, *the theoretical memory and compute gains have not yet been fully realized in hardware.* This is the most immediate next step.
- *Theoretical boundaries of zero-ratio collapse.* "Non-learnable absmean self-regulation" is an elegant engineering fix, but whether stabilizing at 26% sparsity is optimal, and whether the sparsity level can be actively controlled to trade for higher compression ratios, are open questions.

* Insights

Viewing the entire river as a whole, the surface story is a technical progression of "ever lower bits, ever lower cost," but underneath lie *three cognitive shifts*:

1. *From "compress after" to "compress during"* (BitNet): quantization is not a cleanup task after training but should be part of training itself. Let the model know the constraint in advance and it will adapt to it.

2. *A constraint can itself be a capability* (the zero in BitNet b1.58): giving the model a "go to zero" option looks like a restriction but actually grants it the freedom to prune. The key to expressiveness sometimes lies not in "more slots" but in "precisely that one slot that can express 'nothing'."

3. *From "learn from scratch" to "adapt what already exists"* (Ternary Mamba) — this is the deepest leap. Everyone before assumed "low-bit representations must be learned from zero." This paper punctures that assumption: *in a well-trained model the knowledge is already there; switching numerical format is just asking it to change clothes, not to be reincarnated.* The cost of adapting can be three orders of magnitude lower than the cost of learning.

Running as a dark thread through the entire river is: *architecture determines how errors propagate, and how errors propagate determines what remedies you can use.* Transformer errors are additive and independent, so linear corrections work; Mamba's recursion makes errors structural and history-dependent, rendering that entire toolkit useless. This reminds us — *quantization has never been a universal tool that is agnostic to architecture; it must mesh with how information flows through the architecture.*

Where is the river most likely to flow next? *Software-proven gains must be realized in hardware.* The algorithm has demonstrated that ternary Mamba is small, accurate, and cheap, but without matching inference kernels those gains remain confined to the paper. The next stretch of this river is almost certainly "algorithm–kernel co-design" — connecting training methods like Ternary Mamba with custom kernels like those in TernaryLLM, so that edge devices can actually run the result.

* Takeaways

The biggest methodological lesson from this evolutionary line — specifically from the leap that Ternary Mamba makes — is: *it invented nothing new; it questioned a premise that no one had questioned.*

All five preceding papers were competing on "who learns the low-bit representation best." Nobody asked "why do we have to learn it from scratch at all?" The entire force of this paper comes from taking the unexamined assumption — "train from scratch on 150B tokens" — and turning it into a question: "we already have a trained checkpoint; why wouldn't we use it?" Once that question is asked, cost collapses by 1,000×.

The implication is practical: *on any mature research line, look first for the assumption that "everyone does this, but nobody has articulated clearly why it must be done this way."* The more mature the line and the more people involved, the more valuable those collectively unexamined premises become — precisely because they have never been scrutinized. The cheapest innovation is often not addition (one more trick) but subtraction (remove an unnecessary assumption).

A second actionable takeaway: *when you remove an old assumption, watch for the new pit that the old assumption was quietly blocking.* Training from scratch would never produce zero-ratio collapse; it is precisely the new combination of "pretrained checkpoint plus learnable scaling" that creates it. Every new path has new pitfalls — whether you can quickly identify and fix them (here: replacing learnable scaling with non-learnable absmean self-regulation) determines whether your new idea actually lands.
