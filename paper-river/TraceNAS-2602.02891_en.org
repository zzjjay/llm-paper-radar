* The Problem River

A large model has tens of billions of parameters. You want to cut it in half — but which half?

This question has gone unsolved for over thirty years. First it was a few-megabyte network where you picked which connections were useless. Then it became a hundred-million-parameter CNN where you chose which filters were redundant. Today it is a hundred-billion-parameter LLM where you decide which attention heads and which MLP dimensions can be discarded. Scale has multiplied ten-thousandfold, but the core tension has never changed:

- To prune accurately, you need to "see the whole picture" — what is the downstream effect on the model's final behavior when this cut is made?
- To see the whole picture, you need to "actually run it" — train each candidate configuration and compare.
- But LLMs are so large that "train and compare" is computationally out of the question.

So every paper on this line over the past thirty years has maneuvered in the same narrow gap: either sacrifice accuracy for cheapness (local heuristic scoring), or sacrifice cheapness for accuracy (prune-while-training). What TraceNAS sets out to do is claim both sides at once — no training, yet a global view. It introduces a new concept called *inheritance* to hold that claim together.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1990 | OBD: Second-Order Derivatives Tell You Which Weights Are Least Useful

*** What Problem It Saw

Early pruning was entirely intuitive: prune whichever weights have the smallest values. LeCun argued this was unreliable — a small weight does not imply a small effect on the loss. The weight might occupy a critical position where even a tiny perturbation sends the loss jumping.

*** Its Solution

He applied the mathematical "gold standard": near the optimum, expand the loss in a Taylor series to second order. The increase in loss caused by zeroing out each weight can then be estimated from the diagonal elements of the Hessian matrix. That is a rigorous definition of "importance."

*** Why It Worked (and What New Trap It Set)

It established a paradigm: *pruning = estimating each parameter's contribution to the loss*. Every method for the next thirty years is fundamentally doing this — they differ only in how they estimate it.

But the Hessian has O(d²) complexity (O(d³) to invert). This was already untenable at a million parameters. Once deep learning arrived and d reached hundreds of millions, this path was simply closed off.

** 2015 | Deep Compression: Skip the Hessian, Look at Weight Magnitude

*** What Problem It Saw

Computing the Hessian was infeasible, yet deep networks had to be compressed to run on mobile devices. What to do?

*** Its Solution

Han et al. found that in over-parameterized networks, magnitude (absolute value) correlates strongly with importance. Prune small weights, then retrain briefly, and the model barely degrades. Combined with quantization and Huffman coding, AlexNet could be compressed 35×.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Trap It Set)

Cheap, scalable, and the default starting point for nearly all pruning work from 2015 to 2022. But it buried a land mine: it assumes "large weight = important," which holds for CNNs where activation distributions are relatively uniform. LLMs are different — a small number of "outlier features" carry activations tens of times larger than average, and the corresponding small weights matter enormously. Magnitude pruning kills these features and the model collapses.

** 2023 | SparseGPT + Wanda: "Local Patches" for LLMs

*** What Problem It Saw

After GPT-3, researchers wanted to prune LLMs by half. Applying magnitude pruning directly blew up immediately — perplexity exploded. The outlier problem surfaced for the first time at scale.

*** Its Solution

Two directions:

- *SparseGPT* (Frantar & Alistarh): revives the OBS (sibling of OBD) second-order framework, but applies it only locally per layer — computes a local Hessian from that layer's input features, then finds a sparse weight set that keeps this layer's output as unchanged as possible.
- *Wanda* (Sun et al.): a more aggressive simplification. Importance = |weight| × |activation norm of the corresponding input channel|. One line of code, no backprop, no Hessian.

Both methods allow LLaMA-7B to remain largely intact at 50% sparsity.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Trap It Set)

By being "activation-aware," they partially resolved the outlier problem. But two fundamental limitations remained:

1. *Local optimum ≠ global optimum.* Each layer is independently optimized for reconstruction error, but errors accumulate and interact across layers. A layer that "looks unchanged" may send completely corrupted signals to the next.
2. *Scores are incomparable across layers.* Each layer's scoring scale is different, so there is no way to answer "should I prune layer 5 or layer 25 more aggressively?" — only uniform sparsity across all layers is possible.

Yet the sensitivity of LLM layers varies enormously; uniform sparsity leaves performance on the table.

** 2023 | LLM-Pruner: Pruning Individual Weights Is Pointless — Prune Structures

*** What Problem It Saw

SparseGPT and Wanda both perform unstructured sparsity — zeros scattered randomly through the weight matrices. Theoretically, parameters drop by half; in practice, there is no speedup — GPUs gain nothing from random sparsity patterns.

*** Its Solution

Ma et al. proposed structured pruning: instead of individual weights, prune "coupled groups" — an entire attention head, an entire MLP column, an entire hidden dimension. The remaining model is a dense smaller model that can be deployed directly.

Scoring uses a first-order Taylor expansion to compute a gradient sensitivity score for each structural group.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Trap It Set)

Real speedups. LLaMA-7B compressed to 5.4B and still usable.

But the Taylor expansion is still *local and first-order* — it asks "if I remove this head, how large is the immediate loss impact?" not "what chain reactions will it trigger across the next 20 layers?" The core difficulty of global dependencies remains unsolved.

And the sparsity ratio for each group is still set manually as a uniform proportion.

** 2023 | ShearedLLaMA: Forget Scoring — Let the Model Learn Its Own Mask

*** What Problem It Saw

Every prior approach followed "score → rank → prune." Xia et al. changed the frame: rather than using an imperfect proxy to score, make "what to prune" a learnable parameter and let the model tell you the answer through training.

*** Its Solution

Attach a 0/1 mask variable to each prunable structure and use L0 regularization (hard-concrete relaxation) to push masks toward zero during training. Set a target sparsity; the learned masks converge to the desired number of nonzeros.

After pruning, continue training so the surviving parameters can compensate. After 50B tokens of continued training, ShearedLLaMA-2.7B outperforms a same-size model trained from scratch.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Trap It Set)

For the first time, global structural dependencies were truly accounted for — the masks are learned end-to-end and naturally encode how budget should be distributed across layers. Also the first time *non-uniform* sparsity (thick in some layers, thin in others) was obtained.

The cost: it is expensive. The mask-learning phase is itself a small-scale training run, followed by continued training. The paper shows that capping the token budget at 20B drops performance significantly — it depends heavily on large token counts. Computationally it is roughly equivalent to training a small model from scratch.

** 2024–2025 | DarwinLM / PUZZLE / LPZero / Pruner-Zero: Search for an Architecture, or Search for a Formula

*** What Problem It Saw

ShearedLLaMA entangles search and recovery in one training run, which is why it is so expensive. Can the two be separated — *search without training, train only once at the end*?

*** Its Solution

Two branches:

- *DarwinLM / PUZZLE* follow training-aware NAS: each candidate architecture undergoes lightweight fine-tuning or knowledge distillation before comparison. Search and full recovery are separated, but each candidate still requires training.
- *LPZero / Pruner-Zero* pursue zero-cost proxies: use symbolic regression to search over candidate scoring formulas (various combinations of grad × weight × activation) for the one that best predicts post-pruning performance, then use that formula to score all candidates.

The latter is an extension of zero-cost NAS work from vision (NASWOT, ZiCo) into the LLM domain — finding a proxy that predicts quality without any training.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Trap It Set)

DarwinLM reduced cost somewhat but still trains every candidate. LPZero/Pruner-Zero gained speed, but the proxy design has a fundamental flaw:

These methods inherit vision NAS proxies (NASWOT/ZiCo/SynFlow/GradNorm), which measure *trainability* — "if we train this architecture from random initialization, will it converge well?"

But in LLM pruning, the model is not trained from random initialization — it inherits the weights of an already-trained large model. What we care about is not "can it be trained?" but "does it resemble the original model and how easily can it recover?" These are completely different questions.

Using a trainability proxy to select a pruning architecture is pointing in the wrong direction. Pruner-Zero shows high correlation on MMLU but breaks down on PPL — a model that recalls common knowledge but speaks incoherently is precisely the symptom of this misalignment.

** 2026 | TraceNAS: Redefine the Proxy — Not "Can It Train?" but "Does It Resemble?"

*** What Problem It Saw

Malettira et al. state the problem on this line directly: every prior zero-cost proxy has been measuring the wrong thing. The essence of LLM pruning is *inheritance*, not *trainability*. A good pruning candidate is not one that "can learn something from a fresh initialization" but one that "already stands inside the original model's convergence basin and needs only a small nudge to return to the original model's performance level."

A new proxy is needed that directly measures this.

*** Its Solution

The inspiration comes from TracIn (Pruthi 2020), a tool that uses gradient traces to track which training examples contribute to a final prediction.

TraceNAS inverts this idea:

1. On a small calibration dataset, record the gradient directions of the original model at each step. This is the original model's "gradient trace."
2. For each candidate pruning architecture (no training — use the original weights' subset directly), record its gradient trace on the same data.
3. *Compute the directional alignment between the two traces* (using a scale-invariant correlation metric).

High alignment = the pruned model's loss landscape is still roughly parallel to the original's = it still sits inside the original model's convergence basin = a small amount of fine-tuning will recover it.

Use this alignment score as fitness and run a NAS (evolutionary algorithm) to search for a non-uniform pruning scheme across depths and widths.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Trap It Set)

Three things that are finally "right":
- *Right direction*: directly measures inheritance, no longer borrowing a trainability proxy.
- *Right scale*: the scale-invariant alignment metric makes scores comparable across layers and structures, enabling genuine cross-layer budget allocation in the search for the first time.
- *Right efficiency*: each candidate requires only one forward-backward pass over a calibration set of 16K tokens. 15,000 candidates plus subsequent 20B-token recovery training completes in 8.5 hours on a single GPU — roughly 10× cheaper than ShearedLLaMA or DarwinLM.

On Llama and Qwen, TraceNAS matches training-aware methods with only one quarter of their token budget.

New traps: the method depends on a calibration set, and the choice of set affects results (the domain-bias problem is not fully addressed). Additionally, gradient traces have limited capacity to capture long-context behavior — whether a 16-sequence calibration set reflects the model's behavior at 100K context length is an open question.

* Frontier Extensions

As of the search date (May 2026), TraceNAS is among the newest work on this line, released in February 2026. No published paper explicitly critiquing TraceNAS has appeared yet.

Anticipated directions (speculative, not published work):
- Extending the *inheritance* concept from "gradient alignment" to "hidden activation alignment" or "functional alignment" — gradients are just one proxy.
- The calibration-set domain problem: making the proxy more robust to distribution shift.
- Long-context behavior: all current calibration sets use short sequences; the role of pruned heads/layers in long-context settings is invisible to the proxy.

* Insights

On the surface this line is "iterative pruning-technique improvements." Underneath it is a deeper cognitive shift.

*First layer*: the "importance" of a parameter for pruning has never been an intrinsic property of the parameter itself. OBD uses the Hessian; Magnitude uses the absolute value; Wanda uses activations — everyone is answering the same question "which parameter matters?" but the answers increasingly acknowledge that importance does not live inside the parameter. It lives in the relationship between the parameter and the rest of the model, and between the model and the data distribution.

*Second layer*: a "proxy" is fundamentally a substitution of questions — replacing "will performance be good after pruning?" with "some metric computable at pruning time." Every step along this substitution chain raises the same challenge: does the substituted metric actually measure what drives the final outcome?

TraceNAS's real contribution is not "yet another new proxy." It is that *it deliberately shifts what the proxy measures*: from "mathematical properties of this architecture in isolation" to "the relationship between this architecture and a specific pretrained model." That shift is paradigm-level — because it reframes "pruning" from an independent optimization problem into a problem of *inheritance*. The pretrained model is no longer merely an initialization; it becomes the target that is respected, referenced, and aligned toward.

Where is the next step most likely to go? *Making the proxy itself learnable.* TraceNAS currently hand-specifies the alignment formula (cosine similarity and related measures). If this formula were itself learned via meta-learning, each base model could have its own tailor-made inheritance proxy. LPZero already took a first step (searching over symbolic formulas), but it searched in the space of trainability-type objectives. Connecting LPZero's search framework to TraceNAS's inheritance framework is a natural synthesis.

* Takeaways

If your research habit is "find a popular paper and immediately hunt for improvements around it" — this approach has consistently failed to produce major results on the LLM pruning line, because everyone was improving "local heuristic scoring formulas" and the direction itself was wrong.

Every genuine turning point on this line came from *redefining the problem*:
- SparseGPT redefined "global reconstruction" as "layer-wise reconstruction" — a dimensionality reduction of the problem.
- ShearedLLaMA redefined "scoring" as "learning a mask" — turning a discrimination problem into a generation problem.
- TraceNAS redefined "what the proxy measures" as "inheritance rather than trainability" — swapping out the entire objective.

Practical takeaways:

- *Deploying LLMs*: when compressing an LLM, try TraceNAS-style methods first (zero-cost proxy + non-uniform sparsity) rather than defaulting to Wanda/SparseGPT. Non-uniform sparsity ratios are now achievable — there is no reason to keep using uniform sparsity.
- *Doing related research*: if you want to work on "zero-cost NAS in the LLM era," the key is not to build yet another trainability proxy. First get clear on what "good" genuinely means in your setting — is it trainability, inheritance, or something else entirely? Once that is settled, the technical implementation is secondary.
- *Building thinking habits*: whenever you encounter a "scoring formula" paper, habitually ask — does this formula actually measure what we want, or is it an approximation that has been carried forward unquestioned for years? Very often "changing what is being measured" opens a far larger door than "improving the existing formula."
