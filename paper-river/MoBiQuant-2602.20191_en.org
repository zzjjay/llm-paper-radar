* What Problem This River Is Solving

A large model must serve two very different users at once: one running on a
phone, happy to wait 2 seconds, wanting to save power and memory; another
running on a server, demanding sub-second responses. The catch is that you do
not want to train a separate quantized model for each scenario — that is far
too expensive. The ideal state is: *one copy of the weights in memory, with
bit-width switched on demand at runtime* — 2-bit when speed matters, 8-bit
when accuracy matters, all from the same weight file.

Every paper in this river is chasing that goal, but each step gets bitten by
the same obstacle: when you compress FP16 weights into low-bit integers, a
handful of unusually large values — "outliers" — stretch the quantization
range wide open, squeezing the majority of small values into the same bucket
and wasting all the precision.

Digging backward, you can see how this obstacle was tamed step by step — and
how it grew a new form in the "one set of weights, many precisions" setting.

* Forward Narrative

** Stop 1: GPTQ — Making Post-Training Quantization Work at LLM Scale

Before GPTQ, compressing a ten-billion-parameter model to 4-bit was nearly a
dead end. Classical post-training quantization (PTQ) worked on small models
but fell apart on LLMs — one misquantized weight column turned its error into
a mountain by the time it propagated through later layers.

GPTQ's solution is *greedy column-wise error compensation*: each time a column
of weights is quantized, the resulting error is immediately redistributed onto
the as-yet-unquantized columns, so that future columns "know how much debt has
already accumulated" and adjust themselves to compensate. Mathematically, the
inverse Hessian distributes the compensation weights.

This turned "LLM + PTQ" from impossible into a baseline. But GPTQ addressed
*algorithmic stability*; it did not look at a more fundamental issue: a small
number of very large outliers in the weights and activations were dominating
the quantization range.

** Stop 2: SmoothQuant — Moving Outliers from Activations to Weights

Outliers in activations (the intermediate values computed at each layer) are
far more extreme than those in weights. Some activation channels can be
hundreds of times larger than the median. This means that if you also want to
quantize activations (for faster matrix multiplications), those few outlier
channels will stretch the entire quantization range.

SmoothQuant's insight is elegant: *the matrix product X·W equals (X/s)·(s·W)*,
where s is a diagonal scaling matrix. So you divide activations by s and
multiply weights by s — activations become well-behaved, at the cost of
slightly harder-to-quantize weights. But because weight outliers are much
milder to begin with, offloading some of the activation extremity onto them
leaves both sides quantizable.

This is "problem relocation." The total outlier mass does not decrease; it
simply moves from the harder-to-quantize place (activations) to the
easier-to-quantize place (weights).

** Stop 3: AWQ — Protect Rather Than Relocate

SmoothQuant assumed outliers are spread uniformly, but AWQ noticed that *only
about 1% of weight channels truly matter* — the ones whose corresponding
activation magnitudes are largest. If you can preserve that 1% at higher
precision and aggressively compress the other 99% to 4-bit, model quality
barely drops.

AWQ does not actually store that 1% at a separate higher precision (hardware
would hate that). Instead, it *multiplies those important channels by a scaling
factor* to reduce their quantization error, and inversely scales the
unimportant channels to cancel the effect on the matrix product. The
mathematical output of the matrix multiplication is unchanged, but
quantization error is redistributed to places that affect the output least.

This refined the outlier problem from "global relocation" to "channel-level
fine-grained accounting." But it is still per-channel: an entire column is
either an outlier channel or it is not.

** Stop 4: QuaRot / SpinQuant — Spreading Outliers via Rotation

If outliers always concentrate in a few channels, can they be *scattered*?

QuaRot and SpinQuant introduce a new tool: before quantization, multiply both
weights and activations by an orthogonal rotation matrix R (e.g., a Hadamard
matrix). Because R·R^T = I, the matrix-product result is unchanged, but in the
rotated tensor the outliers that were concentrated in a few channels are *spread
evenly across all channels*.

An analogy: originally 100 cups hold a kilogram of water in one cup and a few
grams in each of the others; after rotation every cup holds roughly the same
amount. With similar magnitudes everywhere, quantization wastes no precision.

This was a major victory in the outlier war. By this point, LLM quantization
at *a single fixed bit-width* was largely solved. The GPTQ → AWQ → QuaRot
line brought 4-bit to near-lossless quality.

But all these methods share one assumption: *the target precision is fixed*. A
rotation matrix and scaling factors tuned for 4-bit are no longer optimal at
2-bit.

** Stop 5: Any-Precision LLM — One Set of Weights, Many Bit-Widths

Why share one set of weights across multiple precisions? Because deployment
scenarios are constantly shifting: peak daytime load demands speed at 3-bit;
low overnight load permits 8-bit for accuracy. Storing a separate copy of the
weights for every precision is memory-prohibitive.

Any-Precision LLM uses *incremental upscaling*: first quantize a 3-bit model,
then treat those 3-bit integers as the "lower 3 bits" and append bits upward
to get 4-bit, 5-bit, … up to n-bit. With one n-bit weight in memory, you read
only as many bits as you need; a bit-plane memory layout makes this
"read-by-bit" efficient.

This step changed *quantization from a one-shot decision into a nested
hierarchy*. But it still treats all tokens identically — at 3-bit every token
goes through 3-bit.

** Stop 6: MatQuant — Integers Are Already Nested

MatQuant pushes "nesting" to its logical extreme. Its key observation: the int8
data type *already has* a Matryoshka structure — the high 4 bits of an int8 are
an int4, and the high 2 bits are an int2. So you do not need to "separately
store" lower-precision models; the lower-precision model *already lives inside*
the higher-precision one.

Its training objective simultaneously optimizes all three slices — int8, int4,
int2 — through co-training and co-distillation, so that the low-precision loss
feeds back as a constraint on the high-precision weight distribution. The
result is that int2 accuracy is 4–7% higher than a standalone int2 model,
because it benefits from the co-optimization with int8.

At this point, "one model, many precisions" looks very clean at the *model
level*. But one thing remains unaddressed: *the variation between tokens*. Does
a simple function word really need the same quantization precision as a complex
technical term?

** Stop 7: MoBiQuant — Outlier Migration and Token-Adaptive Precision

The authors of MoBiQuant watched the per-token quantization error as they
switched to 4-bit, 3-bit, and 2-bit, and noticed something nobody had caught
before — they call it *outlier migration*:

#+begin_quote
At different bit-widths, the "hard-to-quantize tokens" are not the same tokens.
#+end_quote

In other words, the tokens that cause model degradation at 4-bit are a
different set from those that cause degradation at 2-bit. This means that
MatQuant's "one set of weights, uniform precision for all tokens" approach must
always sacrifice something — there will always be a batch of tokens that is
particularly difficult to handle at some precision.

MoBiQuant's answer is a two-step approach:

1. *Many-in-One Recursive Residual Quantization*: a recursive residual structure
   embeds multiple precisions into a single set of weights without introducing
   new scaling factors every time the precision changes (a cost that
   AnyBCQ/MatQuant still pay).

2. *Token-Aware Router*: a lightweight learned router dynamically assigns a
   precision to each token. Simple tokens take the 2-bit path; "sensitive
   tokens" that cross the outlier migration boundary take 4-bit or even 8-bit.

This amounts to carrying the outlier war down to *token granularity*. Previous
methods fought outliers at the level of weight channels and model layers;
MoBiQuant is the first to say "outliers also migrate in the token dimension."

The result is throughput 1.34× higher than the previous any-precision SOTA,
while quality matches or exceeds PTQ baselines tuned for a single fixed
precision.

* What Is Really Happening Beneath This River

On the surface this looks like "quantization becoming ever more refined." But
the deeper transformation is: *outliers have shifted from enemies to be
eliminated into resources to be modeled*.

- GPTQ era: outliers are error sources, held down by algorithmic compensation
- SmoothQuant: outliers are burdens, moved to shoulders that can bear them
- AWQ: outliers are signals, telling us which channels matter
- QuaRot: outliers are a distributional problem, dissolved by rotation
- MatQuant: precision switching is a nesting structure, let the data type carry it
- MoBiQuant: outliers are *dynamic*, they migrate with precision — so assign precision per token

Each step looks at the object "outlier" with *finer resolution*: from static,
global, and in-need-of-elimination "problems," to dynamic, local, and
routable "features."

* Where Does the River Go Next

If outliers migrate in the token dimension, do they also migrate in the
*request dimension*? That is, different users' queries may have inherently
different "outlier profiles" — could precision scheduling be done at the
batching layer?

Another direction: MoBiQuant's router is learned offline. The next step may be
a *dynamically adaptive router* — one that adjusts in real time based on the
current KV cache state, the current batch load, and the current hardware power
budget. That would transform quantization from a "model property" into a
"runtime property" entirely.

* Honest Annotations

- The GPTQ → SmoothQuant → AWQ → QuaRot line of outlier handling is the
  widely accepted evolutionary arc in LLM quantization, with clear citation
  relationships
- The Any-Precision LLM → MatQuant step is a reasonable inference (both pursue
  "one set of weights, many precisions"), but MatQuant's primary baselines are
  OmniQuant + QAT, not Any-Precision LLM. The two are better described as
  parallel explorations rather than a strict critique-and-response relationship
- MoBiQuant explicitly lists AnyPrecision LLM, AnyBCQ, and MatQuant as
  baselines; the critique relationship at that layer is stated directly in the
  paper
- "Outlier migration" is a term coined by this paper; the narrative here
  interprets it as "hard-to-quantize tokens shift their identity across
  precisions" — a reasonable reading based on the abstract, but the precise
  definition should be confirmed against the paper's experimental section
- The "Where does the river go next" section is purely speculative
