* The River of Problems

An engineer wants to run a small model on a laptop — one that understands Japan's
"River and Erosion Control Technical Standards" — offline, with only 8 GB of VRAM,
and whose answers must be able to cite specific clauses. What can be done?

Everyone in this river shares the same underlying question:
*How do you teach a multi-billion-parameter language model a specific domain's knowledge
on ordinary hardware, in a way that it neither forgets nor collapses under compression?*

It sounds like a pure engineering problem, yet every step is blocked by three gates:
- Training gate: the model is too large; VRAM cannot hold the gradients.
- Data gate: domain corpora are small — too much causes overfitting, too little doesn't teach.
- Deployment gate: after training the model must be compressed to a runnable size,
  and compression almost always costs accuracy.

Each gate has been pried open one layer at a time by prior work. This paper invents no
new gate. Instead it pushes all three open at once and asks one plain question —
*when all three must be cleared together, which one breaks first?*

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2018 | BERT Full Fine-Tuning: to use a pretrained model, retrain the whole thing

*** What problem it saw

The vision community had already proven "pretrain + fine-tune" on ImageNet, but NLP
never truly learned how to exploit pretraining before BERT. For each new task, the
options were either to train a model from scratch or to take the entire BERT and update
every layer and every weight.

*** Its solution

Whether layers were frozen mattered less than this: pretrain + full fine-tuning =
one set of weights is enough.
*"One BERT to rule all tasks"* — that was the rallying cry.

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it dug)

Engineers quickly noticed: each new client, each new domain required storing a full
copy of 340 M or more parameters. Ten tasks meant ten models; storage and deployment
exploded.
*Could we store only the "diff," not the whole model?* — that became the starting point
for the next paper.

** 2019 | Adapter Tuning: insert small modules, train only those

*** What problem it saw

A full fine-tuned copy was too heavy. But simply freezing BERT and training an
external classification head couldn't learn deep semantics.

*** Its solution

Insert a small "adapter box" (a bottleneck MLP) between every Transformer layer.
Freeze the backbone; train only these boxes. Each task only adds a few MB of adapter
weights.
*Pack the "diff" into detachable small modules.*

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it dug)

Parameter efficiency genuinely improved — storage pressure dropped by two orders of
magnitude. But two new problems appeared:
1. Adapters are inserted serially, so every layer traverses an extra small box at
   inference time — *latency increases*.
2. During training, the backbone still sits in VRAM at full fp16 — *no VRAM savings*.

The latency problem became the fuse for the next step.

** 2021 | LoRA: instead of "inserting modules," decompose the diff into low-rank matrices

*** What problem it saw

Adapters add a new serial computation path. But the change that fine-tuning actually
needs to make is likely low-rank in nature — updating a large d×k weight matrix W,
the number of meaningful directions of change is small.

*** Its solution

Decompose the update ΔW into the product of two slim matrices: ΔW = B·A,
where B is d×r and A is r×k, with r much smaller than d and k (commonly r=8 or 16).
During training only B and A are updated; at inference B·A can be merged back into
the original weights —
*zero inference latency*, the decisive win over Adapters.

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it dug)

The low-rank assumption fits empirical evidence well: r=8 is often sufficient.
Storage drops from "hundreds of millions" to "a few million" parameters; deployment
becomes friendly.

But one longstanding problem at training time remains unsolved:
to train a 65B model, *the 130 GB+ of fp16 base-model weights must sit in VRAM* —
LoRA only reduces gradient and optimizer-state memory; *it doesn't slim the base*.
Consumer GPUs (24 GB) are still out of reach for large models.

The next paper would compress the base model itself.

** 2023 | QLoRA: quantize the base model to 4-bit during training as well

*** What problem it saw

LoRA slimmed down *what to train* but not *what to load*.
A 65B Llama — just reading the weights into an A100 takes 130 GB;
a single card is completely out of the question.

*** Its solution

Three things at once:
1. Base weights compressed to 4-bit *NormalFloat (NF4)* — a nonlinear quantization
   format designed for normally distributed weights.
2. *Double quantization* — re-quantize the scale/zero-point values themselves.
3. *Paged optimizer* — spill gradient peaks to CPU memory when VRAM overflows.

During back-propagation, gradients "pass through" the 4-bit base to compute LoRA
adapter gradients; the base is never updated. Result: *a 65B model fits on a single
48 GB card*.

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it dug)

NF4 + double quantization compress weights with little accuracy loss;
the paged optimizer distributes VRAM peaks to CPU. For the first time the combination
"consumer hardware + large-model fine-tuning" became practically achievable.

But QLoRA solves the quantization problem at *training time*.
After training the model is back in fp16/bf16 (LoRA weights are typically
dequantized when merged into the base). To actually deploy on a laptop
another round of *inference-time quantization* is needed — usually GGUF Q4_K_M.
*Does stacking two rounds of quantization cause trouble?* — nobody had systematically
verified this.

Nor had anyone systematically asked:
*For a low-resource language (Japanese) plus a low-resource domain (civil engineering
standards), exactly how much data is needed?*

These two open questions are exactly what this paper sets out to fill.

** 2026 | JaSAQ: open all three gates together — training scale, architecture, deployment quantization

*** What problem it saw

Applying QLoRA to Japan's River and Erosion Control Technical Standards, the author
quickly ran into three engineering problems that could not be looked up in any prior paper:

1. *Scale gate*: only a few thousand domain QA pairs exist. Is 1,000 insufficient?
   Will 10,000 cause overfitting?
2. *Architecture gate*: the same QLoRA recipe fed to Swallow-8B (Llama-3 with Japanese
   continual pretraining), ELYZA-JP-8B (Llama-3 with Japanese instruction tuning),
   Qwen2.5-7B (GQA multilingual), and Tanuki-8B (Llama-3 + llm-jp tokenizer) —
   which base is best suited to a Japanese technical domain?
3. *Quantization gate*: after QLoRA training, run GGUF Q4_K_M for deployment.
   How much accuracy is lost?

*** Its solution

No new method is invented. *Rigorous controlled experiments* are run in three stages:

- *Stage 1 — Scale*: train LoRA on Swallow-8B at five sizes n=1k/2k/3k/4k/5k,
  all with identical r=16 configuration. Evaluate with test-set NLL (not training loss).
- *Stage 2 — Architecture*: use the optimal n=4000 found in Stage 1 and run identical
  QLoRA (r=32, alpha=16, dropout=0.05) on four Japanese base models.
  Use Qwen2.5-14B as judge, scoring 0–3 with emphasis on citation of specific clauses.
- *Stage 3 — Quantization*: convert all four fp16 models from Stage 2 to GGUF Q4_K_M,
  re-run the same benchmark, and compare how many points each architecture loses.

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it dug)

Three findings are each direct but had never been stated systematically:

1. *Training loss kept falling, yet test NLL bottomed out at n=4000 (1.127)
   and rebounded +17% at n=5000* — a validation of LIMA's "less is more" principle
   applied to domain adaptation.
2. *Japanese continual pretraining (Swallow / ELYZA) beat multilingual breadth (Qwen2.5)
   across the board* — under the dual constraint of low-resource language + narrow domain,
   a language-specialist base matters more than general-purpose width.
3. *The most counter-intuitive finding*: Llama-3-family models actually *gained slightly*
   after Q4_K_M, while Qwen2.5 (GQA architecture) degraded sharply by −0.280 points.
   The author points to GQA: shared KV heads amplify quantization noise across multiple
   query heads.

The remaining open question is explicit:
*Why does GQA not tolerate Q4_K_M?* Is it KV sharing amplifying noise, or does
GGUF's K-quants mixed-bit strategy treat GQA unfavorably? What remedy should be used?

This is precisely the starting point for the wave of "architecture-aware quantization"
research emerging in 2026.

* Frontier Extensions

This is a new paper from March 2026; forward-looking follow-on work has not yet
settled into clear form. But the "GQA + aggressive PTQ degradation" problem it
identifies maps neatly onto several active lines from 2025–2026:

- *MXFP4 / FP4 microscaling floats*: the MXFP4 format promoted by AMD Quark and
  NVIDIA Blackwell is reportedly more robust than INT4 on GQA models because it
  preserves floating-point dynamic range. If this line of work consistently beats
  Q4_K_M, the degradation curve in JaSAQ will need to be redrawn.
- *Architecture-aware mixed precision*: SpQR and SqueezeLLM proposed long ago to keep
  sensitive layers at 8-bit while compressing the rest to 4-bit. JaSAQ's finding adds
  a new dimension to that idea — *attention mechanism type (MHA vs. GQA)*
  should be a feature in determining which layers are sensitive.
- *Instruction tuning + quantization co-effects*: no published paper has yet
  systematically studied the degradation mechanism of the "QLoRA training →
  GGUF quantization" serial pipeline. JaSAQ is the earliest known empirical work
  that treats this combination as an independent variable.

To be honest: I have not found any later paper that explicitly cites JaSAQ.
The above is a reasonable inference about who is likely pursuing the same problems,
not a documented citation relationship.

         The decade-long progress in "using large models" is essentially
         the gradual disassembly of "ship everything" into
         "ship only the diff," then "ship only the compressed diff,"
         and finally "compressed diff + compressed base + compress again."
         JaSAQ's contribution is the first systematic demonstration that
         the final compression step is not equally survivable by all architectures.

* Insights

Stepping back to view the entire lineage, *every generation of papers is slicing the
same action from a different angle*: moving some part of "using a large model"
from full-precision to lean.

- BERT → Adapter: the fine-tuning diff goes lean
- Adapter → LoRA: the diff goes from serial modules to low-rank matrices
- LoRA → QLoRA: the base model goes lean at training time (4-bit NF4)
- QLoRA → the problem JaSAQ reveals: the base goes lean again at deployment time,
  *but the cost of that leanness begins to couple with architecture*

The deeper shift across this lineage is *the collapse of the universality assumption*.
The first four generations all assumed "compression methods work for any Transformer" —
whether INT8, INT4, or NF4, the theoretical analysis only examined weight distributions,
not attention mechanism design.

JaSAQ is the first paper in this lineage to break that assumption through engineering
evidence:
*"The effectiveness of a compression method depends on specific choices made upstream
in the architecture."*
The KV sharing that GQA introduced for faster inference happens to create an amplification
channel for quantization noise.

This is a fork in the road. Future quantization research can no longer simply state
"my method loses X points on LLaMA" — it must specify
"how much is lost under MHA / GQA / MLA / at different head counts."
*The relationship between quantization and architecture has moved from background
variable to first-class citizen.*

Where does the next step most likely lead? Two directions:
1. *Architecture-calibrated quantization*: every time a new architecture ships,
   the quantization method must provide corrections "for GQA," "for MoE," "for MLA."
2. *Training-time awareness of deployment quantization*: QLoRA's NF4 is training-time
   compression; deployment's Q4_K_M follows a different set of rules. The next generation
   of PEFT methods may well train directly in the deployment target's quantization format,
   closing the loop on the loss that double compression currently incurs.

* Takeaways

For someone building a "low-resource language / narrow domain LLM deployment":
this paper offers not a new algorithm but *a workable engineering roadmap* —
roughly 4,000 carefully curated QA pairs beat 10,000; a Japanese continual-pretraining
Llama-3 base (Swallow) beats multilingual Qwen; for GGUF Q4_K_M deployment prefer
Llama-3-family bases over GQA-family bases.
This means *you can run this recipe on your own laptop tonight*.

For someone doing quantization research:
the era of "universal quantization methods" is ending.
Your next PTQ paper's experiment table *must be sliced by architecture* —
you cannot just run LLaMA-7B and publish; you need at minimum MHA / GQA / MLA coverage.
JaSAQ's −0.280 is an unambiguous warning: cross-architecture generalization is no longer
a free lunch.

For someone making research judgments:
pay attention to the value of *"engineering-empirical papers that surface counter-intuitive
findings."* JaSAQ proposes no new method, claims no SOTA, proves no theorem —
but with a solid set of controlled experiments it puts a previously overlooked coupling
(architecture × quantization) squarely on the table.
Papers like this may not attract impressive citation counts, yet they *change what
subsequent research is required to report* — a form of influence that is longer-lived.
