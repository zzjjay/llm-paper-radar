* The Core Question

Can you take a large model that is good at solving math problems, compress it small enough to run on a phone, and have it remain good at solving math problems?

That single sentence captures the heart of this river. It sounds like an engineering optimization, but it has stumped multiple generations of researchers. The difficulty lies in a fundamental mismatch: model reasoning and model quantization are operating on entirely different ledgers. Quantization cares about "how few bits each weight occupies"; reasoning cares about "not making a single mistake across thousands of steps of chain-of-thought." Shave even a tiny slice of precision from the former, and the latter amplifies the error exponentially. Early intuition said "compress first, fine-tune afterward"; experience taught us otherwise — on math benchmarks, a quantized model doesn't just perform slightly worse, it loses the ability to solve problems altogether.

Everyone working on this river, generation after generation, is answering a different facet of the same question: where does accuracy drop? Once it has dropped, can it be recovered? And if so, by what means?

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Hinton-KD: How a Large Model Teaches a Small One "How It Thinks"

*** What Problem It Saw

The dominant compression approach at the time was to train a small model from scratch. The problem: a small model has limited capacity and, learning on its own, simply cannot become as capable. There was a persistent intuition — a well-trained large model doesn't just know "the answer is 7"; it knows "probability 0.7 for 7, 0.2 for 9, 0.001 for 1." That soft probability distribution encodes judgments about class similarity, carrying far more information than a one-hot label. But nobody had systematized this intuition.

*** Its Solution

Rather than fitting the small model to one-hot labels, have it fit the full output distribution of the large model. Concretely: apply a temperature parameter to the softmax to flatten the distribution, then use KL divergence to approximate it. The small model learns not just "the correct answer" but "the teacher's view of every option."

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Left

Soft labels carry inter-class relationships at a much higher information density than hard labels. But this was 2015, and the subject was small image-classification networks. When the LLM era arrived and the task became "compress a model with tens of billions of parameters," the KD hammer was still there — but nobody had specifically verified whether it was still optimal at extreme low-bit regimes (3-bit, 2-bit). That gap would take a decade to fill systematically.

** 2022 | GPTQ: One-Shot Compression to 4-Bit Without Collapse

*** What Problem It Saw

LLMs are too large; retraining every layer to adapt to low precision (i.e., QAT) is computationally unaffordable. The common approach at the time was round-to-nearest (RTN) — simple and blunt. But LLM weight distributions have a particular property: a small minority of weights have an outsized influence on the output, while the majority matter very little. RTN treats all weights equally, so small errors on critical weights balloon into catastrophic errors.

*** Its Solution

Layer-wise optimal compensation. Frame the quantization error in each layer as a least-squares problem and use a precomputed Hessian matrix as a guide: when quantizing one weight, redistribute its error — along the direction indicated by the Hessian — onto the remaining not-yet-quantized weights. The entire process requires no training, only a single forward-pass calibration over a small dataset.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Left

PTQ became the default entry point for LLM compression: fast, cheap, and nearly lossless at 4-bit. But it has a hard ceiling: compress further to 3-bit or 2-bit, and Hessian compensation can only do so much before severe degradation sets in. Moreover, at this stage nobody distinguished between "reasoning tasks" and "ordinary tasks," because the era of clearly bifurcated "reasoning models" versus "chat models" had not yet arrived.

** 2023 | LLM-QAT: To Push to the Limit, You Have to Train Again

*** What Problem It Saw

GPTQ harvested the low-hanging fruit at 4-bit, but below 3-bit, no amount of PTQ compensation could recover the lost accuracy. The reason: at very low bit-widths, quantization introduces errors so large that no "local optimum" fix can repair them — the entire model must re-adapt to living in a low-precision world.

*** Its Solution

Bring QAT back into the LLM era. Insert fake-quantization operators into the forward pass — quantize weights then dequantize them, so the model feels quantization noise during training — and handle the non-differentiable quantization step in the backward pass using a straight-through estimator (STE). Simultaneously, use the full-precision model as a teacher for knowledge distillation, providing a supervision signal far denser than token-level labels.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Left

The model is no longer "trained, then compressed" but "trained while being compressed," so it naturally adjusts its weight distribution to tolerate low precision. However, LLM-QAT used general-language data and validated on perplexity and commonsense QA — whether this recipe works equally well for complex multi-step reasoning was never tested. The full-parameter training requirement also imposed heavy compute costs, which spawned the "memory-efficient QAT" branch that followed.

** 2024 | EfficientQAT and BitDistiller: The Economical and the Extreme

*** What Problem It Saw

LLM-QAT was directionally correct but prohibitively expensive — full-parameter QAT on a model with tens of billions of parameters exhausted both GPU memory and training time. Meanwhile, another group was asking: can we push all the way to 2-bit or 3-bit? And if so, should distillation be redesigned for that regime?

*** Its Solution

EfficientQAT took a "two-stage, fewer parameters" approach — freeze most parameters and tune only quantization-related components first, then do a global fine-tune — slashing training cost. BitDistiller took a different angle: instead of using a full-precision teacher, use self-distillation (the low-bit version of the same model teaching itself) and apply a confidence threshold to filter which tokens to learn from; it pushed the frontier to sub-4-bit.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Left

These two branches resolved the engineering challenges of QAT for general LLMs. But the outside world had shifted: DeepSeek-R1, Qwen3, OpenAI o1 — a wave of "thinking models" — had appeared, whose core capability is thousands of steps of chained reasoning. The problem: none of the existing QAT or PTQ methods had been specifically validated on these models. A critical bet began to surface: might the low-bit behavior of reasoning models be fundamentally different from ordinary LLMs?

** 2025 | Liu et al. (Evaluation): Quantization Is a Disaster for Reasoning Models

*** What Problem It Saw

The benchmarks in prior PTQ/QAT literature were almost exclusively perplexity, Winogrande, HellaSwag, and similarly lightweight tasks. Nobody had systematically run quantized models on AIME (math competition problems) or MATH-500.

*** Its Solution

Take reasoning models such as R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B and Qwen3-4B, compress them to 3-bit and 2-bit, and compare performance on reasoning benchmarks versus non-reasoning benchmarks side by side.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Left

The results were dramatic. The same 3-bit GPTQ configuration dropped only 1–3% on standard tasks such as HellaSwag and Winogrande, but dropped 11.67% on AIME-120 and 12.80% on MATH-500 — an order-of-magnitude difference. This evaluation paper offered no solution; it simply planted a stake in the ground: PTQ is nowhere near sufficient here, and QAT pipelines must be redesigned from scratch for reasoning models. That observation directly motivated the target paper.

** 2026 | Reasoning-QAT: Dissecting QAT for Reasoning Models Variable by Variable

*** What Problem It Saw

Every prior QAT approach failed on reasoning models:
- RTN initialization + SFT training: at 3-bit, average accuracy drops 35–68%
- Applying RL directly to the quantized model: the model cannot produce valid outputs to yield a meaningful reward signal — complete collapse
- Using general corpora (Wikitext) as QAT training data: reasoning ability cannot be recovered

More fundamentally, the key variables in QAT — training objective (SFT vs. KD), initialization (RTN vs. PTQ), whether to apply RL, and which data to use — had never been isolated in controlled experiments on reasoning models. Every prior method bundled all these choices together and shipped them in one go.

*** Its Solution

Turn each of these four variables into a controlled experiment, yielding four conclusions:

1. *Training objective*: KD significantly outperforms SFT. The hypothesized reason: the soft distribution in KD preserves the teacher's uncertainty about "which step to hesitate on," while SFT's hard labels erase that signal — especially fatal for models that depend on stable, step-by-step reasoning.

2. *Initialization*: PTQ (GPTQ) initialization greatly outperforms RTN initialization. GPTQ's Hessian compensation yields a much lower starting loss, so QAT only needs to "fine-tune" rather than "retrain," improving both speed and final accuracy.

3. *Placement of RL*: Applying RL directly to a quantized model is a disaster (the model cannot even produce valid outputs, so the reward signal is pure noise). But if KD first "warms" the quantized model to a usable state (cold-start), then GRPO can add another +2–3% on top of KD. RL's role is entropy reduction — making the model's outputs more decisive, thereby maintaining stability under quantization noise.

4. *Data selection*: The domain of QAT training data must align with the PTQ calibration data, and it must be reasoning-domain data (e.g., OpenR1-Math, not Wikitext). A mismatch causes the model to first recover, then forget.

Connecting these four conclusions yields the Reasoning-QAT workflow: *GPTQ initialization → KD fine-tuning → Cold-start GRPO*.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Left

On Qwen3-0.6B at 3-bit, MATH-500 accuracy rises from GPTQ's 13.27% to 57.80% (+44.53 points); on R1-Qwen-1.5B at 2-bit, from 3.67% to 55.00%. The paper openly acknowledges it introduces no new algorithm — it merely assembles four existing components in the correct dependency order.

Remaining open questions: (1) Does this pipeline hold at 1-bit or mixed precision (e.g., W2A4)? (2) RL was only validated with GRPO — how would PPO, DPO, or different reward designs compare? (3) The optimal scale and mix ratio of reasoning data is left unanswered. (4) The entire framework assumes a strong full-precision teacher already exists — if the teacher itself is also quantized (end-to-end low-precision training), does the approach still hold?

* Frontier Extensions

This paper appeared in January 2026, so a new wave of responses has not yet materialized. But several parallel exploration directions are visible contemporaneously:

- *ParetoQ (Liu et al., 2025)* / *BitNet (Wang et al., 2025)*: Train in low-bit precision from the very beginning, bypassing the "train then compress" paradigm entirely. If this line succeeds, the entire PTQ-init assumption in Reasoning-QAT is circumvented.
- *KV cache quantization* (KVTuner, AttentionPredictor): Reasoning models generate especially large KV caches due to long chain-of-thought outputs; this is an independent compression battlefield that complements weight quantization.
- *RL directly training a low-bit policy*: Reasoning-QAT established that zero-shot RL collapses, but did not delineate exactly where the collapse boundary lies. Whether reward shaping can be designed to make RL work at even lower bit-widths is an open question.

As of now, "low-bit training for reasoning models" has just been named as an independent subfield. The downstream of this river is wide open.

* Insights

On the surface, this line of work is about "iterating on quantization methods." Underneath, it is about *the quiet substitution of the evaluation target*.

The QAT/PTQ progress from 2022 to 2024 was built on soft metrics like perplexity and commonsense QA — if one token is wrong, the overall score dips only slightly. So differences between methods were small and amenable to gradual iteration. Then reasoning models arrived, and the evaluation target became AIME-style problems where *a single wrong step means the entire problem is wrong*. Methods that had looked "roughly equivalent" suddenly revealed their essential differences: the gap between a method that can handle math problems and one that cannot is 0 versus 50, not 50 versus 55.

This shift matters because it forced a new understanding of what compression actually does: it *injects noise into the model*. Noise tolerance is linear for perplexity-style tasks and exponential for reasoning tasks. This implies that any future "compression method" paper must report two sets of metrics — one general, one reasoning — or it will not be credible.

The deeper transformation is this: QAT is no longer an "algorithm problem" but a *dependency-management problem*. Reasoning-QAT introduces no new formula; it merely says "do A, then B, then C — get the order wrong and everything collapses." This suggests that model compression has entered an "engineering integration" era — the space for single-point innovations has been filled by prior work, and the next breakthroughs come from correct composition and from explaining *why this ordering*.

Where is the field most likely to move next? Two directions: (a) direct low-bit pretraining (bypassing the entire "train then compress" paradigm), and (b) co-designing RL reward shaping with quantization noise — since quantization is noise, should RL explicitly learn a *noise-robust policy*?

* Takeaways

**If you do research**: When a field appears to have "converged" — all papers improving the same set of metrics with ever-shrinking gains — be suspicious. The issue may not be that the field has run out of room; it may be that *the evaluation bench can no longer reveal real differences*. Switching to tasks with sharper right/wrong distinctions (reasoning, long-horizon planning, strict-execution tasks) often re-separates "roughly similar methods" by a factor of ten.

**If you do engineering**: Don't rush to invent new operators. Every component in Reasoning-QAT — GPTQ, KD, GRPO — belongs to someone else. Its value lies in *getting the dependencies right*. This means that in a mature field, "systematic combination + controlled variable experiments" yields more than "single-point novelty." Enumerate all reasonable orderings of existing tools and run them before deciding to build a new wheel.

**If you care about deploying reasoning models**: It is already possible to run a 3-bit reasoning model and retain roughly 90% of its capability. But 2-bit still has a meaningful gap. Practically: in compute-constrained settings (on-device, edge), prioritize the 3-bit + Reasoning-QAT recipe for deploying reasoning models, and treat 2-bit as a target for the coming year — keep a close eye on the "direct low-bit pretraining" branch for that breakthrough.
