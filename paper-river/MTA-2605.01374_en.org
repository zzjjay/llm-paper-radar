* The Problem River

A capable large model has learned how to answer questions. How do you transfer what it knows to a much smaller model?

Where is the difficulty? The most obvious approach is to have the small model copy the large model's final answers. But the large model's power doesn't live in its answers alone — during the reasoning process it transforms token strings into meanings, and assembles meanings into thoughts, layer by layer. Copying only the answers is like asking a student to memorize an answer key without ever learning to reason.

But if you want the student to copy the entire "thinking process," you immediately hit the next wall: what does that process actually look like? Is it the hidden states of each layer? The changes between states? The relative relationships across positions? For a decade, researchers working on this problem have all been circling the same question — *how do you compress a mind that can think into a smaller mind, without losing the thinking?*

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Hinton-KD: Teaching the Small Model to Speak Like the Large Model's Probability Distribution

*** What Problem It Saw

At the time, large models were already showing their strength but were too costly to deploy — slow, expensive, memory-hungry. Training a small model from scratch could never match the large model's performance. And giving the small model only the hard labels (one-hot) from the large model wasted the most precious thing the large model had: even for "almost correct" answers it maintained a reasonable probability distribution, and that "soft" distribution encoded the similarity structure among classes.

*** Its Solution

Have the small model mimic the large model's output probability distribution — not just learning "this image is a cat," but learning "this image is 80% cat, 15% leopard, 3% dog." Technically this means minimizing the KL divergence between the two models' output probabilities, along with a temperature parameter that flattens the distribution so that those originally negligible small probabilities can still be transmitted.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Dug)

The "dark knowledge" in soft labels — for instance, that cats and leopards are more similar than cats and dogs — is absent from hard labels; the small model thereby learns the relational structure among categories.

But the pit is obvious: the method focuses only on the final output layer. The large model's dozen or more intermediate layers, each doing something different, are all compressed into one final probability distribution and discarded. The small model learns "what to answer," not "how to think."

** 2019 | TinyBERT / Patient-KD: Don't Waste the Intermediate Layers Either

*** What Problem It Saw

After BERT arrived, researchers realized that a large model's intermediate layers have distinct responsibilities — shallow layers handle syntax, middle layers handle local semantics, deep layers handle global reasoning. Hinton's approach distills only the final output, which is like compressing a multi-stage production line into a single finished product and asking an apprentice to copy it. The apprentice can imitate the form but not the craft.

*** Its Solution

Align the intermediate layers directly — select a few teacher-student layer pairs (e.g., teacher layer 4 paired with student layer 2) and make the corresponding hidden states and attention matrices as consistent as possible. Patient KD fits against multiple intermediate layers; TinyBERT additionally includes attention maps and the embedding layer in its alignment targets.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Dug)

The student is no longer looking only at the final answer; it is forced to walk the same path as the teacher, stop by stop, so its intermediate representations are closer to the teacher's, and transfer performance on downstream tasks rises immediately.

But two pits remain. First, alignment happens on "frozen snapshots" — it looks at each layer's static state, not at how states flow from one layer to the next. Second, every teacher-student layer pair uses the same alignment loss (MSE or cosine), which amounts to measuring everything with a single ruler.

** 2024 | DistiLLM: Rebuilding KD from Scratch for Autoregressive LLMs

*** What Problem It Saw

The classic KD recipe was designed for classification models (one label at a time). Transplanting it to generative LLMs like GPT — which produce one token at a time — reveals problems: standard KL is unstable at the tails of the distribution, and when the teacher is strong and the student is weak the loss can push the student into a strange corner. Furthermore, during training the student sees only "sentences generated by the teacher," but at inference time it faces "sentences it generated itself" — a distributional mismatch.

*** Its Solution

Two things. First, introduce a symmetrized skew KL — making the loss produce reasonable gradients for both the case where the teacher is high and the student is low, and the reverse, so neither side blows up. Second, on-policy sampling — during training, occasionally let the student generate its own output and have the teacher score it, closing the training-inference distributional gap. DistiLLM-2 (2025) builds on this by adding contrastive signals.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Dug)

It directly addresses the engineering question of "how to distill an LLM," stabilizing performance and narrowing the gap.

But DistiLLM is fundamentally still a token-level output alignment method — it resolves "which distribution to align" and "on which data to align," but does not answer "how deeply into the model's internals alignment should penetrate." The intermediate layers remain in the same "static alignment" paradigm as TinyBERT.

** 2025 | FDD: Learn Not Just the States, But How They Flow

*** What Problem It Saw

FDD (Gong et al.) treats a Transformer's depth as a continuous-time dynamical system — each layer is one frame. From this perspective, all prior methods share a hidden flaw: they align "each frame's image," not "the motion between frames." Two students can look very similar to the teacher at every individual frame while following a completely different — jittery, discontinuous — trajectory.

*** Its Solution

At selected intermediate layers, do two things:
1. *Trajectory loss*: project each layer's hidden state through the language model head into vocabulary space, obtaining a distribution over "what the model would predict if it stopped here." Force the teacher and student to agree on this distribution at every layer.
2. *Derivative loss*: align the first-order difference between consecutive layers' projected distributions — that is, "from layer 5 to layer 6, the direction the teacher's beliefs shifted" must match the direction the student shifts in the same step.

Treat depth as time, and frame distillation as trajectory fitting.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Dug)

For the first time distillation has a "dynamic" concept — the student not only resembles the teacher at the endpoint, but along the entire reasoning path, and downstream performance rises accordingly.

But the pit hides in the "LM-head projection" ruler: every layer is mapped to the same vocabulary space and compared using the same KL. This implicitly assumes that every layer does qualitatively the same kind of thing — yet linguistics and interpretability research have long established that *shallow layers handle morphology and part of speech (Tom is a noun, run is a verb) while deep layers handle compositional semantics (the intent of the whole sentence, who did what to whom)*. Using one ruler throughout forces shallow layers to pursue high-level semantics and deep layers to focus on surface word forms — a poor fit at both ends.

** 2026 | MTA: Words for Shallow Layers, Phrases for Deep Layers, Aligning Geometry Not Predictions

*** What Problem It Saw

MTA directly tears open FDD's "single-ruler" problem: shallow and deep layers do qualitatively different things, so using the same objective for all of them is inherently limiting. Moreover, FDD compares "prediction distributions" — but prediction distributions are a compressed product of passing through the LM head, and they discard the relative geometric structure among representations. Two completely differently organized internal spaces can produce very similar prediction distributions; the distilled student may look nothing like the teacher internally.

*** Its Solution

Two new principles:
1. *Layer-wise granularity selection*: use a syntactic parser (spaCy) to segment sentences into "word spans" and "phrase spans (noun phrases, verb phrases)." Alignment in shallow layers is anchored to word spans — grounding token-level morphology; alignment in deep layers is anchored to phrase spans — grounding compositional semantics.
2. *Align relational geometry, not absolute positions*: at each layer, select a set of spans, compute pairwise distances between spans (attention-weighted cosine distances), and require the teacher and student to agree on this "span relationship map." In other words, what matters is not where the word "cat" sits, but that the distance between "cat" and "red" matches the teacher's. A lightweight hidden representation alignment loss (Hidden Alignment) is added as a safety net.

The whole system is built as a plug-in that can be attached to DistiLLM, DistiLLM-2, or FDD.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Dug)

For the first time, the distillation objective acknowledges that "the model has internal structure and different depths do different kinds of work." Across three teacher-student pairs — GPT-2, Qwen1.5, and OPT — stacking MTA on top of FDD, DistiLLM, and DistiLLM-2 each yields a further gain in ROUGE-L.

Remaining pits: the method relies on an external syntactic parser to identify phrases, which hard-codes a linguistic prior into the distillation target. In domains like code or mathematics, where natural-language phrase structure is absent, this path breaks down. Additionally, the binary split of "shallow = words, deep = phrases" is still coarse — the real layer-wise semantic hierarchy is likely a continuous gradation.

* Frontier Extensions

This paper was posted in May 2026 and represents the latest step along this line as of now. The likely directions ahead include:
- Replacing the hard-coded syntactic granularity with a learnable per-layer granularity — letting the model decide for itself what unit each layer should align to.
- Extending the "phrase relational geometry" idea to non-natural-language domains (code ASTs, mathematical expression trees).
- Merging with on-policy distillation / RLHF pipelines — MTA is currently still offline supervised distillation.

* Insight

On the surface this line of work is about "increasingly elaborate distance functions," but underneath there is a cognitive shift:

*KD is not about imitating outputs — it is about imitating structure.* Every generation's progress came from redefining "what is still present in the teacher that hasn't been transferred yet." Hinton saw that "the output distribution contains dark knowledge." TinyBERT saw that "intermediate layers have distinct roles." FDD saw that "there is flow between layers." MTA saw that "different depths do qualitatively different things, and relational geometry matters more than absolute values."

Looking one level deeper, this line has continually borrowed from *interpretability research*. FDD borrowed the ODE perspective of "depth as time"; MTA borrowed the layer-wise semantic findings from the "BERT probing" literature. Distillation is shifting from a pure engineering problem into a scientific one: first understand how the large model thinks, then isolate the structural aspects of that thinking and teach them to the student.

The next step may not be designing a sixth new loss function, but asking a more primitive question: *does the teacher contain an internal structure that is inherently compression-friendly?* That is — can we shape a large model's representations during pretraining to be "easily distillable," rather than struggling after the fact to align an internal space that was never designed with distillation in mind?

* Takeaways

For researchers: every step along this line was not about inventing a new model, but about *viewing the same object from a new angle*. The Transformer is still the same Transformer; KD is still the same KD — but treating depth as time, treating representations as geometry, treating different layers as operating at different linguistic units: each perspective shift opens a new direction.

This means you can:
- Take any object that has already been studied extensively, and ask "can it be seen as X?" — place it inside a new conceptual framework, and structures that were previously invisible will emerge.
- When working on compression, distillation, or transfer, pause and ask: "Am I aligning surface outputs or internal structure? Am I using one indiscriminate ruler for everything I assume about the internal structure?"
- When choosing what counts as a "signal," notice that *relations* are often more transferable than *absolute values* — this is MTA's cleanest methodological lesson.
