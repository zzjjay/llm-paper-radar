* Reading TurboAngle in Reverse: A History of "Finding the Right Coordinate System" for KV Cache Quantization

One sentence to frame the whole arc: *The evolution of KV cache quantization is the story of researchers moving step by step from "fighting outliers in the original coordinate system," to "change the coordinate system first," and finally to "after changing the coordinate system, the quantization target itself turns out to be uniformly distributed — so just apply the theoretically optimal uniform grid."* TurboAngle is the current endpoint of this arc. It did not invent anything new; it simply picked up the property of angular uniformity that earlier work had dropped on the floor and walked away from.

* First Stop [2024]: KIVI and KVQuant — Fighting Outliers Head-On in the Original Coordinate System

Wind the clock back to early 2024. By then, KV cache had already consumed every byte of GPU memory during large-model inference — the longer the context, the larger the KV cache, dwarfing the model weights themselves. Compressing the KV cache had become a hard requirement.

The most natural idea was simply to quantize the KV tensors to INT8, INT4, or INT2. But the moment you tried, a familiar problem surfaced: KV activations contain *outliers*, scales vary across channels by orders of magnitude, and the distribution is far from a clean Gaussian. Apply a single uniform quantization grid and you either waste bits covering the outer tail of outliers, let the outliers get clipped and destroyed, or drown the signal in channel-scale mismatches.

KIVI's response was *fine-grained treatment*: asymmetric per-channel quantization for the K cache (each channel estimating its own range) and per-token quantization for the V cache, all requiring a calibration pass to estimate statistics. KVQuant pushed the same approach further, adding per-vector quantization and explicit outlier handling to stretch context to 10 million tokens.

*What they saw*: the distribution of KV tensors is hard to handle directly, so you must do careful per-channel and per-vector parameter estimation.

*What they left behind*: mandatory calibration data and per-model tuning. Every new model requires another calibration run. In edge deployment, frequent model iteration, or serving a zoo of fine-tuned variants, calibration is a real burden.

And fundamentally, all that fine-grained engineering is *wrestling with the shape of the data in the original coordinate system*. What if you stepped back and asked: instead of fighting in the original coordinate system, can we just change it?

* Second Stop [2025-02]: PolarQuant — The First Coordinate-System Change, but Only Halfway

PolarQuant was the first work to seriously ask "can we change the coordinate system?" It did two things:

1. Added a *random preconditioning* step — essentially multiplying the input by a random matrix to break the alignment structure of the original coordinates.
2. Decomposed each pair of consecutive coordinates (y_{2i}, y_{2i+1}) into *polar form*, obtaining a radius and an angle, then quantized them separately.

Why is this idea interesting? Because radius and angle are *orthogonal degrees of freedom* — radius captures "energy," angle captures "direction." If you can handle them independently, you can allocate bits to each in a targeted way.

But PolarQuant did not finish the journey. The problem was its random preconditioning: after its rotation, the angle distribution is *concentrated*, not uniform. A concentrated distribution requires a non-uniform quantizer — specifically, PolarQuant used k-means to learn a codebook.

*What it saw*: the original coordinate system is hostile to quantization; it is better to switch to (radius, angle) space.

*What it left behind*: even after changing the coordinate system, angles are still non-uniform, so calibration is again needed to learn a codebook. The coordinate-system change was not taken far enough — the specific random rotation chosen did not make the angles uniform.

As we will see, TurboAngle uses a "more correct" rotation that directly produces uniform angles, making the codebook unnecessary — a uniform grid suffices. But in early 2025, nobody had realized this was possible.

* Second Stop, Side Branch [2025-AAAI]: QJL — Another Route via Random Projection

While PolarQuant was working on polar coordinates, QJL (Johnson-Lindenstrauss Quantization) took a different, more radical path: use a JL random projection to map the original vector into a new space, then keep only the *sign bit* (1-bit sign quantization).

QJL shares the same spirit as TurboAngle — *both use a random projection to scatter the original coordinate system and make quantization easier*. The difference is that QJL goes to the extreme of keeping only 1 bit, incurring large loss; TurboAngle retains a few bits of angle information, keeping loss small.

Placing PolarQuant and QJL side by side: by early 2025, the community had reached a consensus — "change the coordinate system, then quantize" is more principled than "fight in the original coordinate system." The remaining question was: *which rotation is best?*

* Third Stop [2025-04]: TurboQuant — Found the Right Rotation, but Quantized the Wrong Target

TurboQuant identified the key rotation: *FWHT (Fast Walsh-Hadamard Transform) + random ±1 diagonal rotation*.

Concretely: y = H · D · x, where H is a normalized Hadamard matrix (computed in O(d log d) via fast transform) and D is a diagonal matrix with independent random ±1 entries. Apply this transform to KV tensors first, then do scalar quantization.

This combination has two elegant properties:
- *Outlier suppression*: the random ±1 diagonal breaks inter-channel correlations, and the Hadamard butterfly spreads any outlier energy evenly across all output channels.
- *Near-Gaussian marginals*: each output y_j is a sum of d randomly weighted terms; the central limit theorem drives it toward a Gaussian.

So TurboQuant's story is: "after rotation the data is approximately Gaussian, outliers are gone, scalar quantization works well."

*What it saw*: PolarQuant's random preconditioning was too weak to suppress outliers, which is why angles remained concentrated. Switching to Hadamard + random signs flattens outliers much more effectively.

*What it left behind* — and this is the crux of TurboAngle's story — TurboQuant *did not realize* that after this rotation the true target to quantize is not the Cartesian coordinates but something else entirely.

It treated "the distribution becomes approximately Gaussian, kurtosis decreases" as the main benefit, and naturally slapped on a scalar quantizer. But that is a *side effect*, not the *primary phenomenon*.

* Fourth Stop [2026-03]: TurboAngle — Same Rotation, Picking Up the Uniformity That Was Left Behind

TurboAngle's core insight can be stated in one sentence:

*After FWHT + random ±1 rotation, treating each pair of consecutive outputs (y_{2i}, y_{2i+1}) as a point in the 2D plane, their polar angle θ is not merely approximately uniform — it is strictly Uniform([0, 2π)).*

Why? Because:
- After rotation, each y_j is a sum of d ±1-weighted terms → central limit theorem → approximately Gaussian marginal.
- The random ±1 diagonal destroys inter-coordinate correlations → the pair (y_{2i}, y_{2i+1}) approaches a *spherically symmetric* 2D Gaussian.
- The polar angle of a spherically symmetric 2D distribution = strictly Uniform([0, 2π)).

This is a clean mathematical result. Spherical symmetry is the key — as long as the distribution is spherically symmetric, the angle is guaranteed to be uniform. And Hadamard + random signs deliver exactly spherical symmetry.

Given a uniform distribution, *what is the information-theoretically optimal quantizer?* A *uniform grid*. Divide [0, 2π) into n equal intervals and you are done — nothing needs to be learned.

*What it saw wrong in TurboQuant*: TurboQuant, using the same rotation, fixed its gaze on "distribution becomes approximately Gaussian" — a *secondary phenomenon* — and applied a scalar quantizer (good enough for a Gaussian, but far from optimal for the *exactly* known angle distribution). The *precisely known* distributional property — strict angular uniformity — was completely unused.

*What it saw wrong in PolarQuant*: PolarQuant also pursued polar coordinates but chose a rotation that produced concentrated angles, forcing k-means learning. TurboAngle uses a different instance of the same class of random rotation (Hadamard + ±1, giving spherical symmetry) that makes angles directly uniform, rendering k-means irrelevant — a fixed uniform grid, zero calibration.

*What it saw wrong in KIVI/KVQuant*: those methods did per-channel and per-vector parameter fitting in the original coordinate system, all fighting the messy data distribution. TurboAngle switches to a coordinate system where *the distribution is known a priori*, requiring no data at all.

Compressed into three sentences: *TurboQuant found the right rotation but quantized the wrong target; PolarQuant found the right quantization target but chose the wrong rotation; TurboAngle combined the correct half from each side.*

** Second Contribution: Per-Layer MixedKV Early-Boost

Uniform angle quantization alone is not enough, because transformer layers differ in sensitivity — early layers encode general features and are more sensitive to quantization error; later layers are more robust. TurboAngle adds one more idea: *let each layer independently choose the codebook size for K and V*, allocating extra bits to the most critical early layers ("early boost") while keeping the rest at baseline.

The findings this produces are themselves interesting — across seven models, *which layers are critical, whether the bottleneck is K-dominated or V-dominated, and even whether "negative-transfer layers" exist (where giving more precision actually hurts) all vary from model to model*. There is no universal optimal configuration, but there are discoverable per-model patterns.

** Third Contribution: Asymmetry in Norm Quantization

After separating (radius, angle) quantization, TurboAngle further discovers a *10–20× asymmetry in norm sensitivity between K and V*: K norms require 8-bit linear quantization, while V norms are fine with 4-bit log-space quantization. This parallels the K/V asymmetry found on the angle side, and both repeatedly point to the same conclusion — K and V play fundamentally different roles in attention and should be quantized asymmetrically.

* Insights

** What Is Really Changing Along This Arc

On the surface, this looks like iterative improvement in KV cache quantization — from KIVI's 2-bit to TurboAngle's 3.28-bit angles. But the deeper change is not in the bit count; it is a shift in *quantization paradigm*:

1. *From "opposing the data distribution" to "exploiting the data distribution"*: KIVI/KVQuant treated the data distribution as the enemy — messy distribution, so use per-channel parameters to fit it. TurboAngle treats the distribution as a friend — if I can transform it into a *known* distribution (uniform), I can apply the theoretically optimal quantizer.
2. *From "heuristic" to "information-theoretically optimal"*: the R-D optimal quantizer for a uniform distribution is a uniform grid — a hard result from information theory. TurboAngle's "algorithm" has essentially no free parameters: n equal divisions and done. All the cleverness lives in the choice of coordinate system, not in the quantizer.
3. *From "calibration-driven" to "calibration-free"*: once you move to a coordinate system with a known prior, calibration data becomes irrelevant. For deployment — serving many model variants, edge deployment, frequent model updates — this is a qualitative change.

At a higher level of abstraction: this is the victory of *"turning an engineering problem into a math problem."* The KIVI lineage is engineering-minded — address each data pathology (outliers, misaligned scales, asymmetry) with a targeted engineering fix. The TurboAngle lineage is math-minded — find a transform that maps "messy data" to a "clean distribution," then let quantization become a mathematical exercise.

** Where the Next Steps Most Likely Lead

1. *Automated per-layer budget allocation*: TurboAngle demonstrates that layer-sensitivity patterns are model-specific, but configuring the budget still requires manual grid search. The obvious next step is some form of lightweight calibration — or more ambitiously, a calibration-free heuristic — to automatically determine each layer's K/V codebook size.
2. *Extending the "known distribution" idea beyond attention*: FFN activations, attention outputs, weight quantization — wherever a suitable random transform can produce a known prior distribution, the same paradigm applies. Expect a "Turbo-X" family extending this trick to all LLM quantization scenarios.
3. *End-to-end latency gains*: the current paper focuses on quality (perplexity), but the real-world inference latency of trig lookup + FWHT still needs engineering validation. Whether TurboAngle inside a fused attention kernel runs faster than INT4 is the key question for practical adoption.
4. *Combining with sparse attention / KV eviction*: quantization and sparsification are orthogonal compression dimensions, and TurboAngle's calibration-free nature makes it especially suitable for composing with dynamic KV eviction schemes (H2O, StreamingLLM, etc.).

* Honest Caveats

- *Certainty of citation relationships*: the main KIVI/KVQuant → PolarQuant → TurboQuant → TurboAngle arc drawn here is based on explicit comparisons and critiques in TurboAngle §5 (Related Work). These citation relationships are confirmed.
- *The PolarQuant → TurboQuant connection* is an inference based on overlapping authors (Zandieh, Han, Mirrokni appear in both) and the logical progression of methods; the paper itself does not explicitly state that TurboQuant is a direct successor to PolarQuant.
- *Layers before KIVI* have not been traced further back. The earlier work cited by KIVI (GPTQ, AWQ, LLM.int8(), etc.) belongs to the weight quantization main line, which diverges from the KV cache quantization trajectory. Stopping at KIVI reflects the community consensus that it is the modern starting point for KV-cache-specific work.
- *Subsequent papers*: no work explicitly critiquing or improving upon TurboAngle has been identified. The paper was published in 2026-03, only two months ago (as of 2026-05), and community responses have not yet settled. Leaving this section blank is honesty, not an oversight.
