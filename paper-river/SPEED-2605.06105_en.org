* The Problem River

Why is long-context inference so expensive? Because every time the model generates a new token, it must reload the "memory" (KV cache) of thousands of preceding prompt tokens from GPU memory — and it must do this for every single layer.

The difficulty is structural: KV cache size = sequence length N × number of layers L × hidden dimension. When N grows to 128K and L is 32, every generated token requires reading the entire cache from scratch. The result: Time-To-First-Token (TTFT) explodes, Time-Per-Output-Token (TPOT) explodes, and memory explodes.

Generation after generation of research has asked the same question: *Out of this enormous pile of KV entries, how many are actually useful?* Early work said "many tokens don't matter, discard them"; later work said "it's not just token redundancy — there's redundancy across layers too"; still later came the realization that "the needs of the prefill phase and the decode phase are actually different." SPEED stands at the far end of this line and pushes the observation to an extreme: *the upper layers simply don't need to let decode tokens see the prompt's KV at all.*

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2023 | Pope et al. - Efficiently Scaling Transformer Inference: Getting the Accounting Right

*** What Problem Did It See?

In the era of GPT-3-scale models with tens of billions of parameters, the conventional wisdom was that "slow inference" came from model size. Pope et al. were the first to systematically open the books — and found that at long context, the bottleneck is not the model parameters but *the repeated transfer of the KV cache*.

A concrete scenario: you hand the model a 32K-token document and ask it to summarize. The model first runs the entire 32K through to generate KV (prefill phase), then generates the summary word by word (decode phase). During decode, every single generated token requires reading KV entries of size 32K × layers × hidden dimension. Memory bandwidth cannot keep up.

*** Its Solution

This work is not itself a "slimming" method; it is a *decomposition and measurement* framework. It explicitly splits inference into a prefill phase and a decode phase, and separately measures TTFT, TPOT, and memory bandwidth utilization.

*** Why It Worked — and What Pit It Left Behind

Clearing up the accounting let every subsequent researcher know exactly where to cut. The pit: it identified KV cache as the bottleneck but did not say which parts of the KV cache could be removed. That became the starting point for all work that followed — *which KV entries are actually important?*

** 2023 | Zhang et al. - H2O: Horizontally Pruning "Along-for-the-Ride" Tokens

*** What Problem Did It See?

The H2O team did something simple: plot the attention-weight heat maps. The result was striking — *the vast majority of prompt tokens are almost never attended to during generation*, with attention concentrated on a small fraction.

An analogy: you read a 5,000-word article and, when answering questions, you actually refer back to only about 50 key phrases. The KV entries for the remaining 4,950 words sit in memory the whole time but are accessed essentially zero times.

*** Its Solution

Keep only the KV of "heavy hitters" and discard the rest. How to identify heavy hitters? Track cumulative attention scores over history — whoever accumulates the most attention stays.

*** Why It Worked — and What Pits It Left Behind

It worked because attention distributions are inherently sparse. Two pits remained:
- Pit 1: Dropping the initial tokens causes the model to immediately go haywire (this pit directly inspired the next paper).
- Pit 2: This is *horizontal* pruning — finding redundancy along the token dimension. But redundancy exists in another direction too (a pit that would take another half-year for anyone to notice).

** 2023 | Xiao et al. - StreamingLLM: Discovering the "Attention Sink"

*** What Problem Did It See?

The StreamingLLM team wanted to support infinitely long streaming conversations — tokens outside the window would simply be dropped. They found that *discarding just the very first token caused the model to immediately start producing gibberish*.

This was deeply puzzling. What meaningful information could the very first token (usually BoS, the beginning-of-sequence symbol) possibly carry?

*** Its Solution

After diagnosis, they found that the model treats the first few tokens as an *attention drain* — softmax must distribute probability mass somewhere, and when there is nothing meaningful to attend to, everything spills into the initial tokens. The BoS token is not carrying information; it is absorbing attention overflow.

Solution: always retain the first few tokens as anchors; the rest can slide out of the window freely.

*** Why It Worked — and What Pit It Left Behind

It gave future researchers an extremely cheap stabilizer: *if you want a model to remain stable after major KV modifications, just keep a BoS anchor.* Three years later, SPEED would pick up this gift and use it unchanged.

The pit: StreamingLLM was concerned with streaming sliding windows and never touched the question of inter-layer redundancy.

** 2024 | Brandon et al. - CLA: Vertical Pruning — Sharing KV Across Layers

*** What Problem Did It See?

Even after H2O's pruning along the token axis, the KV cache was still large. Brandon et al. changed perspective: they compared KV tensors from different layers of the same model and found that *upper-layer K matrices look very similar to lower-layer K matrices* — especially in deeper regions.

This implies that storing an independent KV copy for every layer amounts to repeatedly storing nearly identical information.

*** Its Solution

Cross-Layer Attention (CLA): let several adjacent layers *share a single KV cache*. For example, every group of 4 layers shares one cache, immediately cutting KV memory by 4×.

*** Why It Worked — and What Pit It Left Behind

It worked because deep-layer KV is inherently highly similar. The pits:
- Shared KV means every layer in the group sees exactly the same KV — too rigid.
- During prefill, every layer still must be computed — *only storage is saved, not compute*.

The next paper would be aimed squarely at "can we also save compute?"

** 2025 | Qiao et al. - SwiftKV: Growing Upper-Layer KV from Lower Layers

*** What Problem Did It See?

CLA's sharing was too coarse. The SwiftKV team asked: instead of simply copying lower-layer KV to upper layers, could the upper layers *learn a projection from the lower-layer representations*? This would preserve the difference between upper and lower layers while allowing the bulk of upper-layer compute to be skipped during prefill.

Going further: skip upper-layer compute during prefill, then reconstruct the upper-layer KV via the learned projection when needed at decode time.

*** Its Solution

Train a lightweight projection module: directly project upper-layer KV from lower-layer hidden states. During prefill, run only the lower layers; upper-layer KV is *constructed* via projection.

*** Why It Worked — and What Pit It Left Behind

It worked because upper-layer KV is in practice some function of lower-layer representations and can be approximated by learning. The pits:
- The projection still costs compute, and *the upper-layer KV still must be written into memory*.
- The projection module requires training, adding a training cost.

The next paper would ask: what if we skip even the projection?

** 2026 | He et al. - POP: Dropping Deep-Layer Compute Entirely During Prefill

*** What Problem Did It See?

SwiftKV had already cut deep-layer prefill compute, but it still *reconstructed* upper-layer KV and stored it in the cache. The POP team pushed further: is upper-layer prefill KV actually necessary to read repeatedly at decode time?

Their observation: upper-layer attention during decode already pays little attention to prompt tokens (something the H2O heat maps had already hinted at).

*** Its Solution

POP (Prefill-Only Pruning): run only the lower layers during prefill; skip the upper layers entirely. Unlike SPEED, however — at decode time POP uses a dedicated KV projection mechanism to *reconstruct* upper-layer prefill KV for decode use, accompanied by carefully engineered boundary handling.

*** Why It Worked — and What Pit It Left Behind

It worked by confirming that "upper-layer prefill compute can be saved." The pits:
- POP still *retains* the concept of upper-layer prompt KV — it just changes its source from "computed" to "reconstructed."
- In memory terms, upper-layer prompt KV is still occupying space.
- It adds the engineering complexity of a dedicated projection plus boundary handling.

SPEED stood next to POP and asked a radically simple question: what if we *simply don't have* upper-layer prompt KV at all?

** 2026 | SPEED (This Paper): Upper-Layer Decode Simply Doesn't See the Prompt

*** What Problem Did It See?

The heat maps drawn by the SPEED team told the whole story: on Llama-3.1-8B, *decode-token attention to prompt tokens peaks in the middle layers and approaches zero in the upper layers*. In other words, upper-layer decode is not meaningfully attending to the prompt at all.

If it's not looking anyway, why keep upper-layer prompt KV in memory? POP was laboring to "reconstruct" it, but once reconstructed, nobody looked at it.

*** Its Solution

Phase-asymmetric KV visibility — shallow prefill, deep decoding. The approach is strikingly simple:
- Prompt tokens pass through only the first K layers (default K = 24, i.e., 75% of layers).
- Decode tokens pass through all L layers (default 32 layers).
- In the upper layers, decode attention can only see previously generated decode tokens plus one BoS anchor.

The BoS anchor is borrowed directly from StreamingLLM (2023) — keeping the initial token as an attention sink prevents upper-layer softmax from collapsing when there are no prompt tokens to attend to.

*** Why It Worked — and What Pits It Left Behind

It worked because:
- Upper-layer decode was already paying little attention to the prompt (confirmed by attention heat maps).
- Decoding itself still runs at full depth, so *generation quality is not compromised*.
- The BoS anchor keeps softmax stable.

Numbers: with 75% of layers processing the prompt, Llama-3.1-8B's OLMES average is 51.2 vs. 51.4 for full depth (negligible difference); at 128K context, TTFT −33%, TPOT −22%, memory −25%.

The pits:
- Choosing K currently still requires a sweep (though the authors provide a layer-wise diagnostic tool to assist).
- Detailed experiments were conducted only on Llama-3.1-8B; scaling to larger models remains to be validated.
- "Upper-layer decode sees no prompt at all" is a hard cutoff — more extreme tasks that require suddenly revisiting the prompt late in generation (e.g., multi-step reasoning) could be affected.

* Frontier Extensions

SPEED is a May 2026 paper and the most recent representative on this "depth-asymmetric KV" evolutionary line. As of this writing, newer work on this line is sparse, but several directions are likely already in progress:

- *Adaptive K*: instead of scanning to choose K, let the model itself decide — based on prompt content — at which layer to "stop looking at the prompt."
- *Task-aware visibility*: different heads or head groups use different cutoffs, exploiting redundancy at finer granularity.
- *Combination with token-level eviction*: stacking SPEED's layer-dimension pruning on top of token-dimension pruning methods such as H2O or SnapKV, yielding multiplicative gains in theory.

The uncertain part: no clear critical follow-up work on SPEED has appeared yet. This may be because the paper is too new, or because the idea itself is approaching the floor of "everything that can be cut has been cut" — further cuts would begin to harm generation quality.

* Insight

Reading through the entire line, what is really happening is not "finding new algorithms" — it is *a progressive deepening of the understanding of what "redundancy" actually means*:

- First-order insight: token-dimension redundancy (H2O: many words don't matter).
- Second-order insight: layer-dimension redundancy (CLA: many layers' KV look alike).
- Third-order insight: phase-dimension redundancy (SwiftKV/POP: upper-layer compute in the prefill phase can be saved).
- Fourth-order insight: visibility-dimension redundancy (SPEED: upper-layer decode simply does not need to see the prompt's KV).

Every generation asked "is what the previous generation called redundancy really the limit of redundancy?" — and every generation pushed the definition of "redundancy" into a finer dimension. The underlying movement is *a progressive refinement of our understanding of how transformers work internally* — from treating the model as a black box (H2O only looks at attention scores), to acknowledging shared structure across layers (CLA), to acknowledging the prefill/decode asymmetry (SwiftKV/POP), to acknowledging that *prefill information flows only through the middle layers* (SPEED).

Where is the next step most likely to go? I would bet on two directions:
1. *Head-level / group-level visibility splitting* — not "all heads in the first 24 layers attend to prompt, all heads in the last 8 layers don't," but different heads having different per-layer cutoffs.
2. *Dynamic visibility* — automatically determine K based on the actual information density of the prompt: cheap prompts use smaller K, demanding prompts use larger K.

* Takeaways

This evolutionary line offers two lessons for how to do research:

*Takeaway 1: Diagnose before you optimize.* H2O plotted attention heat maps; CLA compared upper- and lower-layer KV; SPEED plotted decode-to-prefill attention heat maps. Every breakthrough was preceded by a *clear visual diagnosis* that revealed "this region is empty." Optimization is the natural action that follows *seeing the empty space* — it is not conjuring a new algorithm from thin air.

What this means in practice: before optimizing any system, first visualize the distribution of work inside it. Map out what is hot, what is cold, what is redundant — then decide where to cut.

*Takeaway 2: Extreme simplicity is the destination, not the starting point.* SPEED's "upper-layer decode does not see the prompt" would have sounded insane to someone in 2023 — how can you not look at the prompt and not collapse? But it works because H2O, StreamingLLM, CLA, SwiftKV, and POP spent three years solidifying the understanding that "upper-layer prefill doesn't matter."

What this means in practice: when you see a field accumulating many complex engineering solutions (like POP's dedicated projection plus boundary handling), ask yourself — *"does this thing actually need to be done at all?"* Complex engineering solutions are often transitional forms; the real next step may simply be to delete the entire operation.
