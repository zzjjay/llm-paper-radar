* The River of the Problem

Training a large model can consume tens or even hundreds of yottaflops of compute — so how do you make every multiplication use fewer bits without letting the model drift further and further off course?

At its core, this line of work is a tug-of-war between format design and error accumulation. Every time engineers reduce training precision by one level, the model fails in some new way — sometimes numerical overflow, sometimes vanishing gradients, sometimes training collapse, and sometimes a deeper, harder-to-see systemic shift. Four bits is the next hardware frontier, and every paper along this line is another iteration of "we thought we found the answer, but it turned out we only treated the symptom, not the root cause."

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022 | FP8: The First Engineered Attempt to Halve Training Precision

*** What Problem It Saw

Before 2022, the mainstream training paradigm was "FP16 + BF16" mixed precision — weights stored in BF16, computation in FP16, gradient accumulation in FP32. This scheme was stable enough, but at 175B parameters every multiplication consumed enormous compute. Micikevicius et al. asked a simple question: if we compress computation from 16 bits down to 8, can the model still converge?

*** Its Solution

The answer was: yes, but two formats are needed. They designed *E4M3* (4 exponent bits, 3 mantissa bits) and *E5M2* (5 exponent bits, 2 mantissa bits). Intuitively this is like giving different types of numbers different magnifying glasses: E4M3 is high-precision, suited to the concentrated distributions of weights and activations; E5M2 has wider range, suited to the broad distributions of gradients. Experiments on CNNs, RNNs, and Transformers all matched the FP16 baseline.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

The intuition of matching two formats to two distribution characteristics was correct — that is why it worked. The trap it laid: they still used *per-tensor* scaling, where an entire tensor shares a single scale factor. Once a block contains a few abnormally large outliers, the precision of every other element in that block collapses. In a 4-bit world, this becomes lethal.

** 2023 | MXFP4: Breaking the Granularity, the First Push to 4 Bits

*** What Problem It Saw

FP8's per-tensor scaling is a coarse comb — it sweeps an entire weight matrix in one pass. LLM weight distributions are highly uneven; certain blocks within certain layers can have value ranges a hundred times larger than others. Rouhani et al. (a joint team from Microsoft, AMD, Intel, NVIDIA, and others) asked: can the scaling granularity be refined to "one scale per 32 elements"?

*** Its Solution

This is the core idea of *Microscaling*. Each block of 32 elements has its own E8M0 scale factor (pure exponent, 8 bits), while the elements themselves are stored in a narrower format. Pushing to 4 bits, they chose *E2M1* as the element format — 2 exponent bits for dynamic range, 1 mantissa bit for precision. This was the first-ever demonstration of sub-8-bit quantization of weights, activations, and gradients simultaneously on generative language models.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

Per-block scaling did address the coarse-granularity problem. But the paper itself acknowledged that "MXFP4 accuracy remains a significant challenge." They chose E2M1 for its dynamic range — but this format's *grid point distribution is non-uniform*: the exponent bits make large-value intervals sparse and small-value intervals dense, with bin sizes that vary exponentially across the value range. This geometric property buried a landmine that others would only discover later.

** 2025 | FP4 All The Way + Quartet: Proving Full FP4 Training Is Feasible

*** What Problem It Saw

MXFP4's demonstration was a proof of concept, but no one had proven it could train stably at true LLM scale (tens of billions of parameters, hundreds of billions of tokens). Chmiel et al. (Intel Gaudi2 team) and Panferov et al. (from IST Austria) published almost simultaneously in May 2025, attacking the problem from different angles.

*** Its Solution

*Chmiel (FP4 All The Way)*: First to quantize weights, activations, and gradients all to FP4 (NVFP4 format: E2M1 elements + E4M3 scale, 16 elements per block), validated on a 7B model. They also derived a threshold: when the gradient norm falls below √3 times the quantization noise, quantized training begins to fail — providing practitioners with a measurable red line.

*Quartet I (Panferov)*: Focused on NVIDIA Blackwell hardware, discovered "low-precision scaling laws" (how precision loss scales as a function of bit-width and training volume), and delivered the Quartet recipe — all linear layers computed in FP4, dedicated Blackwell CUDA kernels, nearly 2× faster than FP8.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

What these two papers accomplished was proving feasibility. But both inherited E2M1 for their format choice. The non-uniform grid problem was still there — just not prominent enough at that scale to be obvious. The next paper would stretch training to 10T tokens, and the problem would begin to surface.

** 2025 | NVFP4: NVIDIA Scales FP4 to 12B Parameters and 10T Tokens

*** What Problem It Saw

In prior work, training runs spanned at most a few hundred billion tokens. NVIDIA pushed this to 10 trillion tokens — at that scale, previously ignorable details became fatal. The specific problem was *outliers*: in certain LLM layers, weight or activation values suddenly spike to extreme magnitudes. A single such outlier monopolizes the entire block's scaling range, collapsing the precision of all other 15 elements in the block.

*** Its Solution

NVIDIA introduced the *Random Hadamard Transform (RHT)* — applying a random rotation to the values before quantization to spread outliers evenly across the whole block, so no single element can monopolize the scale range. Stochastic Rounding (SR) was used for gradient estimation, which is theoretically unbiased. The final 12B model matched the FP8 baseline loss over 10T tokens — the longest FP4 training run on public record.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

RHT did suppress outliers. But UFP4 would later reveal that RHT is not a cure — it actually has a chemical reaction with the E2M1 grid. Hadamard rotation spreads values that were previously concentrated in one interval out into the most non-uniform region of the E2M1 grid, actually *amplifying the shrinkage bias*. Nobody saw this at the time, because nobody yet had the concept of "shrinkage bias."

** 2026 | Quartet II: Perfecting Gradient Estimation, But Still Missing the Root Cause

*** What Problem It Saw

Stochastic Rounding (SR) is unbiased, but "unbiasedness" has a cost — to make the expectation land on the true value, SR occasionally maps numbers to grid points farther away from the truth, wasting the already-sparse representational capacity of E2M1 and making actual quantization error much larger than the theoretical optimum. Quartet II asked: can we keep unbiasedness while reducing the error?

*** Its Solution

The answer was *MS-EDEN (MicroScaling EDEN)*: instead of independently stochastically rounding each FP4 element, move the randomness to the *block-scale level* — let each block's scale factor carry a random perturbation, while the elements themselves use deterministic rounding. Unbiasedness is maintained at the block granularity, but element-level noise drops substantially. Quantization error is 2× lower than SR, validated on 1.9B parameters over 38B tokens, and runs 4.2× faster than BF16 on Blackwell GPUs.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

Gradient estimation genuinely improved — that is real progress. But Quartet II changed "how to round" and left the E2M1 grid itself completely untouched. Shrinkage bias seeps out of the geometric shape of the grid; no matter how clever the rounding strategy, it is just pouring water into the wrong container. The next paper will simply replace the container.

** 2026 | UFP4: Finding the Root Cause — Replace the Grid, Don't Patch the Rounding

*** What Problem It Saw

Zhao et al. re-examined E2M1 from a geometric perspective: if you plot all 16 E2M1 representable values on a number line, the bin sizes (intervals between adjacent representable values) in the positive region are non-uniform — bins near zero are narrow, bins near 1 are wide. When a true value falls in a bin and is rounded, the expected value of the rounded result is *systematically shifted in the negative direction* (biased low). This is *Shrinkage Bias*.

More seriously:

1. Matrix multiplication is a chain of multiplications. If every multiplication introduces a downward bias, after N layers the bias accumulates multiplicatively (O(N)), not additively (O(sqrt(N))). This explains why deep networks collapse sooner than shallow ones.

2. The RHT introduced by NVFP4 spreads value distributions into the *most non-uniform region* of the E2M1 grid. The result: RHT not only fails to alleviate shrinkage bias — it amplifies it. This directly explains why the NVFP4 recipe restricts RHT to specific steps of the backward pass — an empirical "we know where it will blow up," without knowing why.

*** Its Solution

The solution is geometrically clean: replace E2M1 with *E1M2* (1 exponent bit, 2 mantissa bits) or *INT4*. Both have uniformly spaced grid points — all bin sizes are equal, so rounding introduces no systematic directional bias. Geometrically, shrinkage bias is eliminated rather than suppressed.

Results: across a 1.5B Dense model, a 7.9B MoE model, and a 124B MoE model, UFP4 (E1M2/INT4) shows substantially lower loss degradation relative to BF16 compared to E2M1-family recipes, and requires no RHT.

*** Why It Worked (and What It Leaves Behind)

It works because it attacks the root cause, not the symptom. The prior five years of work (RHT, SR, MS-EDEN) optimized within the E2M1 framework — finding local optima. E1M2/INT4 steps outside that framework, and the solution simplifies dramatically as a result.

What remains: E1M2 has a narrower dynamic range than E2M1 (one fewer exponent bit). For tensors with extremely wide value ranges, a uniform grid can sometimes pay a precision price. This trade-off is the entry point for future work.

* Frontier Extensions

** 2026 | MixFP4: Adaptive Grid Selection, Not a Single Bet

MixFP4 (Zou et al., arXiv:2605.31035) was independently published three weeks before UFP4's submission, and its conclusions overlap halfway with UFP4.

*The problem it saw*: using a single format (whether E2M1 or E1M2) for every block is itself a mismatch. Different layers and different blocks in an LLM have wildly different numerical distribution shapes. Blocks with dispersed value ranges suit E2M1 (wide dynamic range); blocks with dense value ranges suit E1M2 (uniformly fine grid).

*Its solution*: each block adaptively selects E2M1 or E1M2 at quantization time. The format choice is encoded in the sign bit of the E4M3 block-scale, requiring no extra metadata. Both formats decode uniformly into the E2M2 compute format, so no dual data path is needed. Hardware overhead is only 3.1% area and 1.5% power.

From an evolutionary perspective, MixFP4 and UFP4 are two teams that "saw the same geometric truth at the same time" — UFP4's choice is to abandon E2M1 entirely, while MixFP4's choice is to let E2M1 and E1M2 coexist. The convergence of these two directions itself demonstrates that E2M1's grid problem can no longer be ignored.

* Insights

What is really happening along this line is not "precision getting lower and lower" — that is just the surface.

*The substance is a cognitive evolution around the question of whether quantization error has structure.*

All the work over the preceding five years treated quantization error as "random noise" — so the solutions were various tricks for "driving the expectation to zero" (SR, MS-EDEN). These tricks genuinely help, but they assume the error is symmetric and directionless.

UFP4's contribution is revealing that *E2M1 quantization error is not random noise — it is a directional, systemic bias*. This distinction is qualitative: once the error has a direction, it accumulates multiplicatively across layers, with complexity jumping from O(sqrt(N)) to O(N). That is why large models collapse sooner than small ones. All the rounding tricks were "reducing variance," while the real problem was "the mean is not zero."

There is also a subtler insight: RHT was always an engineering heuristic — "empirically found to help" rather than "theoretically understood why." Practitioners learned "use RHT in bwd_dw" but no one asked why using it elsewhere made things worse. UFP4 supplies the theoretical explanation: RHT pushes the distribution into the most non-uniform region of the E2M1 grid; on the wrong grid, rotation is harmful.

*Where the field is most likely to go next*:

1. E1M2/INT4's dynamic range is narrower than E2M1, which is a liability for extreme-range tensors (e.g., activation spikes in FFN layers). Adaptive mixing of both grid types (the MixFP4 route) may be the practical industry compromise.

2. A deeper question remains untouched: is there an optimal correspondence between grid design and *training objective* (i.e., the curvature of the loss landscape)? Current work is all "given the grid, let training adapt." The reverse direction — "let training drive grid design" — has barely been explored.

3. At the hardware level: AMD MI350 and NVIDIA Blackwell/Rubin chips are optimized for E2M1. If the software community converges on E1M2/INT4, this creates pressure to renegotiate hardware formats — a transition that cannot move much faster than the algorithm community drives it.

* Takeaways

This line of work carries a methodological lesson: *optimizing within an existing framework and stepping outside to find the root cause are two fundamentally different kinds of work.* The former usually publishes faster and accumulates citations faster, but the latter determines where the line ultimately goes.

Quartet II and UFP4 appeared almost simultaneously but pointed in opposite directions: Quartet II is elegant in-framework optimization (MS-EDEN is genuinely beautiful); UFP4 excavates the foundation of the framework and examines it (the geometric analysis is more fundamental). When resources are limited, the ability to distinguish "I am patching a fundamentally wrong format" from "I am approaching a local optimum" is an extraordinarily valuable intuition.

*The practical takeaway*: in any low-precision training scenario, ask one question first — "Is the grid I'm using uniformly spaced? If not, which direction carries more error?" This is more foundational than reaching straight for SR or RHT, and cheaper. Detecting Shrinkage Bias only requires computing the sign distribution of the mean quantization error per layer — a few lines of code — but it tells you whether the problem lies in the grid or in the rounding strategy.
