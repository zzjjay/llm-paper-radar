* Who Is the Protagonist

The protagonist of this thread is not any single paper — it is a question that has gone unanswered for thirty years:

*When you compress a pile of numbers, which ones do you delete and which do you keep — and on what grounds?*

LeCun asked it first in 1989, Han asked it again in 2015, Frantar re-asked it for large models in 2022, Lin approached it from a different angle in 2023, and QuantLRM in 2026 is still asking. Each generation answers differently, but the question itself has not changed by a single word.

The thread chosen here is *compression importance metrics* — from the earliest "second-order derivatives," to "weight magnitudes," to "activation magnitudes," to "traces left by fine-tuning." Every generation has been racing to answer the same question: which numbers inside the model are worth more?

* How the Problem Grew, Step by Step

** Generation One: LeCun Wants to Slim Down Neural Networks; Nobody Listens (1989)

The story starts in a forgotten corner. In 1989, neural networks were ten thousand times smaller than today's, yet researchers were already frustrated by "too many parameters." LeCun wrote *Optimal Brain Damage*: if you delete a weight, how much does the loss increase? Compute it via the second-order term of the Taylor expansion — weights with large second derivatives hurt when removed; weights with small ones can be removed painlessly.

The mathematics were elegant, but the Hessian (the matrix of second derivatives) was prohibitively expensive to compute. Once the model grew at all, it became completely intractable. Hassibi refined the mathematics further in 1993 with *Optimal Brain Surgeon*, but the fundamental cost did not change.

The result was that throughout the 1990s and into the early 2010s, the question of "how to select which weights to delete" was shelved. Everyone went off to solve "how to train better models" instead.

** Generation Two: Han Goes Blunt, and the Bluntness Works (2015)

By 2015, deep learning had taken off, models were enormous, and deploying them to phones required compression. Han et al. wrote *Deep Compression*. They did not touch the Hessian; they simply declared: *remove weights with small absolute values*.

This judgment was crude enough to be suspicious. Why should a small number be unimportant? LeCun had already proved that importance relates to second derivatives, not to magnitude. But Han's experiments spoke for themselves: prune, retrain, and the model is just as good as before. AlexNet compressed 35×, VGG compressed 49×, with almost no accuracy drop.

Why did it work? Looking back, it is because deep networks are heavily over-parameterized; the weights that actually carry information tend to be the ones with large magnitudes, so the blunt cut happened to capture the bulk of it. This paradigm of "use magnitude as importance" — simple enough to memorize — became the baseline color of all compression work for the following decade, including the line in QuantLRM's introduction that explicitly pays homage: "inspired by classical magnitude pruning."

But Han's method carried a hidden assumption: you can retrain. Prune then fine-tune, and accuracy recovers. When models were small, this cost nothing. Once models grew to tens of billions of parameters, retraining became a luxury no one could afford.

** Generation Three: GPTQ Revives the Hessian — but Only Layer by Layer (2022)

Jump to 2022, the LLM era. The 175B GPT-3 was sitting there, and a single retraining run cost millions of dollars. Han's "prune then retrain" cycle was completely dead. What people needed was *post-training quantization* (PTQ): the model is already trained, no further training allowed — just compress it to 4-bit directly.

Frantar et al. wrote *OBC (Optimal Brain Compression)* and promptly followed it with *GPTQ*. Their insight was clever: bring back LeCun's Hessian mathematics, but *compute the Hessian only locally, one layer at a time*. Quantize greedily layer by layer, using second-order information to decide how each weight should be rounded.

This allowed a 175B model to be compressed to 4-bit on a single GPU in a few hours with minimal accuracy loss. The Hessian was finally resurrected in the LLM era — not computed globally, but locally.

Yet GPTQ also had limits. It assumes that the importance of a weight can be derived from the within-layer Hessian, entirely ignoring one thing: *under different input activations, the same weight can differ enormously in how much work it actually does*. A weight may be numerically small with an unimpressive Hessian entry, yet if it is always multiplied by a large activation, its actual effect is large. GPTQ cannot see this.

** Generation Four: AWQ Shifts the Lens — Look at Activations, Not Weights (2023)

Lin et al. proposed *AWQ (Activation-aware Weight Quantization)*. Their intuition was entirely different from GPTQ's: what determines whether a weight is important is not the weight itself, but *the activation value it is multiplied by*.

In practice: run a small calibration dataset through the model and observe how large the activations are on each input channel. For channels with large activations, *scale up* the corresponding weights before quantization — since round-to-nearest error is fixed in magnitude, scaling the value up first reduces the relative quantization error — then fold the scale back in on the other side to cancel it out.

This approach is extremely cheap: no Hessian, no training, one calibration pass. On 4-bit general LLMs, AWQ matched or exceeded GPTQ at much higher speed, becoming the de facto industry standard.

But AWQ left a loose end: *it only looks at activations and is blind to the model's own "character."* In other words, it does not know how the model was trained — whether it went through ordinary SFT or was deeply shaped by reinforcement learning into a reasoning model makes no difference to AWQ. If the activation distributions look the same, they are treated the same.

** Generation Five: QuantLRM — Don't Forget the Model Was Fine-Tuned (2026)

By 2026, large reasoning models (LRMs) had taken center stage — DeepSeek-R1, Qwen3, Olmo3 — each having undergone extensive post-training: SFT + DPO + RL, layer upon layer. These models hold up at 4-bit but visibly collapse at 3-bit — AWQ and GPTQ both fail there.

Why? Zhang et al.'s diagnosis: *what carries the most information in these models is not the activation distribution but the traces that fine-tuning left on the weights themselves*.

Concretely: subtract the pre-fine-tuning weights W from the post-fine-tuning weights W' to get ΔW. This ΔW is the "fine-tuning fingerprint." AWQ never looks at it; neither does GPTQ. Yet clearly, weights that fine-tuning changed heavily and weights that fine-tuning barely touched should not be treated as equally important.

Here is where it gets interesting — their experiments showed that the intuition "larger ΔW means more important" is *wrong*. Using the absolute value of ΔW directly as an importance score performs worse than AWQ.

Why? Because *small updates also matter*. A weight that was almost never changed may be encoding the model's "general-purpose capabilities" — language understanding, instruction following. These are foundational abilities that fine-tuning was not meant to destroy. If you protect only the heavily updated weights, you inadvertently discard these foundations.

Their solution: *"protect both ends"* — weights with the smallest updates and weights with the largest updates both receive high importance scores; those in the middle receive low scores. The shape is a U-curve.

Going further, they noticed that a substantial fraction of weights (over 1%) have ΔW = 0 — "completely untouched" weights deserve the most protection. So they add a mechanism that counts how many zero-update entries exist in a channel and folds that count into the importance score.

Finally, this importance signal is fed into AWQ's scaling framework (a key advantage of AWQ: the scaling factors are pluggable), and the optimal scaling strength α is found by search.

Results: on 3-bit quantization of R1-Distill-Qwen-32B, an average gain of 6.55% for RL fine-tuned models and at least 1.65% for SFT models. The cost? One extra "pseudo fine-tuning" pass (for models without a publicly available pre-fine-tuning checkpoint) to collect the ΔW signal — but this is far cheaper than QAT.

** Frontier Extensions: Where Does This Thread Go Next

The work following QuantLRM is still very new, but several directions are already visible:

1. *Reasoning-aware calibration*: Zhang 2025 ("Reasoning meets compression") and Liu 2025 are systematically benchmarking quantization failures in reasoning models and using the distribution of *reasoning tokens* itself as a calibration signal — a natural extension of AWQ's idea, but now targeting the reasoning chain.
2. *Weight-diff interpretability*: Goel 2025 is investigating how to "read" weight changes in natural language, which provides finer tools for understanding which ΔW encodes which capability.
3. *Coupling quantization with post-training*: Since fine-tuning traces are so informative, the natural next step is to *prepare for quantization during fine-tuning itself* — a lightweight QAT revival, but applied only in the final training stage.

At a deeper level, the trend is clear: importance metrics are moving from *static* (weights themselves, Hessian) to *dynamic* (activations, training trajectories). The next step may be from "training trajectories" to "inference trajectories" — determining which weights to protect based on which ones are frequently activated during reasoning.

* The Core: What Is Actually Changing in This Thread

On the surface, this is a sequence of "swap the metric" papers: second derivative → magnitude → Hessian → activation → fine-tuning delta.

But at a deeper level, what is truly changing is *where one looks to read out a weight's importance*:

1. *Read it from mathematical structure* (1989–2015): importance is the second derivative of the loss, or the magnitude of the weight itself — everything lives in a static, differentiable object.
2. *Read it from data* (2022–2023): importance is hidden in the activation distribution; you can only see it by running real data through the model — a concession that "model importance" is tied to "input distribution" and cannot be discussed in isolation from data.
3. *Read it from training history* (2026–): importance is hidden in *how the model came to be what it is* — a concession that the model is not a static object but a *system with a past*, and that past is itself information.

This is a progressive expansion of perspective: from *the thing itself (weights)* to *a relation (weights and data)* to *a history (the evolution of weights)*. Each expansion was forced by the previous perspective proving insufficient at a new scale of model or a new training paradigm.

Where does it go next? In all likelihood, someone will begin using *inference-time trajectories* (not only training-time trajectories) to define importance — for instance, which weights are frequently active during chain-of-thought, and which are simply "asleep." This is the natural extension of the historical perspective: a model has not only a training history but an inference history.
