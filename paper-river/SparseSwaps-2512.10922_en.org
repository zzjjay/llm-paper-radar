* The River of the Problem

You have a 175-billion-parameter model in front of you. You want to throw away half the weights to make it run faster — but you cannot retrain; retraining is too expensive. The question becomes: *which half do you throw away?*

People have been asking this question for nearly 40 years. The difficulty sits in two places. First, the "importance" of each weight is entangled with every other — dropping A may be harmless, but dropping A and then B might be catastrophic. Second, you are not dealing with a few million independent numbers but tens of billions of mutually coupled values; the combinatorial explosion is enormous. So every generation does the same thing: *find an approximation that is workable on the hardware of its era.*

The LLM era has pushed this to the extreme: approximations cheap enough to compute are too coarse, and approximations fine enough are too expensive to compute. The line that SparseSwaps sits on is the story of how that trade-off has been rebalanced, generation by generation.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1989 | OBD: Second-Order Derivatives Instead of Intuition

*** What Problem It Saw

Before OBD, pruning was done by gut feel — e.g., remove the smallest weights. LeCun et al. said: that is not rigorous. A weight's small absolute value does not imply that removing it causes little harm. What you really want to know is: *if I remove this weight, how much does the loss increase?*

*** Its Solution

Estimate that increase with a Taylor expansion. Expand the loss around the current weights to second order; assume we are at a local minimum (first derivative = 0); then the loss change caused by deleting weight w_i is approximately (1/2) * H_ii * w_i^2, where H_ii is the diagonal entry of the Hessian.

In plain terms: *importance = curvature of this weight × its own square*. High curvature means the loss is "steep" in that direction — even a small perturbation hurts.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because it finally grounded "importance" in a precise mathematical quantity rather than intuition.

The pit it dug: it assumes the Hessian is diagonal, i.e., weights are mutually independent. That is plainly false. If two neurons do the same job, removing either one alone may be fine, but removing both destroys the model. OBD cannot see this coupling.

** 1993 | OBS: Accounting for Coupling

*** What Problem It Saw

OBD pretends weights do not interact, but real networks are full of coupling. Hassibi and Stork said: stop pretending — use the full Hessian inverse.

*** Its Solution

Compute the deletion cost for each weight using the full inverse Hessian, and after deleting a weight, push all remaining weights toward the new optimum to compensate for the loss. One operation, two effects: select + compensate.

Analogy: OBD looks for the soldier with the smallest contribution and cuts them. OBS cuts that soldier and then repositions the others to cover the gap, minimising the overall loss in fighting power.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

Theoretically optimal under the second-order approximation.

The pit it dug: computing the Hessian inverse for a layer with N weights requires inverting an N×N matrix — O(N^3). For a layer with hundreds of millions of parameters, that is simply intractable. OBS therefore remained confined to small networks throughout the 1990s.

** 2015 | Magnitude Pruning Revived: If Second-Order Is Intractable, Skip It

*** What Problem It Saw

With the deep learning era, networks grew from tens of thousands of parameters to tens of millions overnight. The OBS second-order framework was completely unrunnable on AlexNet or VGG.

*** Its Solution

Han et al.'s Deep Compression resurrected the oldest idea: *the smaller a weight's absolute value, the less important it is — prune it directly.* Paired with an iterative prune–retrain–prune–retrain cycle, this achieved up to 90% sparsity on CNNs with almost no accuracy loss.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked on CNNs partly because CNN weight distributions are relatively "uniform" — there are no extreme outlier weights single-handedly holding up the whole model.

The pit it dug: in the Transformer era this framework broke completely. Transformers contain *activation outliers* and *super-weights* — a small number of extremely large activations or weights that carry the bulk of inference. In that regime, pruning by absolute magnitude is, in the words of the SparseGPT paper, "no better than random pruning for LLMs."

** 2023 | SparseGPT: Bringing OBS Back to LLM Scale

*** What Problem It Saw

Magnitude pruning no longer works on Transformers, but the OBS second-order approach cannot compute a Hessian inverse at 175B parameters. What now?

Frantar and Alistarh noticed a key fact: *during pruning we do not actually need a global Hessian.* If we only look at the layer-wise reconstruction error ||WX − (M⊙W)X||², the Hessian for that layer is XX^T, independent of downstream layers. Better still, this Hessian is *shared* across all rows — every row's optimisation uses the same XX^T.

*** Its Solution

Decompose by rows: handle each row independently. Apply an OBS-style second-order update to each row, but because the Hessian is shared, its inverse is computed only once. Pair this with a clever sequential sweep (not deleting everything at once, but scanning column by column, pruning as you go and compensating the remaining weights in that row) to bring OBS back to 175B models at roughly 10 minutes per layer.

Analogy: OBS was "reassign the entire army from scratch." SparseGPT is "group by row, sweep column by column within each group, prune whoever you reach, then let the remaining people in that row shift to cover."

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because it sidestepped the impossibly large global Hessian and computed only the input-input correlation matrix for a single layer.

The pit it dug: 10 minutes per layer is still slow at LLM scale. Weight updates happen at every step, the method is fairly complex, and there are non-trivial hyperparameters. Could this be simpler?

** 2023 | Wanda: Drop the Weight Updates, Do the Simplest Thing

*** What Problem It Saw

Sun et al. stared at SparseGPT's objective and asked: can we find an ultra-simple approximation — no weight updates, no matrix inversion — that performs almost as well?

*** Its Solution

They applied Jensen's inequality to the quadratic form in SparseGPT's objective to obtain an upper bound. That upper bound has a beautiful closed form: *the importance of each weight = |w_ij| × ||x_j||₂.* That is, the absolute weight value times the norm of the corresponding input activation.

No Hessian inverse, no weight updates. The scoring rule fits in a single line of numpy. On LLaMA the results nearly match SparseGPT, and runtime drops from 10 minutes to 4 minutes per layer.

Analogy: SparseGPT hires a specialist team to precisely compute each employee's marginal contribution. Wanda ranks employees by "salary × hours worked" and cuts the bottom 50%. Crude — yet surprisingly accurate.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It works because the scale variation of activations in Transformers is enormous (outliers again), and weight × activation norm happens to capture "how much actual work this weight does in practice."

The pit it dug: Wanda optimises an *upper bound*. Jensen's relaxation discards all within-row interactions among weights — each weight is scored independently, and coupling vanishes. That guarantees a gap between the mask Wanda finds and the truly optimal mask.

** 2024 | DSnoT: Since the Mask Is Suboptimal, Swap

*** What Problem It Saw

Zhang et al. looked at Wanda's mask and reasoned: since it solves an upper bound, there should theoretically exist *swaps* — replacing a retained weight w_a with a pruned weight w_b — that lower the true objective. Can we find these swaps dynamically?

*** Its Solution

Borrow from Dynamic Sparse Training: start from Wanda's mask, then iteratively drop some weights and bring others back. Two *proxy signals* determine which to drop and which to recover:
1. The estimated reduction in reconstruction error from the swap (approximated)
2. The variance of the weight across different inputs (more stable = more worth keeping)

Analogy: Wanda gives you an initial deployment; DSnoT continuously sends scouts to rotate soldiers — but the scouts use simplified criteria rather than observing the battlefield directly.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because the direction of swapping is correct — any mask has room for local improvement.

The pit it dug: *proxy signals provide no guarantee of monotone decrease in the true objective.* After swapping a pair, DSnoT predicts improvement, but computing the true objective may reveal the situation worsened. The whole process is like hiking a mountain in thick fog — you cannot tell whether each step actually goes downhill.

** 2025 | SparseSwaps: Making Swaps Exact

*** What Problem It Saw

Zimmer et al. looked at DSnoT and thought: swapping is the right idea, but proxy signals are a cheap substitute. Can we *exactly* compute the effect of swapping a pair of weights on the true objective, and only accept swaps that provably decrease it?

In principle yes — the loss change from one swap is a quadratic form involving the Gram matrix XX^T, and the formula can be written down. But naively evaluating every candidate pair (a, b) requires matrix operations whose count grows combinatorially.

*** Its Solution

The key trick: *assume equal sparsity per row.* This one extra constraint decouples all rows — each row can be optimised independently, reducing the layer-level optimisation to thousands of independent small problems.

Within each small problem, given the current mask, find the swap (a, b) that decreases the true objective the most. The authors derive that this optimal swap can be looked up in O(1) using a precomputed Gram matrix. The algorithm thus becomes:
1. Start from a mask produced by Wanda or RIA
2. Each row independently and repeatedly performs the optimal 1-swap
3. Every swap provably decreases the true objective (theoretical guarantee)

After 100 swap iterations, per-layer reconstruction error is reduced by up to 60% compared to Wanda. Everything runs on GPU with almost no hyperparameters.

Analogy: DSnoT decides whether to rotate soldiers based on a weather forecast. SparseSwaps installs an *accurate loss gauge* — it only accepts swaps where the gauge reading truly drops. And the "equal sparsity per row" constraint, which seems like a sacrifice, actually decouples the problem so that all rows can be parallelised on GPU.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

Two reasons it works:
- *Exact objective computation* — the Gram matrix gives a closed-form expression for the effect of each swap
- *Row decoupling* — reduces what would be an NP-hard layer-level problem to thousands of independent small problems

The pit it dug (acknowledged by the authors): layer-wise reconstruction error is itself only a *proxy for final model quality*. SparseSwaps optimises this proxy to near perfection, but sometimes per-layer error drops while perplexity does not — indicating that *the real bottleneck may no longer be mask selection but the layer-wise objective framework itself.*

* Frontier Extensions

Several threads follow SparseSwaps:

- *LLM-Barber* (2024): another mask-refinement route using block-aware reconstruction with weight gradient information, focusing on cross-layer coordination rather than optimal single-layer swaps.
- *ProxSparse* (2025): learns masks via proximal operators, improving over SparseGPT by 18% on LLaMA-2-7B. Converts mask selection from discrete combinatorial optimisation into a continuous differentiable problem.
- *"A Free Lunch in LLM Compression: Revisiting Retraining"* (2025): a different angle — re-examining lightweight retraining after pruning. Finds that with proper execution, simple Wanda + retraining outperforms complex SparseGPT.

These three threads represent three different answers to the question "what do you do once you accept the layer-wise framework has limits": cross-layer reconstruction (Barber), continuous relaxation (ProxSparse), or partly returning to retraining (Free Lunch).

* Insight

On the surface this line is "pruning getting smarter." What is really happening underneath is a *tug-of-war between the objective function and available compute*:

- 1989–1993: compute allows a precise objective (full second-order Hessian), but only for small networks
- 2015: networks suddenly grow large; no one can compute the Hessian; *forced retreat to the crudest possible objective* (magnitude)
- 2023: LLMs make magnitude pruning completely invalid; *the objective must be upgraded* — SparseGPT finds a compromise in "row-wise Hessian decomposition"
- 2023+: Wanda walks back in the other direction — *coarsening the objective again* to gain speed, but because it found an unusually good approximation (weight × activation norm), accuracy holds
- 2024–2026: precision returns — since Wanda/SparseGPT solutions are not optimal, *refine them*. SparseSwaps achieves the unification of "exact optimisation" and "GPU scalability" at this step

*The true turning point is not that some algorithm got smarter. It is that the field finally acknowledged: a one-shot pruning decision is insufficient. The right approach is two-stage — a fast method for a warm start, then a slow but accurate method for refinement.* This is a paradigm shift from "one-shot optimisation" to "warm start + iterative refinement."

Where might things go next? The vulnerability SparseSwaps itself exposes points the way: *the layer-wise reconstruction error is approaching its own ceiling.* To make further progress, either cross-layer joint optimisation (the LLM-Barber direction) is needed, or the objective must change from "reconstruct the current layer" to "approximate the first- or second-order signal of the downstream task loss." Pruning may need to reconnect to end-to-end signals.

* Takeaways

For researchers:

1. *A constraint that looks like a sacrifice may be the key to decoupling.* SparseSwaps added "equal sparsity per row" — seemingly a weakening — but that is precisely what reduced an NP-hard problem to a set of parallelisable small problems. *Do not rush to tackle the most general version; first find a constraint that reveals structure.*

2. *"Warm start + refinement" is an underrated paradigm.* In many problems, "one-shot optimal algorithm" is inferior to "find a decent fast solution + apply strict iterative improvement." SparseSwaps does not replace Wanda; it stands on Wanda's shoulders. That is a humble and highly effective position.

3. *When reviving old methods, find the right lever.* OBS was strangled by compute in the 1990s; 30 years later SparseGPT resurrected it — not because compute got cheaper in the relevant way, but because someone noticed that *row-wise decomposition lets all rows share the Hessian*. This kind of "find a different decomposition that makes an old method tractable again" still has room to run in many fields.

Actionable takeaways:
- If you are working on any *combinatorial optimisation × large model* problem, check first whether adding a "uniform per-row / per-block" constraint decouples it
- If your baseline is some upper-bound or approximation, *running refinement on top of its output* very likely yields a free lunch
- Do not treat "approximate algorithms" and "exact algorithms" as opposites — they should be chained together
