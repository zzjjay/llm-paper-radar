* The River of Questions

Does knowledge distillation actually help during LLM post-training? Once you have enough data, does it become wasted effort?

Why is this hard to answer? Because from 2015 to 2024, distillation research was mostly conducted on small models, small tasks, and small datasets. Everyone said "distillation works," but no one had answered: when your training data grows from 10,000 to 1,000,000 examples, when the teacher and student learn from the exact same data, does distillation still have an edge? More crucially — what is distillation actually teaching the student? Is it something absent from the data?

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2006 | ModelCompression: Compressing a Large Model's "Knowledge" into a Small One

*** What Problem Did It See

In 2006, Bucila, Caruana, and colleagues faced a practical dilemma: the most accurate industrial models were ensembles of hundreds of sub-models, requiring hundreds of forward passes per prediction and incurring prohibitive deployment costs. Small models were fast but far less accurate. Could you have both?

*** Its Solution

Their insight was disarmingly simple: since an ensemble can produce an "answer" for any input, let it label a large collection of unlabeled data with pseudo-labels, then train a small model on those labels from scratch. The small model learns not "which class is correct," but "which class the large model considers more likely."

Result: a model a thousand times smaller and a thousand times faster that nearly matched ensemble accuracy on multiple datasets.

*** Why It Worked, and What Hole It Left

Pseudo-labels carry implicit *ranking information* — they don't just say "this is a cat," they say "this looks more like a cat than a leopard." That is strictly more informative than a one-hot label.

But one obvious hole remained: pseudo-labels are still effectively "hard labels" — dominated by the highest-scoring class, with the subtle differences among the remaining classes almost entirely buried. The teacher's uncertainty was lost.

** 2015 | KnowledgeDistillation: Temperature — Letting the Teacher's Uncertainty Flow to the Student

*** What Problem Did It See

Hinton, Vinyals, and Dean revisited Bucila's method and located the problem in the softmax. When the teacher assigns probability 0.999 to "cat" and 0.001 to "leopard," after softmax the student can barely perceive that 0.001 — as if the teacher were murmuring and the student could not hear at all.

*** Its Solution

They introduced a *temperature* T. Dividing the softmax exponent by T makes the distribution "softer": the larger T is, the smaller the gap between class probabilities, and the more of the previously buried information is exposed. They called this extra information *dark knowledge* — the teacher's implicit belief that "cat and leopard are more similar to each other than cat and car" is encoded in the softened distribution.

The loss function is a weighted sum of two terms: one against the soft targets, one against the true labels (T = 1).

*** Why It Worked, and What Hole It Left

Intuitively: the teacher's uncertainty is a meaningful prior. A teacher uncertain between "cat" and "leopard" tells the student that these two classes are close in feature space — richer geometric information than any label alone can convey.

New hole: Hinton's experiments were primarily on image classification (MNIST, speech), where each sample is independent. In NLP, especially sequence generation (translation, summarization), every prediction depends on all previous decisions — word-level distillation began to struggle.

** 2016 | SeqKD: Upgrading Distillation from Word Level to Sentence Level

*** What Problem Did It See

When Kim and Rush applied Hinton's distillation to neural machine translation (NMT), they found that word-level soft-target distillation offered limited benefit. The culprit was the *exposure bias* of autoregressive models: during training the teacher always tells the student which word to pick next, but at inference the student must predict from its own past outputs — this training–inference distribution mismatch accumulates errors over long sequences.

*** Its Solution

They proposed Sequence-Level Knowledge Distillation (SeqKD): instead of matching the teacher's distribution at every token, let the teacher *generate complete sentences*, then treat those generated sentences as new "gold answers" for training the student. In essence, the teacher's beam-search output becomes training data.

Result: the student runs 10× faster than the teacher with negligible performance loss, and it can generate fluent sequences without beam search — because it has learned to produce them directly.

*** Why It Worked, and What Hole It Left

Teaching the student "what the teacher is most likely to say" rather than "which word the teacher is most likely to pick at each step" makes the student a clone of the teacher's overall behavior, not a word-by-word imitator.

New hole: in the LLM era, "letting the teacher generate sequences" becomes far more complex. The teacher is no longer a translation model but a general-purpose instruction-following model, and the student's inference distribution diverges sharply from the teacher's.

** 2024a | MiniLLM: Why Switch to Reverse KL

*** What Problem Did It See

Gu et al. discovered a systematic problem when bringing distillation to GPT-style LLMs: standard forward KL (pushing the student's distribution toward the teacher's) forces the student to cover the teacher's entire support, including token sequences the teacher almost never produces. The student therefore over-estimates paths the teacher actually dislikes.

Think of it this way: the teacher says "I would almost never take this path," but the student — obligated to cover the full distribution — still expends effort on it, and the resulting text begins to contain odd sentences.

*** Its Solution

Switch to *reverse KL* (pushing the teacher's distribution toward the student's, equivalent to making the student mode-seek the teacher's high-probability region). Reverse KL is mode-seeking: the student focuses exclusively on the teacher's preferred modes and simply ignores the low-probability regions.

To optimize reverse KL they used policy gradients (a training procedure similar to RLHF): the student samples, and the teacher provides a reward signal.

*** Why It Worked, and What Hole It Left

Reverse KL makes the student more "conservative" but more accurate. Generated responses are higher quality, better calibrated, and more stable over long text.

New hole: the training procedure is more complex, resembling RL, and requires variance reduction. A more fundamental issue also remains: the student learns from the teacher's data *during training*, yet must generate its own sequences *at inference* — the distribution mismatch persists.

** 2024b | GKD: Let the Student Learn from Its Own Outputs

*** What Problem Did It See

Agarwal et al. stated the same issue more precisely: all existing distillation methods (including SeqKD and MiniLLM) train the student on teacher-generated sequences, yet at inference the student generates its own sequences — these two distributions differ, and the more you train, the better the student gets at mimicking the teacher's output, not necessarily at generating high-quality output of its own.

It is like teaching a student to write by having them copy the teacher's notes — no matter how faithfully they copy, their own writing ability is never genuinely exercised.

*** Its Solution

*GKD (Generalized Knowledge Distillation)*: during training, let the student first generate a batch of sequences (on-policy sampling), then use the teacher to score those student-generated sequences and use those scores as the supervision signal.

A parameter λ controls the mix of "student-generated sequences vs. teacher-generated sequences": λ = 1 is fully on-policy; λ = 0 degenerates to standard off-policy distillation. They also tested multiple divergences (forward KL, reverse KL, JSD) and found that the best choice is task-dependent.

*** Why It Worked, and What Hole It Left

Letting the student learn from its own "mistakes" rather than only imitating the teacher's "correct demonstrations" directly targets exposure bias.

On task-agnostic instruction-following distillation, GKD outperforms standard KD by roughly 2% on BBH and 1% on MMLU.

New hole: on-policy sampling increases training compute; and all of these methods (MiniLLM, GKD) were validated on task-specific or small-to-medium-scale settings. Nobody had answered: in the *large-scale post-training* setting for general instruction following, does KD still help?

** 2024c | DistiLLM: Driving Down the Compute Cost of GKD

*** What Problem Did It See

Ko et al. found that GKD-style online distillation is computationally expensive: every update requires the student to sample a fresh batch of sequences, making it several times slower than pure off-policy training.

*** Its Solution

Two improvements: first, a *Skew KL divergence* (SKL) that theoretically unifies the advantages of forward and reverse KL; second, an adaptive off-policy scheme — maintain a replay buffer of historical student outputs, and use a scheduling policy to decide when to reuse them versus sample fresh ones, dramatically reducing the number of on-policy sampling steps.

Result: DistiLLM is up to 4.3× faster than GKD with comparable or better instruction-following performance.

*** Why It Worked, and What Hole It Left

The replay buffer prevents "historical student outputs" from going to waste while avoiding fresh sampling at every step. The skew parameter in SKL provides a tunable knob to flexibly trade off "focus on high-probability regions" against "cover more of the distribution."

Yet the entire line of work — MiniLLM, GKD, DistiLLM — was devoted to demonstrating "KD beats SFT." Nobody asked: *under what conditions does KD's advantage disappear?*

** 2026 | Liu et al.: When Does KD's Advantage Break Down

*** What Problem Did It See

Liu et al. noticed an industry-scale phenomenon: large-scale post-training (e.g., instruction fine-tuning of the Llama-3 series) typically uses hundreds of thousands to nearly a million instruction examples, yet all KD research experiments with only a few thousand to tens of thousands of samples. When data is plentiful, the student can learn a great deal directly from the data — can the teacher still supply additional "dark knowledge"?

They systematically tested GKD vs. SFT at varying data scales on the Tulu 3 dataset (939,000 instruction–response pairs) using Llama-3 and Qwen2.5 model families.

*** Its Solution and Findings

The experiments revealed two clear patterns:

*Pattern 1 — Data volume determines KD's reach*: at 10,000 examples, GKD outperforms SFT by roughly 5 percentage points; as data grows the gap narrows; above 150,000 examples SFT overtakes GKD; at the full 939,000 examples, the two converge to nearly identical performance (~51.5 average score).

*Pattern 2 — The teacher must be stronger than the student*: when the teacher and student train on the same data, the teacher cannot supply knowledge absent from that data — distillation saturates. Switching to a stronger teacher (Llama3.3-70B-Instruct), KD still yields roughly 4% improvement even at full data scale.

*Core thesis*: KD is effective when — and only when — the teacher can supply "knowledge the student cannot readily obtain from the training data."

They also proposed a *two-stage distillation* scheme for low-resource domains (e.g., African low-resource translation, medical summarization): Stage 1 uses teacher-generated synthetic data for SFT; Stage 2 applies GKD on a small amount of real annotated data. With only ~1,000 real examples, this outperforms pure SFT or pure GKD by 1–3 percentage points.

*** Why It Worked, and What Hole It Left

This work is not about proposing a new algorithm; it is about *finding the boundary conditions*. Its contribution is articulating precisely when KD applies: not "more data makes KD better," not "KD always beats SFT," but "the information gap between teacher and student" is the core variable.

Open holes: smaller models benefit more from KD than larger ones (1B > 3B > 8B), but why? A stronger SFT initialization outperforms distillation from scratch, but the interaction between the two remains unclear. Whether distillation at the RLHF/RLAIF stage (rather than the SFT stage) yields different conclusions has not been explored.

* Frontier Extensions

The target paper is the latest arXiv preprint as of June 2026, with no direct citing follow-up work yet. However, two related directions are active in the same period:

*DeepSeek-R1-style reasoning distillation (2025)*: distilling the CoT outputs of a strong reasoning model (DeepSeek-R1 671B) into small models such as Qwen2.5-7B. Simple SFT on these outputs enabled the 7B model to surpass the specifically designed QwQ-32B-Preview. This independently corroborates Liu et al.'s core claim — when the teacher is sufficiently strong and can supply "reasoning chains" absent from the data, distillation works dramatically well.

*Extensions of two-stage distillation (e.g., REDI, DPO-on-distilled)*: on 1.5B-scale small models, first SFT on positive CoT traces, then preference optimization (DPO/SimPO on negative traces), already surpasses single-stage distillation. The conceptual alignment with Liu et al.'s two-stage scheme is high; the difference is a focus on reasoning rather than general instruction following.

As of June 2026, "the data-scale boundary of KD in large-scale post-training" remains an open problem, and Liu et al. represents the latest waypoint on that line.

* Insights

From Bucila to Liu et al., the deeper transformation along this line is not "distillation keeps getting better" — it is that *researchers' understanding of "knowledge" has grown progressively more concrete*.

In 2006, "knowledge" meant the class rankings embedded in pseudo-labels. In 2015, "knowledge" meant the dark information hidden in soft distributions. In 2016, "knowledge" meant the complete sequences generated by the teacher. In 2024, "knowledge" meant the teacher's correction signal applied to the student's own outputs. In 2026, Liu et al. finally offer the sharpest definition: *the teacher can tell the student "things it cannot learn from the training data"* — this is the necessary and sufficient condition for KD to be effective.

This definition has a direct corollary: distillation is not a question of "teaching style" but a question of *information gap*. When the gap exists, distillation works; when the gap disappears (teacher and student learn from the same data), distillation degrades into a noisy version of SFT.

The most likely next step: *actively sensing the information gap* — dynamically identifying which examples carry teacher knowledge that the data alone cannot supply, prioritizing distillation on those examples, and skipping examples where teacher and student have already converged. This would transform distillation from "wholesale transfer" into "targeted remediation."

* Implications

This evolutionary line offers one very concrete lesson about how to do research: *every landmark paper turns a vague "X is good" into a precise "X is good under these specific conditions."*

Bucila turned "ensemble models are accurate" into "transferable under large-scale pseudo-label data." Hinton turned "pseudo-labels are useful" into "soft distributions are useful at temperature T." GKD turned "distillation is good" into "good with on-policy sampling and the right divergence." Liu et al. turned "KD is good" into "good when the teacher's information gap is large enough."

This suggests a reusable strategy for finding the next open problem: take a widely accepted "X is good" conclusion and ask "under what conditions is X not good?" — that question is almost always more valuable, and almost always overlooked, because everyone is busy competing to make X even better and nobody wants to challenge X itself.
