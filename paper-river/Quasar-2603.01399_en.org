* One-liner

Why is LLM inference slow? The field spent three years squeezing every drop out of "guessing the next token," only to discover that the real bottleneck was never the guessing — it was the *verification*. This river traces exactly how that discovery was forced into view, step by step.

* Reading Backward: The Story in Five Layers

** Layer 0. The origin of everything: autoregression is serial

To generate a sentence, a large model emits one token at a time, running a full forward pass for each. A 200-token reply means 200 complete forwards. GPU compute is actually sitting idle, because every step must wait for the previous token to land. This was *the pain point everyone was staring at in 2022*.

** Layer 1. 2022, Leviathan & Chen: "Why not let a small model guess first?"

Two independent groups arrived at the same idea: recruit a small model (say, 7B to draft for 70B) to speculatively generate K tokens in a row. Then let the large model *verify all K guesses in a single parallel forward* — one forward pass yielding multiple tokens, amortizing latency. A mathematically elegant rejection-sampling rule guarantees that the *output distribution is identical to running the large model alone*, with no accuracy loss.

Reported 2–3× speedup; ICML 2023 Oral. The Speculative Decoding paradigm was established.

*What it left behind*: you must maintain two models — two KV caches, two sets of weights, plus coordination overhead in distributed deployments. And the K serial draft steps still introduce uneliminated latency.

** Layer 2. 2024, Medusa: "Does the drafter have to be a separate model?"

Medusa's response: if the pain is "maintaining a second model," then just *let the large model grow extra heads*. Attach a few lightweight parallel decoding heads to the final layer, each predicting a future token position. One forward pass simultaneously outputs the next token, the one after, and the one after that — verified via tree attention.

No "two models," no distillation alignment — pure *self-speculation*.

*What it left behind*: Medusa's heads are trained independently and share no context with each other. The drafted token sequences are therefore incoherent, and acceptance rates fall short. In short: the drafter got faster but dumber.

** Layer 3. 2024, EAGLE: "We're drafting at the wrong layer"

EAGLE strikes deeper. It observes: doing autoregression at the token layer is intrinsically hard because tokens are discrete and information-poor. But *the penultimate-layer feature is continuous and information-dense* — autoregression there should naturally be easier.

Switching to the feature layer alone isn't enough, though — features carry residual uncertainty. EAGLE's key move: *feed the drafter a token sequence shifted one time-step ahead*, using it to disambiguate and make feature prediction precise.

The relay: EAGLE-2 adds a dynamic draft tree; EAGLE-3 further opens the information bottleneck. By EAGLE-3, acceptance length reaches impressive heights and draft-stage overhead is negligible.

*What it left behind*: the drafting path seems to have hit its ceiling — acceptance rate near the top, draft overhead near zero. *So why isn't end-to-end speedup continuing to rise?* That is the stone Quasar picked up.

** Layer 4. 2026, Quasar's discovery: "The jam is in verification, not drafting"

Quasar did something nobody had bothered to do carefully before: decompose end-to-end latency segment by segment. The finding — the drafter is so fast it barely registers; the entire latency is now *carried by the verification stage*.

Why is verification so slow? Because no matter how accurate the drafter is, you must load the entire large model's weights from HBM to SRAM and run a full forward pass. This is a *memory-bandwidth-bound* operation — the bus is only so wide, and verifying more tokens simultaneously cannot save you. Quasar names this the *memory wall* on the verification side.

Every prior speedup had asked "how do we run the large model fewer times?" Quasar asks instead: *can we move less data each time we run it?*

* Side Track: What Was Quantization Doing All Along?

Answering that question requires quantization — but quantization had been applied to the wrong place throughout:

- *SmoothQuant (2022)*: solved the core obstacle to W8A8 quantization in LLMs — activation outliers stretch the quantization range and degrade ordinary values. SmoothQuant uses an equivalence transformation to migrate the "hard-to-quantize" burden from activations into weights, making W8A8 truly viable.
- *AWQ (2024)*: selects salient weights by activation magnitude and quantizes to 4-bit while preserving critical-channel accuracy.
- *Kim 2023 / SpecExec 2024 et al.*: these works begin combining quantization with speculative decoding, but all share the implicit assumption that *verification must remain high-precision* — quantized models serve only as drafters; verification still runs in FP16.

The unspoken assumption that Quasar tears away is precisely that one.

* Reading Forward: The Full Arc in Sequence

Flipping the four layers over and telling the story forward reveals a remarkably clean cognitive evolution:

*Act I (2022).* The community identifies that LLM inference is slow because generation is serial. Leviathan/Chen say: let a small model draft, let the large model verify in parallel. The paradigm is established, but it introduces the engineering burden of two models.

*Act II (early 2024).* Medusa says: why maintain two models at all — can't the large model just grow extra heads? The paradigm simplifies, but draft quality drops.

*Act III (mid-2024).* EAGLE thinks through which layer the drafter should observe — the feature layer, fed with look-ahead tokens. Draft quality reaches the ceiling; draft overhead reaches the floor. Everyone assumes speculative decoding is nearly played out.

*Act IV (2026).* Quasar pulls out a stopwatch and retimes everything, finding that the real bottleneck has quietly changed hands — *verification is now the traffic jam*. Not a compute jam — a bandwidth jam. So the right fix is not to optimize "how to compute" but "how to move less." Quasar applies W8A8 quantization to the verification model, cuts weight-transfer volume in half, and gains another 1.28× throughput end-to-end.

In the Discussion, Quasar runs a dedicated ablation to answer the natural follow-up — "if you want verification to be faster, why not pruning?" The answer: pruning *removes entire layer transformations*, creating a feature gap between the drafter and verifier; acceptance rate collapses to ~1.03, nearly all drafts rejected. Quantization, by contrast, merely *adds small uniform approximation noise to each weight* — the network topology is intact, the rejection-sampling step can dequantize logits back to high precision at the end, and the accuracy loss is bounded throughout.

One-sentence summary of the cognitive shift: *the speculative-decoding bottleneck moved from "computing slowly" to "moving data slowly," and breaking the new wall requires compression, not amputation.*

* Forward-Looking Extensions: What Comes After Quasar?

Quasar is a beginning, not a conclusion. It opens the door to "the verification side can be touched too," and at least three directions lie beyond that threshold:

1. *More aggressive quantization*: W4A8 / mixed-precision verification, cutting bandwidth by another half. The challenge is that logit shift can no longer be fully recovered by simple dequantization.
2. *Sparse attention on the verification side*: since verification is also memory-bound, KV reads for attention are a major component. Sparse or linear attention designed specifically for the verification pass may emerge.
3. *Joint draft-verification scheduling*: when both sides are pushed to extremes, scheduling itself becomes the new bottleneck — when to submit a draft batch, when to abandon it, when to vary depth.

* The Core Takeaway

The most important thing to carry away from this river is not the fact that "Quasar uses quantization" — it is a *research sensibility*:

When an entire field is optimizing the same metric (draft quality, acceptance length) and the headline number (end-to-end latency) begins to plateau — pull out a stopwatch and decompose the time budget again. In all likelihood, the true bottleneck has quietly changed hands. *Any domain that feels "nearly played out" is worth one more latency profiling pass.*

At its core, Quasar's story is this: while everyone was racing to improve the guessing, it went back and re-weighed the verification.
