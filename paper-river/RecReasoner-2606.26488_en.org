* The River of the Problem

Edge chips (MCUs, 4–8 MB SRAM) can run reasoning models, but only compressed versions of them. The primary compression tool is quantization — replacing 32-bit floats with 8-bit integers (INT8) or even 4-bit integers (INT4). For ordinary language models, this pipeline is well-established and mature.

But when the model being compressed is a *Recursive Reasoner*, every intuition breaks down. These models do not simply "generate tokens left to right"; instead they feed the same batch of internal states back through the same network for H rounds. Quantization error does not spread along the token sequence — it accumulates across recursive loops. The result: local accuracy (per-cell correctness) stays intact, global accuracy (whole-puzzle solve rate) collapses to zero.

This paper is the first work to study this phenomenon systematically. To understand it, we first need to trace how the "recursive reasoner" lineage grew.

* Forward Evolutionary Narrative

** Chapter Zero: An Old Question — Can a Model "Think a Little Longer"?

When humans solve problems, harder ones take more time than easy ones. Neural networks before 2017 could not do this: no matter how complex the input, every data point passed through the same number of layers and performed the same amount of computation.

In 2019, Dehghani et al. proposed the *Universal Transformer* (UT) to break this constraint.

The idea is surprisingly simple: instead of stacking 64 different layers, reuse the same layer's parameters in a loop for H steps. Weights are shared in the depth direction, parameter count drops substantially, and in principle each input token can be assigned a different number of computation steps (Adaptive Computation Time, ACT). Theoretically, architectures of this kind can even be shown to be Turing-complete.

UT outperformed ordinary Transformers on algorithmic reasoning tasks. But the experiments contained a peculiar split: *more loops were not always better*. Beyond a certain threshold, model performance actually declined. Why?

** Chapter One: More Thinking, Worse Results — Bansal et al., 2022

Bansal et al. gave this phenomenon a name: *overthinking*.

Their finding: after a recurrent network loops past a certain point, behavior begins to degrade — like a person who has argued themselves into a dead end.

Even more troubling was the *absence of a diagnostic signal*. You could only know whether the model had "thought too much" after seeing the answer. No real-time signal during inference could tell you "stop, that's enough."

Their mitigations were: the Recall architecture (keeping a copy of the original input inside the network to prevent forgetting) and progressive training (teaching the model behavior that is invariant to the number of loops).

These measures alleviated overthinking but left a deeper question untouched: *when you compress or deploy these models on constrained hardware, how does error propagate?* Nobody asked.

** Chapter Two: Recurrence as a Compute Bank — Geiping et al., 2025

By 2025, allocating compute for large-model inference had become a hot topic. Chain-of-Thought was the dominant conversation: let the model generate more tokens to "think more."

Geiping et al. proposed a different direction: rather than generating more tokens, let the same internal representations (carry state) cycle through the same network for more rounds. *Token generation is horizontal compute expansion; recursive looping is vertical compute expansion.*

They established the first large-scale pretraining scheme for a recurrent language model, demonstrating that this path is viable — loop count can be adjusted dynamically at inference time, and the model can learn behavior that converges progressively as loops deepen.

But their model ran on FP32, targeting server inference. *Edge deployment and model compression were not touched.*

** Chapter Three: Beating Billion-Parameter Models with 27M Parameters — HRM, 2025

In June 2025, a team from Singapore released the *Hierarchical Reasoning Model* (HRM).

They pushed the question to an extreme: large LLMs rely on massive pretraining and chain-of-thought data to perform complex reasoning. Can a small model be designed that needs none of that?

HRM used two nested recurrent modules: a "slow system" for high-level abstract planning and a "fast system" for rapid local computation, running at different tempos (the slow system fires once every K fast-system cycles). The inspiration came from dual-process theory of human cognition.

The results were striking: 27M parameters, trained on only 1,000 examples, surpassed language models with orders of magnitude more parameters on ARC-AGI (a hard benchmark for human-like reasoning) and extremely hard Sudoku.

*But the dual-network design was puzzling.* The authors themselves admitted the mechanism was "not yet well understood." What happens if you remove the slow system? Is the hierarchy truly the key, or is recurrence alone sufficient?

** Chapter Four: Stripped Down as Far as Possible — TRM, 2025

Jolicoeur-Martineau turned these questions into a systematic ablation study.

He dismantled HRM's dual modules, replaced them with a single 2-layer network, cut parameters from 27M to 7M, and validated one by one whether each design choice was actually necessary.

The conclusion was surprising: *simpler is better*. TRM (Tiny Recursive Model) achieved 45% accuracy on ARC-AGI-1 and 8% on ARC-AGI-2 — surpassing DeepSeek R1 and Gemini 2.5 Pro at the time.

The most important ablation finding: *what truly matters is recurrence itself, not the hierarchy*. Removing the hierarchy and letting a single network loop repeatedly actually produced better results.

But TRM still ran at FP32 precision. Even 7M parameters impose a huge burden on edge MCUs; squeezing into 4 MB of SRAM requires INT4. And that question — *what happens when you compress a recursive reasoner* — had never been studied systematically.

** Chapter Five: What Survives After Compression — Jim et al., 2026 (Target Paper)

Jim et al. tackled this question head-on.

The objective was clear: quantize TRM and HRM for deployment on edge MCUs with only 4–8 MB of SRAM and roughly 500 GFLOPs/puzzle of compute budget.

Standard wisdom from LLM compression: INT8 is usually lossless, INT4 degrades accuracy, and QAT (quantization-aware training) or distillation can recover the loss.

*Jim et al. found that every one of these intuitions fails.*

The reason lies in a structural difference:

In an ordinary LLM, quantization error propagates along the token chain — each position independently introduces a small amount of noise, and downstream tokens carry a bit of that noise. This is error diffusion in an averaging sense, which can be mitigated by token-level loss functions in QAT or distillation.

In TRM, the same batch of carry states is fed back through the same network for H rounds. *The quantization error from round one enters round two; round two's error piles on top of it; and so on — error accumulates systematically in the loop direction.* This is an entirely different error dynamic.

**Finding One: Architecture Determines Robustness**

Same task (extremely hard Sudoku), same FP32 accuracy (~73%), but INT4 performance diverges sharply across three architectures:

#+begin_example
Architecture         FP32      Naive INT4   carry fidelity (phi)
------------------------------------------------------------------
TRM-MLP-Mixing      73.8%      10.2%         0.35  (catastrophic collapse)
TRM-Attention       73.1%      69.5%         0.87  (near-lossless)
HRM (different family) 47.7%  48.4%         0.98  (even slightly improved)
#+end_example

MLP-mixing's local updates accumulate error across loops; the attention mechanism's global dependencies provide some form of self-correction across recurrent iterations.

**Finding Two: Local Accuracy Survives, Global Accuracy Collapses**

Naive INT4, pruning, distillation, and QAT all exhibit the same failure pattern: cell-level prediction accuracy (local) is largely preserved, while whole-puzzle solve accuracy (global) drops to near zero.

This means the problem is not "poorly calibrated quantized parameters" — the model has learned to emit locally plausible tokens at each cell, but its ability to satisfy global constraints (i.e., actual reasoning) has been quietly destroyed. QAT scores each cell with cross-entropy rather than scoring the whole puzzle, *effectively rewarding superficially reasonable local behavior while ignoring global collapse.*

**Finding Three: A New Diagnostic Metric — Carry-Trajectory Fidelity (φ)**

Jim et al. introduced a label-free real-time diagnostic metric: run both the FP32 model and the compressed model through H recurrent rounds, then compute the cosine similarity between their final carry states.

- Consecutive carry similarity (between adjacent rounds) is nearly insensitive to quantization (change ≤ 0.04 before vs. after compression).
- φ (FP32 final state vs. compressed final state) is monotonically informative: when INT4 is harmless, φ ≥ 0.99; when collapse is catastrophic, φ = 0.35.

This metric requires no labeled evaluation set and can be computed in real time at deployment — the first label-free compression diagnostic tool adapted to recursive reasoners.

**Deployment Recipe**:
- 8 MB SoC: INT8 + flash-streaming embeddings (stream-load the 99.4 MB embedding matrix, keeping active SRAM at 2 KB) → 500 GFLOPs/puzzle, accuracy near FP32
- 4 MB MCU: calibrated INT4 (per-channel quantization, no retraining) → TRM-Attn architecture, extremely hard Sudoku accuracy recovers from 10.2% to 71.9% (FP32 baseline: 73.8%)

** Chapter Six: Frontier and Open Questions

The target paper was released on June 25, 2026, with no known downstream papers yet.

The questions it leaves behind are clearly framed:

1. *Can φ become a training objective?* Current QAT uses cross-entropy; if we directly maximize φ (aligning the compressed model's carry trajectory with the FP32 trajectory), can we fix the objective-misalignment problem at its root?
2. *What is the robustness mechanism behind attention recurrence?* We know it is more robust, but not why. Can that mechanism inform the design of recursive architectures specifically optimized for edge compression?
3. *Does φ remain stable across tasks?* Current experiments focus on Sudoku and ARC; broad generalizability remains to be verified.
4. *Joint optimization of dynamic depth and precision?* Recurrence depth H and quantization precision are two tunable variables, currently studied separately. Would a joint search find a better Pareto frontier?

* Deeper Insight

On the surface, this evolutionary line is about "recursive Transformers moving from theoretical toy to edge chip."

But the deeper shift is this: *researchers' understanding of "computation granularity" underwent two discontinuous jumps, and each jump produced an entirely new error-propagation structure.*

First jump: from "layer" to "token." Chain-of-Thought treats each generated token as one step of thought; quantization noise is an averaging phenomenon at the token level, and standard bit-level loss functions can repair it.

Second jump: from "token" to "loop." Recursive reasoners treat each carry-state update as one step of thought; quantization noise becomes cumulative drift across loops. The failure point of previously adequate tools (QAT, distillation) shifts accordingly — not because they are imprecise, but because they are aimed at the wrong granularity.

Jim et al.'s core contribution is not a new quantization algorithm; it is *making this granularity shift visible*: carry-trajectory fidelity turns "trajectory deviation at the loop level" into something that can be measured and optimized.

This implies that if recursive reasoning models continue moving toward smaller, more edge-bound deployments, the quantization community will need to fundamentally redesign its loss functions and diagnostic tools — upgrading from the token level to the loop level.
