* The Problem River

Take a model with tens of billions of parameters, slash it from FP16 down to INT4, and make it still speak coherently — and after the slashing, you cannot let the small per-layer errors snowball through the stack and wreck the model entirely.

Why is this hard? A large model's parameters are computed through interlocking layers. Nudge the first layer even slightly, and the second layer receives a different input, the third layer receives a different input still … by the final layer, an error that started at 0.01 can turn into complete nonsense. Thirty years of research have produced successive schemes for computing "which weight can be moved at the lowest cost," yet each generation has had to wrestle with the "too expensive to compute" or "not accurate enough" problems left by the previous one. RPIQ is the latest piece of that puzzle, dated 2026.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1990 | OBD: The First Time a Network Could Say "Which Parameter Is Expendable"

*** What Problem It Saw

Pruning at that time followed an unspoken rule: delete weights with small absolute values. But this was crude — a numerically small weight might sit on a critical path, and removing it could break the network. Nobody could rigorously answer "if I delete this weight, how much does the model's loss actually increase?"

*** Its Solution

LeCun argued: expand the loss function as a Taylor series around the current weights. The first-order term is zero (training has already converged), so the impact of removing weight w_i on the loss is dominated by the second-order term (the Hessian). Specifically, deleting w_i raises the loss by approximately ½ · H_ii · w_i² — so weights should be ranked by H_ii · w_i² and removed smallest-first.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Left Behind)

It gave, for the first time, a mathematically rigorous answer to "whom to delete." But OBD took a shortcut: it used only the diagonal of the Hessian, pretending weights were independent. In reality, neural-network weights are highly coupled — move one, and the optimal positions of others shift too. OBD offered no compensation for this knock-on effect.

** 1993 | OBS: When You Delete One Weight, Let the Others Adjust to Compensate

*** What Problem It Saw

Hassibi and Stork directly named OBD's flaw: the diagonal-Hessian assumption does not hold. Weights interact, and when you remove one, the rest should be adjusted to minimize the resulting damage.

*** Its Solution

Use the full inverse Hessian H⁻¹. When weight w_i is chosen for deletion, every remaining weight w_j is updated simultaneously by δw_j ∝ -H⁻¹_ji · w_i / H⁻¹_ii — this single adjustment nearly cancels the loss increase caused by removing w_i.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Left Behind)

Intuitively: pull out one bolt, and tighten the adjacent bolts a fraction — the whole structure stays stable. OBS was far more accurate than OBD. The cost, however, was computing H⁻¹, a matrix whose size scales as the square of the parameter count. Feasible for a hundred thousand parameters; completely intractable for a billion. So OBS was largely forgotten after the 1990s and only rediscovered in the large-model era.

** 2022 | OBQ: If OBS Can Delete Weights, It Can Also Round Them

*** What Problem It Saw

OBS was designed for pruning. But quantization is fundamentally also about moving weights — rounding 0.7314 to the nearest grid point (say 0.75) is equivalent to changing the weight by 0.0186. OBS's logic of "move one weight, then compensate the rest with H⁻¹" applies in principle just as well. Remarkably, nobody had connected the two before 2022.

*** Its Solution

Frantar and Alistarh transplanted OBS directly into quantization: pick one weight, round it to the nearest quantization grid point, then use H⁻¹ to spread the rounding error across all remaining unquantized weights so they absorb it. Round one, compensate the group — repeated for every weight.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Left Behind)

This elevated quantization from "independently round each number" to "round one, adjust the crowd." Accuracy was indeed higher. But it fully inherited OBS's old burden: processing weights one by one, with hundreds of millions of parameters per layer, makes the compensation updates alone take days. OBQ was fine for quantizing BERT; quantizing OPT-175B was out of the question.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Making OBQ Fast Enough for Large Models

*** What Problem It Saw

OBQ's algorithm was correct — just too slow. Every time one weight was quantized, all remaining unquantized weights had to be updated. One hundred million parameters per layer means one hundred million updates; for a 175B model, the computation would take years.

*** Its Solution

GPTQ did three things:
1. *Column-wise blocking*: Instead of processing one weight at a time, it works column by column, applying the same compensation to an entire block of columns.
2. *Lazy batch updates*: After quantizing a small block (e.g., 128 columns), accumulated compensation is applied all at once to the next block — fast processing within blocks, one accounting step between them.
3. *Cholesky decomposition*: H⁻¹ is expressed as a Cholesky factor, avoiding repeated matrix inversion and improving numerical stability.

Result: OPT-175B can be quantized to 4-bit on four GPUs in a matter of hours while retaining accuracy. GPTQ rapidly became the de-facto standard for large-model quantization.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Left Behind)

GPTQ cleverly reduced OBQ's O(n²) per-element updates to O(n) per-block updates. But this speedup came at a price — block-to-block processing is one-way greedy:

- When block 1 is quantized, all subsequent blocks are assumed to still be floating-point (exact).
- When block 2 is quantized, block 1 is already frozen; block 2 can only "accept reality" and adapt.
- When block M is quantized, all M-1 preceding blocks are frozen, and all accumulated error presses down on this last block.

Errors cascade like dominoes — a small disturbance at the front, and the tail collapses. On tasks where errors compound through layers — long-context understanding, multi-turn dialogue — the model produces noticeably incoherent output. This is the problem RPIQ sets out to solve.

** 2026 | RPIQ: Run GPTQ, Then Iterate Again and Let Blocks Compensate Each Other

*** What Problem It Saw

The authors state GPTQ's core pathology plainly: "block-by-block optimization process lacks a comprehensive consideration of inter-block dependencies, leading to the accumulation of errors across blocks."

Specifically: GPTQ is *one-shot* — once a block is committed it is never revisited. But since errors accumulate, earlier blocks should be allowed to fine-tune themselves in light of how later blocks actually turned out, and later blocks should be allowed to re-tune given the quantized output of earlier blocks. GPTQ has no such feedback channel.

Furthermore, conventional multi-round iterative schemes (BRECQ, AdaRound) reload the entire calibration dataset on every pass — infeasible on the memory-constrained edge devices used for visually impaired assistance.

*** Its Solution

Three moves in combination:

1. *Phase 1 is just GPTQ*: Run GPTQ verbatim to obtain an "acceptable but not great" initial quantized result. Crucially, keep the global Hessian matrix and the initial quantized weights *in memory* — do not discard them the way GPTQ normally would.

2. *Single Instance Calibration*: The second phase requires no new calibration data. It reuses the *last batch* of calibration inputs X_last already in memory, together with the original full-precision output Y_orig. One batch is enough — because the global Hessian was built during phase 1 and already "remembers" the second-order statistics of the entire calibration set. This preserves global information while eliminating the cost of reloading data.

3. *Gauss-Seidel inter-block iteration*: In the second phase, traverse blocks in order; for each block i, compute the current output residual D = Y_orig - Y_quant, then absorb this residual into the current block's weights using H⁻¹. The key: *when block i is being updated, blocks 0 through i-1 already reflect this round's latest updates* — the Gauss-Seidel style (as opposed to the Jacobi style, which would use last round's stale values throughout). Blocks can now "hear" each other; errors no longer accumulate unidirectionally.

If GPTQ is a single-pass assembly line, RPIQ is "run the assembly line once, then have every station join hands and calibrate each other for a few rounds."

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Left Behind)

Intuitively: phase 1 (GPTQ) already deposits you near a decent valley floor, so phase 2 does not need a long trek — a few rounds of Gauss-Seidel local refinement are enough to descend to a deeper minimum. Across OPT, Qwen, LLaMA, and CogVLM2, 4-bit quantization accuracy tracks full precision closely.

Remaining issues:
- Phase 2 does not reload data, but it must keep an extra copy of the global Hessian in memory — non-trivial overhead for very large models.
- The effectiveness of single-instance calibration depends on "the last batch adequately representing the distribution" — if the calibration data distribution has drifted, phase 2 could push the model in an unexpected direction.
- Gauss-Seidel convergence speed depends on matrix properties (diagonal dominance, etc.); the paper does not provide a convergence proof.

* Frontier Extensions

Several parallel lines from the same period also attempt to patch GPTQ's shortcomings:

** 2024 | GPTQT: Split Quantization into "Linear + Binary Code" Two Stages

GPTQT observes that GPTQ stops once weights are mapped to integers. But those integers can themselves be re-encoded (e.g., decomposed as a combination of binary bases), and the residual information can be used to further reduce output error. Both GPTQT and RPIQ use two stages, but they optimize different things — GPTQT optimizes the *encoding*, RPIQ optimizes *inter-block compensation*.

** 2025 | COMQ: Replace the Greedy Search with Coordinate Descent

COMQ recognizes that GPTQ's "greedy" approach is a coarse form of optimization. It applies coordinate descent (moving only one quantized weight and its scale factor at a time) and iterates multiple rounds within a layer. Compared with RPIQ, COMQ goes *deeper within a layer*, while RPIQ goes *wider across blocks* — the former digs fine-grained within a small scope, the latter coordinates at a medium scale.

** 2025 | GPTAQ: Introduce Asymmetric Calibration

GPTAQ achieves higher-accuracy local solutions, but like COMQ it starts iterating from an uncalibrated initial point and therefore needs more rounds to converge. RPIQ's advantage is that GPTQ hands it a good starting point, so only a few rounds suffice.

* Insight

What has truly been evolving along this lineage is not "quantization technology" per se, but *the scale at which researchers understand the mutual influence among parameters* — and that scale has kept expanding.

- *1990*: Parameters are pretended to be independent of one another (OBD diagonal assumption).
- *1993*: Parameter coupling is acknowledged, but compensation happens only once per deletion event (OBS).
- *2022 OBQ*: That single compensation is applied individually to every weight — fine-grained, but expensive.
- *2022 GPTQ*: Granularity expands from single parameters to *blocks*, speed recovers, but coupling between blocks is once again ignored — a retreat to a "block-level OBD diagonal assumption."
- *2026 RPIQ*: Block-level coupling is picked back up and addressed with Gauss-Seidel inter-block closed-loop — essentially re-enacting the spirit of OBS at a *higher scale*.

History has executed a spiral ascent across scales — from weight coupling, to block coupling. The next step is almost foreseeable: as models grow toward the trillion-parameter range, *inter-layer coupling* and *inter-expert coupling (MoE)* will be the next scale to be noticed, then neglected, then patched. Someone will write "Layer-level Gauss-Seidel" or "Expert-level OBS."

A second thread worth watching: RPIQ's "single instance calibration" hints at a broader trend — *reusing intermediate results already in memory to avoid any recomputation*. As the bottleneck of large-model quantization shifts from compute toward memory bandwidth and I/O, more and more algorithms will be shaped by this constraint.

* Takeaways

- *When hunting for research problems*: Look for the compromises the previous generation made in the name of speed — the holes they left are your opportunity. GPTQ sacrificed inter-block coupling for throughput; RPIQ builds directly on that sacrifice. Every speedup inevitably leaves a scar somewhere.
- *When reading papers*: Don't just read the method — read the related work and note *whom it is criticizing*. The target of the critique plus the specific point of criticism usually tells you more about where the field is genuinely stuck than the proposed method itself does.
- *When engineering*: When someone says "not enough memory," the answer is not necessarily new hardware. Ask: "Is there any intermediate result I could reuse?" RPIQ's single-instance calibration embodies exactly this — the global Hessian is expensive global information, but it's already in memory; why reprocess the calibration data from scratch?
- This means you can ask, in your own work: "Is my heaviest cost something I could actually retrieve from memory?" — that question is usually ten times cheaper than buying more GPUs.
