* The River of the Problem

A large model is brilliant but too large and expensive — we want to compress it into a small model. How do we teach that small model?

On the surface this looks like "a teacher teaching a student," but the devil is in the details. When the teacher gives the student a question, should it only say "the correct answer is A," or should it hand over the entire probability table — "I think A at 60%, B at 30%, C at 10%"?

Intuitively the latter seems obvious — more information. But for ten years this route has repeatedly hit a wall: the information-richer approach often loses to the information-poorer one. Even stranger — the more the student resembles the teacher during training, the worse it performs at inference.

This is an evolutionary line that has been wrestling with the "train-inference gap" for a decade. Each generation spotted one detail their predecessors missed, and left one new pitfall for the next. The Bridge-Garden paper is the most recent move as of 2026. Its message is: "The earliest simple scheme was already enough — nobody just explained why."

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Hinton et al.: The First to Say "The Teacher Tells the Student Not Just the Answer, but Also Its Hesitation"

*** What Problem It Saw

Before Hinton, the standard way to compress a large model into a small one was to feed the training data through the small model again and let it learn from scratch. But the small model has limited capacity, and brute-force learning simply cannot capture the large model's nuanced judgments.

Hinton noticed something: when a large model decides an image is a "cat," its output is not a simple "cat 100%," but rather "cat 92%, dog 5%, tiger 2%, other 1%." The "dog 5%, tiger 2%" encodes enormously valuable information — it tells the student that cats and dogs and tigers are similar to each other, and very different from tables or cars. This "dark knowledge" is the source of the large model's intelligence.

*** Its Solution

Have the student learn not the final label but the teacher's entire probability distribution. Technically, KL divergence measures the gap between two distributions, and the student's distribution is trained to track the teacher's. This is soft-label distillation.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfall It Buried)

Every number in the soft label is one supervision signal, effectively turning a single label into thousands of signals, making information density explode. So the small model can learn the large model's "judgment style," not just its conclusions.

The pitfall left behind: Hinton worked on image classification, where each image has one label and the "distribution" is a one-shot event. But language models are sequential — a sentence is generated token by token. This means the distribution must be aligned at every position, and one mistake propagates through the entire sentence. In 2015, nobody yet realized how serious this would be.

** 2015 | Bengio et al.: In the Same Year, Another Team Dug Up the Root Cause of Sequential Generation — Exposure Bias

*** What Problem It Saw

Bengio's team, working on RNN sequence generation, discovered a strange phenomenon: training performance was excellent, but inference collapsed. Closer inspection revealed that during training the model sees the true preceding words, but during inference it must consume its own generated words. Once it makes a small error (say, generating "a" instead of "the"), it continues generating from a state it has never seen, drifting further and further off course.

They named the phenomenon *exposure bias* — the model is "exposed" at inference to an input distribution it never encountered during training.

*** Its Solution

Scheduled sampling: at each training step, randomly decide whether the next input is the ground-truth word or the model's own freshly generated word, so the model is pre-exposed to the messy states arising from its own output.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfall It Buried)

The core intuition is "the training distribution must approach the inference distribution." All subsequent distillation methods for generation are fundamentally addressing this gap.

The pitfall left behind: scheduled sampling was considered insufficient for the LLM era — it cannot learn from a strong teacher; it only trains on its own output. It was only when knowledge distillation entered the picture that people realized: exposure bias can actually be mitigated by the teacher.

** 2016 | Kim & Rush: Since Distribution Matching Is So Troublesome on Sequences, Take a Different Road — Let the Teacher Generate Data, Student Fits with MLE

*** What Problem It Saw

Kim and Rush found, while working on machine translation distillation, that Hinton's soft-label approach performed very poorly on seq2seq tasks. One reason: computing KL at every step of a sequence is expensive. Another: strong teacher models from large companies (such as ChatGPT) are completely closed-source — you can obtain generated sentences but cannot access the logits.

*** Its Solution

Abandon soft labels entirely. Let the teacher generate a large set of (input, output) pairs, treat them as a new training corpus, and have the student fit them with standard maximum likelihood estimation. This is *hard-label / sequence-level KD*.

If you take Hinton's approach and gradually sharpen the teacher's full probability distribution — sharper, sharper, sharper — until only the argmax token remains, you arrive at Kim & Rush's approach. The two lines are simply the two ends of the same spectrum.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfall It Buried)

Engineeringly trivial: no need for the teacher to supply logits online during training. Later work — Alpaca, Vicuna, and all the "distill a small model with ChatGPT" projects — are intellectual descendants of this line.

The pitfall left behind: hard labels discard all information about alternative responses in the teacher's distribution. In theory this is strictly worse than soft labels. Yet strangely, in practice it often does not lose. Why? This mystery was not answered head-on until Bridge-Garden, a decade later.

** 2024 | Gu et al. (MiniLLM): The LLM Era Arrives; First, Audit Hinton's Old Work — Forward KL Was the Wrong Choice

*** What Problem It Saw

In 2024, LLM distillation became hot and people applied Hinton's forward KL directly. MiniLLM identified a fundamental flaw: forward KL is *mode-covering* — it forces the student to cover every token the teacher might output, including tokens the teacher would produce with only 0.001% probability. The result is a student that "says a little of everything," generating verbose, watery, off-topic text.

*** Its Solution

Switch to *reverse KL* — which is *mode-seeking*: the student only needs to concentrate on the few tokens the teacher outputs most often and need not cover the tail probabilities.

If you flip the direction of forward KL — previously "the student must have probability mass wherever the teacher is nonzero," now "wherever the student has mass, the teacher must also approve" — you get reverse KL.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfall It Buried)

The essence of generation is "pick a good answer," not "cover all possible answers," so mode-seeking is actually the right fit. MiniLLM achieved solid results with reverse KL plus some on-policy tricks.

The pitfall left behind: changing the divergence did not resolve exposure bias. The student still trains on the teacher's sequence prefixes and must use its own at inference. The gap remains.

** 2024 | Agarwal et al. (GKD): Since the Gap Is the Core Issue, Let the Student Train on Its Own Output — with the Teacher Correcting Along the Way

*** What Problem It Saw

GKD targeted directly the exposure bias Bengio had already identified: the training distribution and the inference distribution do not match. The argument is that no matter how the divergence is changed, as long as training data consists of the teacher's sequences, the gap cannot close.

*** Its Solution

*On-policy distillation*: at each training step, let the student generate a span of tokens, then have the teacher provide its "correct" distribution at every position; align with Jensen-Shannon divergence. This way, what the student sees during training is the same distribution it will encounter during inference.

Take Kim & Rush's approach and make one substitution — replace "the teacher's fixed offline dataset" with "tokens the student generates in real time during the training loop," and replace "MLE hard labels" with "the teacher's soft distribution" — and you get GKD.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfall It Buried)

It directly aligns the training and inference distributions, which is theoretically the cleanest solution. It achieves state-of-the-art results on many benchmarks.

The pitfall left behind: *extremely expensive*. Every training step requires the student to first generate a span — and since the student's parameters are still changing, this rollout must be re-run every step, causing training cost to explode by 5–10×. For industrial deployment this is nearly a luxury.

** 2024 | Ko et al. (DistiLLM): A Patch for On-Policy — The Student Is Too Weak Early On and Derails Training; Scheduling Is Needed

*** What Problem It Saw

GKD had a concrete failure mode: early in training the student outputs near-random tokens; forcing it to generate on-policy means the teacher computes gradients over garbage, which actually corrupts training.

*** Its Solution

Design the *skew KL* divergence and adaptively adjust "what fraction of training uses on-policy student data vs. teacher data" as training progresses — a form of curriculum.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfall It Buried)

DistiLLM is more stable than GKD, but the cost problem is not fundamentally resolved. Student rollout is still required.

The pitfall left behind: everyone assumed "eliminating exposure bias requires on-policy training." Is that assumption itself an illusion?

** 2026 | Wang et al. (Bridge-Garden): Reversal — the Long-Ignored "Hard + Soft Simple Mixture" Is Already Enough; Nobody Just Explained Why

*** What Problem It Saw

Wang et al. ran an experiment that should not have had a surprising result: simply weighted-sum the hard-label loss and the soft-label loss (λ·soft + (1−λ)·hard), and compare against on-policy methods such as GKD. The outcome — the hybrid version often wins, at roughly 1/9.7 the training cost of on-policy.

Even stranger: during training, the hybrid version's "student-resembles-teacher" score is clearly *worse* — it is not fitting the teacher better in the training phase. But at inference it simply wins.

This contradiction led them to realize: the performance gain does not come from "being more like the teacher at training time," but from "having less bias at inference time." Exposure bias is the key, but it does not require on-policy training to resolve.

*** Its Solution

They propose the *Bridge-Garden decomposition theory*: classify every generation step into one of two categories —

- *Bridge*: this step must choose one specific token; getting it wrong collapses everything downstream (e.g., a syntax symbol in code, a specific number in a math problem, the next word of a fixed collocation)
- *Garden*: this step has many reasonable choices; any of them is fine (e.g., an adjective, a connective, one of several paraphrases for the same meaning)

They then prove:
- On Bridge positions, hard labels are best — they concentrate all probability on the one correct token, preventing random drift
- On Garden positions, soft labels are best — they preserve the teacher's preference distribution over multiple valid options, maintaining diversity
- Pure hard labels = Bridge strong, Garden weak; pure soft labels = Garden strong, Bridge weak; the mixture = both sides covered

Going further, they provide an upper-bound proof that hybrid supervision reduces exposure bias — the first theoretical explanation of "why the mixture is enough."

Take Agarwal's approach and make one substitution — replace "real-time student rollout + JSD alignment" with "teacher offline generation + token-level mixing of hard and soft labels" — and you get Bridge-Garden. The cost is losing perfect train-inference alignment, but Bridge-Garden decomposition proves this cost is traded for a much cheaper way to mitigate exposure bias.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfall It Buried)

Theoretically: for the first time, exposure bias is decomposed into "which positions carry bias and which do not." All prior methods treated every position in a sequence as equally important, but Bridge positions are a minority that bear the overwhelming share of exposure bias. Hard labels pin down the small number of critical positions; soft labels give freedom everywhere else — this spatial mixture is far more cost-effective than a temporal on-policy rollout.

Engineering-wise: training cost returns to the level of ordinary offline KD, with no student rollout overhead, making industrial deployment feasible.

New pitfall: how do you automatically identify which positions are Bridge and which are Garden? The paper offers several heuristics (based on confidence, entropy, curriculum, risk), but provides no single unified optimal criterion. This is exactly the entry point for follow-up work.

* Frontier Extensions

As of 2026-05, this paper has just been released (accepted at ICML 2026) and has no clear follow-up work yet.

Predictable extension directions:
- Automated Bridge/Garden identification — possibly leveraging RL signals or the teacher model's uncertainty estimates
- Extending the Bridge-Garden framework to RLHF / DPO settings, analyzing which tokens truly carry preference signal
- Organic fusion with on-policy methods: on-policy for Bridge positions, off-policy soft matching for Garden positions

* Insights

On the surface, this ten-year line looks like a technical iteration over "which divergence to use / how to supply labels." But the deeper cognitive shift underneath is:

*Phase 1 (2015–2016)*: debate "soft labels vs. hard labels," treated as a binary choice.

*Phase 2 (2024)*: realization that the true enemy is not "information quantity" but the "train-inference gap." Everyone rushes toward on-policy solutions; engineering costs soar.

*Phase 3 (2026, Bridge-Garden)*: reflection — on-policy is not the only solution, and exposure bias is not uniformly distributed across every token. It is a *local* phenomenon — only a small number of critical positions are genuinely fragile. So the more cost-efficient engineering move is fine-grained spatial scheduling (mixing by token type) rather than expensive temporal rollout.

A deeper observation: every paradigm leap came not from inventing an entirely new scheme, but from seeing the *structure of the problem itself* more clearly. Hinton saw "soft labels contain dark knowledge"; Bengio saw "distributions drift"; Bridge-Garden saw "the drift is not uniform." Each time, a "global problem" was cut into a "local problem," opening up a cheaper solution space.

Where does this go next? Most likely: extending this "position-sensitivity" analytical framework — Bridge vs. Garden — to the alignment domain (RLHF / DPO). Within a single response, which tokens truly carry user preference, and which are merely expressive degrees of freedom? Once that cut can be made, the sample efficiency of alignment will jump another level.

* Takeaways

The biggest takeaway from this ten-year line is: *do not be intimidated by expensive "theoretically optimal" solutions.*

GKD is theoretically impeccable — it directly eliminates the train-inference gap, and nobody can argue with that. But it pushed the simple approach into a corner, making everyone stop seriously asking "why does that simple approach sometimes still win?"

What Bridge-Garden does, stripped to its essence, is take seriously that anomalous experimental result — how can something as naive as a hybrid scheme keep winning? Most researchers would chalk it up to "margin of error" or "task-specific quirks" and move on. Wang et al. spent an entire paper explaining this victory that should not have existed, and in the process excavated an entirely new theoretical framework.

This suggests you can:
- In research, keep your eye on *anomalously strong simple baselines* — if they win when they shouldn't, there is almost certainly a structure the field has not yet seen
- Don't trust the naive intuition "more information = better scheme" too readily; in sequence generation the bottleneck is often not information quantity
- When doing engineering optimization, ask first "is the problem actually uniform?" — many seemingly global problems have only 5% of positions that are genuinely hard
