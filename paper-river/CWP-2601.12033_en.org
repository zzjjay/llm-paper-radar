* The Problem River

If you compress a large language model small enough to fit on a phone, will it still harbor biases — and will it still hold the line on safety?

The difficulty is this: compression looks like nothing more than "throwing away precision," yet a model's capacity for courtesy, non-discrimination, and refusal to explain bomb-making is not evenly distributed across all parameters. You imagine you are proportionally shrinking a photograph, but what you are actually doing is randomly punching out individual pixels — usually fine, but occasionally you punch out the catchlight in someone's eye. The trouble is that nobody knows where those catchlight pixels are.

Generation after generation of researchers has wrestled with this because the *commercial pressure* to compress (on-device inference, cost reduction) is enormous, and the *social pressure* to maintain *alignment* (regulation, brand risk) is equally enormous. Two forces pulling in opposite directions, with this middle line ploughed over and over again.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022 | GPTQ: Making 4-bit LLMs Actually Usable

*** What Problem It Saw

Before GPTQ, compressing LLMs to 8-bit (LLM.int8()) was already considered an engineering marvel. But even 8-bit was not enough to fit a 70B model on a single GPU. Everyone wanted 4-bit, but 4-bit collapsed immediately — with only 16 discrete levels, a single rounding step could amplify downstream errors exponentially.

*** Its Solution

Not "round to nearest," but "round gracefully according to importance." GPTQ borrowed the old intuition from Optimal Brain Surgeon: each time a weight is quantized, Hessian information is used to absorb the resulting error into the remaining, not-yet-quantized neighbors. In other words, if the first cut goes slightly wrong, the next cut compensates.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Hole It Dug)

It assumes "error can be redistributed," which works well in layers where weights are strongly correlated. But it does not address a dirtier problem — *monsters in the activations*. The activations of certain tokens can be hundreds of times larger than average; these outliers blow up the quantization scale for an entire layer, cramming all the normal channels into just a few bits at the bottom. GPTQ only touches weights, not activations, so it is helpless against this monster.

** 2023–2024 | SmoothQuant / AWQ: Fighting Activation Outliers

*** What Problem They Saw

GPTQ had compressed weights nicely, but inference was still slow — because many practitioners also wanted to quantize activations to INT8 to save memory bandwidth. The moment they did, they found: the model was accurate on common inputs but produced gibberish on certain rare tokens. Digging down revealed activation outliers as the culprit.

*** Their Solutions

SmoothQuant's trick is a kind of "redirect the calamity": since activations are hard to quantize, migrate the "hardness" to the weights — multiply activations by a small scalar and the corresponding weight columns by a large scalar; the product is unchanged, but the dynamic range of activations is flattened, and the weights can already handle a wide range.

AWQ takes a different route: acknowledge that some weights matter more than others, estimate "which channels are critical" from activation magnitudes, keep those channels at high precision, and compress the rest to 4-bit.

*** Why They Worked (and What New Hole They Dug)

AWQ explicitly introduced a key insight — *weights are not equal*; a small minority prop up most of the performance. Every subsequent "critical weight protection" approach borrows this insight.

But its definition of "critical" only considers *task performance*, i.e., perplexity. It never asks: might the "less important" weights it discards be precisely where the model's conscience lives?

** 2023 | Gonçalves & Strubell, Ramesh et al.: The First Systematic Measurement of Bias After Compression

*** What Problem They Saw

The entire compression community was competing on perplexity and MMLU scores. Nobody was testing one crucial thing: after compression, does the model become more or less biased against different genders, races, or religions?

*** Their Solutions

They applied existing bias benchmarks — StereoSet for intrinsic bias, BBQ for bias in downstream QA — to models before and after compression. Gonçalves & Strubell found that *dynamic* PTQ could actually reduce intrinsic bias slightly; Ramesh et al. found that extrinsic bias in downstream tasks actually worsened.

*** Why They Worked (and What New Hole They Dug)

These two papers were the first to connect "compression" and "fairness" in a shared evaluation framework. But they only *observed phenomena* — what they could not do was *actively protect* fairness during the compression process. And both were English-only.

** 2024 | Kumar / Belkhiter / Hong: Compressed Models Are More Easily Jailbroken

*** What Problem They Saw

While fairness was already raising alarms, the safety findings were even more alarming. Kumar et al. found that a well-aligned Llama, once quantized, started accepting requests it would previously have refused when hit with a set of jailbreak prompts. Belkhiter compared robustness of compressed models under both direct and transfer attacks, reaching the same conclusion: degradation. Hong ran a full sweep with DecodingTrust; scores dropped across the board.

*** Their Solutions

They had no "solution" — they were sounding the alarm. They made visible the fact that compression is not a "lossless" or "nearly lossless" operation; for the safety guardrail, compression can be structurally destructive.

*** Why They Worked (and What New Hole They Dug)

Their contribution was quantifying the problem clearly. The hole they left: now that we know compression causes harm, the options are either *realign* (too expensive) or *protect safety during compression itself* (nobody had done it).

** 2024 | Guo et al. (FairQuantize): Establishing the "Critical Weight Protection" Paradigm in Medical Imaging

*** What Problem It Saw

Guo was not working on language models but on medical image classification. What he noticed was: the model's diagnostic accuracy was already unequal across patients of different skin tones and genders. Aggressive quantization amplified these gaps — because the parameters used to recognize features of minority groups were themselves a minority, and easy to discard.

*** Its Solution

Assign each weight a *fairness score*: how important is this weight for maintaining balanced accuracy across demographic groups? Lock the high-scoring minority of weights at high precision; compress everything else freely.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Hole It Dug)

This was the paradigm shift. AWQ said "select critical weights by performance"; FairQuantize was the first to say "select critical weights by *a value*" — and that value could be fairness, or anything else you care about. This opened the door.

But Guo only worked on images and only on fairness. Do language models have "fairness-critical weights"? What do safety-critical weights look like? Nobody picked up the thread.

** 2025 | Q-resafe: Bringing "Critical Weights" to LLM Safety

*** What Problem It Saw

After Kumar et al. had spent a year warning that compressed models are unsafe, Q-resafe confronted the problem directly. It observed that re-running full RLHF on a quantized model is expensive and slow; but if you could identify the "safety-relevant parameters damaged by quantization," a targeted fix should suffice.

*** Its Solution

Use SNIP (a classic pruning importance metric) to scan the model and identify the small set of weights most sensitive to safety loss. Then run DPO on a tiny set of safety preference data, fine-tuning only those weights — at a fraction of the cost of full realignment.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Hole It Dug)

It transferred the "critical weights" idea from Guo's imaging world to LLM safety and demonstrated that "small data + a few parameters = safety recovery" actually works.

Three holes remained:
- It only fixes safety; *fairness is untouched*.
- It is "repair after compression" rather than "protect during compression" — information is already gone by the time the fix is attempted.
- Experiments covered English only; the multilingual world is entirely dark.

** 2026 | Critical Weight Protection (This Paper): Preserving Fairness + Safety in One Pass, Inside the Quantization Process

*** What Problem It Saw

Pulling together all the threads above, an awkward situation emerges:
- The AWQ lineage only protects performance.
- FairQuantize only protects fairness, and only in medical imaging.
- Q-resafe only protects LLM safety, and only as a post-hoc fix.
- Yet in real deployment — especially releasing globally under regulatory scrutiny — you need performance, fairness, *and* safety simultaneously, ideally without an additional training run.

What is even more glaring: nearly all prior work was English-only. A quantization scheme that looks fine in English may have its safety guardrails snap entirely when switched to Korean or Arabic.

*** Its Solution

It merges the "critical weight scoring" ideas from Guo and Chen, then does two things:

First, compute *two scores simultaneously*. For each weight θ:

- FAIRSCORE(θ) = I_fair(θ) − β · I_gen(θ)
- SAFESCORE(θ) = I_safe(θ) − β · I_gen(θ)

I(θ) is the mean squared gradient of the loss with respect to the parameter — essentially a diagonal approximation of Fisher information, measuring how much the loss would fluctuate if this weight moved by a small amount. I_fair is probed with the stereotype/anti-stereotype contrastive loss from StereoSet; I_safe is probed with the harmful-instruction refusal loss from AdvBench; I_gen is probed with next-token prediction loss on Wikipedia and Dolly.

Subtracting β·I_gen prevents weights that are "important for everything" from gaming the ranking — the goal is to identify weights that are *especially* sensitive to fairness/safety but do not have an outsized monopoly on general capability.

Second, sum FAIRSCORE and SAFESCORE, keep the top-k% in FP16, and quantize the rest to INT4 as usual. One quantization pass; fairness and safety both protected.

No realignment, no post-hoc DPO, no new annotation.

*** Why It Works (and What New Hole It Digs)

The core insight is that fairness and safety are not mystical emergent properties of training data — they are *measurable attributes that can be localized to specific parameter subsets*. Fisher information is the locator. Once you can localize them, "protecting them" becomes a mechanical operation.

New holes opened:
- Keeping the top-k% in FP16 means the model is no longer pure INT4 — hardware acceleration is partially forfeited, and the marginal gain for on-device deployment shrinks.
- The probe datasets (StereoSet, AdvBench) are themselves biased, meaning "protected weights" are critical in the eyes of these two datasets; results may drift with different probes.
- Multilingual validation covers only a handful of languages; how to handle unvalidated languages remains an empirical question.

* Frontier Extensions

As of this paper (early 2026), the most recent extensions along this "critical weight protection" lineage involve expanding the "value labels" beyond fairness and safety to additional dimensions — privacy (not leaking training data), copyright (not reproducing copyrighted text), faithfulness (not hallucinating). Each additional dimension is in essence one more Fisher probe stacked on top.

A parallel direction is moving from "score weights in advance" to "adapt dynamically during quantization" — switching precision on the fly based on loss rebounds halfway through the process. This line is still in its early stages.

* Insight

On the surface, this lineage traces the evolution of quantization techniques; at its core, it represents a philosophical shift in how model engineering is understood:

*From "parameter egalitarianism" to "parameters have politics."* In the GPTQ era, all parameters were implicitly treated as equal — differing only in numerical magnitude. AWQ introduced "rank by performance." After Guo and Chen, parameters began to carry *value orientations* — this weight handles fairness, that one handles refusals, another handles task performance. Compression is no longer a numerical game; it is a small-scale political act of deciding who to protect and who to sacrifice.

*From "repair after the fact" to "protect during the process."* Q-resafe still operated in a "compress first, patch later" mode; this paper moves protection upstream into the quantization process itself. This is the same rhythm as "shift-left testing" in software engineering: the earlier a constraint is embedded in the pipeline, the cheaper it is.

*From monolingual to multilingual — the genuinely hard part still ahead.* Are critical weights shared across languages? Are the "safety weights" for Korean the same set as those for English? This paper provides multilingual *evaluation*, but the question of how to perform multilingual *critical weight localization* remains unanswered. That is the entry point for the next paper.

The most likely next step: replace the Fisher information probe with cheaper proxy metrics (stochastic gradient approximations, low-rank estimates), making "critical weight scoring" light enough to run online inside a training loop rather than as an offline analysis pass. Once that cost comes down, every "value" — privacy, faithfulness, cultural sensitivity — can be plugged in like a module.

* Implications

On research methodology: when a field encounters "seemingly harmless engineering improvements that produce systematic side effects" (quantization harming fairness, quantization harming safety), the answer is rarely to patch technical details — it requires *making values explicitly optimizable objects*. Score first, then optimize, then engineer.

On finding problems: pay attention to "combinatorial gaps." Fairness had its researchers, safety had its researchers, quantization had its researchers — but the gap of "managing both fairness and safety inside quantization" sat unfilled. Compound gaps are often more valuable than single-point gaps, because they imply a real deployment scenario — and scenarios are the true origin of engineering problems.

This means you can:
- Take any "post-hoc repair" approach, ask "could this be moved upstream into the process?" — very likely a paper.
- Take any "single-value" critical weight scorer, ask "can stacking another Fisher probe extend it to a second value?" — very likely another paper.
- Take any alignment finding "only tested in English," ask "does it hold in a low-resource language?" — this is the most underrated vein of paper gold in 2026.
