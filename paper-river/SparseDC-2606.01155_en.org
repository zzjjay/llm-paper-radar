* The River of Problems

Training large models has, for years, been like eating at an all-you-can-eat buffet: bigger models are better, more data is better, more compute is better — as long as you're willing to spend, adding in any direction yields lower loss. This "addition logic" has ruled the entire field.

But one resource is running out: high-quality human text. The good data available on the internet grows at roughly 1.03× per year; meanwhile, the rate at which training jobs consume data grows at 4× per year. When those two curves cross, the conclusion is harsh — good data is running out fast. When data is fixed and finite, you have no choice but to feed the same corpus to the model repeatedly (running multiple epochs). This is called "data-constrained" training.

So the question has changed. The old question was "should compute go toward the model or toward data?" — with data assumed infinite. The deeper question now is: *when data runs dry, is the ceiling on model quality determined only by "how large the model is," or also by "how that capacity is allocated and optimized"?*

This paper offers a counterintuitive answer: making a network *sparse* — zeroing out most weights and keeping only a small subset active — is merely a "cost-saving tool" when data is abundant, but becomes a *game-changing mechanism* when data is scarce: it makes the model more tolerant of repeated data, pushing the wall of diminishing returns further back.

Where is the difficulty in this river? It lies at the *confluence of two independently developed streams*. One is the "data axis" — what to do when data runs short; the other is the "sparsity axis" — can a sparse network be trained from scratch? Each side has its own mature scaling law, but no one had seriously asked: when both happen simultaneously, does a 1+1≠2 chemical reaction occur?

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2020 | Kaplan et al.: The First Monument to "Bigger-Model Worship"

*** What Problem It Saw

Before it, training language models was largely guesswork — how large a model, how much data, how long to train were all decided by intuition. No one could answer "given this much compute, how should I spend it most efficiently?"

*** Its Solution

It discovered that loss follows clean power-law relationships with model size, data volume, and compute. In other words, performance is *predictable* — you don't need to finish training to know how much loss will drop if you double the parameters. This was the beginning of scaling laws.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Trap)

Power laws work because they turn "black art" into "engineering." But Kaplan drew a conclusion later shown to be biased: given more compute, *prioritize spending on parameters*; data can be relatively scarce. This directly gave rise to the GPT-3-generation of models with "massive parameters, relatively underfed data" — 175B parameters paired with only about 1.7 tokens per parameter. That bias toward parameters over data was exactly the trap the next paper would correct.

** 2022 | Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al.): Turns Out Everyone Was Starving Their Models

*** What Problem It Saw

It directly challenged Kaplan: was your conclusion to "prioritize stacking parameters" the result of running experiments with too little data, inadvertently over-crediting the parameters? The mainstream large models of the time might have all been *severely undertrained* — large models that hadn't eaten enough data, wasting their capacity.

*** Its Solution

It wrote loss as a clean formula: =L(N, D) = A/N^α + B/D^β + E=. The first two terms are the excess loss from insufficient parameters and insufficient data respectively; the final term E is irreducible noise. After fitting more than 400 trained models, the verdict flipped: parameters and data should scale *proportionally* together — double the model, double the data, with a golden ratio of roughly 20:1. Using the same compute budget but with Gopher shrunk by 4× and 4× more data, Chinchilla thoroughly outperformed much larger competitors.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Trap)

The intuition is simple: parameters are "containers for knowledge," data is "the knowledge to be stored," and container and content must match — a big empty container is wasteful. But Chinchilla's formula has a quietly hardcoded assumption — =D= is "infinite fresh data," every token seen only once. In 2022, when the data buffet hadn't run out yet, this was fine. But once data really runs short and you're forced to repeat, the formula starts to break down — it has no concept of "repetition" at all. That is the starting point for the next paper.

** 2023 | Muennighoff et al.: Data Has Run Out — Is Repeating It Still Worth Anything?

*** What Problem It Saw

It pierced Chinchilla's "infinite data" assumption: high-quality text on the internet is nearly exhausted, and future large-model training will require feeding the same corpus repeatedly. The question is — in the Chinchilla formula, what should =D= represent: the number of unique tokens, or the total tokens fed (including repeats)? Is a repeated token still worth as much as a fresh one?

*** Its Solution

It replaced the true data volume =D= in the Chinchilla formula with an "effective data volume =D'=": =D' = U_d + U_d·R_d*·(1 - e^{-R_d/R_d*})=. In plain terms — the first few repetitions are nearly as valuable as new data; the further you repeat, the more each token's value drops, eventually approaching zero, with =D'= hitting a ceiling. The empirical rule derived is very concrete: *within 4 epochs of repetition, loss barely changes; by 16 epochs, returns begin to diminish severely*, and beyond that each repeated token retains only about 63% of its value.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Trap)

This "effective data" perspective is elegant: it quantifies "repetition" as "discounted fresh data." It also corrected Chinchilla's allocation strategy — under data constraints, one should *train smaller models for more epochs* rather than pile on parameters. But it shares another hardcoded assumption with Chinchilla: the network is *dense*, with all parameters updated at every step. It never asked — if the training method changed, would the value of repeated data change too? This "dense assumption" is the crack this paper pries open.

** 2020 | RigL (Evci et al.): Sparse Networks Don't Need to "Grow Fat Before Going Thin"

*** What Problem It Saw

(This is where we switch to the other tributary — the sparsity axis.) The standard recipe for obtaining a sparse network was "train a large dense model first, then prune away unimportant weights." But this has a deadlock: the largest sparse model you can obtain is bounded by the largest *dense* model you can afford to train. Sparsity is supposed to save memory and compute, but during training you still have to carry the dense heavyweight — the savings never land where they matter.

*** Its Solution

RigL (and the earlier SET) proposed Dynamic Sparse Training (DST): the network is *sparse from the very beginning*, fixing only a small subset of nonzero weights. During training it periodically does two things — prune the currently smallest weights, then reconnect the "most deserving" links based on gradient information. Prune a batch, grow a batch; the topology evolves throughout training, but the total number of nonzero parameters stays constant. The result: a sparse network trained from scratch can match or even exceed a "grow-then-prune" scheme, while using less compute throughout.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Trap)

The intuition: a sparse network with a fixed topology easily gets stuck in poor local optima, while allowing the topology to evolve dynamically is like constantly finding a new path for optimization, enabling escape from dead ends. RigL also left a key observation: *sparse networks particularly benefit from longer training* — more training time keeps yielding performance gains, and total FLOPs are often lower than dense training. But RigL was done on vision tasks with abundant data; it has no scaling law, and never touched the combination of "language models + data exhaustion."

** 2024 | Frantar et al.: A Scaling Law for Sparsity

*** What Problem It Saw

It found that although sparse training was effective, it lacked a predictive formula like Chinchilla's: given a parameter count and a data volume, what is the optimal sparsity? No one could compute it. Sparsity had remained a "tuned trick," unable to graduate into "predictable engineering."

*** Its Solution

It produced the first scaling law unifying "sparsity, nonzero parameters, and data volume" into one framework. The core insight in one sentence: *sparsity is equivalent to multiplying "effective model size" by a scaling factor* — a sparse network with fewer nonzero parameters is equivalent to a larger dense model. From this a clean conclusion follows: *for a fixed nonzero-parameter budget, the higher the data volume, the higher the optimal sparsity*.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Trap)

It elevated sparsity from "tuning black art" to "extrapolatable law," putting it on equal footing with Chinchilla. But note its premise — like Chinchilla, it was derived under the assumption of *infinite data, compute-optimal training*, every token seen only once. It can tell you "more data means higher optimal sparsity," but cannot answer "what about when data is *insufficient* and must be repeated?" At this point both rivers have reached their individual ends: Muennighoff understands "data repetition" but assumes dense networks; Frantar understands "sparsity" but assumes infinite data. Between them lies a territory no one has yet explored.

** 2026 | SparseDC (This Paper): Letting Sparsity Rewrite Both "Effective Data" and "Effective Parameters"

*** What Problem It Saw

It immediately identifies the gap: real large-model training often involves *both* sparsity and data repetition simultaneously (e.g., MoE/DST combined with multi-epoch training), yet the two scaling laws are isolated from each other and neither can predict this combination. More critically, it identifies a neglected mechanism — sparsity does not only change "model capacity," it also changes "how frequently each parameter is optimized": dense training repeatedly updates the same set of parameters on limited data, accelerating saturation; sparse training spreads updates across different parameter subsets, *potentially making repeated data more durable*. Once these two factors become nonlinearly entangled, predicting them separately must be inaccurate.

*** Its Solution

What it does is essentially *weld* the formulas of the two preceding rivers together. Starting from Muennighoff's =L(N', D')=, it inserts sparsity =S= into three places simultaneously:

#+begin_example
The final sparse-data-constrained scaling law:

   L(N', D', S) = A( (1-S)^ε + P·S^μ ) / N'^α  +  B / D'^β  +  E
                  \______________________/
                   Sparsity discounts "effective parameters" (P<0):
                   same nonzero parameters, but sparse --> equivalent to a larger model

   Effective parameters:  N' = U_n + U_n·R_n*(S)·(1 - e^{-R_n/R_n*(S)})
   Effective data:        D' = U_d + U_d·R_d*(S)·(1 - e^{-R_d/R_d*(S)})
                                           \_______/
                  Key: the data saturation threshold R_d* is now a function of sparsity!
                  R_d*(S) = R_d*·(1 + λ1·S + σ1·S^2)
#+end_example

If you fix the data saturation threshold =R_d*= and then let it bend as a function of sparsity =S=, you continuously deform Muennighoff's formula into this paper's formula. The full law is fitted on 500 training runs and extrapolated to a held-out 7.68B-parameter model, validating at =R²=0.914=.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buries a New Trap)

Three empirical findings, each advancing one step beyond its predecessor:

1. *Sparsity makes data more durable (delayed saturation)*: The data saturation threshold rises from 4.4 at dense, to roughly 6.9 at 50% sparsity, peaking at =S≈0.66=. This directly corrects Muennighoff's "hitting the ceiling by 16 epochs" — sparsity can push that wall back. Intuition: sparsity spreads updates across different parameter subsets, "squeezing" the same data for longer.

2. *Loss-optimal sparsity ≈ 50% (moderate)*: For a fixed data budget, the sparsity that minimizes loss consistently lands in the 45%–50% range, almost regardless of data volume.

3. *Compute-optimal sparsity grows with data*: If the goal is "reach dense-optimal performance with minimum FLOPs," the optimal sparsity rises from ≈0.6 at 1.3B tokens to ≈0.7 at 13B/130B tokens — exactly continuing Frantar's trend of "more data → higher optimal sparsity," but now transplanted into the data-constrained world. On the cost side: compute-optimal sparse models can reach dense-equivalent performance with roughly 8–10× fewer FLOPs.

New trap buried: the loss-optimal (~50%) and compute-optimal (~70%, rising with data) do not coincide, and which one to follow depends on how you define "compute" (sparse FLOPs vs. dense FLOPs) — while current hardware often fails to realize the theoretical speedup of sparsity. This kicks the ball to the systems/hardware layer.

* Frontier Extensions

As of this writing (2026-06), this paper is the latest stop on the "sparsity × data-constrained" intersection, and no follow-up papers directly critiquing or improving it have yet been found.

Confirmed parallel / adjacent directions (which this paper itself cites and distinguishes):
- *Compression scaling laws* (extensions of the Frantar lineage): unifying sparsity and quantization into an "effective parameter multiplier," but still under the infinite-data assumption.
- *Other escape routes from data constraints* (Muennighoff lineage): code-data augmentation, relaxed filtering, stronger regularization and ensembling (Kim et al. 2026) — all of these increase effective data *within the dense paradigm*, without rewriting the scaling mechanism at the architecture/optimization level as this paper does.

In other words, this paper's genuine novelty lies not in "shaving a bit more off the loss," but in pointing out that *sparsity is a mechanism that actively improves the trade-off curve under data scarcity, not merely an efficiency switch*. That positioning has no successor challenging it yet.

* Insights

On the surface this river is a technical iteration of scaling laws; underneath it is a *migration of cognitive center of gravity*:

- *From "addition" to "allocation."* The question in the Kaplan/Chinchilla era was "where to add compute," with the premise that resources (especially data) could be added without limit. Once good data runs out, the question is forced to become "with a fixed budget, how to *allocate* and *optimize* for the best return." This paper is a clear landing point for that shift — it even says that sparsity's value is "not as an efficiency tool, but as a mechanism that improves the trade-off."

- *"Effective-ization" is the same move this river makes again and again.* Chinchilla's =N, D= became Muennighoff's "effective data =D'="; then this paper added "effective parameters =N'=". Each generation does the same thing: replace a "nominal quantity" with a "discounted effective quantity," then describe the discount with a saturation function. This is nearly the meta-pattern of scaling-law evolution — *the next quantity to be "effective-ized" is likely data quality or compute (the gap between sparse FLOPs and dense FLOPs is already surfacing at the edges of the formula).*

- *"Sparsity makes repeated data more durable" points toward a deeper conjecture*: a model's "digestive capacity" for data may not depend on total parameter count, but on "the frequency distribution with which each parameter is updated." Dense training updates all parameters at high frequency, leading to rapid saturation; sparsity spreads updates thin, extending the data's "shelf life." This suggests that future training paradigms may tune not "how large the model is," but "how updates are distributed across parameters."

Where is the next step most likely to go? Two directions: (1) actually realize the theoretical speedup of "sparse FLOPs" in hardware, otherwise compute-optimal sparsity remains a paper benefit; (2) generalize this "effective parameters × effective data" framework to MoE, quantization, and other forms of "sparsification," unifying them into a single compression-data scaling law.

* Implications

For "how to do research, how to find problems," this river offers three very practical pointers:

1. *The richest problems often grow at the confluence of two mature rivers.* Muennighoff and Frantar are each complete, widely cited works, but neither asked "what happens when both things occur simultaneously?" This paper invented no new algorithm; it simply connected two known lines and obtained an entirely new scaling law. The implication: *watch for two settings in your field that are each isolated in the literature yet always co-occur in practice — the uncrossed intersection is very likely an open paper.*

2. *The assumptions previous authors hardcoded are your entry points.* Chinchilla hardcoded "infinite data" — Muennighoff pried it open and got a NeurIPS paper. Muennighoff hardcoded "dense network" — this paper pried it open and got an ICML paper. Every good paper drives a wedge into the previous paper's "of course." The implication: *when reading a paper, specifically hunt for the line "we assume…" and ask "what if this doesn't hold?" — the next paper is very likely hiding there.*

3. *"Effective-ization" is a reusable modeling recipe.* When a nominal quantity (data, parameters, compute) is "discounted" under some condition, don't tear down the whole formula — instead replace it with an "effective quantity + saturation function." This transformation has been validated repeatedly throughout this river. The implication: *when you encounter a phenomenon where "X is worth less than face value under condition Y," don't rush to build a new model — try attaching an effective-ization discount term to X; it often slots seamlessly into existing scaling laws.*
