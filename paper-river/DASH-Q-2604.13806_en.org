* One-liner

Strip out GPTQ's elegant machinery of "propagate quantization error to neighbours via the Hessian", keep only the diagonal, and find that at extreme 2-bit precision this actually recovers 7 percentage points.

* Five-Layer Reverse Reading: What Each Paper Saw

** Layer 5 (1990) OBD: First Acknowledgment That "Not All Weights Matter Equally"

*Problem*: Trained neural networks are large and some weights must be pruned. But which ones? The naive approach — prune those with the smallest absolute value — ignores where a weight sits on the loss surface. A small weight may lie along a very steep direction; remove it and things collapse.

*Solution*: Score weights using second-order derivatives (diagonal of the Hessian). The intuition: the quadratic approximation of the loss around weight w_j gives a loss increase of roughly (1/2) * H_jj * (Δw_j)^2. Weights with large H_jj are painful to touch; weights with small H_jj can be safely pruned.

*Open problem*: Using only the diagonal assumes weights are independent of one another. But the off-diagonal entries of the Hessian say: "if you prune w_j, you can simultaneously adjust w_k to compensate." OBD throws that compensation away.

** Layer 4 (1993) OBS: Expand the Diagonal to the Full Hessian, Prune One and Patch Many

*What it saw wrong in OBD*: OBD prunes and walks away, ignoring the fact that the remaining weights can still be fine-tuned. Hassibi reasoned: given the full Hessian, after pruning weight w_j the optimal adjustment to all remaining weights can be written in closed form — a Lagrange multiplier problem.

*Solution*: Retain the full Hessian H. When pruning w_j, simultaneously shift all other weights along the j-th row of H^{-1} (the "coupling direction" revealed by the off-diagonal entries). Prune one, patch many — the resulting loss increase is an order of magnitude smaller than OBD's.

*Open problem*: Storing a d×d Hessian and its inverse, where d is the layer's input dimension, is feasible for the small networks of the 1990s. On LLMs with billions of parameters and d=4096 or larger per layer, this becomes outright impossible.

** Layer 3 (2022a) OBQ / OBC: Port OBS from Pruning to Quantization

*What it saw wrong in OBS*: OBS was designed for pruning — setting weights to zero. But the same quadratic-approximation framework describes "snapping a weight to its nearest quantization grid point" equally well. Frantar & Alistarh rewrote it: "prune w_j" becomes "quantize w_j to q_j", and the compensation formula carries over unchanged. This is Optimal Brain Quantization.

*Solution*: Proceed layer by layer (layer-wise reconstruction), with each layer's objective being min ||WX - Ŵ X||². This is exactly solvable with the OBS second-order framework: quantize each weight in turn, then apply H^{-1}-based compensation to the remaining weights.

*Open problem*: After quantizing each weight, H^{-1} must be updated (a rank-1 update per the OBS formula), and the next least-painful weight must be re-identified. Total complexity is O(d_col^3) — a single layer of an LLM can take hours.

** Layer 2 (2022b) GPTQ: Make OBQ Tractable at LLM Scale

*What it saw wrong in OBQ*: OBQ searches for the "currently least painful column" at every step, producing a different ordering each time — cache-unfriendly and O(d^3) slow. Frantar et al. found that on LLMs the column ordering barely matters in practice, so they simply fixed it left to right.

*Solution*:
1. Fix the column processing order ahead of time (decided once)
2. Pre-compute H^{-1} via Cholesky decomposition to avoid repeated inversions
3. Process weights in blocks for GPU efficiency — a 175B model can now be quantized in a few hours

This made "Hessian-informed LLM PTQ" practical for the first time, and GPTQ became the de facto baseline for LLM quantization.

*Open problem*: GPTQ assumes the calibration set (typically 128–512 sentences) yields a sufficiently accurate H = X X^T. At low bit-widths (2–3 bit), quantization errors are large, demanding precise error propagation — which in turn demands an accurate H. Around this time people began noticing: GPTQ's perplexity looks acceptable, but downstream task accuracy collapses badly (reported by Lin 2024 and Lee 2024). Nobody could explain why.

** Layer 1 (2026) DASH-Q: The Off-Diagonal Entries Are Just Noise

*What it saw wrong in GPTQ*: The authors ran a simple experiment — compute H on two different batches of 128 calibration sentences and compare. The diagonal entries were stable; the off-diagonal entries wandered all over the place. In other words, GPTQ's elaborate "off-diagonal compensation" is compensating for sampling noise, not for genuine curvature.

More damning: H has d^2 off-diagonal entries and only d diagonal ones. The sheer number of off-diagonal terms means they dominate the total estimation error of H. At 2-bit precision, quantization errors must be propagated via H^{-1} — but the direction of propagation is itself noise, so the errors are amplified rather than corrected.

*Solution*: Reduce H to a diagonal matrix D = diag(h_jj), where h_jj = Σ x_jk^2 is the energy of input feature channel j. This single cut:
- Fully decouples optimization across input channels into d independent one-dimensional weighted least-squares problems
- Yields a closed-form optimal scale s and zero-point z per group
- Adds a ridge term λs² to prevent scale collapse in sparse groups

It sounds almost too simple for a 2026 paper — just "weighted-LS with weights = activation power, solved per group, in closed form." But the numbers speak: on Llama-3.1-8B at 2-bit / group-size 32, GPTQ's average zero-shot accuracy falls to 38%; DASH-Q pulls it back above 50%.

*Open problem*: DASH-Q explicitly acknowledges this as a bias–variance trade-off — it uses a biased but stable H estimate. This means that with a sufficiently large calibration set (tens of thousands of examples), full-Hessian methods should eventually pull ahead. The paper does not benchmark this crossover point. Also, DASH-Q is orthogonal to methods that use orthogonal rotations to flatten outliers (QuaRot, SpinQuant) — whether the two directions can be stacked is an open question.

* Frontier Extensions: After DASH-Q

** The QuaRot / SpinQuant / QuIP# Line (2024–2026)

These works do not challenge GPTQ's Hessian; instead they attack from the other end: a random orthogonal matrix spreads activation outliers uniformly across all channels (post-rotation, the variance of X is more homogeneous), and then GPTQ quantizes the result. The essence is "make H better-conditioned" rather than "replace H."

DASH-Q's paper acknowledges this line as complementary — meaning one could apply QuaRot rotation first, then run DASH-Q's diagonal-weighted LS on top. In theory the gains stack. The paper does not test this.

** Where This River Flows Next

The evolution of this lineage traces an interesting round trip:

| Stage | Attitude toward the Hessian |
|-------+--------------------------------|
| OBD   | Only the diagonal — too costly to compute more |
| OBS / OBQ / GPTQ | Full Hessian — the more precise the better |
| DASH-Q | Back to the diagonal — but for a completely different reason |

OBD used the diagonal because computing the full Hessian was infeasible. DASH-Q uses the diagonal because the full Hessian estimate is noise. Same formula, thirty years apart, but the justification reversed — the former is an engineering compromise, the latter a statistical insight.

* Core Insight: What Is Really Happening Along This River

On the surface this is an iteration of LLM quantization techniques. One level deeper, it is the same question being asked again and again: *"When should you trust the numbers you've computed?"*

OBS/GPTQ assume the Hessian estimate in hand is trustworthy, so exploiting it precisely is optimal. This holds in the 1990s setting of small networks with abundant training data. LLM PTQ inverts the scenario: the model is enormous (H's dimension explodes) while the calibration data is tiny (order of hundreds), pushing statistical reliability to its limit.

DASH-Q essentially imports the *bias–variance trade-off* from statistics into PTQ design. Its key contribution is not "diagonal Hessian as a method" but "first prove the off-diagonal is unreliable, then discard it." The analytical framework (Hessian shrinkage estimator, batch-to-batch stability metric) is more worth remembering than the method itself.

The most likely next steps:
1. *Adaptive shrinkage*: automatically decide ρ (how much of the off-diagonal to retain) as a function of calibration set size, rather than forcing a binary 0-or-1 choice
2. *Stacking diagonal weighting with orthogonal transforms*: QuaRot/SpinQuant flatten activations, making the diagonal assumption more accurate; combining the two should compound the gains
3. *Apply the same analysis to KV cache / activation quantization*: those settings are equally starved of calibration data
