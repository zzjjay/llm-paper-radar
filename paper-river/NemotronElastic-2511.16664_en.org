* The River of Problems

When a company releases a large model, they typically ship an entire family: 12B, 9B, 6B, 4B… Why? Because the cloud runs the large one while phones need the small one.

But training a whole family is terrifyingly expensive — each size must be trained from scratch, hundreds of billions of tokens at a time, with electricity bills that climb into the astronomical.

Step back: can you train a large one and then trim it down? Yes. But every trim requires retraining on hundreds of billions of tokens to bring it back to life. That's the Minitron approach.

Step back further: can you do "train once, get multiple sizes"? Train a 12B that already nests a 9B and a 6B inside it naturally — at deployment, just pull out whichever size you need, no further training required?

That is the problem this river sets out to solve. It flows from a small embedding-learning paper in 2022, through Transformers, through Mamba, all the way to late 2025's reasoning-scale models — each step answering the same question: *how do you fit multiple models inside a single model?*

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022 | MRL: Packing Multiple Granularities into One Vector

*** What Problem It Saw

In that era, if you trained an embedding model at 512 dimensions, 512 was all you got. If one day you said "my phone only has room for 64 dimensions," sorry — please retrain a 64-dimensional model from scratch. One model per size: inelegant and expensive.

What stung even more was this: the 512-dimensional vector already contained uneven information density. The first few tens of dimensions could typically separate broad categories; the remaining hundreds just filled in the fine details. But you couldn't simply take the first few tens of dimensions as a small model, because training had never forced them to be self-sufficient.

*** Its Solution

Simple and direct: during training, slice the same embedding into several prefixes, attach an independent classification head to each prefix, compute a loss for each, sum them all, and back-propagate together.

This forces the first 8 dimensions to be independently classifiable, the first 16 too, the first 32 too… After training, you pick the granularity you want and truncate there. One vector, dozens of sizes, for free.

Like Russian nesting dolls — open the large one and there's a smaller one inside, open that and there's an even smaller one, each a complete figure in its own right.

*** Why It Works (and Where a New Pit Opens)

It works because the loss function carves "the first k dimensions must be self-sufficient" directly into the gradients. The model has no choice but to push the most discriminative information to the front.

But this only solves one layer: the output embedding. What about the whole deep network? What if every layer, every block, wants to nest?

—— That new pit is dug by the next paper.

** 2023-10 | MatFormer: Stuffing Nesting Dolls into the Transformer

*** What Problem It Saw

MRL only touched the output vector. Yet 60% of a Transformer's parameters live in the intermediate dimensions of FFN (feed-forward) layers. If only the final embedding layer nests, the compute savings are negligible.

More practically: deploying to cloud, desktop, and mobile requires three model sizes. Under the traditional approach, every size demands its own distillation, alignment, and inference code. Can one model carry all three sizes internally?

*** Its Solution

Port MRL's nesting logic into the intermediate dimension of the Transformer's FFN.

Concretely: the FFN intermediate dimension d_ff is divided into g nested intervals, e.g. [d_ff/8, d_ff/4, d_ff/2, d_ff]. During training, each step randomly samples a granularity and runs only that granularity's forward and backward pass. Because weights are shared, updating the small granularity also updates the corresponding part of the large one.

After training, every layer natively contains g extractable nested sub-FFNs. A further trick called "Mix'n'Match" lets each layer independently choose its granularity, producing an exponential number of sub-model combinations — hundreds of sub-models, essentially for free.

*** Why It Works (and Where New Pits Open)

The nesting logic naturally suits weight sharing: training the large granularity is simultaneously training the small one; training the small granularity is fine-tuning the most critical parameters of the large one. The two directions do not conflict.

But the pits are obvious:

First, *only FFN is touched*. The number of attention heads is unchanged; every layer uses the same granularity. Flexibility is limited.

Second, *the sizes of extracted sub-models are rigid*. Training sets g=4, so in deployment there are essentially four options (Mix'n'Match is manual assembly), with no intelligent mechanism to decide dynamically based on input or budget.

Third, *training compute nearly doubles*. The paper itself reports FLOPs per token that are 2.58× higher than a standard Transformer — the price of nesting.

Next step: can we add a learned router that lets the model decide how large to go at each layer?

** 2024-06 | Flextron: Giving the Nesting Doll a Router

*** What Problem It Saw

When extracting sub-models from MatFormer you must manually specify each layer's granularity. Two annoyances follow:

First, *inflexible sizing*. Manually choosing g=4 slots means intermediate sizes can only be approximated by intuition.

Second, *no consideration of input variation*. A simple query and a hard query use the same sub-model size — obviously wasteful.

Third, *only FFN is elastic, not attention*. Attention heads can consume 70% of GPU memory in long-context settings; leaving them untouched means the biggest piece of meat stays on the table.

*** Its Solution

Add a router. Two modes:

- *Static router*: given a latency/memory budget, outputs an optimal per-layer granularity configuration.
- *Adaptive router*: for each incoming token, the router decides which sub-network to use.

This time *both MLP and attention heads are elastic*. Attention heads are ranked by importance and nested from small to large; the router selects the top-k.

For training, because routing is non-differentiable, a surrogate model predicts the final loss, and the router gradient-descends on the surrogate — circumventing the broken-gradient problem.

*** Why It Works (and Where New Pits Open)

It moves "which sub-model to select" from human to model, the critical leap from manual switching to self-adaptation.

But problems remain:

First, *router training is two-stage*: train the elastic network first, then train the router — backbone and router cannot truly co-train end-to-end.

Second, *the surrogate model is a stand-in*: you optimize the surrogate's loss, not the true loss, so there is always a bias.

Third, *Transformer only*. In the second half of 2024, Mamba emerged as a strong contender, with state-space models several times faster than Transformers on long sequences, and the entire elastic recipe was incompatible with them.

Next step: push nesting to Mamba.

** 2024-10 | MatMamba: Nesting Dolls Cross Over to Mamba

*** What Problem It Saw

Mamba2 arrived, several times faster than Transformers at long context. But the Mamba block structure is entirely unlike attention + FFN; the MatFormer/Flextron trick of nesting FFN intermediate dimensions cannot be directly applied — Mamba has no such intermediate dimension.

Mamba also has hard constraints of its own: the number of channels, heads, and groups in the state space is not arbitrary; slicing incorrectly causes the model to collapse.

*** Its Solution

Forcibly transplant the Matryoshka idea into the Mamba2 block. Specifically, the model's main dimension d_model is divided into nested intervals (typically [d/8, d/4, d/2, d]); during training, each step randomly samples a granularity and runs forward and backward at that granularity only.

As with MatFormer, training one large model yields g nested extractable sub-models.

*** Why It Works (and Where New Pits Open)

It proves that the nesting paradigm does not depend on attention — any layer with a channel dimension can participate.

But the remaining pits become increasingly concrete:

First, *pure Mamba models are rare in production*. The true SOTA is hybrid Mamba+Attention (e.g., Jamba, Zamba, Nemotron-H), and MatMamba ignores those attention layers.

Second, *no router*. Granularity is still selected manually.

Third, *no reasoning model support*. Reasoning models require long CoT chains and long context; the training curriculum is entirely different, and MatMamba has never addressed this.

Fourth, *no distillation*. Relying solely on multi-granularity loss to bring small models back to life falls short compared to having a teacher model guide them.

—— All of these pits are left for Nemotron Elastic to fill.

** 2024-07 (Parallel Track) | Minitron: Skip Nesting, Do Pruning + Distillation

While the nesting line evolved rapidly, another NVIDIA approach ran in parallel: train a large model, prune it down by importance, then distill hundreds of billions of tokens with the large model as teacher to revive the small one. That is Minitron.

Minitron's advantage: each small model carries none of the constraints that nesting imposes — it is a clean, independent model. Its disadvantage: every size requires its own separate pruning and distillation run over hundreds of billions of tokens; wanting three sizes means retraining three times.

Minitron is not an ancestor of Nemotron Elastic but its *primary competitor* — Nemotron Elastic benchmarks itself against Minitron throughout: "I produce 12B+9B+6B from 110B tokens in one run; Minitron-SSM needs 750B tokens just for one 9B. I'm 7× faster."

Keep this comparison in mind; the next section depends on it.

** 2025-11 | Nemotron Elastic: Pushing Nesting to Reasoning-Scale Models

*** What Problem It Saw

By 2025, truly competitive models look like this:

- Architecture is *hybrid* — most layers are Mamba (efficient at long context), a few critical layers are Attention (preserving reasoning quality). Representatives: Nemotron-H, Jamba.
- Use case is *reasoning* — long chain-of-thought CoT, outputting tens of thousands of tokens of thinking before answering.
- Multiple sizes are *required* — servers run 12B, edge devices run 6B.

Pull in all four prior papers and none of them fit:

| Paper      | Hybrid support? | Reasoning training? | Router? | Heterogeneous layers? |
|------------+-----------------+---------------------+---------+-----------------------|
| MatFormer  | No (pure Trans) | No                  | No      | Partial (Mix'n'Match) |
| Flextron   | No (pure Trans) | No                  | Yes     | Yes                   |
| MatMamba   | No (pure Mamba) | No                  | No      | No                    |

And while the Minitron baseline works, it burns hundreds of billions of tokens separately per size — far too expensive.

*** Its Solution

Four things done simultaneously, each indispensable:

1. *Group-aware SSM elastification* — Mamba's heads/groups cannot be sliced arbitrarily; the nesting here strictly preserves group structure so that any extracted sub-model remains a valid Mamba.
2. *Heterogeneous MLP elastification* — instead of every layer using the same granularity, each layer independently selects its best intermediate dimension, learned by a router.
3. *Normalized MSE for depth selection* — not just width variation; depth can also be pruned (entire layers dropped). Which layers are droppable? Normalized MSE measures each layer's contribution to the output; lowest-contribution layers are dropped first.
4. *End-to-end router + knowledge distillation* — given a budget (6B, 9B, 12B), the router outputs a set of differentiable masks determining which channels/heads/layers to keep; simultaneously the large model acts as teacher, distilling all budget sub-models together.

The training curriculum has two stages: first pre-train on 8K context, then a dedicated 49K long-context stage specifically for long CoT reasoning — something none of the prior elastic papers had addressed.

Total cost: 110B tokens, one training run, producing three nested models: 6B/9B/12B.

*** Why It Works (and Where New Pits Open)

The key to its success is *weaving four independent research threads together* — the elastic paradigm, Mamba constraints, reasoning training, and router distillation — each previously isolated. Nemotron Elastic is the first to connect them all.

The savings come from two layers:

- vs. Minitron: one run produces three sizes; compared to Minitron-SSM's 750B tokens for a single 9B, 110B tokens for three sizes is a 7× savings.
- vs. from-scratch training: 360× savings.

In terms of quality, Nemotron-Elastic-12B scores an average of 77.41 across 7 reasoning benchmarks, nearly matching the original Nemotron Nano V2-12B's 77.38 — meaning the 9B and 6B come essentially *for free*.

New pits:

- *Single checkpoint, multiple sizes* — the upside is fixed memory at deployment; the downside is that training must accommodate every budget simultaneously, and each budget must be "good enough," with all of them perpetually competing for gradients.
- *Only scales to 12B so far* — does this approach scale to 70B or 100B?
- *Dense architectures only* — what if MoE architectures want elastic experts too?

The last pit is filled by the same team in their next paper.

* Frontier Extensions

** 2026 ICML | Star Elastic: Pushing Nesting into MoE

Four months after Nemotron Elastic, the same NVIDIA authors published Star Elastic (accepted at ICML 2026).

It applies the same ideas to Nemotron 3 Nano 30B-A3B (a MoE model, 3B active parameters): embedding dimension, attention heads, Mamba heads, and *MoE expert count* — all made elastic.

One 30B parent model nests into 12B, 23B, and 30B sub-models, trained with 160B tokens — just 0.6% of the parent's pretraining budget (25T).

The key technical extension is treating "which experts are included at a given budget" as an additional router decision, so that MoE sparsity becomes another dimension of nesting.

As of May 2026, this is the latest development on the elastic LLM research line.

* Insights

Reading the whole line, *what is truly evolving is not "how to compress a model" but "how to decouple the training paradigm from the deployment paradigm."*

In the traditional paradigm, one model size maps to one training run, maps to one deployment artifact — all three bound together. Every step this river takes loosens one of those ties:

1. *MRL* loosened "one vector, one granularity."
2. *MatFormer* loosened "one model, one size."
3. *Flextron* loosened "every inference uses the same sub-model."
4. *MatMamba* loosened "nesting only works on attention-based architectures."
5. *Nemotron Elastic* loosened "reasoning models can't nest" + "heterogeneous architectures can't nest" + "depth can't be elastic."
6. *Star Elastic* loosened "sparsity can't be elastic."

Every loosened tie gives the deployment side one more degree of freedom. The destination is clear: *one checkpoint carries the entire product line* — cloud, desktop, phone, IoT all pulling from the same checkpoint.

Where might this go next? Three guesses:

- *Modality elastic* — one checkpoint can serve pure text, or optionally pull out a vision sub-model.
- *Context-length elastic* — dynamically choose granularity based on input length (short queries use the small sub-model, long CoT uses the large one).
- *Training-time dynamic elastic* — in the early stages of training focus mostly on small sub-models to save compute, then shift to large ones later.

The Minitron pruning approach will likely be gradually phased out — unless it finds an advantage elastic cannot match (e.g., extreme cleanliness of a purpose-built target-size model).

* Takeaways

The most portable lesson from this river: *design "what training optimizes" and "what deployment uses" as separate concerns.*

The conventional instinct is: you train what you use. MRL was the first to split these two things apart — *training optimizes "any prefix must be usable"; deployment takes whichever prefix it wants.* Every subsequent elastic work is a variation of this idea across different dimensions.

This means you can:

- *In research* — when you see a problem of the form "training once equals training N times," ask: "can I pack the losses for all N targets into a single training run?" The prerequisite is usually that the N targets have a natural nesting or containment relationship.
- *In engineering* — when you need to deliver models in multiple sizes or configurations, your first instinct should not be "train multiple copies" but rather "can I find a nesting structure that lets them share weights?"
- *In reading papers* — track the thread of "decoupling training paradigm from deployment paradigm." It still has room to grow in every direction: SSM, MoE, multimodal, reasoning models.

The bottom line is practical: the next time you face a "family of models" requirement, at least ask the question first — "can we use nesting dolls?" You should no longer default to training three separate runs without a second thought.
