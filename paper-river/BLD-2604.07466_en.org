* The River of the Problem

How can a large model transfer "what it has learned" to a small model — when the two models don't speak the same "internal language" to begin with?

This is not an abstract question. Today DeepSeek uses one vocabulary, Qwen uses another, and GPT-OSS uses a third. If you want to train a lightweight model that inherits wisdom from all three, you hit a wall at step one: the teacher's output is a probability vector of length 50,000; the student's output space has length 30,000; the index meanings are entirely different; you can't even say the word "alignment" with a straight face.

Why has this problem resisted a decade of effort? Because knowledge distillation, ever since Hinton proposed it in 2015, has a hidden assumption baked into its bones: teacher and student share the same vocabulary. Once that assumption breaks, every standard tool — KL divergence, cross-entropy — has nowhere to grip. These tools compare two distributions over the same probability simplex, but two different vocabularies correspond to two different simplices that don't live in the same space at all.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Hinton et al.: Let the Small Model Learn the Teacher's "Dark Knowledge"

*** What Problem It Saw

At the time, large ImageNet models (ensembles with hundreds of millions of parameters) performed well but were impossible to deploy. How do you compress them to something that runs on a phone? The conventional approach was to retrain a small model on the large model's hard labels ("this is a cat") — but that discards exactly what makes the large model valuable.

The large model is actually telling you: "This image is 80% cat, 15% dog, 4% fox, 0.1% car…". The 15% dog probability carries information — "cats and dogs look alike". That shape of the probability distribution is something a hard label can never express. Hinton named it: dark knowledge.

*** Its Solution

Have the student match the teacher's full softmax distribution rather than the hard label (using temperature T to flatten the distribution and make dark knowledge more visible). The loss becomes KL(teacher distribution ∥ student distribution).

*** Why It Worked — and What New Trap It Laid

It worked because dense distributions carry orders of magnitude more information than one-hot labels. But it rested on a quiet and fatal prerequisite: teacher and student softmax must have the same dimensionality and the same meaning at each index — i.e., they must share a vocabulary.

In computer vision this is not a problem (just 1,000 output classes). But in the large-language-model era, every company uses its own BPE vocabulary, and that prerequisite collapses. The boundary of Hinton's framework is the starting line for all subsequent cross-tokenizer work.

** 2016 | Kim & Rush: Stop Comparing Distributions — Have the Teacher "Dictate Notes"

*** What Problem It Saw

In NMT (neural machine translation), the student and teacher often use different BPE segmentations. Hinton's KL loss doesn't apply because the output spaces differ.

The deeper issue: even with the same vocabulary, in autoregressive generation the token sequences are dynamic — the teacher produces "the cat sat" and the student produces "the kitten sat", and comparing distributions position by position runs into misalignment.

*** Its Solution

Abandon per-token distribution matching. Have the teacher generate answers via beam search ("pseudo-labels"); have the student train directly on those pseudo-labels. In essence, distillation degrades to "the teacher writes a workbook; the student studies it".

*** Why It Worked — and What New Trap It Laid

It worked because it completely sidesteps the output-space mismatch — you compare what the teacher said with what the student says, not their distributions.

But the cost is steep: you only get the teacher's argmax (the single most likely token), throwing away entirely the dense-distribution dark knowledge that Hinton kept emphasizing. For the next decade, cross-tokenizer distillation kept returning to the same question: can we bypass the vocabulary mismatch while still recovering the dense distribution?

** 2024-25 | Boizard / Wan / Zhang: Forcing Two Vocabularies to Align

*** What Problem It Saw

Kim & Rush's text distillation discards dark knowledge; dense-distribution distillation must be recovered. But teacher and student vocabularies genuinely differ — a token like "_unbelievable" may be one token for the teacher and three tokens ("_un" + "believe" + "able") for the student.

This is the core tension: "dense distributions require per-token comparison" collides head-on with "token boundaries simply don't line up".

*** Their Solutions

Different camps offered different alignment schemes:

- *Boizard (ULD)*: optimal transport theory. The two vocabularies are treated as two "geographic distributions" of probability mass; find the cheapest "mass transport plan" to move the teacher distribution onto the student distribution. Theoretically elegant, but computationally expensive in practice, and agnostic to token semantics — it sees only distribution shapes, not what tokens mean.
- *Wan (FuseLLM)*: manually find teacher–student token correspondences at each position; fill non-aligned positions with heuristics.
- *Zhang (dual-space)*: project teacher and student outputs into a shared "semantic space" and compare there.

*** Why They Worked — and What New Trap They Laid

They all worked — to varying degrees they recovered some dark knowledge. But all relied on heavy heuristics: how do you align? What do you do with mismatched segments? What if lengths differ? Each scheme introduces a pile of hyperparameters and engineering complexity.

The deeper issue: these schemes share no common theoretical framework. Switch to a different teacher–student pair and you may have to re-tune everything. The field began to feel like it was patching patches.

** 2025 | Minixhofer et al. (ALM): Finding a Unified Framework for "Alignment"

*** What Problem It Saw

The previous wave of work produced one-off solutions. ALM asked: can we find a unified mathematical framework for cross-tokenizer distillation that no longer depends on hand-crafted alignment heuristics?

*** Its Solution

Frame cross-tokenizer distillation as a "likelihood matching" problem: regardless of how tokens are segmented, what ultimately needs to be matched is the overall likelihood each model assigns to the raw text (the byte sequence). Approximate algorithms then perform this matching.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Trap It Laid

ALM is the cleanest approach in this line of work. It provides a theoretically grounded objective for "alignment".

But it still performs its approximation at the token-sequence level — as long as you operate in token space, the ghost of "misaligned chunk boundaries" never leaves. The algorithmic complexity is also non-trivial.

By this point the field had reached a tipping point: all "align in token space" approaches had begun to prompt a deeper doubt — maybe the whole premise was wrong?

** 2025 | Vieira / Phan: An Inconspicuous Theorem Opens a New Path

*** What Problem It Saw

These two papers are not about distillation directly. They ask a foundational mathematical question: given a token-level language model P(t|context), can one rigorously derive the corresponding byte-level language model P(b|context)?

*** Their Solution

They prove the answer is yes. The probability of a byte sequence b is the sum of probabilities over all token sequences that "cover" b. They also provide fast approximation algorithms.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Trap It Laid

This looks like a purely academic result, but it unlocks one crucial thing: any token model can be projected onto a common space — byte space — without any training.

This built the scaffolding for the path "out of token space". The papers themselves don't say "use this for distillation", but BLD picked it up.

** 2026 | Singh et al. (BLD): No More Alignment — Project Everything to Bytes

*** What Problem It Saw

The entire ALM/ULD line does one thing: insist on finding correspondences in token space. But token space is by definition "each model's own private construction" — the harder you squeeze into it, the more awkward it feels.

Meanwhile, Vieira/Phan had just proved that a common floor is right underfoot: bytes. No matter how any tokenizer segments, at the byte level everything converges.

So why keep trying to align at the vocabulary level?

*** Its Solution

Three steps:

1. Use Vieira's fast approximation to project the teacher's token distribution into a byte-level distribution P_T(b|…).
2. Add a lightweight byte-decoding head O_b to the student (in parallel with the existing token decoding head; output dimension is just 256).
3. Perform KL-divergence distillation in byte space. After training, discard the byte-decoding head; the student remains a standard token model.

Think of it as a continuous deformation from ALM: if you replace ALM's "approximate alignment in token space" with "project both sides into byte space", you arrive at BLD.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Trap It Laid

It works because bytes are the genuine common language — no alignment needed, no heuristics, no chunk-boundary matching. The entire scheme is strikingly simple.

But the paper honestly admits: it beats ULD/ALM on some benchmarks and loses on others. "No method (including ours) consistently outperforms all others across all tasks."

This is an honest but telling conclusion. CTD has not been solved — it has been *repositioned*. BLD demonstrates that "complex alignment heuristics can be replaced by a clean byte interface", but simultaneously reveals that cross-tokenizer knowledge transfer may have an intrinsic information-loss ceiling that no change of interface can remove. A genuine breakthrough may have to wait until byte-native models (the H-Net line) mature enough that "both teacher and student are byte models" becomes the norm.

* Frontier Extensions

** The Parallel Branch: Byte-Native Models

BLD's approach is "teacher token, student token, bytes as the bridge in between". But simultaneously another group of researchers was asking a more radical question: why not simply make the student itself a byte model?

- *MambaByte (Wang 2024)*: SSM architecture that ingests raw byte sequences directly.
- *BLT (Pagnoni 2025) / H-Net (Hwang 2025)*: models that learn to dynamically chunk bytes themselves — end-to-end tokenizer-free.

These two lines are now converging: if future mainstream models abandon tokenizers altogether, the CTD problem dissolves on its own. BLD's value becomes that of "a bridge for the transitional era" — for as long as tokenizers still exist, it enables models with different vocabularies to teach each other.

** Where This Line Is Most Likely to Go

Short term: BLD's byte-decoding head will be adapted in many papers — swapping in smarter decoders (small autoregressive heads instead of a linear layer), mixing objectives (distilling from multiple teachers simultaneously), applying the technique to multilingual settings.

Medium term: someone will try to fuse BLD with H-Net — using BLD to distill knowledge from multiple token-based teachers into a byte-native student. This could be the critical path for giving tokenizer-free models high-quality training signal.

* Insight

What truly happened along this trajectory is not "algorithms kept improving" — it is that *the level of abstraction kept descending*.

- Hinton assumed a consistent output space — working at the *vocabulary layer*.
- Kim & Rush retreated to the *text layer* (one level higher, sparser).
- ULD/ALM tried to patch the alignment deficiencies of the *vocabulary layer* — adding complexity.
- BLD dropped down to the *byte layer* (one level lower, denser).
- H-Net simply erases the vocabulary layer altogether.

A recurring pattern: when heuristics for "alignment" at a given layer start piling up, the problem is usually not that the algorithms at that layer are insufficiently clever — it is that the layer itself was never the right place for alignment to happen. The real solution is often to *move the problem to a lower, more universal abstraction* where the conflict evaporates automatically.

BLD's "simplicity beats complexity" victory is yet another instantiation of this principle: when you can find a common floor that all approaches share, you no longer need to translate at a higher layer. Bytes are to tokens what assembly is to C++, what IP packets are to HTTP — at a low enough level, all protocols are the same thing.

Yet BLD itself admits "no consistent win", which reveals something further: merely finding the common substrate is not enough. Bytes may be universal, but the byte distribution that a token model generates is fundamentally a "projection" down from the token layer, and projection inevitably loses information. That loss may be the permanent ceiling of CTD. A true breakthrough may require waiting for byte-native models (the H-Net line) to mature enough that "both teacher and student are byte models" becomes routine.

* Takeaways

1. *Ask about the layer first, then ask about the algorithm.* When your solution is accumulating more and more heuristics, stop and ask: is this problem being solved at the wrong level of abstraction? Dropping one level down is usually more thorough than adding another patch.

2. *Simple solutions often win by changing the interface, not the algorithm.* BLD's core code is probably an order of magnitude smaller than ULD's, but it wins by choosing a better "interface surface". Next time you face a "very complex alignment problem", ask first: is there a common substrate where alignment simply disappears?

3. *Honestly admitting "no clean win" is more valuable than force-claiming SOTA.* The paper's title is not "BLD: state-of-the-art CTD"; the abstract plainly states "CTD remains an open problem". That honesty is rare in paper writing, but it leaves genuine open questions for follow-on work — which is precisely the mark of a good paper.

4. *Without the enabling technology, the story cannot be told.* Vieira/Phan don't do distillation, but without their byte-probability conversion theorem, BLD cannot be grounded. When doing research, pay attention to "seemingly useless pure-theory results" — they are often the key to the next engineering breakthrough.

5. This implies a transferable practice: on whatever "cross-system alignment" problem you face (not just LLMs — any data alignment between systems applies), ask a simple question: "What is the lowest-level common representation that both systems depend on?" Then try to perform the conversion at that level rather than translating at the surface. That is the most portable methodological legacy BLD leaves behind.
