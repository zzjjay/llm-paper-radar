* The River of Problems

Take a model with tens of billions of parameters and compress it until each weight is represented by a single bit — +1 or −1. That is 1-bit quantization. And the model still has to make sense. This is not a minor memory-saving trick; it is the key to fitting a cloud-scale model into your phone.

Why is it so hard? Every weight in a neural network was originally a 32-bit float capable of expressing fine-grained nuance. Cut it to 1 bit and each weight collapses to two possible values. Can the model still "think straight"? For thirty years — from pruning to 4-bit quantization to 1-bit — researchers have slammed into walls one generation at a time. Each generation saw through its predecessor's blind spot, then left a new pit for the next. Let us walk this river upstream to its source, then back down again.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1993 | OBS: Remove a Neuron, Use Math to Fix What Remains

*** What Problem It Saw

Neural networks in the nineties had only a few thousand weights, yet they were already too bloated. The prevailing pruning strategy was to look at weight magnitudes — small values get cut. But Hassibi discovered that magnitude alone is the wrong signal. A small-valued weight might be the switch for a critical pathway in the network; removing it could dramatically shift the output. The already-existing Optimal Brain Damage (LeCun 1990) examined only the diagonal of the Hessian, which amounts to assuming all weights are independent of one another — obviously wrong.

*** Its Solution

Use the full Hessian matrix (second-order derivatives of the loss with respect to every pair of weights) to evaluate each candidate removal. The Hessian tells you "if I prune weight i, how much does it perturb all the other weights?" A closed-form formula simultaneously answers two questions: which weight is the least costly to remove, and how should the remaining weights be adjusted to compensate for the resulting loss.

An analogy: demolishing a building. OBD asks "which pillar is thinnest?" OBS asks "which pillar, once removed, lets the remaining pillars shift slightly so the building still stands?" — the latter removes more and leaves the structure more stable.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Left Behind)

It captured the fundamental truth that weights are not independent. But the Hessian matrix costs O(n²) to store and O(n³) to invert — once a network exceeds tens of thousands of parameters, the computation is infeasible. That pit would remain open for thirty years.

** 2022 | OBQ: Swap "Pruning" for "Quantization", Keep the Same Formula

*** What Problem It Saw

By the 2020s, models had grown a thousandfold, and pruning was not saving enough memory. The real memory-saving breakthrough was quantization — compressing 32-bit floats to 4-bit integers. But quantization research and pruning research were two separate communities working in parallel. Frantar noticed: pruning forces a weight to 0, quantization forces a weight to the nearest grid point — both are "weight was forcibly changed; how do we compensate?"

*** Its Solution

Directly transplant OBS's second-order analysis with a new objective: instead of "minimise output change when weight becomes 0," it becomes "minimise output change when weight snaps to the nearest quantization grid point." The OBS loop — "pick the next weight to process, then update all other weights simultaneously" — is kept intact.

The transformation: replace "delete a weight" with "quantize a weight" in the OBS formula. Everything else carries over.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Left Behind)

Unifying two independent research directions under one mathematical framework immediately inherited thirty years of pruning results. But the computational complexity of OBS/OBQ was unchanged — a single layer with 100M parameters was barely tractable in a few hours; a 175B GPT model would take essentially forever.

** 2023 | GPTQ: Same Formula, Three Tricks, Ten-Thousand-Fold Speedup

*** What Problem It Saw

OBQ finished BERT-base in four hours; applied to OPT-175B, the estimate was months. The Frantar team needed the algorithm to be orders of magnitude faster while preserving accuracy, otherwise "quantizing large models with OBQ" was pure wishful thinking.

*** Its Solution

Three tricks, all grounded in the observation that large models do not need such fine-grained treatment:

1) *Arbitrary order*: OBQ always quantizes the weight with the smallest induced error first. GPTQ finds that for large models, quantizing column-by-column from left to right achieves nearly identical quality. The intuition is that as more weights are quantized, the room for adjustment shrinks, so the effect of ordering differences gets "diluted."
2) *Lazy batch updates*: instead of updating the full Hessian after every single weight, accumulate a batch of columns and update all at once — the memory access pattern shifts from random to sequential, which GPUs love.
3) *Cholesky decomposition*: compute the full Hessian inverse in one shot via Cholesky, avoiding the numerical instability that accumulates through repeated iterative updates.

The transformation: OBQ's formula is unchanged; only the "selection order" and "update schedule" are replaced.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Left Behind)

Overnight, a 175B model could be quantized on a single A100. But GPTQ's safe zone is 3–4 bit. Push further down — 2 bit, 1 bit — and its core assumption breaks: "the perturbation from snapping to the nearest grid point is small." At 1 bit there are only two grid points, +1 and −1; almost every weight is forcibly rewritten, and the second-order approximation simply fails.

** 2024 | BiLLM: To Reach 1 Bit, You Must Treat Two Classes of Weights Differently

*** What Problem It Saw

Why does GPTQ collapse at 1 bit? Because LLM weight distributions are bell-shaped — the vast majority of weights cluster near zero, but a small fraction of "salient weights" have large magnitudes. Mapping both classes uniformly to ±1 destroys the signal in those salient weights, and they happen to carry the model's most critical semantic content.

*** Its Solution

Accept that weights are not equal. Use the Hessian to score each weight's importance, then identify "salient weights" organised along column/row structure — these receive a residual approximation (one 1-bit pass over the weight, then another 1-bit pass over the residual error, effectively 2 bits); the remaining non-salient weights are split at an optimal threshold into two segments, each quantized to 1 bit independently.

The transformation: inside GPTQ's layer-wise reconstruction framework, add a preprocessing step that separates weights by importance — important weights get one extra bit, unimportant weights accept the split. The average bit-width ends up at ~1.08 bit.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Left Behind)

It directly confronted the uneven distribution problem and was the first method to achieve usable 1-bit performance on LLMs. But BiLLM is one-shot — once the weights are quantized, there is no iterative refinement. If the initial salient/non-salient boundary was misjudged, there is no going back.

** 2024 | ARB-LLM: If One Shot Is Not Enough, Iterate

*** What Problem It Saw

BiLLM makes one pass and walks away. But the error from 1-bit quantization is so large that a single pass is clearly insufficient. Furthermore, LLM weights suffer from "column bias" — magnitude varies enormously across columns, so sharing one scaling factor across an entire row or column sacrifices one end.

*** Its Solution

Propose Alternating Refined Binarization (ARB): decouple "find the best scaling factors" from "find the best binary matrix," fix one and solve the other, then swap — iterate until convergence. Like two people taking turns tuning the treble and bass of a piano.

This gives rise to two variants:
- *ARB-RC*: alternating calibration along both the row axis and the column axis — the *weight alignment* route, with objective ‖W − αB‖ (minimise the distance between the quantized weight and the original).
- *ARB-X*: bring the calibration data X into the objective — the *output alignment* route, with objective ‖XW − X̂Ŵ‖ (minimise the gap between the quantized model's output and the original model's output).

The transformation: BiLLM's "estimate once and leave" becomes "estimate, then go back and refine." Adding X is an attempt to make the alignment even more direct.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Left Behind)

ARB-LLM-RC was the first 1-bit PTQ model to surpass an FP16 model of the same size. But something strange happened: intuitively, "output alignment" should be more direct than "weight alignment" — after all, we care about outputs, not weights — yet ARB-X consistently underperformed ARB-RC in practice. Nobody explained it. That was the pit left behind.

** 2025 | AMP (This Paper): Exposing the Secret Behind "Output Alignment That Backfires"

*** What Problem It Saw

The authors stared at ARB-X's failure and asked two questions:

1) ARB-X's objective is ‖X̂W − X̂Ŵ‖ — it uses the quantized activations X̂ from the previous layer, not the original X. But quantization errors accumulate: by deeper layers, X̂ has drifted far from X. You are calibrating against a reference that is itself wrong — the target is drifting. This is *target drift due to error accumulation*.
2) Even if you replace X̂ with the true X, output alignment has a subtler trap: in LLaMA-style models, the attention mechanism is extremely sensitive to the *directional relationships* between tokens (because RMSNorm normalises each token to unit length, leaving only direction). But the output-alignment optimizer tends to reduce error on large-magnitude channels first — this shrinks Euclidean distance but distorts direction. Once attention looks in the wrong direction, the entire layer is compromised. The paper calls this *anisotropic distortion*.

*** Its Solution

Two targeted fixes:

1) *Selective output alignment*: stop applying output alignment at every layer. Within a transformer block, apply output alignment only to the *final linear layer* (aligning the block's overall output); all other layers fall back to ARB-RC's weight alignment. Simultaneously, replace X̂ with the true X in the objective, eliminating target drift.
2) *Attention Matrix Preservation (AMP)*: add a protection mask to the attention projection layers. The mask is derived from the gradient of a token-similarity preservation loss Tr[ŴᵀMŴ] — the gradient identifies which weight elements, if perturbed, would break the directional structure of attention. Updates are only permitted where the mask allows.

The transformation: ARB-X applies output alignment at every layer and uses X̂. This paper applies output alignment only at each block's final layer, uses the true X, and adds a directional protection mask to attention layers.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Left Behind)

It decomposed the counter-intuitive phenomenon — "output alignment is worse" — into two distinct mechanisms: error accumulation and anisotropic distortion, then plugged each leak individually. Results: perplexity on OPT-1.3B/2.7B drops by 4.85/3.42 relative to ARB-RC; QA accuracy on LLaMA improves by 0.78%.

What pit remains? The method is built on top of the ARB-RC framework — it is still fundamentally layer-wise reconstruction. If a completely different quantization paradigm emerges in the future (for example, a hybrid QAT-PTQ approach that jointly optimises quantization and training), the entire strategy of "incrementally patching inside the PTQ framework" may be bypassed wholesale.

* Frontier Extensions

The target paper appeared at the end of 2025, and as of May 2026 public citations are still sparse. Two notable recent directions in this space:

- *Rethinking 1-bit Optimization Leveraging Pre-trained LLMs* (arXiv 2508.06974): proposes using the pre-trained model's own knowledge to guide 1-bit optimisation, rather than relying purely on a calibration set.
- *MemeBQ* (AAAI 2026): focuses on memory-efficient 1-bit quantization, optimising the storage overhead of the ARB-style iterative refinement process itself.

The trajectory of this line has evolved from "how to represent weights in 1 bit" → "how to preserve model behaviour under the 1-bit constraint" → "how to make these algorithms themselves cheaper" — three distinct layers of the problem advancing simultaneously.

* Insight

Reading this thirty-year lineage, I see a pattern that recurs again and again: each generation of researchers believed they had identified the *correct optimization objective*, and the next generation discovered that *the objective itself was flawed*.

- OBD thought the target was "weight magnitude"; OBS said no, it is "impact on the output."
- Early quantization thought the target was "quantization error"; GPTQ said no, it is "layer reconstruction error."
- BiLLM thought the target was "weight reconstruction"; ARB said no, it should be iteratively refined.
- ARB-X thought the target was "output alignment"; this paper says no — output alignment conceals two hidden failure mechanisms.

The real progress in this field has not been algorithmic showmanship; it has been the *progressive clarification of the objective function*. Every clarification moves the question "what exactly are we optimising for?" closer to its true answer. The next step along this line will almost certainly involve someone demonstrating: "what you call output alignment is still not the real target — the real target is X." X might be task loss, some preservation criterion on the attention map, the semantic fidelity of token representations — but it will be something closer to "what we actually use the model for" than "distance between output vectors."

A second observation: when a field's methods grow increasingly complex and patches accumulate on patches, it usually foreshadows a paradigm shift. ARB-LLM already split into two branches, RC and X; this paper adds AMP on top of RC. That "patch on a patch" pattern has, historically, always been superseded by a fundamentally different new framework — for example, approaches that jointly optimise quantization and training.

* Implications

The greatest lesson this lineage offers for *how to do research* is: **look carefully at the counter-intuitive failure cases**.

In the ARB-LLM paper, ARB-X underperforming ARB-RC was the kind of fact most engineers would note and move on from — they would use ARB-RC and not ask why X was worse. But the authors of this paper treated that anomaly as a doorway, and found behind it two distinct mechanisms: error accumulation and anisotropic distortion.

This suggests a practical heuristic: treat any method in any field that "should have worked better but didn't" as a research gold mine. Those places harbour genuine cognitive blind spots, and uncovering them is far more valuable than chasing marginal SOTA improvements.

A second takeaway: when your method fails especially badly on a specific architecture (here, LLaMA), resist the temptation to immediately tune hyperparameters. First compare the architecture's inductive biases (here, RMSNorm making direction hypersensitive) against your optimization objective, and ask: "Is what my objective is pushing toward aligned with what this architecture is trying to preserve?"
