* The River of the Problem

Reasoning models (o1, DeepSeek-R1, Qwen3) can write sixteen thousand words of chain-of-thought for a single question. The issue is not whether they can think — it is that they eat GPU memory alive while doing so. Every newly generated token requires storing its Key/Value pair, and every subsequent attention step must read the entire cache. The longer the KV cache grows, the more memory it consumes and the more bandwidth it burns.

For seven years, from H2O's first discovery that "most tokens are actually unimportant" to LongFlow, the whole lineage has been answering the same question: *which tokens can be discarded, and how do we judge that quickly and accurately?* Each generation believed it had solved the problem; each generation was shown new holes by the next.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2023 | H2O: Attention Is Sparse — Keeping Just the Heavy Hitters Is Enough

*** What Problem It Saw

Before H2O, the core tension in long-context LLMs was this: attention must look at all past tokens, so the KV cache must be kept in full. A 7B model with a 32k context has a KV cache heavier than the model weights themselves. The standard workaround was blunt truncation — drop the earliest tokens when the context grew too long.

But truncation throws away critical information (e.g., instructions in the system prompt), and performance collapses immediately.

*** Its Solution

H2O made one key observation: *real attention distributions are highly sparse*. The vast majority of tokens contribute negligible attention weight most of the time. A handful of tokens — dubbed heavy hitters — persistently attract the bulk of attention.

So why not keep only the heavy hitters and evict the rest? The decision criterion: *cumulative attention score*. At each generation step, each historical token accumulates the attention weight it receives. Tokens with the lowest accumulated score are the eviction targets.

*** Why It Works (and What New Hole It Dug)

It works because transformer attention is naturally sparse — a property that emerges from the architecture itself, not an assumption H2O invented.

But the hole it dug is large: *the cumulative scores themselves must be stored*. To save KV cache, you end up keeping a floating-point "scorecard" for every token. The longer the generation, the larger the scorecard. Worse, every generation step must update all token scores — a non-trivial cost over long sequences.

H2O solved "should we evict?" but left "how much does the judgment itself cost?" completely unanswered.

** 2024 | SnapKV: Decide Early and Be Done — Settle Fates Right After Prefill

*** What Problem It Saw

H2O's running scorecard is especially wasteful with long inputs. If you give the model a 50k-token document to summarize, the prefill phase already reveals which tokens matter — yet H2O keeps updating scores throughout decoding.

Moreover, H2O's rolling judgments mean the KV cache memory layout changes constantly, which is very unfriendly to system-level implementations.

*** Its Solution

SnapKV ran a deceptively simple experiment: use a *small window of queries at the end of the prompt* (the observation window) as representative queries, run one attention pass over the full context, and retain whoever scores highest.

Once prefill ends, the KV cache is compressed to a fixed size and never touched again during decoding.

*** Why It Works (and What New Hole It Dug)

It works because the prompt has typically already "asked its question." The attention distribution just before the model generates its first token is already a strong predictor of which tokens will be needed later — a claim LongFlow later verifies experimentally in Section 3.1.

The new hole: *it only compresses prefill*. If the input is short and the output is long (the reasoning-model regime), SnapKV barely compresses anything, because the compression target (the prompt) had few tokens to begin with.

** 2024 | VATP: Don't Just Look at Attention — Value Norms Count Too

*** What Problem It Saw

H2O and SnapKV both equate attention score with importance. VATP called out this false equivalence.

The attention output formula is ~o = Σ α_i · v_i~. A token's actual contribution to the output is the product of α and v — if α is high but the norm of v is tiny, the token contributes almost nothing; conversely, a middling α with a large v norm may have far greater impact.

H2O treats the norm of v as a constant and ignores it — effectively reading only half the story.

*** Its Solution

Redefine importance as ~α_i × ||v_i||~ — weighting both the attention score and the actual magnitude of the value vector. This is a one-line change, but it is theoretically closer to "how much does evicting this token actually hurt the output?"

*** Why It Works (and What New Hole It Dug)

It works because it targets the true objective — changes to the output — rather than the proxy of attention score magnitude.

The new hole: *the cost rises further*. VATP must compute not only attention scores but also value norms for every candidate token at every step. Over long-output scenarios, this overhead, stacked on top of attention computation itself, consumes a significant slice of wall-clock time.

And VATP still leaves H2O's root problem untouched: importance estimation is a separate step that runs after attention and cannot be folded into FlashAttention's fused operator.

** 2025 | R-KV: Reasoning Models Repeat Themselves — Repeated Tokens Can Be Evicted More Aggressively

*** What Problem It Saw

By 2025, o1 and R1 had changed the game. The paradigm had been "long input, short output"; now it was "short input, long output." CoT traces run tens of thousands of tokens, and large swaths of them are semantically repetitive ("Let me reconsider," "Wait, what I said earlier...").

H2O/SnapKV/VATP's importance metrics are unaware of this. A repeated segment of reasoning trace may exhibit a similar attention pattern to every other segment, get judged "important" repeatedly, and leave behind a pile of redundant KV pairs.

*** Its Solution

R-KV adds a *redundancy penalty* to the importance metric: if a token's semantics closely resemble those of already-retained tokens, its priority is lowered. Purpose-built for the repetitive structure of reasoning traces.

*** Why It Works (and What New Hole It Dug)

It works because it is the first method to explicitly acknowledge that "KV cache compression for reasoning models is a distinct problem." In accuracy terms, R-KV is the strongest baseline in the LongFlow paper.

The new hole: *the more accurate, the more expensive*. Judging redundancy requires computing inter-token similarity, which is itself a non-trivial cost. The LongFlow paper explicitly notes that R-KV's "more complex importance estimation logic" consumes more memory.

At this point the entire lineage had drifted into a peculiar split: each generation, striving for higher accuracy, introduced more extra computation — yet the whole point of compressing the KV cache was to be *fast*.

** 2026 | LongFlow: Importance Is a Byproduct of Attention — Compute It for Free

*** What Problem It Saw

LongFlow distills the entire lineage into three indictments:

1. *Prefill-only doesn't work*: methods like SnapKV that only compress at prefill are nearly useless in long-output scenarios.
2. *Importance computation is too expensive*: H2O/VATP/R-KV all run a separate scoring pass on top of attention; the cumulative cost is enormous over long generations.
3. *Cannot be fused with FlashAttention*: every baseline's scoring logic is an independent step that follows attention. Forcing fusion requires shuttling data between HBM and SRAM, erasing the bandwidth savings FlashAttention had already achieved.

The third indictment is the most damning: you think you are saving KV cache, but in practice you are trading a smaller memory footprint for a slower operator — a bad deal.

*** Its Solution

Two core principles:

*Zero-History Estimation*: ignore history; use only the current query. SnapKV already demonstrated experimentally that a very small observation window works fine — so why not shrink it to 1, i.e., the current query?

*Zero-Cost Estimation*: stop running a separate scoring step after attention. Derive importance directly from attention's intermediate results.

The derivation: the ideal eviction criterion for token i is to minimize ~‖o_t − o_t^(\i)‖~. Under the assumption that many tokens are being attended to, the softmax denominator is approximately unchanged, so this difference is approximately ~α_i · v_i~ — which is exactly the weighted value term already being computed in the attention output formula. Taking its L1 norm yields the importance score at zero additional cost.

A Triton kernel then packages FlashAttention, importance estimation, and token eviction into a single operator, slashing attention latency from 47 ms to 8 ms.

*** Why It Works (and What New Hole It Dug)

It works because, for the first time, "deciding which token matters" is no longer an independent task — it is a byproduct of the attention computation itself. This shift in perspective lets LongFlow sidestep all the accumulated cost problems of the previous four generations.

It honestly acknowledges two limitations:

1. *Depends on query stability* — the theoretical guarantee holds only when adjacent queries are similar. In CoT generation adjacent queries are indeed similar (verified experimentally), but sudden topic shifts, tool calls, or high-temperature decoding may break the approximation.
2. *Decoding only, not prefill* — the current kernel is designed for query length = 1. Long-input-dominated scenarios must fall back to a prefill method such as SnapKV.

* Frontier Extensions

LongFlow is a fresh paper (v2 in 2026-04, first posted 2026-03) and no explicit critiques or follow-up improvements have appeared yet. The river currently stops at LongFlow.

Expected next directions (based on the limitations it identifies itself):

- Unify prefill and decoding under a single operator, eliminating the need to switch between methods
- Find new proxy signals to replace the "current query" assumption in scenarios with large query drift (multi-turn, tool use, agent loops)
- Generalize the zero-cost principle beyond KV cache — e.g., attention head pruning, layer skipping

* Insight

On the surface, this evolutionary line is about "increasingly refined importance metrics." What is actually happening underneath is *a descent in perspective from the algorithmic layer to the system layer*.

The first four generations (H2O → VATP → R-KV) all operated at the algorithm level: how do we define importance more accurately, more in line with "output loss"? Each generation introduced new statistics, new correction terms, new metadata. Accuracy did keep rising — but every increment of accuracy cost another increment of computation and storage.

LongFlow's breakthrough is not at the algorithm level. Its approximations (substituting the current query for future queries, ignoring softmax denominator changes) are actually *coarser* than R-KV's. The breakthrough is that it changed the evaluation coordinate system: *importance estimation cannot be discussed in isolation from the attention operator*.

Once you accept that constraint, everything else is determined: importance must be computable on-the-fly within FlashAttention's block-wise computation flow. This forces you back to the algebraic structure of the attention output, ~α · v~, and to extracting a signal from that intermediate tensor that already exists. The once-sophisticated importance metric reduces to "take the L1 norm of the weighted value" — simple enough to make you doubt it.

Going deeper: this is a recurrent pattern in LLM inference optimization — *when the algorithmic layer is exhausted, descend to the system layer and reshuffle the deck*. FlashAttention's original story was exactly the same: the attention algorithm did not change; only the IO pattern was rewritten, and performance jumped a full tier.

The most likely next step: push the "zero extra computation" principle upward from KV cache to higher-level optimizations — attention head pruning, layer skipping, draft models for speculative decoding. Any optimization that currently requires "an independent scoring pass" is a candidate for being recast as "a byproduct of the main path" using the same idea.

* Takeaways

For researchers: *when your field starts stacking correction terms, stop and examine the coordinate system*. The H2O → VATP → R-KV chain is a textbook example of incrementally improving a metric. This kind of additive research easily traps itself in a local optimum — everyone competes on "whose metric is more accurate" and no one asks "why does the metric have to be computed independently?" LongFlow's winning move was to change the question: not "what metric is more accurate?" but "what metric can be computed for free inside the FlashAttention kernel?"

Practical takeaways:

- *If you are doing inference optimization*, you can use LongFlow's kernel directly (the paper has open-source code), especially when deploying long-CoT models such as R1 or Qwen3.
- *If you are designing system-level algorithms*, remember that "system-layer constraints dictate algorithm shape in reverse" — treat the most expensive operator (e.g., FlashAttention) as immovable infrastructure and design your algorithm to fit it, not the other way around.
- *If you are reading someone else's paper*, "coarser approximation than the competition yet dramatically lower cost" is almost always a signal worth pausing on — it usually means the competition was asking the wrong question.
