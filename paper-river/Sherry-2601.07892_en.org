* The River of the Problem

How many bits does a single weight really need — enough to be both "small" and "fast"?

That sounds like a trivial question — fewer bits means a smaller model, which runs faster, right? Wrong.
Everyone who has worked on this over the past decade has tripped over the same hole, again and again: information theory says a ternary weight drawn from {-1, 0, +1} needs only 1.58 bits; but the moment you actually pack it into 1.58 bits, CPUs slow to a crawl. Pack it into 2 bits instead, and it flies — but you have wasted 25% of your storage for nothing.

This is a math problem that hardware geometry keeps bending out of shape.
"Theoretically optimal" and "hardware-runnable" have never quite reached each other. Every generation looks for a middle ground — and ends up sacrificing accuracy, speed, or both. The 2026 paper Sherry gives a surprisingly elegant answer: 1.25 bits per weight, lower than 1.58, yet faster than 2-bit. How?

To understand it, we need to wade down this river from its source.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2016 | TWN: Adding a "Do Nothing" Level to Binary Weights

*** What Problem It Saw

The story begins even earlier, with BinaryConnect (2015) — compressing CNN weights to {-1, +1}.
That was a radical idea at the time: one bit per weight. Multiplications collapse into additions and
subtractions, theoretically giving inference a several-tens-of-times speedup.

The cost was equally blunt: accuracy dropped sharply, several points on ImageNet.
Why? Because the set {-1, +1} is too "rigid" — every weight must take a stance, either
a positive contribution or a negative one, with no option to say "I don't care about this connection."
Yet in practice, a large fraction of neural-network weights naturally belong near zero.
Forcing them to ±1 is effectively injecting noise into the model.

*** Its Solution

TWN's fix is almost self-evident: add a third level, 0.
The weight set becomes {-1, 0, +1}. Weights that need to speak do; weights that don't, stay silent.
The information content rises from 1 bit to roughly 1.58 bits (log₂(3)), hardware cost barely
changes (still just additions and subtractions), and accuracy recovers substantially.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Dug

The intuition is simple: give the model a "silence" option and it no longer has to lie.
But TWN's stage was CNNs with relatively small weight counts, so the question of how to fit
ternary weights into hardware never surfaced seriously.
That hole only opened up in 2023, when the same idea was transplanted into large language models.

** 2023 | BitNet: Force-Feeding "Binary" into a Transformer

*** What Problem It Saw

After LLMs exploded in popularity, the cost of FP16 weights at billion-parameter scale became
untenable. Researchers looked back at the BinaryConnect lineage and asked: can binary
quantization, proven on CNNs, be transplanted to Transformers?

Direct transplantation failed — prior binary methods were mostly post-training compression, which
collapses entirely at LLM scale.

*** Its Solution

BitNet's key move: do not compress after training. Train with binary weights from scratch
(via BitLinear layers), so the model "knows from birth that it lives in a 1-bit world"
and adapts to that constraint during learning.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Dug

This was the first time a Transformer could genuinely be trained at 1 bit.
But TWN's old problem resurfaced immediately: pure-binary expressiveness is still too rigid,
and there remains a visible gap versus the FP16 baseline.

The very same authors turned around and addressed this in the next paper.

** 2024 | BitNet b1.58: Retreat to Ternary — "The Era of 1-bit LLMs"

*** What Problem It Saw

Their own BitNet's accuracy was insufficient. The FP16 baseline still led on perplexity.
The authors realized: what was actually bottlenecking accuracy was not "can we save bits"
but "can weights equal zero."

Forcing every weight to ±1 is equivalent to leaving in a large number of connections that
contribute nothing — or worse, contribute noise. LLM weight distributions are long-tailed;
a great many small values naturally belong at zero.

*** Its Solution

Take one step back: from {-1, +1} back to the {-1, 0, +1} that TWN had established eight years
earlier. Pair it with AbsMean, a simple and stable quantization rule: compute the mean absolute
value of each row of weights, use it as a threshold — values below it map to 0, the rest take
their sign.

The accuracy problem was solved directly — at 3B-parameter scale, the 1.58-bit model matched
the FP16 baseline. The slogan "The Era of 1-bit LLMs" was coined here.

*** Why It Worked — and What New Hole It Dug

Accuracy: checked. But: how do you store 1.58 bits on real hardware?
You cannot literally allocate "1.58 bits" per weight — CPUs address memory in bytes,
SIMD registers align to 8/16/32/64 bits; 1.58 is mathematics, hardware only accepts
powers of two.

So BitNet b1.58's real legacy was a deferred problem handed to the next wave:
a theoretically beautiful 1.58-bit model that, in deployment, is either slow or wasteful.

** 2024–25 | T-MAC and BitNet.cpp: Two Compromises for "Rounding Up"

At the deployment stage, researchers split into two camps, each answering the same
forced-choice question: how do you round 1.58 bits into a shape hardware can digest?

*** T-MAC's Path: Abandon Memory Savings, Just Be Fast

Problem it saw: 1.58 is not an integer. Packing tightly means five weights per byte
(5 × 1.6 = 8 bits), and every read requires bit-shifts, masking, and unpacking —
the CPU is exhausted, and the result is slower, not faster.

Its solution: don't bother saving memory. Give each ternary weight a full 2 bits,
aligned exactly like INT2. Then replace matrix multiplication with lookup tables (LUT):
precompute all possible results of "this group of weights × this group of activations,"
and at inference time simply look them up. Multiplication becomes a table lookup,
accelerated by CPU SIMD table-lookup instructions (e.g., AVX2's vpshufb).

Cost: memory footprint identical to INT2. The "savings" of 1.58 bits are gone entirely.
Each weight wastes 0.4 bits — on a 70B model, that is several extra gigabytes.

*** BitNet.cpp / TL2's Path: Fight for Memory Savings

Problem it saw: T-MAC is too extravagant. Reduced memory is the biggest selling point
of ternary quantization.

Its solution: pack 3 weights into 5 bits (3 × log₂(3) ≈ 4.75, rounded up to 5).
Average: 1.67 bits per weight, only 0.09 bits above the theoretical lower bound of 1.58.

Cost: groups of three are completely misaligned with the power-of-two channels of CPU SIMD.
Every unpack requires heavy bit-shuffling, making it even slower than T-MAC.

*** Why Neither Is Enough — The Hole Is Now in Plain Sight

At this point, the tension is sharp:

#+begin_example
                Memory     Speed
  T-MAC (2-bit)    poor      good
  TL2   (1.67-bit) good      poor
  Theoretical 1.58 ???       ???
#+end_example

The diagonal — "good memory AND good speed" — nobody had reached.
Everyone had silently accepted this as an either-or trade-off. Sherry refused to.

** 2026 | Sherry: Use Sparsification to Sidestep the Cursed Number 1.58

*** What Problem It Saw

Sherry's key observation: everyone was fighting to "encode three values efficiently,"
but three values is itself a hardware disaster — 3 is not a power of two.
The real root problem is not "how to pack 1.58 bits" but "why must every position be ternary?"

If some positions are required to be zero (not an optional zero but a mandatory zero),
the remaining positions only need to represent two values, and the world becomes clean again.

*** Its Solution

Enforce 3:4 sparsity: in every consecutive group of 4 weights, exactly 1 is zero
and the remaining 3 are ±1.

This looks modest at first glance, but the arithmetic reveals the magic:
- Choose which 1 of 4 positions is zero: C(4,1) = 4 possibilities
- Signs for the remaining 3 positions (each ±1): 2³ = 8 combinations
- Total distinct states: 4 × 8 = 32
- 32 = 2⁵, which fills exactly 5 bits with zero waste

5 bits for 4 weights = 1.25 bits/weight, lower than the ternary bound of 1.58.
And because 4 is a power of two, SIMD alignment is perfect; the 5 bits split naturally
into 1 sign index + 4 position bits, fitting cleanly into a 16-byte lookup window.

#+begin_example
  4 weights (3 are ±1, 1 is 0) ----> 5 bits -----> SIMD unpacks in one instruction
       ^                              ^                 ^
       |                              |                 |
   Constrained shape space       32 states fill        Hardware-native alignment
   (avoids arbitrary sparsity)   5 bits exactly        (no shuffling)
#+end_example

But a new problem arises: forcing exactly one zero per group of four, with the zero
position uniformly distributed, makes the sparse matrix mathematically too "regular"
— like a Hadamard transform. During backpropagation, all gradients are stirred by
the same structure, and the model can no longer learn distinguishable features.
The authors call this "weight trapping": trained weights become highly polarized,
and the effective rank of the weight matrix collapses.

The second component, Arenas, addresses this: during training, each quantized linear
layer runs a full-precision residual branch in parallel,
Y = X · T · α + λ_t · X · W,
where λ_t anneals from 1 down to 0. In the early phase the residual supports the model
as it explores; it then gradually withdraws. At deployment λ_t = 0, the residual branch
vanishes entirely, with zero additional inference overhead.

*** Why It Works — and Where Its Limits Are

Intuitively, 3:4 sparsity does something counterintuitive:
instead of chasing the theoretical lower bound of 1.58, it attacks from an entirely
different direction (sparsification) and drives the bit count down to 1.25.
No information-theoretic bound is violated — the constraint changed (25% forced sparsity),
and the lower bound shifted accordingly.

The cost is real: 25% of positions are forced to zero. The model loses some expressive
freedom. The authors argue this loss can be recovered by the Arenas residual during
training — as long as 25% sparsity does not cross the "catastrophic collapse" threshold
(empirically around 50%), the approach holds.

* Frontier Extensions

Sherry was published in January 2026. Several parallel lines of investigation were active
at the same time, representing the current downstream branches of this river:

- *TeNet, ELUTQ, Tequila*: all continue digging on the LUT side — for example, making
  lookup tables adaptive or quantizing activations into the table as well. They are
  same-generation competitors of Sherry, not successors.
- *ParetoQ (Liu et al. 2025)*: follows the "zero-point rearrangement" path (SEQ), shifting
  the center of the ternary distribution away from 0. Sherry's paper explicitly critiques
  this approach: once the zero point is no longer at 0, multiplications return, and the
  core "multiplication-free" advantage of ternary quantization is lost.

As of now (2026-05), whether "1.25 bits can be pushed further toward 1.0 bit while
preserving accuracy" remains an open question. Intuitively, the two paths are: continue
up the sparsity axis (3:4 → 5:8 → 7:10), or adopt a completely different hardware
assumption (e.g., a purpose-built ASIC that redefines alignment).

* Insights

Reading the full arc, the deepest takeaway is this: *the real theme of this river is not
"saving bits" — it is the repeated wrestling match between saving bits and hardware geometry.*

Each generation believed it was solving an information-theory problem (1 → 1.58 → 1.25);
in reality, it was always solving a hardware-geometry problem: powers of two, SIMD channel
widths, lookup-table sizes. The information-theoretic lower bound (1.58) turns out to be
an engineering trap — every team that tried to approach it precisely was punished by
alignment overhead. The team that succeeded was Sherry: change the question. From
"how to encode three values" to "how to make the distribution naturally fit a power of two."

A second observation: *constraints can be the cure, not the disease.*
Forcing 25% sparsity sounds like surrendering degrees of freedom, but that very surrender
buys hardware-native alignment. TWN's addition of the third level, 0, in 2016 followed
exactly the same script — adding a constraint (the zero level) untied what looked like
a dead knot. Many breakthroughs in research come not from relaxing constraints but from
replacing a bad constraint with a smarter one.

Where does this go next? Two plausible directions:
1. Continue along the sparsity axis, raising the sparsity ratio from 25% to 37.5% or 50%,
   pressing toward 1.0 bit (though the Arenas annealing scheme will likely become much
   harder to converge).
2. Break out of the CPU SIMD power-of-two assumption entirely and build hardware designed
   natively for 1.58 bits (analogous to NVIDIA introducing Sparse Tensor Cores for 2:4
   sparsity).

Which path wins will depend on how the market between general-purpose and purpose-built
hardware plays out at the edge.

* Implications

This arc offers three prompts for how to find problems worth solving:

1. *The problem you think you're solving is often not the real problem.*
   The surface problem was "how to pack 1.58 bits efficiently"; the real problem was
   "why insist on 1.58 at all." Sherry's breakthrough was reframing the question.
   Reframe the question, and the dead knot loosens on its own.
   Next time you are stuck, ask: among the constraints I have accepted, which ones are
   physical laws, and which are merely inertia?

2. *Old papers are a toolbox for new papers.*
   The zero level TWN added in 2016 was picked up again by BitNet b1.58 in 2024, and
   pushed to its logical extreme (forced zero) by Sherry in 2026. One plain idea from
   eight years ago was reinterpreted three times into new meaning.
   Don't dismiss an idea because it is "old" — the same idea, on different hardware
   at different scales, becomes a new tool.

3. *Something you can do: find the "theoretical lower bound vs. engineering alignment"
   tension line in your own field.*
   It need not involve bit width. It might be batch size, token length, attention head
   count, or communication granularity. On every such line, there may be an opportunity
   to "change the question."
