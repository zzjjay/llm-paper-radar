* The Problem River

In one sentence: a large model "thinks" by writing everything it has computed into a notebook called the KV cache, and the notebook keeps growing until memory gives out — can we write in smaller script (quantization compression) without disrupting the thinking?

The difficulty lies in a hidden trap. Researchers have been active on "KV-cache compression" for several years, but nearly every method has been evaluated under a convenient fiction: a long passage of text is fed in all at once and compressed in one shot (the prefill stage). Real reasoning tasks — say, having the model solve a math problem step by step — are a different story: every token the model emits must be compressed and stored immediately, and the next token is computed from that already-distorted notebook.

The error is therefore not a one-time event; it *snowballs*. The K and V values at a given layer are already slightly off because they were computed from the corrupted cache of the previous step; compress them again and they drift further; the longer the sequence, the worse the drift. More critically, KVarN shows that the overwhelming source of this snowball is not getting the *direction* wrong but getting the *scale* (magnitude) of each token wrong. A token whose amplitude is multiplied by the wrong factor produces errors that grow exponentially.

The entire research lineage is a succession of researchers wrestling with the same adversary — outliers ruining quantization — across weights, activations, and KV caches, from prefill to step-by-step decoding.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022 | LLM.int8(): The First Identification of Outliers as the True Culprit

*** What Problem It Saw

Before this work, compressing large models relied on simple absmax-style schemes: fine for small models, but once a model exceeded roughly 6.7 billion parameters, quantization quality collapsed abruptly — the larger the model, the worse the compression, which was counter-intuitive, and no one had a clear explanation.

*** Its Solution

Dettmers et al. put the activations under a magnifying glass and discovered "emergent outlier features": in large models, a small number of *fixed hidden dimensions (channels)* carry values hundreds of times larger than everything else, present at every layer and for every token. The problem became clear immediately: use a single ruler to measure all values, and these giants consume the entire range, cramming the remaining 99.9% of normal values into one or two tick marks at the far end — completely wasted.

The remedy is blunt: pull those "giant channels" out and process them in FP16; apply INT8 only to the well-behaved remainder. Work around the problem rather than eliminate it.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It works because it precisely separates the few troublemakers from the many well-behaved values. But the pit is equally clear: those high-precision channels are extremely hard to implement efficiently on hardware (mixed-precision holes inside a matrix), and it only reaches 8-bit — no room to go lower. Outliers were *identified* but not *eradicated*.

** 2022 | SmoothQuant: Outliers Are Per-Channel — So Migrate the Difficulty

*** What Problem It Saw

SmoothQuant picked up LLM.int8()'s finding but rejected the "carving holes" approach as hardware-unfriendly. It pressed further and seized on a key structural observation: outliers are not random; they *cluster in fixed activation channels*, and different tokens exhibit similar magnitudes along those channels. In other words, the troublemakers are in the *columns*, not the rows.

*** Its Solution

Since weights are easy to quantize, activations are hard, and the difficulty concentrates in fixed channels — perform an equivalent transformation: divide each activation channel by a smoothing factor s, and multiply the corresponding weight channel by s. The product is unchanged (mathematically equivalent), but the activation peaks are shaved off and the difficulty is transferred to weights, which can handle it. A single hyperparameter α controls how much to migrate.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It works because it exploits the "per-channel consistency" of outliers: a fixed per-channel scalar suffices to cut the peaks, and it can be folded offline into the preceding layer with zero runtime overhead. But it stalls at 8-bit and collapses below 4-bit; and its scaling is *single-axis* (only the input-channel direction), with α still requiring manual tuning. The idea of "redistributing error along two axes simultaneously" would have to wait for SINQ. Here the research stream bifurcates: one branch keeps attacking weights, the other turns toward the KV cache.

** 2023 | QuIP: Scramble the Matrix So Outliers Have Nowhere to Hide (Weight Branch)

*** What Problem It Saw

QuIP aimed for extreme 2-bit weight quantization. It traced the difficulty back to outliers but went deeper: quantization error is jointly determined by the weight matrix W and a "proxy Hessian H," and H is often approximately low-rank — meaning the directions that *must* be quantized accurately are concentrated in a few eigenvectors. If those critical directions happen to align with coordinate axes and carry large magnitudes, rounding error is amplified to a lethal degree.

*** Its Solution

QuIP proposes "incoherence processing." Informally, an incoherent matrix has no element that is a global outlier; energy is spread evenly. The mechanism: multiply W and H on both sides by *random orthogonal matrices* (rotate them). After rotation every eigenvector becomes a random unit vector, elements naturally flatten to O(1/√n), outliers vanish, and the product's loss is unchanged. Kronecker products make the rotation affordable.

This is the *origin* of the "rotation / Hadamard" idea that QuaRot would later inherit. (Note: the original QuIP uses random orthogonal matrices; the true Hadamard transform was introduced in the follow-up QuIP#, which is faster and has stronger theoretical guarantees.)

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It works because "rotation flattens outliers" has a mathematical guarantee — incoherence is shown to be a *necessary* condition for that guarantee. But QuIP addresses *weights* only; activations and especially the KV cache are untouched. Filling that gap would be QuaRot's job.

** 2024 | QuaRot: Carry the Rotation into Activations and the KV Cache (Weight Branch → KV Bridge)

*** What Problem It Saw

QuaRot looked at QuIP and asked: if rotation is so powerful, why confine it to weights? Achieving truly full 4-bit inference — weights, activations, and KV cache all at 4-bit — requires flattening outliers in activations too, yet the field was still using the "retain high-precision channels" workaround.

*** Its Solution

It extends rotation across the entire network's activation space, using the "computational invariance" theorem borrowed from SliceGPT: transforming weights and inter-layer activations with orthogonal matrices leaves model output unchanged. Most rotations can therefore be *folded offline into weights* at zero runtime cost; the few spots that cannot be folded (e.g., K/Q blocked by RoPE) receive an online Hadamard, roughly 1.5 per layer. Post-rotation, activation distributions are approximately Gaussian with no outliers, making 4-bit quantization viable.

(SpinQuant followed immediately with one modification: QuaRot uses a *fixed* random Hadamard rotation, but different random rotations can differ by 13 accuracy points. SpinQuant instead *learns* the rotation matrix via optimization on the manifold, further closing the gap with full precision.)

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

Rotation handles "channel-direction outliers" very well. But QuaRot assigns *one scale per token* for activations (per-token symmetric quantization), leaving intra-token residuals intact; more critically, during autoregressive *decoding*, K/Q cannot be folded offline because of RoPE, requiring an online transform every step for every token, and QuaRot itself acknowledges that "keys are more sensitive to quantization." Neither "per-token scale error" nor "error accumulation during decoding" is addressed by rotation alone.

** 2024 | KIVI: The Two Halves of the KV Cache Must Be Compressed Differently (KV Cache Branch)

*** What Problem It Saw

KIVI took a different fork and targeted the KV cache directly. Its complaint: before this work, KV-cache quantization had almost never been studied at the level of element distributions — practitioners simply applied naive 4-bit nearest-rounding. KIVI was the first to look carefully at what the KV cache actually looks like.

*** Its Solution

It found that the two halves of the KV cache have *completely different structures*: the Key cache has a small number of *fixed channels* with enormous values (per-channel outliers); the Value cache shows no obvious pattern. The conclusion follows naturally — since Key outliers accumulate along channels, compress Key *per-channel*, confining each channel's error to itself; since Value has no such pattern but is the input to attention-weighted summation, compress Value *per-token*, confining each token's error to itself. Empirically: per-token Key compression yields roughly 5× the attention error, and per-channel Value compression roughly 15× — the asymmetry is dictated by the data.

The snag: per-channel Key compression requires spanning multiple tokens, which conflicts with the streaming one-token-at-a-time nature of decoding. KIVI's patch splits the cache into a "grouped" portion (compressed per-channel) and a "recent-token residual" (kept in full precision until a group is complete).

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

Asymmetric quantization captures the genuine structure of the KV cache, making 2-bit just barely usable. But it relies on that full-precision sliding window/residual — a hard memory floor, not a true elimination. On demanding benchmarks like GSM8K, shrinking the residual window drops accuracy. It fixes outliers in the *channel direction*, but in long-sequence decoding the truly lethal problem is *token-scale snowballing* — precisely the target KVarN would later aim at.

** 2025 | SINQ: One Axis Is Not Enough — Scale Along Both Simultaneously

*** What Problem It Saw

SINQ returns to weight quantization but raises a point overlooked by every "single-axis scaling" method including SmoothQuant: when only one set of per-dimension scales is used, *a single outlier drags down every weight that shares its scale*, especially below 4-bit.

*** Its Solution

"Dual-axis scaling": assign *separate scale vectors to rows and columns*, then use a Sinkhorn-Knopp-style fast iteration to balance the variance of every row and column (the objective is to minimize the matrix's imbalance). The second axis lets an outlier's influence be absorbed and redistributed in the other direction rather than crushed into one shared scale. It is also *calibration-free*: it claims to "back-infer" approximate activation-aware column scales from the weight structure alone, achieving activation-awareness-like effects without any calibration data.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

Dual-axis scaling is a powerful remedy for "scale poisoned by an outlier." But SINQ is a *weight* method; its "back-infer from weight structure" rationale does not carry over to the KV cache — KV cache tensors are generated dynamically and *there is simply no weight structure to back-infer from*. Bringing dual-axis scaling to a setting with no calibration data is the problem KVarN sets out to solve.

** 2026 | KVarN: Rotation for Channels, Dual-Axis for Tokens — Targeting the Decoding Snowball

*** What Problem It Saw

KVarN lines up all the prior work and points to a collective blind spot: *everyone has been evaluating quantization in the prefill fiction* (NiaH, LongBench-style one-shot long-context tasks), but reasoning tasks run step-by-step decoding. In true decoding, quantization errors *accumulate across time steps* — a layer reads a corrupted cache, its K/V are already slightly off, they get compressed again and drift further, growing unboundedly. KVarN then performs an error decomposition proving that the snowball's dominant cause is *token-scale mismatch* (wrong amplitude, not wrong direction), and that a mismatched scale multiplier amplifies errors exponentially.

It also names names: KIVI's per-channel / per-token split does not fix token-scale outliers; QuaRot/SpinQuant rotation addresses channel outliers but cannot control token-dimension scale error on its own; KVQuant keeps ~1% of channels in FP16 at significant overhead; SINQ's dual-axis scaling relies on back-inferring calibration from weights, which is impossible for a dynamically generated KV cache.

*** Its Solution

Merge the best of both branches into a single *calibration-free* KV-cache quantizer:

1. *Hadamard Rotation* (inherited from the QuaRot lineage): rotate K and V along the channel dimension, flattening channel-space outliers and driving the distribution toward approximately Gaussian / near-identity. Applied online, O(N log N); not applied along the token dimension because the per-token inverse transform during decoding would be too expensive.

2. *Dual-Axis Variance Normalization* (adapted from SINQ): run Sinkhorn-Knopp-style iterations simultaneously on the *channel axis and the token axis* to equalize variance. This second set of high-precision scales directly corrects token amplitudes — the one thing every prior method missed.

Crucially, the two components are *complementary*: Hadamard first "squeezes" the distribution toward near-identity, and variance normalization "works best precisely in that near-identity regime." The calibration justification that SINQ used for weights (back-inferring from structure) is replaced by a justification that holds for KV caches directly: dual-axis normalization *directly reduces matrix reconstruction error*, suppressing the tail scale errors that would otherwise grow exponentially, so the longer the sequence and the more accumulation, the bigger the advantage.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Digs)

It works because it is the first method to aim squarely at "token-scale snowballing during decoding" rather than static prefill error, using the complementary "rotation + dual-axis" combination to simultaneously address both the channel and token directions. The overhead is minimal: quantization cost ≈0.18%, dual-scale dequantization ≤1.4%; at approximately 2.3 bits/element it achieves 2-bit SOTA on AIME24, MATH500, HumanEval, and IFEval. Remaining gaps: rotation is not applied along the token dimension (online inverse too expensive), so the token dimension is still controlled only by scaling; and the method remains post-hoc quantization rather than a fully fused operator.

* Active Frontier

KVarN is a 2026 paper and direct critiques have not yet appeared. But it stands alongside a cluster of 2024–2026 works that constitute the active frontier of this line (all are confirmed citations in KVarN's reference list):

- *TurboQuant* (Zandieh et al., arXiv 2504.19874, 2025; ICLR 2026): online, data-free vector quantization — random rotation to make coordinates near-independent, then optimal scalar quantization plus 1-bit residual correction to remove inner-product bias, approaching the information-theoretic distortion lower bound within roughly 2.7×. Its "data-free, online" character is the closest to the decoding scenario, but it does *not* propose the "cross-timestep error accumulation" framework — that is KVarN's unique contribution. KVarN uses it as a direct comparison baseline.

- *PolarQuant* (Wu et al., arXiv 2502.00527, 2025): observes that within each RoPE-paired dimension pair, outliers appear in only one of the two dimensions. It treats each pair as a 2D vector and quantizes radius and angle in polar coordinates, also converting query-key inner products into a lookup for faster decoding. KVarN compares against it for 4-bit Key. (Note: a same-named paper exists at arXiv 2502.02617 by Han/Zandieh, and a third same-named work applies polar ideas to weights — do not conflate them.)

- *HIGGS* (Malinovskii et al., arXiv 2411.17525, 2024; NAACL 2025) and *QTIP* (Tseng et al., arXiv 2406.11235, 2024; NeurIPS 2024 spotlight): both are *weight* quantization methods that do not touch the KV cache or discuss decoding error. They are methodological ancestors: the recipe of "Hadamard rotation → coordinates near Gaussian → MSE-optimal grid / lattice coding, calibration-free" is precisely the heritage behind KVarN's calibration-free approach.

As of now, "targeting token-scale error accumulation during autoregressive decoding" as a specific objective leaves KVarN as the leading shot on the frontier.

* Insights

Reading the full lineage, the surface story is "quantization techniques growing ever more refined," but underneath lie three genuine shifts in understanding:

1. *From "single out the troublemakers" to "rearrange the entire distribution."* The earliest thinking — LLM.int8() — was to identify outliers and give them special treatment (mixed precision). SmoothQuant learned to *migrate* the difficulty. By QuIP/QuaRot, the approach became: use rotation to scatter the whole distribution so that outliers no longer exist statistically. The mental model advanced from "local patching" to "globally changing the coordinate system."

2. *From one axis to two.* Virtually every early method implicitly assumed a single direction for its rulers, so one outlier could poison an entire shared scale. The SINQ → KVarN thread recognizes that a matrix is two-dimensional and its errors should be equalized in both directions simultaneously. This is a plain geometric fact that was surprisingly long overlooked.

3. *From "evaluating a convenient fiction" to "restoring the real scenario" — KVarN's sharpest contribution.* It did not invent fundamentally new mathematics; it pointed out that the whole field had been self-evaluating under the wrong scenario (prefill), systematically underestimating one class of error (token-scale snowballing during decoding). Changing the battlefield is often more valuable than changing the algorithm: once the problem is re-framed in the real decoding dynamic, prior methods' blind spots become visible, and old tools (rotation, dual-axis scaling) recombine into a new solution.

Where might the next steps go? Two directions stand out: first, *explicitly modeling* "error accumulation over time" inside the quantizer (KVarN passively suppresses it; future work might actively predict or compensate for drift); second, the token dimension itself — KVarN did not rotate along the token axis because the online inverse is too expensive; whoever finds a cheap way to decorrelate the token dimension can claim the next prize.

* Takeaways

The deepest research lesson in this lineage lives in KVarN: *before inventing a new algorithm, question whether the evaluation scenario everyone is using is even correct.*

The KV-cache quantization field is full of clever people and a steady stream of methods, yet collectively they were spinning inside the "prefill" scenario simply because it is convenient to measure. KVarN's breakthrough was not more dazzling mathematics; it was stepping back to honest autoregressive decoding, performing a genuine error decomposition, and thereby uncovering the hidden real problem — "token-scale snowballing" — that the shared evaluation fiction had been masking. The rest was recombining existing tools.

The practical implication: when you encounter a field that feels crowded and hard to advance, resist the urge to compete on algorithmic cleverness. Instead, audit the field's *evaluation assumptions* — is everyone benchmarking against a distorted simplification of the real deployment scenario? The gap between the evaluation fiction and the real scenario is often exactly where the next good paper lives. Changing the scenario is usually cheaper than changing the method, and frequently more valuable.
