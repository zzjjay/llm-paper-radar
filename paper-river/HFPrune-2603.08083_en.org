* The River of the Problem

The model has been trained, parameters stacked into billions or tens of billions. Now we need to cut some of them away so it can run on smaller hardware. The question is — which ones?

It sounds like picking apples: each parameter (or neuron) gets an "importance score," and you toss the low-scoring ones. The hard part is defining what "importance" actually means.

LeCun started asking this question more than thirty years ago. Generation after generation of researchers gave different answers: some used second-order derivatives, some used first-order derivatives, some skipped derivatives entirely and just looked at weight magnitude. Each new generation of answers arose because the previous one "measured wrong" — it assigned high scores to certain neurons, and after pruning the low-scored ones, the model still collapsed.

The fundamental tension running through this entire line of work is: *we want to know "if we remove this neuron, how much does the model's overall behavior change," but we don't want to actually remove it and run a test*. Every "importance" metric is, at its core, a proxy for that infeasible experiment. The closer the proxy is to reality, the more lossless the pruning.

HFPrune, a March 2026 paper, replaces the proxy from "the model's ability to predict the correct label" to "the information entropy of the model's overall distribution." This is not just another engineering trick — it is a genuinely substantive shift in perspective along this thirty-year research line.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1990 | OBD: The First Mathematical Language for "Which Weight to Delete"

*** What Problem It Saw

Neural networks had just emerged, but people already noticed that many weights in a trained network were "redundant" — zeroing them out barely affected performance. The question was: which ones can be zeroed?

The most naïve approach was "delete small weights." But that doesn't work: a weight with a very small numerical value might be sitting at an extremely sensitive location in the loss landscape, where moving it slightly causes collapse.

*** Its Solution

LeCun thought deeper. He argued: to measure "the impact of deleting weight w on the loss," use a Taylor expansion. Deleting w is equivalent to reducing w to 0; the change in loss is approximately:

ΔL ≈ (dL/dw) * Δw + (1/2) * (d²L/dw²) * (Δw)²

He assumed training had converged, so the first-order term dL/dw ≈ 0, and the second-order term dominates. Importance is then (1/2) * H_ii * w_i², where H_ii is the diagonal of the Hessian.

*** Why It Worked (and Where New Pitfalls Were Buried)

The elegant part: this was the first time "importance = approximation of the true loss change" became a concept. Pruning had a principled foundation rather than being ad hoc.

Two pitfalls were embedded:
- *Computing the Hessian is too expensive.* Even just the diagonal is a nightmare for a network with hundreds of millions of parameters.
- *The "training has converged" assumption is fragile.* In real training, dL/dw almost never truly equals zero. Discarding the first-order term introduces bias.

The next thirty years of research largely orbit these two pitfalls.

** 2017 | Molchanov: Drop the Hessian, Keep Only the First-Order Term

*** What Problem It Saw

By 2017, deep network scale was unimaginable by OBD-era standards (ResNet, VGG with tens of millions of parameters). OBD's second-order approach was completely infeasible. Meanwhile, people noticed that industrially trained networks often *never truly converge* — they early-stop at a "good enough" point. The first-order gradient is nowhere near zero.

*** Its Solution

Molchanov flipped the reasoning: since the first-order gradient isn't zero, *use the first-order gradient*. Importance is defined as:

I(h_i) = |dL/dh_i * h_i|

The intuition: if you change neuron h_i's activation from its current value to 0, how much does the loss change under a first-order approximation?

Cheap. One backward pass gives you dL/dh for every neuron; multiply by the forward-pass activation h, and you're done.

*** Why It Worked (and Where New Pitfalls Were Buried)

This cut was remarkably clean — CNN pruning became standard practice from that point. Countless subsequent papers are variations on this first-order Taylor framework.

But a hidden problem went largely unnoticed: *what is L here?* The default was the training cross-entropy.

In other words: *you define a neuron's importance by whether removing it degrades the model's ability to predict the correct label*. For CNN image classification, this is fine — the label is everything. But when you carry this same framework into the next generation of tasks, that implicit assumption becomes lethal.

** 2023 | LLM-Pruner: Porting Taylor Pruning to LLMs

*** What Problem It Saw

After ChatGPT appeared, the deployment cost of LLMs became the top concern. A 7B model easily requires more than 10 GB of VRAM. Pruning is the natural lifeline.

But directly applying Molchanov doesn't work:
- In CNNs, "deleting a convolution filter" is clean with no collateral damage.
- In LLMs, deleting one neuron drags along an entire chain — attention heads are groups of matrices, MLP channels are coupled in SwiGLU. Structured pruning must remove entire dependency groups together.

*** Its Solution

Ma et al. did two things:
1. Decomposed the Transformer into a "dependency graph," identifying which parameters must be pruned together (Q/K/V/O of one attention head; gate/up/down of one MLP channel).
2. Computed Taylor importance for each dependency group, using a small calibration dataset + cross-entropy loss to compute dL/dh.

It worked. At 20% pruning rate, zero-shot performance degradation on LLaMA-7B was manageable.

*** Why It Worked (and Where New Pitfalls Were Buried)

This is the foundational work for LLM pruning. Every subsequent paper either compares against it or builds on it.

A small detail exposes the buried pitfall: it still uses *one-hot cross-entropy*. That means: "which neuron matters" is judged by whether removing it makes the model worse at predicting *that one correct token*.

But LLMs are not classifiers. Their output is a probability distribution over the full vocabulary (V ≈ 32,000 or 128,000 dimensions). The model might assign high probability to both "the" and "a," and a non-trivial probability to "however" — these "sub-optimal predictions" carry a huge amount of semantic information (fluency, style, near-synonym switching). CE is blind to all of it.

After pruning, the model can still guess the label token correctly, but the "shape" of the distribution has collapsed. The model's outputs become more dull and impoverished.

** 2025 | SDMPrune: Don't Use the Label — Use the Entire Distribution

*** What Problem It Saw

SDMPrune's authors (notably: the same group as HFPrune, so this is an internal evolution along a single research line) zeroed in on LLM-Pruner's CE flaw.

Their argument: if you want to measure "how much does removing this neuron change the model overall," then *actually measure the overall change*, not just the label slot.

*** Its Solution

Introduce self-distillation:
- Keep a copy of the original model as the teacher.
- The pruning candidate acts as the student.
- The loss is not CE but the KL divergence between the student's output distribution and the teacher's output distribution.

Then apply Taylor expansion with this KL loss: compute dKL/dh * h.

Now "the holistic change in distribution" is factored into the score. Experiments showed a clear gain over LLM-Pruner.

*** Why It Worked (and Where New Pitfalls Were Buried)

The direction is right — switching from "measuring label prediction" to "measuring distributional shift" is a genuine conceptual upgrade.

But the cost is twofold:
1. *Compute doubles.* Every pruning scoring step requires a simultaneous forward pass through both teacher and student; VRAM becomes tight (especially painful when pruning 7B models).
2. *The initial zero-gradient paradox.* The student starts as an exact copy of the teacher, so KL(P‖P) = 0. This zero is a *local optimum zero* — the gradient is also zero. Taylor expansion gives dKL/dh = 0 for all neurons; importance scores are all zero, and no ranking is possible.
   In practice they used tricks (perturbation, adding noise) to work around this, but fundamentally the starting point is ill-posed.

HFPrune looks directly at these two wounds.

** 2026 | HFPrune: No Teacher Needed — the Model's Own Entropy Is Enough

*** What Problem It Saw

HFPrune stands on SDMPrune's shoulders but realizes: the "distributional signal" you actually want *does not require* a comparison to a teacher.

The model's output distribution has an intrinsic statistical quantity all its own: *information entropy H(P)*.

H(P) = -Σ p_j * log(p_j)

This quantity tells you how confident the model's prediction is (low entropy = sharp and confident; high entropy = hesitant and diffuse).

*** Its Solution

Replace the Taylor expansion's objective function — CE or KL — with H(P):

I(h_i) = |∂H(P) / ∂h_i * h_i|

This quantity answers: *if neuron h_i is removed, how much does the "shape entropy" of the model's output distribution change?*

If removing it has a large effect on entropy, that neuron is actively shaping the distribution's form — it is important, keep it. If removing it barely changes entropy, the neuron contributes nothing to the distribution's shape — it can be pruned.

*** Why It Worked (and Where New Pitfalls Might Lie)

Three fundamental advantages:
1. *No labels needed.* H(P) is computed entirely from the model's own output. Pruning scores can be computed on any unannotated corpus.
2. *No teacher needed.* The compute overhead of SDMPrune is cut in half.
3. *No zero-gradient problem.* The model's initial output distribution already has nonzero entropy; ∂H/∂h is nonzero from the start, giving Taylor a signal immediately.

Entropy is also philosophically elegant — it measures "the intrinsic structure of the distribution," not "the distance from some reference." The progression CE (label as reference) → KL (teacher as reference) → H (no reference) is a step-by-step move toward greater abstraction and fewer dependencies.

Experimentally: on LLaMA-2-7B at 20% pruning rate, HFPrune achieves 59.0% average zero-shot accuracy, *surpassing the unpruned baseline at 58.3%*. This counterintuitive result (pruned model outperforms the original) hints that entropy regularization may itself have a denoising effect.

New pitfalls exist too, though the paper does not state them explicitly:
- Entropy only sees the "shape" of the distribution, not whether the distribution is "correct." In an extreme case, a model that assigns uniform probability 1/V to every token (maximum entropy) and a model with a near-perfect prediction (near-zero entropy, correct answer) might be treated very differently by this criterion — but whether the direction of that treatment is always right lacks theoretical guarantees.
- Using entropy as a criterion presupposes that the model's pre-trained distribution is already reasonable. If the model's predictions are garbage to begin with (e.g., training hasn't progressed far), entropy-based pruning is simply preserving the shape of garbage.

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026, HFPrune was posted to arXiv only in March 2026, and no direct follow-up work exists yet. But several adjacent threads are worth watching:

** Contemporary | NEPENTHE / DenoiseRotator: Another Use of Entropy

NEPENTHE (Liao et al., 2024) and DenoiseRotator (Gu et al., 2025) also use entropy, but in different places:
- NEPENTHE uses "activation entropy" to identify low-activity layers and removes them entirely.
- DenoiseRotator uses learned rotation matrices to *reduce* the entropy of the weight-importance distribution, concentrating importance onto fewer weights before pruning.

The distinction from HFPrune: those two papers use entropy to *assist* pruning decisions; HFPrune uses entropy *as the Taylor objective function itself*. The former treats entropy as a tool; the latter treats entropy as a principle.

** Likely Next Steps

Following the evolutionary logic of this line, the next paper will likely ask:
- Since entropy is a "reference-free distributional measure," can we go one step further and use other reference-free quantities (Fisher information, predictive variance, mutual information across layers)?
- Pruning + quantization interaction: HFPrune is structured pruning — does the entropy criterion remain valid when combined with INT4/FP4 quantization?
- Entropy pruning + training dynamics: can entropy be used as a regularization term during pre-training or SFT, so the model "grows up already easy to prune"?

* Insights

** What Is Really Changing Along This Line

On the surface, it looks like "importance metrics" iterating. At a deeper level, it is *our understanding of what the model is doing* that iterates.

- OBD era: the model is a function "parameters → loss value"; importance = second-order contribution to loss.
- Molchanov era: the model is a function "parameters → classification accuracy"; importance = first-order contribution to correct classification.
- LLM-Pruner era: the model is a function "parameters → prediction of the next token," but the classification mindset was carried over.
- SDMPrune era: recognition that the model is really a function "parameters → a distribution over the full vocabulary"; KL divergence begins to measure distributional shift.
- HFPrune era: further recognition that the "shape information" of the distribution itself is sufficient — no external reference is needed.

Each transition is accompanied by the sudden realization: *"we have been optimizing the wrong objective function all along."* This is not a technical detail — it is a leap in the level of understanding.

** A More General Pattern

In any research line that uses a proxy to approximate an infeasible experiment, the evolutionary path follows a remarkably consistent template:
1. Find a mathematically elegant but computationally infeasible exact solution (OBD's Hessian).
2. Use engineering simplifications to make it feasible (Molchanov's first-order Taylor).
3. Discover that the original assumptions break in the new setting (LLMs are not classifiers).
4. Reshape the proxy to fit the new setting (SDMPrune's KL).
5. Realize the proxy itself can be made purer (HFPrune's entropy).

This 1→5 trajectory appears in nearly identical form in pruning, quantization, distillation, and neural architecture search.

** Where This Is Most Likely to Go Next

I would bet on two directions:
1. *From "reference-free criterion" toward "self-referential criterion."* For instance, having the model continuously align with a geometric snapshot of itself from before pruning (manifold distance, tangent kernel similarity), but implemented at extremely low cost (unlike SDMPrune's two-pass approach).
2. *A joint criterion for pruning + quantization.* Currently the two are done separately, but they are both doing the same thing: pushing the model toward sparser or lower-precision representations. A unified "information fidelity" measure — entropy is a natural starting point — could collapse them into a single operation.

* Implications

** For How to Find Problems

The most powerful papers don't invent new mechanisms — they *replace an objective function that everyone silently accepted*.

After LLM-Pruner, everyone was computing dCE/dh. Nobody stopped to ask "why CE?" HFPrune asked — and the entire paper is just swapping CE for H(P), leaving the rest of the framework untouched.

This means: in any mature field, find "the loss everyone uses / the metric everyone reports / the evaluation protocol everyone follows," and ask sincerely — *does this thing actually correspond to what I want?* Eight times out of ten the answer is "not perfectly." The two times it's "not at all" — that's your paper.

** For How to Read Papers

The benefits of reading backward are especially vivid here. If you read HFPrune in isolation, you think: "oh, they just swapped CE for entropy — a trick." But tracing the chain OBD → Molchanov → LLM-Pruner → SDMPrune, you realize this is the *third major perspective shift* in a thirty-year research line (loss perspective → distributional perspective → self-referential perspective).

This ability to "locate a paper within a long arc" lets you judge what is a genuine contribution and what is incremental fitting.

** Practical Implications for Pruning / Quantization

If you are pruning an LLM today:
- Stop using LLM-Pruner's default CE setting. Switch to HFPrune's entropy criterion — both theory and experiments support it more robustly.
- If your budget is extremely tight (you cannot afford even one extra forward pass), HFPrune is currently the cheapest "principled" pruning scheme available.
- If your downstream research involves pruning + SFT or pruning + quantization, plugging entropy in as a swappable "proxy function" is more likely to yield new results than continuing to iterate inside the CE framework.
