* One-line positioning

The problem this paper solves: you quantize a large model, accelerate it, drop it into vLLM, run your benchmarks, and find accuracy dropped 0.3% — did the optimization hurt the model, or is that just evaluation noise? The field has been eyeballing means. This paper nails the question down with a 1947 statistical test.

* Forward narrative: how the problem grew

** Act I (1947, McNemar): "The same patients took two drugs — how do we compare them?"

Shift the scene to 1947 medical statistics. You give 100 patients drug A and then drug B; the numbers come back as 45 cured by A and 50 cured by B. Can you conclude B is better?

If these were two separate groups of patients you would simply run an independent-samples z-test and be done. But here it is the *same people*: each individual experienced both A and B. The two cure rates are not independent — a patient with a robust constitution is more likely to be cured under either drug, and that correlation must be absorbed into the test.

McNemar noticed this and wrote down a key observation:

#+begin_quote
"In many situations the sampling variance of the difference between two proportions must take into account the fact that the two proportions are not based on independent samples."
#+end_quote

His solution is elegantly minimal: look only at the *discordant samples*. Patients cured by both drugs, or by neither, carry no information about which drug is better; the signal lives entirely in those cured by A but not B, or vice versa. Place the discordant counts in the two off-diagonal cells b and c of a 2×2 table; the test statistic is (b−c)^2 / (b+c), which follows an asymptotic chi-squared distribution with 1 degree of freedom.

A clean tool — but it was born in medicine, and for decades it lay quietly unnoticed in the ML community.

** Act II (1996–2024, ML reuse): "Which classifier is better?"

After Meshbane & Morris (1996), a handful of researchers remembered the analogy — comparing two ML models' right/wrong answers on the same test set is structurally identical to "the same patients taking two drugs." So McNemar's test was imported to compare SVMs against decision trees. Rainio et al. (2024) followed the same thread.

Yang et al. (2025b) extended it to cross-validation — but at a cost: to remain rigorous in a more complex setting they loosened the null hypothesis boundary so much that the test became *overly conservative*, failing to detect differences it should have caught.

At this point McNemar was still confined to its niche. Almost nobody in the LLM evaluation world had thought to reach for it.

** Act III (2024, Miller): "lm-eval-harness std-err cannot just be differenced"

The turning point came in 2024. lm-evaluation-harness, the de-facto standard for LLM evaluation, reports a standard error for each task. The natural move when comparing two models is to take the difference of means and combine the two variances under a square root, treating that as the standard error of the difference.

Miller pointed out in a thinking machines blog post that this is wrong. Because the two models are evaluated on the *same samples*, their per-question outcomes are correlated. Assuming independence inflates the uncertainty of the difference by roughly sqrt(5) ≈ 2.2× under typical LLM evaluation parameters.

The consequence of overestimating uncertainty: a real degradation gets misclassified as "statistical noise," and you ship the broken quantized model to production.

Miller diagnosed the disease but did not prescribe a cure.

** Act IV (2024 NeurIPS, Dutta): "Accuracy isn't even a good metric"

Dutta et al. (2024) delivered a conclusion that made the whole field uncomfortable:

#+begin_quote
"All [examined] quantization schemes show negligible accuracy differences (0–2%) compared with their 16-bit counterparts, yet model behaviour does change."
#+end_quote

Their reasoning: since accuracy is nearly unchanged while generated outputs clearly differ, accuracy is a poor instrument for detecting degradation. They therefore proposed *flip probability* — the probability that the judgment on a given question flips before and after quantization.

This was a meaningful pivot — from "look at mean differences" to "look at per-instance behaviour changes." This paper acknowledges the value of that direction.

But Dutta missed two things:

1. *Flips happen at baseline too*: even with no optimization at all, running the same model on different hardware, batch sizes, or frameworks produces flips, because floating-point arithmetic is non-associative ((a+b)+c ≠ a+(b+c)). The observation that flips correlate with degradation holds, but flips alone cannot serve as the criterion for degradation.
2. *No statistical significance criterion*: flip probability is just a number — how large does it have to be to matter? No framework was given.

The second gap is explicitly criticized in this paper: "their method lacks quantification of statistical significance."

** Act V (2025, Kurtic & Yuan): "The word 'lossless' is looking shaky"

Kurtic et al. (2025) published a large-scale quantization study whose headline was "FP8 (W8A8-FP) is effectively lossless at all model scales." This paper singles it out as emblematic of the prevailing paradigm: run benchmarks, report means, declare "lossless" when the numbers look close enough. The critique: without uncertainty quantification, nobody can tell whether the "losslessness" is real or whether sampling error is simply hiding genuine degradation.

Yuan et al. (2025) apply pressure from the other side: for reasoning models, even *theoretically lossless* optimizations (e.g., different hardware backends) can produce accuracy swings of up to 9% — well above the 2% Dutta reported. Their focus is on the sources of these swings, but their work sharpens the urgency: the field desperately needs a statistical tool that can separate genuine degradation from variance.

By now every "don't" has been stacked up: don't eyeball means, don't assume independence, don't use flip count as the criterion, don't omit p-values.

** Act VI (2026, this paper): Disassembling and rebuilding McNemar

Now you can feel the pressure that made this solution inevitable. What Kübler et al. do:

*Step 1: Change what you test.* Instead of testing the "accuracy gap," test the *degradation probability* q_down = b / (b+c) — among all discordant samples, the fraction where the new model M̃ fails and the old model M succeeds. The paper states a clean Fact: the new model is worse than the old one if and only if q_down > 1/2.

This translates model comparison into "is this coin fair?" On discordant samples, if the two models are truly equivalent, q_down should be exactly 0.5.

*Step 2: Use the exact binomial, not the asymptotic chi-squared.* McNemar 1947 relied on the (b−c)^2/(b+c) chi-squared approximation. This paper computes the p-value directly from b ~ Binomial(b+c, q_down) — more reliable when sample sizes are small or discordant counts are few.

*Step 3: Make it one-sided.* The original McNemar test is two-sided ("the two models differ"). This paper wants a one-sided test ("the new model is worse"). The change looks minor but lifts statistical power noticeably — degradation detection is inherently one-sided; you do not care whether the new model is significantly *better*.

Connecting these three steps, the paper names the result: the *exact one-sided McNemar's test*.

*Step 4: Aggregating across benchmarks.* LLM evaluation typically runs a dozen or more datasets (as in the Open LLM Leaderboard suite), producing one p-value per dataset. How do you combine them into a single verdict of "did the model degrade overall?" The paper offers three strategies (Fisher combination / Bonferroni correction / voting), each with its own trade-offs.

Empirical result: quantizing the KV-cache and attention of Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct drops accuracy by 0.79%; the test returns p = 1.69e-5. Degradations at the 0.3% level can be reliably detected.

* The core: what is actually shifting beneath the surface

On the surface this is "add a testing tool." Underneath, three deeper transitions are happening:

1. *From a culture of means to a culture of distributions.* The entire LLM evaluation community previously defaulted to "one accuracy number is enough." This line of work pushes everyone to acknowledge that numbers carry uncertainty, and a leaderboard that omits uncertainty is propagating illusions. Miller, Dutta, Yuan, and this paper are different positions in the same camp.

2. *Rehabilitation of paired data.* LLM evaluation is naturally paired (two models answer the same questions), yet the field has been applying unpaired tools. This problem was solved in medicine in 1947; rediscovering it took 77 years. A reminder: recycling old tools across fields often has more leverage than inventing new algorithms.

3. *Quality gates for the optimization era.* With W8A8, KV-cache quantization, and speculative decoding all running in parallel, you need a statistical gate that automatically determines "is this optimization safe?" — because human eyeballing cannot keep pace. This paper is fundamentally building a *CI/CD gate for LLM optimization*.

* Where this might go next

- *From binary to continuous scoring*: the current framework assumes 0/1 grading. When generative tasks use ROUGE/BLEU/LLM-as-judge to produce continuous scores, different tests are needed (Appendix D of the paper makes a preliminary extension).
- *Online drift detection*: the current setup is an offline comparison of two model snapshots. Rolling live deployments require sequential tests with early stopping.
- *Generative quality beyond task correctness*: the framework captures degradation in "getting questions right." But quantization can also harm writing style, safety alignment, and long-context coherence — dimensions not covered by benchmark question banks. Future work will ask how to bring "non-task" degradation into a statistical testing framework.
- *Integration with leaderboards*: if the Hugging Face Open LLM Leaderboard were to add McNemar p-values as a standard reported field, the entire ecosystem around releasing quantized models would be forced to raise its standards by one tier.
