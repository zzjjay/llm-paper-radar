* The River of Problems

A 70B-parameter model originally requires 140 GB of VRAM. Can we fit it onto a single 8 GB consumer GPU?

That is the ultimate question running through the entire LLM quantization line: each weight is originally a 16-bit float — can we compress it to 1 bit, or even fewer? And after pushing that far, will the model still be usable?

The difficulty is that every bit we cut away causes a cliff-like drop in model capability. And it gets harder the further you go — compressing from 16 bits down to 4 bits has already cost countless researchers bitter lessons; squeezing from 4 bits to 1 bit is a battle of another magnitude entirely; going further to *sub-1-bit* (averaging less than 1 bit per weight) is territory most people have not dared to enter.

Every generation arrives at this river's edge and asks the same question: can we squeeze out one more drop of precision? Can we shave off one more unit of compute?

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2020 | AdaRound: Whether to Round Up or Down During Quantization Is a Learnable Problem

*** What Problem It Saw

Before AdaRound, the default operation in quantization was one sentence: "round to nearest." A weight of 0.37 rounds to 0; a weight of 0.51 rounds to 1. Seemingly obvious.

But Nagel et al. realized something: rounding to nearest makes each weight's decision *independently*, without considering what role that weight plays in the *entire layer's* output. A single weight may appear to need rounding down on its own, but if it and its neighbors all round up together, the whole layer's output actually stays closer to the original model.

In other words, per-element optimality does not equal whole-layer optimality.

*** Their Solution

Treat "up or down" as a 0/1 variable and learn one for each weight. How? The objective is to make *this layer's quantized output* stay as close as possible to *this layer's original output*. This is a combinatorial optimization problem, but they applied a continuous-relaxation trick to make it differentiable so SGD could run.

Done layer by layer (block-by-block), it requires no full retraining and no labeled data — just a small calibration set passed through once.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pitfall It Buried)

It told the community for the first time: "quantization is not a blunt instrument — it is something you can *optimize*." This laid the methodological foundation for all later PTQ (Post-Training Quantization) methods: *use a small dataset + block-level reconstruction* to recover accuracy.

Pitfall buried: AdaRound's optimization is SGD-based, tolerable for a 100M-parameter CNN but completely unscalable for a 10B-parameter LLM.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Compressing LLM Quantization from "Days" to "Hours"

*** What Problem It Saw

LLMs arrived. GPT-3 had 175B parameters. The SGD-based optimization in AdaRound was entirely impractical at that scale — a single layer would have hundreds of millions of 0/1 variables to learn, taking a week or more to finish.

Yet the memory pressure on LLMs was enormous. A 70B model in FP16 needs 140 GB — at least two A100s. Could it be compressed to 4 bits so a single A100 would suffice?

*** Their Solution

GPTQ's core insight: quantization error has *second-order structure*. If you quantize one column of weights, the remaining columns can use that error information to make compensating adjustments and recover part of the loss.

Concretely:

#+begin_example
For each layer, process column by column:
  for col in columns:
    1. Quantize this column
    2. Compute the quantization error
    3. Use the inverse Hessian to "redistribute" the error across remaining columns
    4. Remaining columns are fine-tuned to absorb part of the loss
#+end_example

This is the OBS (Optimal Brain Surgeon) idea — a 1990s neural-network pruning technique — transplanted onto LLM quantization.

The result was striking: on a single A100, OPT-175B was quantized to 4 bits in a few hours with nearly no performance loss.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pitfall It Buried)

It turned PTQ from "theoretically feasible" into "engineering-ready." After this, almost every LLM quantization paper used GPTQ as its baseline.

Pitfall buried: GPTQ excels at 4 bits, barely manages at 3 bits, but precision collapses at 2 bits or 1 bit. The reason is that it treats every weight equally — it assumes "the importance of each column of weights in the loss function is roughly the same." In reality, LLM weight distributions are *extremely non-uniform*: a small fraction of weights carries the vast majority of the "semantic load," while the rest are nearly noise.

** 2024 | BiLLM: Since Weights Are Not Equal, Stop Treating Them Equally

*** What Problem It Saw

The BiLLM team examined LLM weight distributions carefully and found a fact: in most layers, *~5% of the weights account for ~95% of the output contribution*. The remaining 95% of weights — the model can get by even if a large portion is discarded.

This made GPTQ's approach of compressing all weights to 2 bits problematic. If the important weights get damaged, the model is ruined; wasting an extra bit on unimportant weights is wasteful too.

*** Their Solution

Split weights into two classes:

- *Salient weights*: identified using Hessian information. Preserved at higher precision (e.g., 2 bits).
- *Non-salient weights*: binarized directly — each weight uses only 1 bit (+1 or −1, with a floating-point scale factor).

On average, this works out to roughly 1.1 bits per weight. On LLaMA 70B, this was the first time PTQ had been pushed to near the 1-bit boundary.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pitfall It Buried)

It made the community realize: "1 bit" should not be a uniform number but a *budget* — how you allocate that budget across different weights determines final accuracy.

Pitfalls buried:

1. Even with two tiers, BiLLM still suffers notable accuracy loss (perplexity spikes) near 1 bit.
2. Its binarization is a simple sign(W) + scalar: extremely limited in expressivity — only two values, +s and −s, with one shared s per layer.

** 2024 | OneBit: Pair the Binary Matrix with a Float Vector Duo

*** What Problem It Saw

The OneBit team stared at BiLLM's binarization: all weights ∈ {+s, −s}, with s shared across the whole layer. This means *every row and column's scale is forcibly flattened* — yet in LLM weight matrices, the variance across different rows/columns often differs by orders of magnitude.

Forced flattening is information erasure.

*** Their Solution

Decompose the weight matrix as:

#+begin_example
W ≈ sign(W) ⊙ (a · b^T)

where:
  sign(W) is a binary matrix ({-1, +1})
  a is a column vector (FP16)
  b is a row vector (FP16)
  ⊙ is element-wise multiplication
#+end_example

This gives each row its own scale a_i and each column its own scale b_j. Expressivity is far greater than BiLLM — a rank-1 float scaling matrix layered on top of binary signs.

Storage-wise: sign(W) dominates (1 bit per element); a and b are just two vectors (overhead negligible). The average is still about 1 bit per weight, but accuracy improves dramatically.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pitfall It Buried)

It revealed a key "decomposition perspective": the *binary matrix* carries the directional information of W; the *float scaling* carries the magnitude information. The two are decoupled, each handling its own concern.

Pitfalls buried:

1. OneBit requires *knowledge-distillation fine-tuning* — using the original model as a teacher to align the quantized model. This means rerunning a training loop: days to weeks. It is not true PTQ.
2. The rank-1 float scaling is still limited in expressivity. If the weight matrix's "true structure" is rank-3 or rank-8, the rank-1 scaling cannot fit it.

** 2025 | LittleBit / PTQ1.61 / ParetoQ: Multiple Attempts at Sub-1-Bit

*** What Problem They Saw

OneBit advanced 1-bit PTQ significantly, but two gaps remained:

1. *Sub-1-bit* (< 1 bit/weight) was virtually untouched.
2. PTQ + sub-1-bit + no fine-tuning — all three simultaneously — had never been achieved.

This generation of papers (LittleBit, PTQ1.61, ParetoQ, STBLLM, ARB-LLM, etc.) began probing in various directions: sparsifying binary matrices, mixed precision, multi-group scaling … making some progress, but always sacrificing either accuracy or the simplicity of PTQ.

*** Their Solutions

Each took its own route:

- *PTQ1.61*: 1.61 bits, ternary (+1, 0, −1) + salient-weight protection.
- *LittleBit*: Sparsify the binary matrix so 0 dominates, driving the true bit rate below 1.
- *ParetoQ*: A unified training framework across bit widths, emphasizing the Pareto frontier.

Common thread: all still revolve around the "binary matrix + scaling" framework, differing only in how to sparsify or how to allocate bits.

*** Why This Path Hasn't Been Fully Cleared

The fundamental issue: all these methods have decomposition structures that are too rigid. Either rank-1 scaling (OneBit), fixed-pattern sparsity (LittleBit), or a hard salient/non-salient split (BiLLM).

Nobody had asked: could we decompose the weight matrix into a *low-rank combination of multiple binary matrices*? That is,

#+begin_example
W ≈ Σ_k (s_k · B_k)
#+end_example

where each B_k is a binary matrix and s_k is the corresponding scale. This structure can theoretically approximate any W — far more powerful than rank-1 float scaling.

But there is a serious problem: how do you find the optimal B_k and s_k? This is a combinatorial optimization problem — every element of every B_k is ±1, far too discrete for SGD.

** 2026 | NanoQuant: Express Weights as a Sum of Binary Matrices, Solved with ADMM

*** What Problem It Saw

The NanoQuant team saw through the dead end of the previous generation:

1. OneBit's rank-1 float scaling is a *bottleneck in expressivity*.
2. Nobody dared use "multiple binary matrices" because *no one knew how to solve that discrete optimization problem*.
3. Methods that attempted sub-1-bit either relied on fine-tuning (expensive) or on empirical heuristics (unstable).

Their judgment: if the "multi-binary-matrix decomposition" optimization problem could be genuinely solved, the entire path would open up.

*** Their Solution

Two stages:

*Stage 1: Low-Rank Binary Factorization*

#+begin_example
W ≈ Σ_{k=1}^{r} s_k * B_k

where:
  B_k ∈ {-1, +1}^{m×n}, a binary matrix
  s_k ∈ R, a scale factor (can be a vector, per-row or per-col)
  r is the rank (can be much smaller than min(m,n))
#+end_example

Storage-wise: r binary matrices each cost 1 bit per element; averaged out, this can fall below 1 bit per element when r is chosen appropriately.

*Stage 2: Solve the Decomposition with ADMM*

ADMM (Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers) is a classical optimization algorithm from the 1970s, specifically designed for mixed problems where some variables are continuous and others are discrete or constrained.

NanoQuant casts the decomposition as an ADMM problem:

#+begin_example
Alternately perform these three steps until convergence:
  1. Fix B_k, update s_k         (closed-form solution, like least squares)
  2. Fix s_k and dual variables, update B_k  (project each element onto {-1, +1})
  3. Update the dual variables (Lagrange multipliers)
#+end_example

This is a classic recipe, but applying it to "multi-binary-matrix decomposition" is novel.

*Stage 3: Block-Level + Model-Level Reconstruction*

The initialization from ADMM is still not tight enough. Two more refinement passes are applied:

- *Block-level reconstruction*: Within each transformer block, adjust s_k to align block outputs with the original model.
- *Model-level reconstruction*: A final pass over the entire model (only the scale factors s_k are tuned; B_k is frozen, so this is very fast).

*** Why It Works

Comparing to prior generations:

| Method     | Decomposition Form                 | Expressivity | Requires Training |
|------------|------------------------------------|--------------|-------------------|
| BiLLM      | salient (high bit) + sign(rest)    | Weak         | No                |
| OneBit     | sign(W) ⊙ (a b^T)                  | Medium       | Yes (distillation)|
| NanoQuant  | Σ_k s_k · B_k (rank r)            | Strong       | No (ADMM)         |

NanoQuant achieves all three at once:

1. Expressivity (sum of multiple binary matrices, adjustable rank)
2. No training required (ADMM is optimization, not learning)
3. Sub-1-bit (choose r small enough, average bit count < 1)

In practice: Llama2-70B compressed 25.8×, running 13 hours on a single H100; the 70B model fits on an 8 GB consumer GPU.

*** What New Pitfalls It Buries

- ADMM convergence is sensitive to hyperparameters; different models may require tuning;
- How to choose rank r is still an empirical decision;
- Block-level reconstruction depends on calibration data — changing the calibration set may shift accuracy;
- At inference time, Σ_k s_k · B_k must be reassembled into W (or handled with custom kernels), so actual speedup depends on hardware-level support.

* Frontier Extensions

NanoQuant is the latest work from ICML 2026 and, as of now, represents the frontier of the "sub-1-bit PTQ" line. Possible future directions:

- *Learning the rank r*: Currently r is set manually. Can the model learn the best rank per layer automatically?
- *Combining with activation quantization*: NanoQuant only compresses weights; activations remain FP16. Compressing activations to low bits is needed to truly unlock inference speed.
- *Smarter ADMM variants*: Proximal-ADMM, linearized ADMM, etc., may further accelerate convergence.
- *Hardware co-design*: Custom GPU/NPU kernels that natively consume the "Σ_k s_k · B_k" format, turning storage savings into actual inference speedups.

* Insight

Reading through this entire line, what I see is not simply *"quantization getting better"* — it is a deeper cognitive shift:

*Quantization is transforming from "element-wise approximation" to "matrix decomposition."*

The earliest quantization (round-to-nearest) saw only individual weights. AdaRound expanded the view to "one layer." GPTQ added "second-order interactions between columns." BiLLM noticed "weights are not equal." OneBit decomposed weights into "sign ⊙ scale" — already a *linear-algebraic restatement*. NanoQuant goes further and views weights as *a low-rank sum of multiple binary matrices* — making quantization entirely a *matrix factorization problem*.

Once quantization becomes a matrix factorization problem, the entire toolkit from the 1970s — ADMM, SVD, low-rank approximation — can be brought in directly. That is NanoQuant's true leverage: it did not invent a new algorithm; it *translated the problem into a domain with an abundance of existing solutions*.

The most likely next steps:

1. Quantization will no longer be "compression" but "finding a more compact representation of a weight matrix."
2. Training-time weight constraints on the "low-rank binary" manifold — that is, the next generation of quantization-aware training.
3. This decomposition perspective may feed back into model architecture itself: if low-rank binary decomposition is sufficient, why should a model ever store full-precision weights?

* Inspiration

Lessons for "how to find problems":

- *Don't keep chiseling in a dead end.* Generation after generation of quantization methods circled the "rank-1 scaling + binary" framework because everyone believed "multi-binary matrices are unsolvable." The moment you question that self-evident premise ("unsolvable"), a whole new continent appears.
- *Old tools are a gold mine for new problems.* ADMM existed in the 1970s, but LLM quantization is a 2020s concern. Transplanting mature-domain tools into new domains is often far more efficient than inventing new algorithms.

This means you can:

- When pushing forward any field that seems stuck, first ask: "What are the field's unquestioned axioms? Which assumptions are truly immovable, and which are merely path dependence?"
- When you spot a combinatorial optimization or mixed continuous/discrete problem, reach first for the classical toolbox of the 1970s–1990s: convex optimization, ADMM, proximal methods.
