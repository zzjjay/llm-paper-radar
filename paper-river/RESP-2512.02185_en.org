* The River of the Problem

A large model has billions or even hundreds of billions of parameters. The question is: can you cut half of them away, make it run faster and cheaper, without making it dumber?

This is hard because you have no idea which parameters "matter." They come with no labels. Cutting the wrong one here and there might be fine; cut the wrong thousand and the model collapses.

It gets worse: by 2025, when "reasoning models" arrived — DeepSeek-R1, OpenAI o1, Qwen3 and their kin, which think before they answer — the road hit a dead end. Drop just 20% of parameters and accuracy plummets from 96% to below 10%. Thirty-plus years of pruning recipes meet reasoning models and nearly all of them fail catastrophically.

Why? That is the question this river is here to answer.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1989 | OBD: Don't Look at Weight Magnitude — Look at "How Much Does Loss Rise If You Zero It Out?"

*** What Problem It Saw

At the time, people pruned neural networks with a simple rule: cut whichever weight has the smallest absolute value. The implicit assumption was "small weight = unimportant."

LeCun et al. pointed out this assumption is wrong. A weight can be numerically tiny yet sit on a critical path in the network — remove it and the whole model breaks. Conversely, a large weight might simply be redundant.

*** Its Solution

Switch the metric: instead of asking how large w is, ask "if we set w to zero, how much does the loss function rise?" That is the parameter's sensitivity to the loss.

Mathematically, this is estimated via a second-order Taylor expansion. The first-order term is approximately zero at a converged training point, so the second-order term — the Hessian — dominates. To keep the computation tractable, OBD simplifies the Hessian to a diagonal matrix, assuming parameters are mutually independent.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Dug)

For the first time it bound the fuzzy notion of "importance" to something computable: influence on the loss. Every pruning method that followed inherits this core idea.

The pit it dug is that diagonal assumption. Parameters clearly interact — after you remove A, the importance of B changes. OBD simply pretended not to see this.

** 1992 | OBS: Remove the Diagonal Assumption

*** What Problem It Saw

Hassibi and Stork directly exposed OBD's simplification. Their paper states plainly: "OBD's diagonal assumption leads to incorrect scores; OBS uses the full off-diagonal Hessian and outperforms both magnitude pruning and OBD."

The concrete counterexample: when you prune A, the remaining network can compensate for part of the loss by slightly adjusting B. OBD is blind to this compensation — it only evaluates A's importance in isolation.

*** Its Solution

Use the full Hessian. When removing a parameter, simultaneously compute how the remaining parameters should be nudged to compensate, and write that adjustment into a closed-form solution. Hence the name "Surgeon" — it stitches while it cuts.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Dug)

It acknowledged the coupling among parameters. This idea is still alive today in SparseGPT, which uses a Hessian approximation for least-squares reconstruction — the same spirit.

The pit is blunt: computing the full Hessian costs O(N²) in the number of parameters. Fine for thousands of parameters; for billions, it is a pipe dream. This pit would not be bypassed until 2017.

** 2017 | Molchanov: Can't Afford the Hessian? Fall Back to First Order

*** What Problem It Saw

By the CNN era, networks had millions of parameters. The OBS second-order machinery simply could not run.

Yet people still wanted the spirit of OBD/OBS — ranking parameters by their effect on the loss, not by raw magnitude. What to do?

*** Its Solution

Take one step back. At a converged point the first-order term vanishes in theory, but on actual data it still carries a signal. So use the first-order Taylor term:

#+begin_example
Importance(parameter w) ≈ |w * (gradient of loss w.r.t. w)|
#+end_example

This quantity is activation × gradient. The elegant trick: backpropagation already computes gradients, so this adds almost no extra cost.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Dug)

This step pulled pruning from "theoretically beautiful but computationally dead" into "runnable on large networks." Every gradient-based LLM pruning method that followed is essentially a variant of this formula.

The pit it dug: you need to compute gradients "on actual data." That means you need calibration data — feed in a batch of examples and observe how each parameter gets activated. The choice of calibration data quietly became a hidden critical variable. Nobody noticed in 2017; reasoning models in 2025 would make it painfully obvious.

** 2023 | LLM-Pruner: Bring First-Order Taylor to LLMs, but Prune in Groups

*** What Problem It Saw

When LLMs arrived, people found you cannot prune them connection by connection the way you pruned CNNs. In an LLM, attention heads and MLP channels are coupled — remove a head and its corresponding Q/K/V/O projection matrices all need to follow. Pruning arbitrarily breaks the model's structure.

Empirically: on LLaMA-7B, magnitude pruning collapses at 20% sparsity; LLM-Pruner holds up to 60%.

*** Its Solution

Two steps:
- Step 1, "Discovery": identify all mutually dependent parameters in the model and bundle them into "groups." A group is the atomic unit of pruning.
- Step 2, "Estimation": compute a Taylor importance score (first-order + approximate second-order Hessian) for each group, rank and prune by group.

After pruning, recover with three hours of LoRA fine-tuning.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Dug)

It made structured pruning work for LLMs. From this point LLM pruning split into two streams: a gradient-global camp (LLM-Pruner, GBLM, GISP) and an OBS-style local reconstruction camp (SparseGPT, Wanda).

The pit: calibration data was C4, WikiText, general-purpose corpora. Fine for a general LLM, but once a model specializes — say, becomes a reasoning model — there is a growing gap between the calibration distribution and the model's actual working mode. In 2023 nobody thought this gap mattered.

** 2023 | SparseGPT / Wanda: Skip the Gradients — Do Layer-Wise Least-Squares

*** What Problem It Saw

LLM-Pruner's global gradient approach was still expensive — it required backpropagation and gradient storage. Could one go cheaper?

SparseGPT and Wanda's insight: stop thinking globally; solve a small independent problem for each layer. Each layer has an input distribution X; before pruning the output is W·X, after pruning it is W'·X. The goal is simply to keep W'·X as close to W·X as possible. That is a least-squares problem.

*** Its Solution

- SparseGPT: solve the least-squares problem with a Hessian approximation (spiritually the modern realization of OBS).
- Wanda: even more aggressive — use |weight| × |input activation norm| as the importance score, skip solving anything, prune by ranking on this quantity alone.

Both require only a single forward pass over calibration data; no gradients needed.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Dug)

Cheap, effective, easy to deploy. By 2024 it was the de facto standard for LLM pruning.

But they dug a deeper pit than LLM-Pruner: because the objective is layer-wise output alignment, what calibration data you use barely matters — you just need some representative activation range. At the time this was praised as a virtue (robustness). In the reasoning-model era it became a fatal flaw.

** 2025-12 | RESP: Reasoning Models Collapse When Pruned Because Calibration, Objective, and Model Behavior Are All Misaligned

*** What Problem It Saw

Applying SparseGPT / Wanda / LLM-Pruner to DeepSeek-R1-style reasoning models produced a baffling outcome:

#+begin_example
Qwen3-8B accuracy on GSM8K: 96.74%
   |
   | Prune 30% (using Wanda, the strongest available baseline)
   v
Accuracy: 16.38%
   |
   | Prune a bit more, to 40%
   v
Accuracy: 3.64%
#+end_example

Not "performance degradation" — total collapse. The pruned model fails at elementary-school arithmetic; it falls into repetitive reasoning loops it cannot escape.

The authors trace this to three misalignments:

1. *Calibration-data misalignment*: C4 and WikiText are ordinary web text. A reasoning model at inference time generates structured, multi-step reasoning chains — these two distributions are completely different.
2. *Pruning-objective misalignment*: SparseGPT-style layer reconstruction aligns the input-to-output mapping, but a reasoning model's capability lives primarily in the decoding process — the step-by-step token generation — not in how it processes its input.
3. *Model-behavior misalignment*: reasoning models are trained with RL on their own generated trajectories; their reasoning distribution is self-induced. Only samples generated by the model itself reflect how it actually works.

*** Its Solution

Three targeted responses to the three misalignments:

1. *Self-reflective calibration*: abandon C4; instead let the model generate chain-of-thought responses to task prompts, and use these self-generated trajectories as calibration data.
2. *Decode-only gradient importance*: when computing Taylor importance, mask out the loss on the prefill (input prompt) tokens and retain only the loss on the generated decode tokens. This forces importance scores to reflect "how the model emits reasoning tokens" rather than "how it reads its input."
3. *Progressive calibration regeneration*: at pruning milestones (10%, 20%, 30% sparsity, etc.), have the already-pruned model regenerate fresh CoT trajectories to serve as the new calibration set. Rationale: the original CoT was generated by the dense model; after several rounds of pruning the model has drifted, and the old trajectories have gone stale.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Dug)

If you have followed this river from the beginning:

- OBD/OBS taught us: importance = influence on the loss
- Molchanov taught us: estimate that with first-order Taylor
- LLM-Pruner taught us: for LLMs, prune in groups
- SparseGPT/Wanda taught us: how to do it cheaply

RESP overturns nothing in that chain. It simply surfaces three hidden variables that everyone before had treated as irrelevant: which data defines the loss, which tokens the loss is computed on, and which model state generates the calibration data.

The payoff: at 40% sparsity on Qwen3-8B, RESP achieves 81.3% on GSM8K vs. the strongest baseline's 14.40%, and 59.6% on MathQA vs. 12.60%.

The new pit: self-generated calibration requires running inference; progressive regeneration requires running it repeatedly at each milestone. Computational cost has crept back in. And because each task requires its own self-generation pass, the promise of "prune once, deploy everywhere" is partially broken.

* Frontier Extensions

This line of work suddenly heated up in the second half of 2025. At least three independent groups arrived at essentially the same answer from different starting points, almost simultaneously.

** 2025-09 | RAC (Reasoning-Aware Compression)

Directly exposes the fundamental problem with layer-wise reconstruction: standard LLM pruning targets input reconstruction, but reasoning is a decode-dominated task.
Solution: augment SparseGPT so that the reconstruction objective includes both the input and the model's own on-policy CoT trajectories. Plug-and-play.

** 2025-11 | Selective Self-Generated Reasoning (SSGR)

The first work to systematically study which samples within self-generated calibration are most useful. Finding: difficult, medium-length self-generated reasoning examples are the best calibration data.
Validated on DeepSeek-R1-Distill; reasoning capability improves 10–13% over generic pruning.
Note: its title is nearly identical to RESP's (both say "Think Before You Prune") — it is a concurrent independent work from a different team.

** 2025-12 | RESP (This Paper)

The furthest-reaching of the three: not only uses self-generation, but adds "decode-only loss" and "progressive regeneration" on top.
At the same sparsity levels, it outperforms baselines by multiples, not mere percentage points.

Consensus across all three: standard pruning + generic-corpus calibration simply does not work for reasoning models. Calibration data must come from the model's own reasoning trajectories.

* The Core Insight

On the surface, this river looks like "pruning algorithms getting ever more sophisticated." Underneath, something deeper is happening —

*Calibration data has gone from a supporting actor to the lead.*

From OBD to SparseGPT, over thirty years everyone was improving the mathematics of "how to compute importance." Calibration data was taken for granted: grab some samples from the training distribution, they are just there to "measure activations." It does not matter much.

Then in 2025 the camera suddenly cut to the calibration data itself.
- RAC said: the calibration objective is wrong (input reconstruction → should be CoT reconstruction).
- SSGR said: the calibration samples are wrong (general corpus → should be carefully selected self-generated reasoning).
- RESP said: even "what state of the model generates the calibration data" is wrong (dense model → should be the current pruned state).

The underlying conceptual shift is this: *when a model evolves from "general next-token prediction" to "specific behavior reinforced by RL on its own generated trajectories," what the model "is" can no longer be defined by its weights alone — it must be defined jointly by its weights and its decoding trajectories.* To preserve a reasoning model's capability, you must preserve its behavior on its own trajectories, not its behavior on arbitrary text.

Where does this lead next? Three likely directions:

1. *Self-generation + RL closed loop*: the cycle of prune → self-generate → prune again begins merging with the RLHF/RLVR training loop. Pruning evolves from a one-shot post-training optimization into a component of the training loop itself.
2. *Task specialization vs. generality tension*: self-generated calibration inevitably binds the pruned model to a specific task. This will either push deployment toward "prune a dedicated copy per task," or drive research in the opposite direction — finding the shared core parameters that matter for all reasoning tasks.
3. *Extending the same lens to other compression techniques*: quantization, distillation, speculative decoding — their "calibration" is also still defaulting to generic corpora. Expect the "self-generation + decode-side" framework to migrate into those areas within the next 6–12 months.

* Implications for How to Do Research

What this river suggests about research methodology is a very specific posture:

*The hidden variables that everyone takes for granted are often where the gold is.*

For thirty years after OBD, everyone was refining the mathematics of "computing importance" while treating calibration data as background. Then reasoning models arrived and reality crashed into that hidden variable — three independent groups simultaneously realized it was the root of the problem.

Practical takeaways:

- *Find the "taken-for-granteds" in your own field.* Every mature area has a set of shared default assumptions. List them explicitly, then ask one by one: "If the setting changes, does this assumption still hold?" Whichever breaks first is likely your next paper.
- *Be suspicious of "robustness" as a virtue.* Wanda was praised for being "insensitive to calibration data" — which actually meant it was not using the calibration signal at all. A method that is "insensitive" to some variable almost certainly is not being clever; it is simply ignoring that variable. When the variable starts to matter in a new setting, the method collapses.
- *When you see multiple independent groups arrive at the same place almost simultaneously, that direction is ripe.* RAC, SSGR, and RESP all reached "self-generated calibration" from different angles within three months — not a coincidence. The river had bent to this point and everyone could see it. The question is not "should I follow?" but "what is the next hidden variable I can push one step further?" — RESP goes beyond RAC by adding progressive regeneration, and beyond SSGR by adding decode-only loss. Reading all three side by side makes it clear which hidden variable to excavate next.
