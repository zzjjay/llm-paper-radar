* The River of the Problem

Why is LLM inference slow? Because weights are enormous, and the memory bandwidth for moving them from VRAM to compute units is insufficient. So everyone compresses weights — squeezing each 16-bit number down to 4 bits, 2 bits. How hard can you push?

The core question this line of work asks is: *How few bits per weight can you use without losing accuracy?* Once framed mathematically, it becomes an old problem that communication theory wrestled with 70 years ago — given a noisy channel with limited capacity, what is the minimum number of bits needed for the receiver to reconstruct the signal?

The difficulty is not "compress harder," but "know how far you are from optimal." Over the past decade, LLM quantization papers have come out one after another, each claiming SOTA, yet nobody knew whether these engineering tricks were asymptotically optimal or still a mountain away from the truth. This paper (HRQMM-II) puts the "theoretical limit" and "engineering practice" on the same graph and measures the gap between them: *0.25 bits*.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1948-1979 | Shannon / Bennett / Zador / Gersho: The Grammar of Compression

*** What Problem Did It See?

The problem communication engineers faced then was not "LLM quantization" but "how to save bandwidth on a phone call." Converting a continuous voltage signal into a discrete bit stream — should each sample use 8 bits or 4 bits? Fewer bits means more distortion; more bits means more bandwidth. Where is the trade-off?

The subtler question: if different dimensions of the signal have different variances — say, low-frequency components are large and high-frequency components are small — should each dimension get the same number of bits, or should bits be allocated proportionally to variance?

*** Its Solution

Shannon gave the rate-distortion function R(D): the minimum number of bits required when distortion is constrained to at most D. Gersho and Zador then extended this theory to high-dimensional vector quantization, proving a central result —

*Reverse waterfilling*: Draw the variance of each dimension as a row of buckets, then pour water from below. Dimensions below the waterline receive bits proportional to their variance (larger variance gets more bits); dimensions above the waterline (variance too small to be worth coding) are simply discarded and quantized to 0.

The picture is elegant, and entirely abstract. In 1979, nobody imagined it would have anything to do with compressing neural networks 50 years later.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Buried New Problems)

It works because it is fundamentally a *Lagrange multiplier* argument — "fix total bits, minimize total distortion" translates into "each dimension solves its own scalar optimization." But it left two open problems:

1. The algorithm *assumes the variance of each dimension is known* to both encoder and decoder. In the LLM setting, the decoder is a GPU — you cannot stuff a variance matrix into it.
2. It tells you the "theoretical optimum," but not "how to approximate it with cheap INT4 quantizers."

Subsequent work filled these gaps one by one.

** 1993 | Hassibi & Stork — Optimal Brain Surgeon (OBS): Not All Weights Are Equal

*** What Problem Did It See?

Neural networks were too large and needed pruning. Le Cun's group had already been doing this with Optimal Brain Damage, but under a simplifying assumption — treating the Hessian matrix as diagonal. Hassibi saw this was wrong: weights are coupled, and removing one affects all the others nearby.

*** Its Solution

Use the *full Hessian matrix* to decide which weight to prune, then after pruning use the inverse Hessian to nudge the remaining weights in the optimal direction — compensating for the one that was removed.

This is a second-order optimization idea: the loss surface near a minimum is approximately a paraboloid, and the Hessian describes its shape.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Buried New Problems)

It works because it acknowledges that "weights are not independent." But networks in 1993 had only thousands of parameters; the Hessian fit in memory. When LLMs arrived, the Hessian became d×d with d=4096 — impossible to store.

That problem would wait 30 years for GPTQ to fix.

** 2022-2023 | Frantar et al. — GPTQ: Sweep Column by Column

*** What Problem Did It See?

The OBS idea was correct, but LLMs have 70B parameters — computing the Hessian is infeasible. The practitioners of the time — bitsandbytes, LLM.int8 (Dettmers et al.) — could only do round-to-nearest with some outlier handling, quantizing each column of weights independently and completely ignoring correlations between columns.

Error accumulated accordingly.

*** Its Solution

GPTQ made an engineering masterstroke: *perform a Cholesky decomposition H = U^T U* to obtain an upper-triangular matrix U. Then sweep backwards from the last column of weights, and after quantizing each column, propagate the quantization error forward through U to all columns not yet quantized — those columns still have the opportunity to adjust themselves to compensate.

This is exactly OBS's "prune one, compensate once," recast as "quantize one column, compensate once." The Cholesky decomposition reduces the algorithm to O(d²) instead of O(d³), making 70B models tractable.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Buried New Problems)

Engineeringly, it was an instant classic — the de facto standard for all INT4 LLMs. But it has two inelegant aspects:

1. *Every column gets the same number of bits.* Regardless of whether the Cholesky diagonal entry U_{i,i} is large or small, each column gets R bits. This violates the spirit of waterfilling — important columns should get more bits, unimportant ones fewer.
2. *Nobody could explain why it works.* The GPTQ paper used a noise-shaping perspective, but that is DSP jargon with a fuzzy theoretical guarantee.

** 2023-2024 | Chee et al. — QuIP / LDLQ: Arming GPTQ with Theory

*** What Problem Did It See?

GPTQ lost accuracy on certain Hessians — specifically, when the weight matrix had directions of extremely large variance (PCA principal directions), a column aligned with such a direction would have its quantization error amplified. This happened because GPTQ operated in the *original coordinate system*, without removing the inherent bias of that system.

*** Its Solution

QuIP introduced *incoherence processing*: before quantizing, multiply both sides of the weight matrix by a random orthogonal matrix (e.g., a Hadamard transform), spreading the "principal directions" across all coordinate axes. After quantizing, multiply back. QuIP also gave the LDLQ algorithm (an LDL-decomposition variant of GPTQ) and proved a distortion bound under the incoherence assumption.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Buried New Problems)

Random rotation makes the Hessian look approximately isotropic, so all columns are roughly equally important, and GPTQ's equal-bit allocation is no longer so harmful. The cost:

- Extra overhead from storing and applying rotation matrices
- Still no explanation of GPTQ's essence — why does Cholesky decomposition plus column-by-column quantization actually work?

** 2025 | Chen / Birnick: GPTQ Turns Out to Be Babai's 1986 Algorithm

*** What Problem Did It See?

GPTQ had been dominant for three years, but theorists always felt it was mysterious — why does the Cholesky decomposition happen to be exactly right for this? Why does the error-compensation rule look the way it does? Is there a deeper structure?

*** Its Solution

Two independent groups discovered almost simultaneously: *GPTQ is equivalent to the Babai nearest-plane algorithm from lattice theory.*

Babai solved the "closest vector problem on a lattice" in 1986 — given a high-dimensional lattice and a target vector, find the lattice point closest to the target. The standard approach in lattice theory for handling upper-triangular matrices is *successive interference cancellation (SIC)* — quantize from the last dimension first, and after quantizing each dimension, subtract its contribution from the ones ahead. This is identical to GPTQ.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Buried New Problems)

This suddenly connected LLM quantization to 60 years of lattice coding theory. Every tool developed for lattice quantization in MIMO communications, cryptography, and crystallography became available.

New problems immediately surfaced: Babai's algorithm is suboptimal (the exact closest vector problem is NP-hard). How far is GPTQ from optimal? Babai's algorithm uses a *uniform-step lattice αZ^n* — the same step size in every dimension — which circles back to GPTQ's equal-bit-allocation problem.

To approach waterfilling, each dimension needs *its own step size α_i*.

** 2026.04 | Lifar, Savkin, Ordentlich, Polyanskiy — WaterSIC: Grafting Waterfilling onto SIC

*** What Problem Did It See?

Since GPTQ = SIC + uniform lattice, the deficiency is the absence of waterfilling. Can we assign *each dimension its own step size* within the SIC framework?

*** Its Solution

WaterSIC directly transplants the 1979 waterfilling recipe into the SIC algorithm: the step size α_i for the i-th dimension is proportional to |U|^(1/n) / |U_{i,i}| — dimensions with large Cholesky diagonal entries (high information content) get *fine* step sizes (more bits); those with small diagonal entries get *coarse* step sizes (fewer bits).

The algorithm adds just one line over GPTQ: compute the diagonal entries of U first, then set each column's step size accordingly.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Buried New Problems)

Theoretically, it moves from the "arithmetic mean" to the "geometric mean" — the AM-GM inequality guarantees the latter is always smaller. On actual LLMs, it saves 0.1 to 0.5 bits compared to GPTQ.

But the WaterSIC paper (by the same group, April 2026) provided only experimental results and preliminary theory. *How far is it from the information-theoretic limit?* That remained an open question.

** 2026.05 | Ordentlich & Polyanskiy — HRQMM-II (This Paper): Settling the Account

*** What Problem Did It See?

Every prior paper compared "I'm X bits better than you," but none could answer "How far am I from God?" The optimal solution (the information-theoretic R(D) curve) is a line on a plot, and all the engineering algorithms sit somewhere above it. Is the gap 0.05 bits or 5 bits? Nobody had measured it.

*** Its Solution

This paper does two things:

1. *Proves that WaterSIC in the high-rate regime is within a factor of 2πe/12 ≈ 1.4233 of optimal* (which translates to a gap of 0.25 bit/entry). Moreover, this gap is *coordinate-system invariant* — however you rotate W, WaterSIC's performance is unchanged, because it depends only on the determinant of Σ_X.
2. *Proves that GPTQ + random rotation is also close to optimal.* Specifically, on Llama-3-8B, the gap is about 0.1 bits more than WaterSIC. This means QuIP's incoherence processing is not a coincidental trick — it genuinely approaches optimality.

The technical core: reframe the entire SIC quantization procedure as *shaped lattice quantization*, then use Zador's 1982 normalized second moment (NSM) lower bound to measure the distance.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Buried New Problems)

It works because the authors imported the full 70-year toolkit of communication theory — rate-distortion, lattice coding, Voronoi regions, shaping gain — into LLM quantization. Each engineering algorithm is translated into a lattice problem and then measured individually.

New open problems (as listed by the paper itself):

1. *Low-rate theory (< 2 bits)* is still missing — and that is the most deployment-critical regime
2. WaterSIC's entropy coding is slow on GPUs; hardware-friendly shaping is needed
3. Using higher-dimensional lattices (not just Z^n) can shrink 2πe/12 to 2πe·G_d
4. WaterSIC requires amortizing over many columns (a >> 1) per row to be worthwhile — it does not suit small matrices

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026, this is the latest development in this line. A few parallel efforts cited at the end of the paper are worth watching:

- *NestQuant* (Savkin et al. 2025) — nested lattice matrix-product quantization tailored to the specific structure of LLMs
- *Qronos* (Zhang et al. 2025) — corrects errors introduced by past shaping approaches, pushing further toward the limit
- *Provable PTQ analysis of GPTQ and Qronos* (Zhang et al. 2025) — provides tighter theoretical bounds for the GPTQ family

Likely next directions: (1) theoretical limits in the low-rate regime (1–2 bits); (2) GPU-friendly entropy coding; (3) joint vector quantization across multiple weight columns rather than column-by-column SIC.

* Insights

Reading through this line of work, three things are quietly happening:

*First, the convergence of engineering and theory.* When GPTQ appeared in 2022 it was a dirty hack, unrelated to information theory. Three years later everyone discovered it was Babai's 1986 algorithm; a year after that, people upgraded it with 1979 waterfilling; a few months later, people used Zador's 1982 bound to measure how far it is from optimal. *LLM quantization has reactivated a toolkit that communication theory had left dormant for decades.*

*Second, an awakening sense of distance.* Early papers said "I'm 0.3 bits better than GPTQ" — that kind of local SOTA game loses meaning after HRQMM-II. Now everyone knows where the limit is: in the high-rate regime, only 0.25 bits remain to be squeezed. This will shift the research focus from "can I do a little better" toward "can I *prove* I am near optimal," and toward "where exactly is that large unexplored territory in the low-rate regime?"

*Third, behind every engineering trick hides an old theorem.* GPTQ's column sweeping is Babai's algorithm; WaterSIC's unequal bit allocation is waterfilling; QuIP's random rotation is isotropization. This line of work sends a warning to all "engineering breakthroughs" — *first check the information-theory textbooks from the 1970s–80s; your new discovery may have had its lower bound proved by some Soviet researcher 50 years ago.*

Where is the next step most likely to go? The low-rate regime (1–2 bits). HRQMM-II explicitly names it — that is the most deployment-critical interval, and also the interval where high-rate analysis breaks down entirely. Whoever can do the same "how far from the limit" work for the low-rate regime will write the next must-read paper.

* Lessons

This line of work suggests three research methodology principles:

1. *Find the lower bound before chasing SOTA.* If you do not know where the theoretical limit is, you can only achieve local SOTA. Once someone draws the lower bound, all the "I'm 5% better than you" work is devalued. This is the dividing line between rigorous research and incremental paper-chasing.

2. *Tools from old disciplines are gold mines for new ones.* Every core advance in LLM quantization was imported from communication theory. If you are working in a new area, ask what mathematical model your problem is fundamentally equivalent to — often an older discipline has already studied it thoroughly.

3. *Understand first, then optimize.* GPTQ was dominant for three years before anyone realized it was Babai's algorithm. If the authors had known this equivalence from the start, QuIP's incoherence processing might have appeared two years earlier. *Reframing a problem is often more valuable than improving an algorithm.*

The practical takeaway: the next time you encounter a "black-box engineering algorithm," resist the urge to tune hyperparameters immediately. First ask "what is this algorithm in more universal mathematical language?" — that is usually where ready-made optimization paths and distance metrics already exist.
