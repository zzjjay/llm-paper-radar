* The River of Questions

Why does a large model's attention mechanism keep fixating on a handful of positions that seem utterly meaningless?

Open any Transformer's attention heatmap and you'll see something counterintuitive: when processing the 1000th token, the model allocates a huge chunk of attention to the very first token — even if that token is a meaningless ~<bos>~; certain "heads" specialize in staring along the diagonal (i.e., attending only to the few most recent tokens); others behave like librarians, reliably jumping to some distant token with pinpoint accuracy. These "shapes" come in many varieties, yet none of them is random — they appear stably in nearly every LLM.

The problem is this: each time a new shape is discovered, researchers coin a name for it (sink head, retrieval head, diagonal trace…) and each one comes with its own bespoke explanation. The whole field resembles a museum without a catalog — exhibits packed into every room, yet nobody can tell you why they look the way they do. Generation after generation has worked on this puzzle, but a master map has always been missing.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Attention Is All You Need: Any Two Tokens Can Talk Directly

*** What Problem Did It See

Before this, the dominant paradigm was RNNs. An RNN resembles a game of telephone — information from the first word must be passed down the chain, one person at a time, to reach the last word. The farther it travels, the more it blurs. Worse, the "one-after-another" structure prevents parallel training: no matter how many GPUs you add, they sit idle waiting.

*** Its Solution

Abandon the relay entirely and let any two words in a sentence talk directly. Each word asks: "Whom should I attend to?" — the weights for that question are the softmax attention scores. This is the Transformer.

*** Why It Worked — and What Pit It Dug

Direct communication means information travels in one step; long-range dependencies are no longer diluted by distance. Simultaneously, all "conversations" can be computed in parallel, finally giving GPUs something to chew on.

But a first pit was dug here: what should softmax attention *look like*? The paper didn't say. It handed down a formula and let the data fill it in. So a few years later, when researchers opened trained models and looked — the attention maps had all sorts of strange, fixed patterns. Nobody had predicted these patterns would appear, and nobody could explain why.

** 2021 | RoPE: Twist "Position" into Q and K

*** What Problem Did It See

The original Transformer used absolute position encodings — a fixed vector for token 5, a different one for token 100. If the model was trained on sequences of length 512 and then asked to process something of length 4096, it was lost: it had never seen an encoding for position 2000. This is called poor extrapolation.

A later approach switched to relative positions — care only about "how far apart are we," not "which position am I." Beautiful in theory, but implementing it meant sprinkling bias terms all over the attention formula: clunky and slow.

*** Its Solution

Su Jianlin had a clever idea: instead of adding anything externally, embed position *inside* Q and K by rotating them. Concretely — rotate the Q vector of the t-th token by angle ~t·θ~, and similarly rotate the corresponding K. When they are dotted together, the absolute angles cancel and only the positional difference remains. This is Rotary Position Embedding.

*** Why It Worked — and What Pit It Dug

The rotation folds relative position into the dot product in the most natural possible way, requiring no extra additions. Extrapolation is also much better. It became the standard choice for almost every major LLM that followed (LLaMA, Qwen, DeepSeek…).

But the rotation itself introduces a *temporal structure* — Q and K are no longer purely semantic; they continuously rotate with time. This means the shapes visible in attention maps are no longer determined by content alone but by the composition of semantics × rotation. TAPPA would later seize on exactly this rotational structure — yet the RoPE paper had no idea it was planting that seed.

** 2023 | StreamingLLM: Always Keep the First Few Tokens — Don't Ask Why

*** What Problem Did It See

If you want an LLM to keep chatting indefinitely (say, a 24/7 customer-service bot), the most intuitive approach is a sliding window — keep only the most recent 4096 tokens and discard the rest. But Xiao et al. discovered something bizarre: the moment you discard the very first few tokens, the model immediately collapses into incoherence.

This defies all intuition — those tokens are thousands of steps in the past; by rights, their information should have been overwritten by newer content long ago.

*** Its Solution

Blunt and simple: always keep the first 4 tokens (even if they are just ~<bos>~ or a newline character), and apply a sliding window to everything else. These 4 tokens were named "attention sinks" — like drains, they absorb a disproportionate share of attention scores that would otherwise have nowhere to go.

*** Why It Worked — and What Pit It Dug

The intuitive explanation is: softmax forces all attention scores to sum to 1. But often the model has nothing it genuinely wants to attend to — it needs a "trash can" to dump surplus attention, otherwise it is forced to attend spuriously to truly important content, distorting representations. The earliest positions happen to be present throughout training, so they are naturally assigned this trash-can role.

But this is only post-hoc rationalization. Why the beginning? Why 4 rather than 1? With a different positional encoding or different training data, would the sink move? StreamingLLM cannot answer any of these. It provided a patch, not a theory.

** 2024 | Massive Activations / Sink Bias: Giving Sinks a Mechanism

*** What Problem Did It See

After StreamingLLM, the community broadly accepted that sinks exist — but *why* remained an open mystery. Sun et al. (Massive Activations) and Gu et al. (Attention Sink Bias) began digging at almost exactly the same time.

*** Its Solution

The Sun camp found that certain specific dimensions in some hidden layers have activation values grotesquely larger than the rest (tens of times larger). These "massive activations" cause the K vector of sink tokens to dominate the dot product over all other tokens, vacuuming up the attention.

The Gu camp's explanation leans more mathematical: softmax itself has a bias-term effect — when Q's certain directions happen to align with the initial K, attention naturally collapses there.

*** Why It Worked — and What Pit It Dug

Both explanations are elegant, but each only accounts for the sink pattern. Meanwhile, others were discovering retrieval heads (locking onto a fixed distant position) and diagonal heads (attending only to the most recent steps)… Every new shape demanded another new mechanism.

By late 2024 the "field guide" to attention patterns had over a dozen entries, yet no single theory could put them all under one roof. That is the moment TAPPA walks on stage.

** 2024 (Concurrent) | Retrieval Head / H2O: Putting Shapes to Work

*** What Problem Did It See

Wu et al. observed that in long-context retrieval tasks, only a small number of heads actually do the work of "finding the key information" — they called these "retrieval heads." Zhang et al. found that the vast majority of KV cache entries are never seriously attended to — they proposed H2O: rank KV entries by cumulative attention score and prune the low-scoring ones.

*** Its Solution

H2O is a quintessential empiricist solution: never mind why, attention-score-low means useless — prune it. SnapKV, PyramidKV, MInference, and a string of follow-on methods are all variants of the same idea — use attention scores as an "importance" signal to compress the KV cache.

*** Why It Worked — and What Pit It Dug

It genuinely works: you can cut 80% of the KV cache with almost no accuracy drop. But all of these methods rest on an unexamined assumption: *past attention scores* can predict *future attention scores*.

If a head's attention pattern is "predictable," that assumption holds. If it is constantly shifting direction ("unpredictable"), the assumption breaks. Nobody had articulated this — every compression method implicitly treated all heads as equally amenable. That unspoken assumption is TAPPA's precise entry point.

** 2026 | TAPPA: Every Shape Is Q's Temporal Self-Similarity

*** What Problem Did It See

The TAPPA authors realized that sink, retrieval, and diagonal are not distinct phenomena — they are different facets of the same thing: *how the query changes along the time axis*.

If Q evolves in a regular way over time (approximately periodic, approximately linearly drifting, approximately constant), then its dot products with past K vectors will produce a stable "shape" — this is why sink, retrieval, and diagonal look different from each other while actually being different expressions of Q's self-similarity. Conversely, if Q's evolution is itself chaotic, the attention map will look "random" — this is an "unpredictable" head.

*** Its Solution

TAPPA introduces a unified framework:

1. Treat attention as a *temporally continuous* process rather than discrete step-by-step computation
2. Measure each head's *temporal self-similarity* of Q along the time axis
3. Classify heads into "predictable" and "unpredictable"
4. For the predictable class, derive closed-form mathematical solutions for three representative scenarios (involving the interaction of Q, K, and RoPE) — directly deducing which attention shape will emerge

This subsumes the previous decade of "name each shape separately" research into a single formula: *tell me what Q's temporal self-similarity looks like, and I'll tell you what shape you'll see*.

*** Why It Worked — and What Pit It Dug

KV cache compression works because most heads happen to fall in the "predictable" category — their attention shapes are stable, so the past genuinely predicts the future. But a minority of "unpredictable" heads have attention scores that are completely uninformative about the future — this is the hidden reason why all H2O-style methods experience accuracy drops on certain long-context tasks.

The TAPPA metric outperforms baselines on both KV cache compression and LLM pruning — but more important than those two numbers is what TAPPA provides for the first time: a master map of the entire "attention shape" landscape. When a new shape is discovered in the future, there is no need to invent a new explanation — just plug it into the TAPPA framework and examine Q's temporal structure.

A new pit surfaces immediately: *why* does training cause Q to spontaneously develop this temporal self-similarity? TAPPA does not answer that — it explains the "shape of the phenomenon" but not the "origin of the shape." The starting point of the next paper almost certainly lies right there.

* Frontier Extensions

TAPPA is a new paper at ICLR 2026; as of this writing (2026-05), no papers explicitly critiquing or extending it have appeared publicly. But two directions are foreseeable:

1. *Training-dynamics explanation*: Why does SGD training cause Q to converge to this "predictably self-similar" structure? Is this some form of implicit regularization from the loss function?
2. *Differentiated compression by head type*: Since heads fall into two classes, two compression strategies should be used — aggressively pruning KV for "predictable" heads while preserving full KV (or applying a different approximation, e.g., low-rank or quantization) for "unpredictable" heads. Existing work (DuoAttention splits heads into retrieval / streaming) moves in exactly this direction; TAPPA gives it a much firmer theoretical foundation.

* Insights

What is really happening beneath this evolutionary arc is not "yet another new shape discovered" — it is *a phase transition in the research methodology itself*: from natural history to physics.

For the first decade-plus, attention research resembled 19th-century naturalists: travel the world, discover new species, name them, record their habits. Sink, retrieval, diagonal, massive activation… each is a "species." But natural history has an obvious ceiling: the more species are discovered, the messier the taxonomy grows, until a Darwinian "unified theory" is needed — one that explains why all species look the way they do.

That is exactly what TAPPA attempts. It says: "Stop naming them one by one — all these shapes arise from the same underlying variable: Q's temporal self-similarity." This is the attempt to pack natural history into physics.

The deeper cognitive shift is: *from a spatial perspective to a temporal perspective*. Previously, people looked at the attention matrix as the 2D matrix ~Q·K^T~ — spatial, static. TAPPA treats it as a process evolving along time — the same Q interacting at each moment with all past K's, and its *trajectory* is the true object of study. This shift in perspective resembles moving from studying "the intensity distribution of light" to studying "the phase evolution of light" — the former can only describe; the latter can predict.

The most likely next step is to apply this temporal perspective to *training dynamics* — why does SGD naturally produce a Q with this kind of self-similarity? If that can be answered, the payoff is not just an explanation of "why attention looks this way" but also "why training converges to this structure" — and that would be close to the true first principles of attention.

* Takeaways

Implications for *how to do research*:

1. *Beware the "phenomenon field-guide" trap*: When a field starts furiously naming new phenomena (sink head, retrieval head, induction head, copying head…), that is almost always a symptom of a missing unified theory. If you are doing this naming work, pause and ask — can you find a single variable that turns all these names into different values of the same thing? That "dimensionality reduction" is the crucial leap from natural history to physics.
2. *Changing the coordinate system beats adding new concepts*: TAPPA invented no new mechanisms; it simply re-read attention as a temporal process rather than a spatial matrix. Changing coordinates often solves problems that piling on new concepts cannot — because much apparent complexity is illusory complexity caused by a bad choice of coordinates.
3. *Hunt for unexamined assumptions*: Every KV cache compression method silently assumed that "past attention predicts future attention," yet nobody had ever verified *when* this holds and *when* it fails. That quietly universal, never-articulated assumption is exactly TAPPA's entry point. Every field has these "underground axioms" — dig one up and you have a paper.

Practical implications:

- If you are working on KV cache compression, *do not treat all heads identically* — first use TAPPA's metric to split heads into predictable and unpredictable, prune the former aggressively, and preserve (or use alternative approximations for) the latter. This directly eats into the space that every baseline leaves on the table.
- If you are doing attention visualization or interpretability research, *plot Q's temporal self-similarity along the time axis before plotting the attention matrix* — the former is the cause, the latter the effect. Reading from cause to effect is far more direct than inverting from effect back to cause.
- If you are looking for new angles on LLM inference acceleration, study the closed-form solutions TAPPA derives for the three representative "predictable" scenarios — a closed form means you do not need to compute online; you can precompute "what should be attended to" offline and look it up at inference time. This is an underexploited class of acceleration opportunities.
