* The Problem River

The bottleneck for running large-model inference cheaply has long ceased to be
the model weights — it is the *KV cache*, which grows fatter with every exchange.
Long conversations, long reasoning chains, long contexts: each additional token
adds another row to the cache, and generating every new token requires the GPU
to read the entire cache back from VRAM. Compressing the KV cache is the most
direct path to cutting costs.

But pushing KV down to *2-bit* (only 4 distinct values per number) is hard.
The trouble is that KV tensors contain "spikes" — a handful of channels whose
values are wildly larger than their neighbors, blowing up the entire
quantization ruler so that every normal value gets crammed into the same few
bins, and the model collapses. This river is the story of successive researchers
wrestling with those spikes — first *dodging* them, then *smoothing* them, and
finally *understanding what attention actually cares about*.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2024-02 | KIVI: The First to Compress KV Cache to 2-bit — and the First to See the Spikes

*** What Problem It Saw

Before KIVI, KV cache quantization used a single scale for the entire tensor —
either per-token or per-channel. The discovery was that *a few channels of K
can be tens of times larger than their neighbors*. A single scale means that
ruler gets blown out by those few channels, and the remaining 99% of values are
squeezed into only a handful of quantization bins, destroying the information.

*** Its Solution

Treat K and V differently:
- *K quantized per-channel* — since spikes cluster in particular channels,
  give each channel its own scale; a large spike stays within its own channel
  and does not contaminate its neighbors.
- *V quantized per-token* — V has no such channel-level clustering, so
  per-row handling is simpler and sufficient.

This was the first time anyone articulated clearly that "K and V are not
homogeneous": K's trouble lives in the channel dimension; V's does not.
No training required — applying the scheme directly keeps most accuracy
at 2-bit.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left)

Per-channel quantization prevents spikes from polluting neighbors. But it
carries two latent problems:

1. *The spikes themselves are untouched* — they are merely given their own
   room. Pushing further to 1.x-bit, or performing operations that mix
   channels, becomes awkward.
2. *It conflicts with efficient attention kernels* — per-channel quantization
   disrupts the memory layout that fused kernels like FlashAttention expect.

These two pits became the starting point for QuaRot.

** 2024-04 | QuaRot: Stop Dodging the Spikes — Spread Them Out

*** What Problem It Saw

KIVI's per-channel was a *defensive* strategy, giving spikes their own
isolation cell. But every layer has KV, every head has its own spike pattern,
and engineering complexity compounds. More fundamentally, *even per-channel
cannot save 4-bit or lower* — the ruler for the isolated cell is already too
coarse.

QuaRot's real insight was: spikes look like spikes only *in a particular basis*.
Could one change the basis so they no longer stand out?

*** Its Solution

Introduce a *Hadamard rotation* — an orthogonal matrix made of +1 and −1.
Multiplying K by this matrix is equivalent to "scattering" each K vector
across all channels. A value of 100 on a single channel becomes
100/sqrt(128) spread across all 128 channels. *The spike is smeared away
uniformly.*

The key property: the rotation is orthogonal, so it can be "multiplied back"
before the attention computation, leaving the math completely equivalent.
At inference time the only overhead is one matrix multiply; *model behavior
is unchanged*.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left)

The statistical properties of Hadamard matrices guarantee that no matter how
skewed the original distribution, after the transform it approaches Gaussian,
and quantization error is spread evenly. Whole-model 4-bit inference
(weights + activations + KV) ran successfully for the first time.

But a fundamental question remained: *Hadamard is data-oblivious* — it
ignores what K looks like, ignores which model you are running, and just
applies a fixed ±1 pattern. It guarantees "uniform," but not "uniformly
in the right directions." If some directions are never read by attention,
spreading error there is wasteful; if some directions are read heavily,
those should carry higher precision.

** 2024-05 | SpinQuant: If the Rotation Matters, Learn It

*** What Problem It Saw

QuaRot settled the question of "rotation or no rotation." SpinQuant asked
"which rotation is best?" Hadamard is a clever initialization, but applying
the same ±1 pattern to every model, every layer, every head is *obviously
suboptimal*.

*** Its Solution

Treat the rotation matrix as a *learnable parameter* — optimize it on the
Stiefel manifold (the set of orthogonal matrices) using a small calibration
dataset, with the objective of making quantized outputs as close as possible
to the original. The learned rotation beats Hadamard by several points on
average.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left)

A learned rotation adapts to the actual distribution of each layer and
naturally outperforms a fixed matrix. The cost, however, is:

1. *Training overhead* — Stiefel manifold optimization is not cheap; every
   model requires a fresh run.
2. *The objective is "reconstruct KV"* — the loss measures how far quant(K)
   is from K. But is that really what attention cares about?

SpinQuant was mainly applied to 4-bit weight quantization. Porting it
directly to 2-bit KV causes it to break down.

** 2025-01 | RotateKV: Bringing Rotation Back to the 2-bit KV Front

*** What Problem It Saw

SpinQuant's optimization pipeline was too heavy, and 4-bit KV was already
adequate for most uses, so the rotation line drifted away from the 2-bit
battlefield for a while. But the hard nut of 2-bit KV remained —
*KIVI's per-channel begins to shake at INT2, and Hadamard at INT2 KV
simply collapses*.

RotateKV returned to 2-bit KV: at this extreme compression, Hadamard's
"mindless spreading" is insufficient, because INT2 has only 4 bins and
any waste is fatal.

*** Its Solution

*Outlier-aware adaptive rotations* — based on the per-layer distribution of
outliers, locally adjust the Hadamard rotation. Rather than learning a single
matrix for the whole model, it *fine-tunes per layer and per head*, applying
more pressure in the directions where spikes concentrate.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left)

Accuracy at 2-bit KV improves noticeably over QuaRot. But the underlying
worldview is still that of KIVI and QuaRot: *the goal remains smoothing the
distribution of KV itself*. RotateKV pushes "eliminating spikes" to the limit
without asking: *after smoothing, is attention actually more accurate?*

That is precisely OSCAR's entry point.

** 2026-05 | OSCAR: Never Mind What KV Looks Like — Look at What Attention Is Reading

*** What Problem It Saw

OSCAR's table-thumping insight:

#+begin_quote
"attention does not consume K and V through their Euclidean reconstruction errors"
#+end_quote

Attention never directly consumes K and V. It consumes ~Q · K^T~
(attention scores) and ~S · V~ (the weighted-sum output). Every preceding
method minimized ~||K - quant(K)||~, but that error and the attention output
error are *two different things*.

Specifically, the downstream error caused by K is
~tr((K - K_hat) · Q^T·Q · (K - K_hat)^T)~.
*The key factor is Q^T·Q* — not K^T·K. This means: wherever Q repeatedly
reads, K must be precise in those directions; wherever Q never reads, K can
afford to be coarser there. The error on V is analogously weighted by
~S^T·S~.

*** Its Solution

Encode this insight directly into the rotation matrices:

1. *Rotation for Key*: collect a small calibration batch, compute Q's
   covariance ~C_Q = (1/N) Σ q^T·q~, perform the eigen-decomposition
   ~C_Q = U_Q · Λ · U_Q^T~, and use *U_Q as the rotation for K*.
   The rotated coordinate axes then align with the directions Q cares about.

2. *Rotation for Value*: compute ~C_S = (1/N) V^T·S^T·S·V~,
   eigen-decompose to obtain ~U_S~, and use it as the rotation for V.

3. Finally stack a Hadamard and a bit-reversal permutation on top to
   further uniformize: ~R_K = U_Q · H · P_br~, ~R_V = U_S · H · P_br~.

The paper proves theoretically that, under the assumption of diagonal
residual covariance, this choice is *optimal* (minimizes the lower bound on
attention output error).

And crucially — all of this is computed *offline*. At inference time, KV
simply incurs one extra multiply by a fixed matrix before quantization.
The method is fully compatible with paged attention, SGLang, and
FlashAttention, and is genuinely deployable.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left)

The results are striking: on Qwen3-4B-Thinking, Qwen3-8B, Qwen3-32B, and
GLM-4.7 (358B), INT2 KV loses almost nothing (gaps of 1–4 points), while
naïve rotated INT2 collapses to near zero. Long-context RULER-NIAH at 128K
also holds steady. On the system side, *KV memory is reduced by 8×, and
throughput improves by up to 7×*.

Open questions left behind:

1. *Calibration data dependence* — computing ~C_Q~ and ~C_S~ requires a
   representative batch; query patterns that lie outside that distribution
   may degrade the rotation.
2. *New architectures such as MoE / MLA* — OSCAR assumes standard MHA / GQA;
   adapting to MLA and other novel attention designs remains to be done.
3. *1-bit or lower?* — 2-bit is close to the ceiling of scalar quantization;
   going further may require vector quantization, low-rank decomposition,
   or abandoning quantization altogether.

* Frontier Extensions

While OSCAR was appearing, the river kept branching:

- *HaPPI* (Kim 2025b): Very close in spirit to OSCAR — Hadamard PCA via power
  iteration, also a "covariance-driven rotation." Best understood as an
  independent arrival at the same insight during the same period.
- *KVLinC* (Saxena 2025): Hadamard rotation plus linear error correction.
  An alternative path: after rotation, acknowledge that residual error remains
  and add a lightweight correction layer to recover it.
- *Kitty* (Xia 2025): A sister work from the same team (Together AI).
  *Dynamic per-channel precision boosting* — temporarily raising precision
  for important channels. Complementary to OSCAR: Kitty cuts along the
  precision dimension, OSCAR cuts along the rotation dimension.
- *TurboQuant* (Zandieh 2025): Online vector quantization, stepping outside
  the scalar quantization framework and pushing in a deeper direction.

* Insight

The deepest punch from reading this line is: *for two full years, everyone
was optimizing the wrong objective function*.

KIVI, QuaRot, SpinQuant, RotateKV — every method's loss looked the same:
"make the quantized KV close to the original KV." This sounds obvious —
quantization means reconstruction, right?

But OSCAR points out: the model never sees KV directly; it sees the
*attention output*. KV reconstruction error ≠ attention output error. At
4-bit, the slack is wide enough that the two are highly correlated and nobody
noticed the gap. At 2-bit, with only 4 quantization bins and an extremely
tight error budget, the cost of "targeting the wrong objective" becomes visible.

This is a story about *task alignment*. It is not a technical leap —
nothing between KIVI and OSCAR required new mathematics; eigen-decomposition,
Hadamard transforms, and PCA are all decades-old tools. The shift was a
*change in perspective*: from "quantize KV" to "quantize the directions that
attention actually consumes."

Where does the river go next? Three branches:

1. *Finer task alignment* — not just attention output, but logits, loss, and
   downstream task metrics. Can the rotation be derived directly by
   back-propagating through the task loss?
2. *Dynamic rather than static* — OSCAR's rotation is fixed offline. If
   different prompts induce different ~C_Q~ distributions, is an *online
   adaptive* rotation needed? (TurboQuant is already exploring this.)
3. *Beyond scalar quantization* — 2-bit is close to the ceiling of scalar
   quantization; going lower requires vector quantization, product
   quantization, or abandoning quantization in favor of low-rank methods.

* Takeaway

What this river teaches about *how to find problems*:

*Watch the loss, not the metric*. Everyone reports "quantization error
reduced by 30%," but that number does not determine how well the model
actually works. OSCAR's entire story begins from a single line of algebra:
~||QK - Q·K_hat||^2 = tr((K-K_hat)·Q^T·Q·(K-K_hat)^T)~. Once you see
that ~Q^T·Q~ sitting there, you cannot unsee it — it is telling you plainly
that "K's error should be weighted by Q's covariance." Yet for two years
nobody wrote it down, because everyone was blocked by the inertia of
"quantization means reconstruction."

The implication: *in any field where a proxy objective has become standard,
you can expand the downstream loss and look at who is weighting whom inside
it*. The gap between the proxy objective and the true objective is,
more often than not, a paper.
