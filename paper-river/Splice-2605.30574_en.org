* The River of Questions

You throw a long prompt at a large language model. It reads the whole thing, then starts generating an answer one token at a time. Throughout this process, it holds your prompt in a cache called the KV cache — its working memory. Here is the question: as it generates, does it keep drawing on every part of that memory from start to finish? Or does it reach a point where most of what is stored there could simply be discarded?

The question is hard because two distinct threads are tangled together, and for a long time no one managed to separate them.

The first thread is "how much can be discarded." Larger caches consume more GPU memory and slow things down; everyone wants to trim them. Generation after generation of methods demonstrated: yes, you can discard — often well over half — with almost no drop in accuracy. But these methods all followed the pattern of "discard and see if things still hold" — they knew it worked, but not why.

The second thread is "what exactly is being discarded." Is the discarded portion genuinely irrelevant content, or some invisible structural scaffold that quietly holds everything together? This line was being pursued by a different community — mechanistic interpretability researchers — who found that the examples and task instructions you provide get "chewed up and compressed" into just a few vectors by the middle layers; by the upper layers, verbatim token-by-token memory of the original prompt appears to be unnecessary.

These two currents flowed in parallel without merging. Until this paper — call it Splice — brought them together and asked a precise question that everyone before had sidestepped: *At exactly which layer, after how many generated tokens, and when replaced with what, does this cached memory truly become dispensable?* The answer upends intuition: what the upper layers actually care about is not "what you asked" but "what shape the conversation should have." Content can be swapped for neutral filler and the model still answers correctly — but zero out those same slots and the model collapses immediately.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2023 | StreamingLLM: Some Tokens Matter for Structure, Not Meaning

*** What Problem It Saw

The most natural way to handle an infinitely long conversation stream is a sliding window: cache only the most recent segment and evict the rest. This sounds reasonable — humans, after all, only remember the last few exchanges. But in practice it explodes. The moment the window slides past the very first tokens of the sequence, perplexity spikes from around 5 to over 5000 and the model babbles incoherently. Strangely, those first tokens are often meaningless start-of-sequence markers. Why should evicting them cause a catastrophe?

*** Its Solution

Xiao et al. discovered "attention sinks": because softmax must distribute attention weights that sum to 1, and because the model sometimes has nothing it actually wants to attend to, it learns to dump this surplus attention onto whichever tokens are always visible — namely the very first ones in the sequence. Those tokens become a pressure-relief valve. The fix is almost absurdly simple: no matter how semantically empty they are, always pin the first 4 tokens in the cache as sinks and slide the rest normally. Perplexity immediately recovers.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Left Behind)

This paper was the first to crack open a crucial insight: *whether a token deserves its slot in the cache may have nothing to do with its semantic content — it can be purely about occupying a structural pressure-relief position*. This "structure vs. content" distinction would become the central theme of Splice three years later. But StreamingLLM itself treated this observation as a minor implementation detail — it fixed only those 4 positions and classified everything else in between as slideable waste. Yet the middle of a document can easily contain the most critical sentences. The natural follow-up: besides the opening sinks, how do you identify and retain the genuinely important tokens scattered throughout the middle?

** 2023 | H2O: Keep Whoever Gets Attended to Most

*** What Problem It Saw

StreamingLLM keeps only the start plus the recent window, effectively assuming "everything in the middle is unimportant." But if a question asks about a specific detail buried in the center of a long document, the critical sentence is right there in the middle. Keeping only the head and the tail guarantees failure on such queries.

*** Its Solution

Zhang et al. observed that at inference time, attention matrices are more than 95% sparse, with a small number of tokens absorbing nearly all the attention weight — they called these "heavy hitters." The trimming rule became: given a fixed budget, reserve half for the most recent tokens and half for the heavy hitters ranked by cumulative attention score. Whoever has been attended to most across the entire generation so far gets to stay.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Left Behind)

This upgraded "who to keep" from "look at position" to "look at attention magnitude," and for the first time could rescue important tokens from the middle of a document. Scoring by attention magnitude became the main melody of all of Stream A from this point on. But the "cumulative score" signal has a soft underbelly: it is a running sum from the very beginning, which means a token that was heavily attended to early on — long since exhausted its relevance — can coast on accumulated credit and permanently occupy cache space on historical inertia alone. Importance expires; cumulative scores do not acknowledge that.

** 2023 | Scissorhands: Importance Expires — Look Only at Recent Popularity

*** What Problem It Saw

H2O scores tokens on lifetime cumulative attention. Scissorhands saw straight through the flaw: scores become monopolized by tokens that were once popular. A token furiously attended to in the early steps of generation accumulates an enormous balance; even if no step in the subsequent three hundred tokens glances at it, it holds its cache slot on seniority alone, crowding out tokens that are genuinely useful right now.

*** Its Solution

It proposed the "persistence of importance" hypothesis: a token that is still being attended to above average within a recent history window is likely still useful in the near future. Scoring therefore ignores lifetime totals and looks only at whether the token remains popular within the recent window — those that fall below average become eviction candidates. This replaces H2O's "lifetime tenure" system with a "recent performance" system.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Left Behind)

This was an important self-correction within Stream A: importance has a shelf life and should be estimated with a sliding recent signal rather than accumulated credit. But Scissorhands shares a deeper blind spot with H2O and StreamingLLM — all three make their decisions *during generation*, using the current handful of tokens as a probe to guess what should be kept. But what makes the probe valid at the very start of generation for predicting what the model will need much later? This "shortsighted probe" problem drove the next step.

** 2024 | SnapKV: Stop Guessing Mid-Answer — Trim Everything Before Generation Starts

*** What Problem It Saw

H2O and Scissorhands both trim during decoding: they watch attention scores at each generation step and decide on the fly what to evict. This has two hard flaws. First, using only the most recent few tokens as queries gives a local, myopic view — it cannot tell whether a token will matter to content the model has not yet generated. Second, due to causal masking, tokens closer to the current position receive systematically inflated scores, causing earlier but actually critical tokens to be misjudged and mistakenly evicted. On top of this, re-evaluating at every step bleeds time continuously.

*** Its Solution

Li et al.'s key observation: each attention head's pattern of which prompt positions it focuses on is largely determined from the very start of generation — and can be predicted in advance using the prompt's own *tail* segment (the last 16–32 tokens, typically the question or instruction itself) as an "observation window." So they moved trimming from "trim during decoding" to "trim once, immediately after prefill completes": let the queries in the observation window vote on which positions across the entire prompt to keep for each head, fix the selection, and never revisit it.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Left Behind)

Shifting the decision point from mid-generation to pre-generation sidesteps the shortsighted-probe bias and eliminates the per-step trimming overhead. But SnapKV (and everything before it) shared a common assumption nobody had examined head-on: *all layers are treated equally*, with the same budget and the same logic applied uniformly. Should all layers really be treated the same?

** 2024 | PyramidKV: Layers Are Fundamentally Different — Lower Layers Get More, Upper Layers Less

*** What Problem It Saw

SnapKV gives every layer the same cache budget. Cai et al. actually looked at the attention patterns across layers and found striking variation: lower layers show broad, "spread-out" attention with many tokens receiving comparable weight; upper layers show highly concentrated attention, with just a few tokens absorbing nearly everything. Equal budgets across layers mean the upper layers retain a heap of useless tokens while the lower layers are starved.

*** Its Solution

A "pyramid information funnel": cache budgets decrease with depth — lower layers get more, upper layers get less, forming a pyramid overall. Within each layer, SnapKV's observation-window voting still selects which tokens to keep.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Left Behind)

PyramidKV's real contribution was making *layer* an explicit, independently tunable axis for the first time — it told the field that any claim about "how much can be discarded" must first specify "at which layer." This step pushed Stream A directly to Splice's doorstep. But PyramidKV still operated on "use attention magnitude to decide how many tokens each layer keeps." It did not ask the sharper question: what are the upper-layer tokens that survive trimming actually *storing* — content, or something else? To answer that, one had to look at Stream B, where a different community had been chasing "what form does context actually take inside the model."

** 2022–2025 | Stream B's Undercurrent: Content Compressed into Vectors, Upper Layers Left with Only the Skeleton

*** What Problem It Saw

Stream A kept demonstrating "you can discard most of it" but could never explain *why*. Answering that required a different community — mechanistic interpretability researchers — asking a different question: where does your context actually live inside the model, and what does it look like?

*** Their Solutions (Four Findings, One Logic)

First, Olsson et al. (2022) dissected induction heads: the ability to "copy" (see [A][B]…[A], predict [B]) is implemented by two heads cooperating across layers — one head in an earlier layer writes "what preceded A" into the residual stream; a head in a later layer reads that out to do pattern matching. The point: *this capability is distributed across a layered collaboration, not stored in any single token*.

Then Hendel (2023) and Todd (2024) found something even more striking: a sequence of in-context examples gets "chewed" by the *middle layers* into a single vector — a task vector or function vector. Injecting just that one vector into a forward pass that has seen only the question and no examples at all is enough for the model to perform the task correctly. In other words, *by the middle-to-upper layers, the verbatim example tokens have been squeezed dry and distilled into a compressed representation; the original token caches are redundant in the upper layers*.

Finally, Queipo-de-Llano et al. (2025) provided geometric confirmation: "compression valleys" — middle-layer representations collapse to near rank-1, with nearly all variation squeezed into one or two directions, redundancy visible to linear algebra itself; past the middle layers, the upper layers enter a "refinement/scaffolding" phase.

*** Why This Worked (and What New Problem It Left Behind)

Stream B supplied the missing "why" for Stream A: the reason upper-layer caches can be aggressively discarded is that content was already compressed and absorbed into the residual stream at the middle layers — what the upper layers hold is more like a skeleton than flesh. For the first time, the logic of the two streams connected. But Stream B was entirely observational — it said "content was compressed away, upper layers are redundant," yet nobody had taken a scalpel to *verify* it: if the upper layers truly hold only a skeleton, then replacing the upper-layer token cache with something else should leave the model intact; replacing it with different things should produce different effects. Who would hand over that scalpel first?

** 2025 | Ganesh et al.: The First Replacement Scalpel (But It's an Attack, and All-Layers-at-Once)

*** What Problem It Saw

Prior work was either "observation" (Stream B) or "trim and discard" (Stream A) — nobody had systematically performed *replacement*: swap this KV for that KV, and observe what happens. Ganesh et al. were the first to act, but their motivation was a security attack: can hijacking the cache steer the model's outputs?

*** Its Solution

Overwrite the KVs at the target sequence's tail positions — *across all layers simultaneously* — with a fixed "donor cache," thereby hijacking subsequent generation to produce whatever the attacker wanted. This demonstrated that "replacing KVs" is both mechanically feasible and remarkably powerful.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Left Behind)

It established *replacement* — as opposed to mere discard or observation — as a valid method for probing the KV cache, and this is exactly the methodology Splice would adopt. But Ganesh's instrument was too coarse: all layers at once (no layer distinction), a single donor cache (no comparison of what you replace with), and the only question asked was "does the attack succeed" — not "what does the cache encode." The scalpel existed, but only one cut had been made, with no controlled comparison.

** 2026 | Splice: Layer + Step + Replacement Content — All Asked at Once

*** What Problem It Saw

Looking back across the entire river, Splice's diagnosis is that everyone had been speaking in "narrow bore." StreamingLLM focused on 4 sink tokens. Stream B focused on a single task vector. Ganesh applied a single donor across all layers at once. Stream A's methods each championed one specific trimming policy. Not a single paper had systematically turned all these knobs together to answer the question that most needed asking: *At which layer, after how many generated tokens, and with what replacement content, does the prompt's KV cache truly become dispensable?*

*** Its Solution

The "splice intervention": a controlled experiment with three knobs — layer cutpoint L (which layers to intervene on), splice onset W (how many generation steps to wait before intervening), and the type of donor cache — swept across a grid. The prompt's KV is replaced in-place with one of five donor types: zeros, blank filler (neutral boilerplate that preserves the dialogue-template shape while removing all content), same-task, different-task, or different-model-family. Accuracy is measured at each point on the grid.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Left Behind)

The elegance of this design is that it can disentangle "form" from "content." The results are decisive:

*Upper layers — they need form, not content.* Replacing the upper-layer prompt cache with "blank filler" (which preserves the conversational scaffold while stripping all substantive content) causes almost no accuracy drop. But zeroing out those same slots causes immediate collapse. This means what actually sustains the model in the upper layers is the structural skeleton of "what shape this conversation should have," not "what you specifically asked." What Stream A has been discarding all along in the upper layers is content; as long as the scaffold remains, the model believes the conversation is normal.

*Lower layers — content is doing real causal work.* In the lower layers, the farther the donor is from the target task, the greater the performance cost — meaning the lower-layer cache contains task content that is causally active and cannot be arbitrarily substituted.

These findings hold across three model families (Qwen3, Gemma 3, Llama 3). Splice opens an entirely new, orthogonal direction for the compression methods of Stream A: rather than carefully *selecting which tokens to retain* in the upper layers by attention magnitude, simply *replace the entire upper-layer cache with a structural placeholder* — because all the model needs up there is the shape. The remaining open questions are clear: what shape of blank filler is most efficient, how should the optimal L and W adapt to different tasks on-the-fly, and can these findings be turned into a concrete deployed compression or acceleration method — all unanswered.

* Frontier Extensions

As of the time of writing (June 2026), Splice is the most recent paper on the question of "which parts of the prompt KV cache are truly dispensable." Its position is distinctive: it is not yet another compression algorithm, but the paper that formally connects Stream A (pragmatists asking "how much can be discarded") to Stream B (mechanists asking "what is being discarded"), and for the first time uses controlled replacement experiments to elevate "upper layers are dispensable" from a long-standing empirical observation to a falsifiable mechanistic conclusion: what is dispensable is content; what is not dispensable is form.

Directions worth watching (not directly covered in the paper — speculative):
One: reverse-engineering the "upper layers need only structural placeholders" finding into a real inference acceleration method — upper-layer caches store no content at all, just a single shared dialogue-template scaffold.
Two: can the "form vs. content" ruler be extended to other cache operations (quantization, low-rank compression), diagnosing whether those operations damage form or content?
Three: can the optimal values of knobs L and W be determined online adaptively, rather than by offline grid search?

* Insights

On the surface, this river is "an iterative refinement of KV cache compression techniques." At its core, it is a *progressive sharpening of the question itself* — from "can it be discarded at all," to "how much," to "at which layer," and finally in Splice, to "is it content or form that is being discarded." Each step of sharpening makes the conclusions harder and more actionable.

At a deeper level, it exposes something that had long been conflated. Whenever Stream A's compression methods worked well, the community attributed success to "the discarded content wasn't important." Splice points out that this attribution is wrong, or at least hopelessly blurry: the reason upper layers can be discarded is *not that the content is unimportant — it is that the upper layers do not rely on content at all; they rely on structural scaffolding*. The evidence is the clean controlled comparison: strip the content (blank filler) and nothing breaks; strip the scaffold (zeros) and everything dies. This is a counterintuitive but rock-solid cognitive shift: in the latter half of generation, the model's dependence on the prompt degrades from "remember what you said" to "remember what shape of conversation we are having." The content was digested into vectors by the middle layers long ago; what the upper layers are guarding is only a scaffold.

This fits seamlessly with Stream B's findings: task vectors say content was compressed into a single vector; compression valleys say middle layers collapse to rank-1; induction heads say the capability is a distributed structure, not content stored in any particular token — all different facets of the same underlying fact. Splice then takes a controlled replacement scalpel and elevates this fact from "observed correlation" to "interventionally established causation."

The most probable next step: since the upper layers need only form, perhaps the ultimate form of "compression" is not "choose which tokens to retain" at all, but "store no content whatsoever in upper-layer caches — share a single global dialogue-template scaffold instead" — collapsing the token-level selection that Stream A has been painstakingly optimizing into a single structural-placeholder substitution.

* Lessons for Research Practice

This river offers three concrete lessons about how to do research:

1. *Take a vague "it works," decompose it into "where, when, and with what" knobs, and you often find a paper.* Stream A proved "you can trim caches" across five or six papers, all stalled at the level of "it works." Splice invented no new algorithm — it simply decomposed the same action into three axes (layer L, step W, donor type) and swept them systematically, immediately surfacing questions nobody had asked and conclusions nobody had reached. The implication: whenever you encounter a phenomenon that is "known to work but unexplained," look for the implicit knobs that have never been turned independently.

2. *"Content" and "form" are worth treating as two separate variables to zero out independently.* The entire river had vaguely attributed "upper layers are dispensable" to "content redundancy." Splice's key design was to construct two distinct donor types — "keep form, strip content" and "strip form too" — so these two variables could move separately. The moment they were separated, the truth reversed. The implication: when a system keeps functioning with "something" removed, don't rush to conclude "that something was useless" — first clarify whether you removed its content or its structure.

3. *Someone else's "attack tool" is your "measuring instrument" under a different framing.* Ganesh used KV replacement as a means to hijack the model — an attack. Splice used the identical operation as a probe to reveal what the cache encodes — an instrument. Same scalpel; the attacker stabs with it, the researcher dissects with it. The implication: when you encounter a "destructive" operation, ask the inverse — if I apply it carefully, with controls and comparisons, what can it measure?
