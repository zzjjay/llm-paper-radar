* The River of the Problem

You've trained a 7B language model with fp16 weights. Every inference, the GPU must ferry those weights from memory to compute — *the transfer* is far slower than *the arithmetic*. This is the memory-bound bottleneck.

The most direct way to save cost: compress each weight from 16 bits down to 4, 3, or even 2 bits. A model four to eight times smaller moves four to eight times faster.

Unfortunately, LLM weights are not uniformly distributed. The vast majority cluster near zero, but *a handful* are abnormally large — tens of times bigger than the rest. These outliers stretch the dynamic range enormously. At 2 bits you only have 4 steps, and they must be evenly spaced: the result is that 99% of all weights collapse into a single bin, and resolution effectively vanishes.

For four solid years, everyone has been circling the same question:

#+begin_quote
How do you compress to 2–4 bits without being destroyed by a few outliers?
#+end_quote

This river started flowing in 1989 with LeCun's Optimal Brain Damage — people were using second-order curvature to compress neural networks, and nobody cared about LLMs. Thirty-plus years later, that same Hessian matrix resurfaces in OSAQ (2026) — this time through its *null space*.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

What follows starts from the oldest paper. The focus is not "what did it do" but "what did it see that its predecessors missed."

** 1989 | OBD: Using Second Derivatives to Decide What to Prune

*** What Problem Did It See

Neural networks were tiny back then, but people were already thinking about compression — pruning away unimportant weights to make models smaller. The prevailing approach was magnitude pruning: remove the weights with the smallest absolute values first.

LeCun's insight was simple: *small weight ≠ unimportant*. A weight of 0.001 sitting on a very steep loss surface will cause the loss to spike if removed; a weight of 0.5 sitting in a flat region can be removed with almost no effect on the loss. Pruning by absolute value alone is pruning blind.

*** Its Solution

To know the true impact of removing a weight, you need to understand the *shape* of the loss surface at that point — which is exactly what the second derivative (Hessian) captures.

OBD assumes the Hessian is diagonal (a simplification) and computes a "saliency" score for each weight:

s_i = (1/2) * H_ii * w_i^2

The weights with the lowest saliency are pruned. The formula says: a weight's true importance is its magnitude multiplied by the local curvature.

*** Why It Worked (and What Hole It Left)

This was the first time *loss geometry* was brought into neural-network compression. Before OBD everyone relied on intuition; OBD provided a theoretically grounded criterion.

But assuming the Hessian is diagonal is equivalent to saying "each weight affects the loss independently." In reality the weights are coupled: pruning w_1 means w_2 ... w_n should be adjusted to compensate. OBD ignores this entirely.

** 1993 | OBS: Adjust the Others When You Prune One

*** What Problem Did It See

The diagonal assumption in OBD is too weak. Hassibi pointed out that the off-diagonal entries of the full Hessian are precisely what encodes the coupling between weights — discarding them is discarding information.

*** Its Solution

Use the *full* Hessian inverse H^{-1}. When weight q is pruned:
- Compute the optimal compensation: Δw = -(w_q / [H^{-1}]_{qq}) * H^{-1}_{:,q}
- Add this Δw to all remaining weights so the loss increases as little as possible

The intuition: pruning one weight is like pushing the loss surface in one direction; OBS nudges all other weights back along the "least-effort" direction given by the Hessian, keeping the loss nearly unchanged.

*** Why It Worked (and What Hole It Left)

OBS upgraded OBD's "independent estimate + single cut" to "coupled estimate + coordinated adjustment." Theoretically sounder, and in practice *much more accurate*.

New hole: computing an N × N Hessian inverse costs O(N^3). Once neural networks grow large, with N in the millions, this is simply intractable. Throughout the 1990s and 2010s OBS was regarded as "theoretically correct but practically impossible."

** 2022 | OBQ: Bringing OBS to Quantization

*** What Problem Did It See

In 2022 Frantar and Alistarh observed: the OBS framework is not limited to pruning. Pruning = setting a weight to zero; quantization = rounding a weight to the nearest grid point — both are instances of "perturb one weight, compensate the others."

So the OBS equations transfer to quantization without modification.

*** Its Solution

OBQ: pick one weight, quantize it (rather than zero it out), then use the Hessian to update all remaining unquantized weights to compensate for the error. Repeat until every weight is processed.

The formula is almost identical to OBS; the only change is replacing "Δw = -w_q / ..." with "Δw = (quant(w_q) - w_q) / ...".

*** Why It Worked (and What Hole It Left)

OBQ was the first time *compensation* was introduced into post-training quantization (PTQ), yielding far higher accuracy than naive rounding. It works well on small models.

New hole: OBS's old problem — it is slow. For a large LLM such as OPT-175B, a single layer contains millions of weights, and running OBQ on one layer takes hours. No one has the patience to process hundreds of layers.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Making OBQ Feasible on LLMs

*** What Problem Did It See

The bottleneck in OBQ is "updating the remaining Hessian submatrix every time one weight is quantized." The Frantar team realized: at LLM scale, carefully choosing the *order* in which to quantize weights yields little benefit — but enormous cost.

*** Its Solution

GPTQ does three things:
1. *Fixed order*: abandon the greedy "quantize lowest-error first" strategy; process columns strictly left to right
2. *Block processing*: handle one column (or even a group of columns) at a time, not one scalar weight
3. *Cholesky decomposition*: replace direct matrix inversion with the Cholesky factor of H for numerical stability and reuse

Result: per-layer quantization time drops from hours to minutes, *with almost no accuracy loss*. The full 4-bit quantization of OPT-175B is done in 4 hours.

*** Why It Worked (and What Hole It Left)

GPTQ was the first to bring the 30-year-old OBS framework into the LLM era. Nearly all subsequent LLM PTQ work uses GPTQ as its foundation.

New hole: GPTQ compensates for outliers *during* quantization — it accepts the outlier distribution as a given, then tries to spread the resulting error as evenly as possible. It never asks: could we *eliminate* the outliers *before* quantizing?

** 2023 | AWQ / QuIP: Smoothing Weights Before Quantization

*** What Problem Did It See

GPTQ treats the shape of the weight distribution as fixed. But weight outliers are related to the activation distribution — channels with large activations are trained to produce weights of correspondingly large magnitude.

What if you applied an *equivalent transform* before quantization to move the outlier energy somewhere harmless?

*** Its Solution (Two Legs)

The key observation: between adjacent layers W_1 and W_2 you can insert a transform T and its inverse T^{-1}:

#+begin_example
   (X * W_1) * W_2
=  X * (W_1 * T^{-1}) * (T * W_2)
=  X * W_1' * W_2'
#+end_example

The output is identical (mathematically equivalent), but W_1' and W_2' have a different shape — you choose T to make them easier to quantize.

- *AWQ*: T is a diagonal matrix (per-channel scaling). Choose s so that channels with large activations have their corresponding weights scaled down, suppressing outliers.
- *QuIP*: T is an orthogonal matrix (rotation). Rotating the weights in high-dimensional space reshapes the distribution toward Gaussian — and Gaussian distributions quantize far better than outlier-heavy ones.

Both are *multiplicative transforms*: they exploit a cancellation between adjacent layers via multiplication.

*** Why It Worked (and What Hole It Left)

AWQ/QuIP moved the problem of "outliers" from "headache during quantization" to "eliminated before quantization." At 4-bit and 3-bit they deliver meaningful gains over bare GPTQ.

New hole (critical — the starting point for OSAQ):
1. These transforms *forcibly couple adjacent layers* — changing W_1 requires changing W_2 in lockstep. The system grows more complex.
2. The multiplicative transform's degrees of freedom are tightly locked by the equivalence constraint — T must be invertible and must be absorbable into the next layer. There is a hard ceiling on what can be achieved.
3. At 2 bits, AWQ/QuIP still fail. Perplexity still explodes.

Everyone had implicitly assumed: suppressing outliers = multiplicative transforms between adjacent layers. That road had reached its end.

** 2026 | OSAQ: Taking the Additive Road No One Has Walked

*** What Problem Did It See

The OSAQ team asked a question nobody had asked:

#+begin_quote
Why must we use *multiplicative* transforms between *adjacent layers*? Can we act on *this layer alone*?
#+end_quote

Intuitively this seems impossible — change W and the output X*W changes, so the loss changes.

But they made an observation:

Take the second-order Taylor expansion of the loss with respect to a weight perturbation:

E[L(w + Δw) - L(w)] ≈ (1/2) * Δw^T * H * Δw

If Δw lies in the *null space* of H (i.e., H * Δw = 0), this second-order term is zero — the loss is unchanged to second order.

The more critical observation: *the Hessian null space is stable across different inputs*. Figure 1 of the paper shows that for a layer of LLaMA2-7B, the smallest batch of Hessian eigenvalues accounts for only 0.01% of total energy; moreover, the null-space directions computed from different calibration samples are nearly identical.

This is a free lunch.

*** Its Solution

The OSAQ workflow (Figure 3):

1. *Compute the Hessian*: for each layer compute H = E[2X X^T] (the same approximation used in GPTQ)
2. *Extract the null space*: eigendecompose H and take the eigenvectors corresponding to the smallest eigenvalues to form matrix N (K × N, where K is the null-space dimension)
3. *Construct ΔW = β * N*: β is a coefficient matrix (M × K) that linearly combines the null-space vectors
4. *Solve for β*: minimize ||W + βN||_∞ — that is, minimize the dynamic range of the weights, thereby suppressing outliers

Step 4 is nominally an ℓ_∞ optimization problem, which is hard. They approximate it using *Softmax-∞* (log-sum-exp smoothing) and arrive at a *closed-form solution* for β — a single matrix operation, no training, no iteration.

Once ΔW is obtained, it is *absorbed directly into W*: W' = W + ΔW. Then W' is fed into GPTQ for final quantization.

*** Why It Worked (and What Hole It Left)

Three key properties must hold simultaneously for the scheme to work:
1. *The null space exists* (Figure 1 of the paper confirms strong low-rank structure in the Hessian)
2. *The null space is stable* (directions are consistent across calibration samples — a data-agnostic geometric property)
3. *A β can be found that suppresses outliers* (in experiments, ΔW brings the weight maximum from ~2 down to ~0.5)

Effect: at 2-bit quantization, OSAQ + GPTQ reduces perplexity by 40%+ compared with vanilla GPTQ.

What is most elegant:
- *No other layers need to change* ("self-absorption" is precisely this)
- *Zero inference overhead* (ΔW is absorbed offline; at runtime the model is identical in structure)
- *Orthogonal to AWQ/QuIP* (multiplicative and additive transforms can be stacked)

Holes that OSAQ itself leaves:
- The null-space assumption relies on low-rank structure in the Hessian. If some layers (or tasks) have a Hessian that is not sufficiently low-rank, the scheme breaks down.
- β is obtained via a Softmax-∞ approximation, not true ℓ_∞ optimality — room for improvement remains.
- The closed-form solution is fast but gives up the possibility of optimizing against downstream loss. Future work may introduce lightweight iteration.

* Frontier Extensions

OSAQ was just published at ICML 2026 (v1: 2026-05-06, v2: 2026-05-09). As of today (2026-05-28), no follow-up work explicitly targeting OSAQ has appeared on arXiv.

But several extension directions are already visible:

1. *Additive + multiplicative combination*: OSAQ already positions itself as complementary to AWQ/QuIP. The natural next step is stacking scaling + rotation + additive absorption together.
2. *Further exploitation of null-space stability*: if the Hessian null space is truly a data-agnostic geometric property, can it be used for pruning, LoRA adaptation, or model merging? OSAQ only mines the quantization seam.
3. *Additive transforms on the activation side*: OSAQ only applies additive transforms to weights. Weight-activation quantization faces the same outlier problem — one can equally ask: where is the null space of the activation Hessian?

* Insights

Reading this entire lineage, what is genuinely changing is *how people think about the Hessian*.

**The Hessian was always there — each era could only afford to use a small piece of it.**

- 1989: compute power only allowed the diagonal (OBD)
- 1993: theory proposed the full inverse, but only small networks were tractable (OBS)
- 2022: Cholesky and block processing squeezed it into LLMs (GPTQ)
- 2026: people start using the Hessian's *null space* — a part everyone had previously looked through (OSAQ)

Thirty-seven years, each generation extracting something new from the same matrix. The Hessian is a mine: people dug from the rich ore (diagonal pivots) down to the lean ore (smallest eigenvalues), only to discover that *the lean ore is the treasure* — the null space is precisely the free lunch of "add anything here without affecting the loss."

A second, deeper shift: *the paradigm for suppressing outliers*.

- GPTQ school: outliers are a fact of life; compensate for the quantization error they cause
- AWQ/QuIP school: outliers can be flattened in advance, but only via multiplicative transforms between adjacent layers
- OSAQ school: outliers can be absorbed *within a single layer* using an additive transform

Notice this is a trajectory of *decoupling*: each generation makes "handling outliers" a more *local* operation — from needing global compensation, to requiring neighboring-layer cooperation, to needing only the layer itself.
The paradigm-level decoupling is more worth remembering than any accuracy number.

Where is the next step most likely to go?

My bet is *null-space mining across modalities*. OSAQ exploits the null space of the weight Hessian, but activation Hessians, cross-layer Hessians, and gradient covariance matrices all share similar low-rank structure. Once people internalize "the Hessian null space is a usable degree of freedom," the thinking will generalize to pruning, LoRA, model merging, and catastrophic forgetting mitigation. There is a lot of ore left in this mine.

* Takeaways

1. *A new coordinate system is worth more than a new algorithm.* From OBD to OSAQ, no one invented a new loss function or switched architectures — they changed *how they looked at the Hessian*. Diagonal → full matrix → Cholesky → null space: each change of coordinates opened new territory. The leverage point in research is often not "do more" but "see a dimension that was previously invisible."

2. *The dual face of a constraint is freedom.* AWQ/QuIP were boxed in by the requirement that equivalent transforms must be invertible. OSAQ abandoned equivalence entirely and replaced it with "second-order loss unchanged" — a strictly weaker constraint. Relax a constraint slightly and the solution space opens dramatically.

3. *The discarded part is often the treasure.* For a long time, the smallest eigenvalues of the Hessian were dismissed as numerical noise to be ignored. OSAQ turns this around — the directions deemed "too small to matter" are precisely what forms the null space. Whatever a field collectively ignores is worth a dedicated look.

4. *Actionable*: if you are working on model compression, PTQ, or any optimization involving second-order information, stop and ask yourself — "Am I using the null space of the Hessian?" Almost certainly you are not. That is the opportunity.
