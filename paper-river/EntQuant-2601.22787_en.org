* The Target Paper

*Float8@2bits: Entropy Coding Enables Data-Free Model Compression* (EntQuant). In one sentence: compute with weights in Float8, but store them in only 2 bits — by using entropy coding to decouple precision from storage cost.

* What Problem This River Is About

There are two long-standing, competing approaches to compressing large models:

- *Fast but coarse*: no data required, finishes in minutes, but collapses below 4 bits. Representatives: NF4, HQQ.
- *Accurate but expensive*: needs calibration data, retraining, and tens of hours or even days, but can compress to 2 bits and still be usable. Representatives: GPTQ, AWQ, QuIP#, AQLM.

Where is the real problem? All of these methods share the same hidden assumption: *compression ratio = bit width*. To achieve 8× compression, you must pack weights into 2-bit slots, leaving each layer with only 4 possible values — expressive capacity is strangled at the source.

The real question EntQuant asks is: *Why must compression ratio equal precision?*

* Forward Narrative: How the Problem Was Forced into View Step by Step

** Starting Point: Han's Deep Compression (2015)

The first to inject entropy coding into neural networks was Han et al. Their recipe was straightforward: prune → quantize → Huffman encode, a three-stage pipeline. But in their hands, Huffman was only an *offline packaging tool* — compress before writing to disk, then decompress and reload at runtime. Nobody asked: could the model read directly from the compressed stream *during inference*?

What was left open: entropy coding was treated as a storage optimization, never as a first-class citizen at inference time.

** Pioneers of the Data-Free Path: NF4 and HQQ (2023)

Dettmers introduced NF4 in QLoRA — observing that neural network weights roughly follow a normal distribution, he designed a 4-bit non-uniform quantization grid whose grid-point density matches normal density. Badri's HQQ took a similar approach, solving each layer's quantization parameters with half-quadratic optimization.

What these methods got right: *completely data-free*, compress a 70B model in minutes. What they got wrong: at 2 bits, an entire layer has only 4 distinct values, the tails of the weight distribution are amputated — LLaMA-2 7B at 2 bits with group=64 sees perplexity spike above 6000. Effectively broken.

Why does collapse happen inevitably? Because they are locked to "compression ratio = bit width." Two bits means four values — no way out.

What was left open: below 4 bits, the data-free path completely fails.

** The Data-Calibration Path Takes Over: GPTQ → AWQ → OmniQuant (2023–2024)

Frantar's GPTQ was the turning point. He noticed that if a small calibration dataset (a few hundred text samples) is allowed, one can compute each weight's second-order contribution to the loss, and compensate the error column by column during quantization — essentially local least squares. This trick made 4-bit compression nearly lossless.

Lin's AWQ identified a specific weakness in GPTQ: it treats all weights equally, yet a small set of "salient weights" — those multiplied by large activation values — have an outsized impact on output. AWQ simply applies fine-grained per-channel scaling to protect those weights: simpler and more accurate.

Shao's OmniQuant noticed another issue: GPTQ/AWQ rely on hand-crafted quantization strategies — why not make all quantization parameters learnable? It introduced learnable clipping thresholds and learnable equivalent transformations, optimized end-to-end.

The cost? GPTQ takes 2–4 hours on an A100-80GiB; OmniQuant takes 9–16 hours. And at 2 bits, OmniQuant accuracy drops ~25%, GPTQ drops ~53%.

What was left open: even the "trade data for accuracy" path stalls at 2 bits.

** The Heavy Artillery of Extreme Compression: QuIP# and AQLM (2024)

Tseng's QuIP# is an engineering monster: Hadamard transform to make the weight distribution more "well-behaved" (incoherence processing) + vector quantization (packing multiple weights into a single codeword) + recovery fine-tuning. It can reach 2 bits while retaining most capability. Cost: *8 × A100, 50 hours*.

Egiazarian's AQLM follows a similar logic — additive vector quantization, then recovery training.

Both methods proved that 2 bits is achievable, but the price is: complex vector codebooks, mandatory training data, and tens of hours of compute. For instruction-tuned or reasoning models whose training data is not public, these methods are completely inapplicable.

What was left open: methods that can reach 2 bits are either absurdly slow or gated behind unavailable data. Can we avoid both?

** Entropy Coding Re-enters: NeuZip and DFloat11 (2024–2025)

Hao's NeuZip and Zhang's DFloat11 finally recalled Huffman, the old weapon. They observed that BFloat16 exponent bits follow a highly non-uniform distribution (most weights' exponents cluster in a handful of values), so Huffman compressing them alone saves 30–50% storage *with zero precision loss*.

But they are still one step short: they only *passively exploit* the entropy that already exists in the weights, without *actively driving the weights toward lower entropy*. Entropy coding is used as an "after-the-fact freebie," not as a design objective.

What was left open: what if the training or fine-tuning process could actively reduce weight entropy?

** EntQuant's "Aha" Moment

EntQuant's core inversion can be stated simply:

*Every prior method tied "representing a weight in N bits" to "storing a weight in N bits." EntQuant unties them.*

How, concretely? Three steps:

1. *Keep compute precision at Float8*: weights still participate in GEMM as 8-bit floats on the GPU, preserving full dynamic range; each layer has on average ~64 distinct values — more expressive than the 16 values of 4-bit quantization.
2. *Optimize entropy, not range*: use a rate-distortion objective: reconstruction error (relative L1 loss) + L1 norm of the quantized weights (as a differentiable proxy for entropy). Only the per-output-channel scale factors are tuned, via L-BFGS + straight-through estimator, finishing a 70B model in minutes. The optimization goal becomes "make the weight distribution as skewed as possible," because a skewed distribution has low entropy and compresses aggressively.
3. *ANS online decoding*: use Asymmetric Numeral Systems (Duda 2013) — same compression ratio as arithmetic coding, but requiring only multiplications and bit-shifts, naturally suited for GPU parallelism. The compressed bitstream lives in VRAM; before each GEMM, NVIDIA nvCOMP decodes it to Float8 and feeds the Marlin kernel. Overhead ~1.5–2× relative to BFloat16.

Result: Float8 precision + 2-bit storage. A 70B model compressed in 30 minutes, no data required, works on instruction-tuned models too.

* What Transformation This River Is Really Carrying

On the surface: uniform quantization → non-uniform quantization → vector quantization → entropy coding.

At a deeper level: *the measuring stick for neural network compression is quietly changing axes*.

- Old paradigm: information theory has the *rate-distortion* theorem, but years of deep-learning quantization research were effectively doing *bit-width distortion* — using bit width as a proxy for compression ratio.
- New paradigm: EntQuant truly imports the rate-distortion theorem into neural network compression. Compression ratio is determined by *entropy*, not bit width. Bit width determines only numerical precision during computation; storage cost is determined by the actual bit count after entropy coding.

One level deeper: *the very dichotomy between "fast path" and "accurate path" is being destabilized*. For more than a decade these two camps criticized and encroached on each other. EntQuant is the first method to simultaneously satisfy:
- Data-free (the fast path's strength)
- Extreme compression (the accurate path's strength)
- Tens of minutes rather than tens of hours

This is not a technical update — it is a redefinition of the problem.

* Where Things Might Go Next

- *Bring entropy coding into training*: EntQuant currently does post-training optimization of per-layer scale factors. The natural next step is to write the entropy constraint directly into the pre-training or fine-tuning loss, so the model grows into an "easily compressible" form from the start.
- *Dynamic bit rates*: since storage cost is determined by entropy, different tokens / layers / batches can share a single decoder while consuming different bit budgets.
- *Apply the same treatment to KV cache*: KV cache is the other major memory consumer at inference time; current KV quantization methods still hold to bit width. Porting entropy coding there could cut memory in half again.
- *Hardware co-design*: nvCOMP's ANS is a stopgap. If a future GPU generation adds hardware-level ANS decode units, the ~1.5–2× overhead could drop to 1.1×, at which point EntQuant's approach would become the default configuration.
