* One-liner

Four years in — from "can FP4 inference hold up?" all the way to "can FP4
training hold up?" — everyone had blamed the failures on activation outliers
and slow software emulation. This paper moves to AMD MI355X *native MXFP4*
hardware and enables FP4 one segment at a time: Fprop alone is fine, adding
Dgrad is still fine, then adding Wgrad *causes training to collapse*.
They then try the full range of "add randomness" remedies — stochastic
rounding, randomized Hadamard — and none of them save the run. Only
*deterministic Hadamard rotation* pulls training back onto the FP8 track,
cutting the token overhead from 26% down to 8%. The verdict is blunt: FP4
training instability is not a matter of insufficient randomness — it is a
*structural micro-scaling error on the Wgrad path*.

* The central question of this line

The whole field has spent four years solving the same compression puzzle:

#+begin_example
Can 4-bit floating-point replace 16/8-bit without causing LLM training
or inference to collapse?
#+end_example

But the word "collapse" means something different at each generation of papers:

- *2023* Everyone was asking: can a trained model be squeezed into 4 bits at
  *inference* time? (PTQ)
- *2024* The question escalated to: can we train from scratch in 4 bits?
  (emulation)
- *2025* Escalated again: can the *full pipeline* (forward + both backward
  passes) use 4 bits? (NVFP4/MXFP4 recipes)
- *2026* This paper asks the sharpest version yet: now that hardware finally
  supports native FP4, *which GEMM path is actually collapsing*, and *do the
  widely used stabilization tricks (stochastic rounding, randomized rotation)
  actually help*?

Each generation spotted a blind spot in the one before it. The blind spot this
paper identifies is the most concrete: it argues that prior "full-pipeline FP4"
work either ran on emulation, or never localized the failure to Wgrad, or
mistakenly assumed *adding noise = adding stability*.

* Evolutionary narrative

** 2022.09 | Outlier Suppression: outliers are not noise — gamma is the amplifier

*** What problem it saw

Before this work, people told a simple story about quantization loss in
Transformers: "the distribution is too spread out and there aren't enough bits."
Wei et al. ran a systematic set of experiments and found a more specific root
cause: the learnable parameter gamma inside LayerNorm is the real "outlier
amplifier." Whichever channels have large gamma values get their activations
stretched arbitrarily; the quantizer sees a few columns shooting to the ceiling
while everything else is crushed to the floor.

*** Its solution

Two steps:
- *Gamma migration*: equivalently fold LayerNorm's gamma into the weights of
  the next layer. The computation is unchanged, but the activation distribution
  flattens immediately.
- *Token-wise clipping*: different tokens have different outlier patterns, so
  clipping thresholds are set per token.

This brought BERT 6-bit PTQ back to fp32-level accuracy.

*** The gap it left for successors

It told everyone one important thing: *outliers are not naturally occurring —
they are structurally generated, and can be "moved" or "dispersed" by
equivalent transforms.* That idea later split into two branches — SmoothQuant
moves them via per-channel scaling; QuaRot disperses them via Hadamard
rotation. This paper follows the second branch.

** 2023.10 | LLM-FP4: 4-bit float inference finally works

*** What problem it saw

8-bit integer inference was stable, but going below 8 bits caused collapse —
especially at W4A4. The culprit was still activation outliers, but Liu et al.
sharpened the observation: *inter-channel variance is extremely high while
intra-channel variance is extremely low.* In other words, values within a
single channel are well-clustered, but channels differ from each other by
factors of tens.

*** Its solution

Since the channels differ so much, assign a separate scale *to each channel*.
Then apply a neat algebraic trick: per-channel activation scale can be
reparameterized into the *exponent bias* of the weights, adding negligible
hardware cost.

LLaMA-13B W4A4 inference accuracy fell only 5.8 points below full precision.

*** The gap it left for successors

All of this is at *inference* time (PTQ). During training, weights are
changing and the activation distribution keeps shifting — how do you maintain
per-channel scales on the fly? Moreover, per-channel scales are not naturally
friendly to hardware GEMM units: you either use vector-wise quantization or
switch to a more structured scheme. MX format took the second path directly.

** 2023.10 | MX Formats: upgrading "per-tensor scale" to "per-block scale"

*** What problem it saw

PTQ techniques required per-channel granularity to suppress outliers, but the
channel dimension does not align naturally with the tile-based GEMM of GPUs.
At the same time, a single per-tensor scale was too coarse — one outlier could
saturate the entire dynamic range.

*** Its solution

Microsoft, AMD, Arm, NVIDIA, Intel, and Qualcomm jointly proposed a family of
data formats:
- Slice the tensor into *small blocks of 32 numbers*
- Each block shares a single 8-bit exponent (power-of-two scale)
- Each element in the block uses narrow floating-point (FP4/FP6/FP8) or integer

This confines the impact of an outlier to its own block, preventing it from
corrupting the entire tensor, while the 32-element block size adds almost zero
overhead to tile-based GEMM.

MX formats also demonstrated *training*-side convergence at sub-8-bit — not
just inference.

*** The gap it left for successors

The format was defined, but nobody had native FP4 hardware. The name MXFP4
spent the next two years in a state of "software emulation + waiting for
hardware." Meanwhile, whether *training* could truly run at *4-bit* remained
an open question.

** 2025.01–05 | Wang / Zhou / Chmiel: FP4 training works at last — but only on emulators

*** What they collectively saw

Although the MX paper demonstrated sub-8-bit training, it mixed in FP6 and
was not pure FP4. True FP4 training had two major blind spots:
- Weight gradients (Wgrad) have a much wider dynamic range than forward
  activations; 4 bits simply cannot hold them.
- Activation outliers drift in real time during training; the static scaling
  of PTQ fails completely.

*** Wang 2025 (Optimizing LLM Training using FP4)

The first end-to-end FP4 training framework. Two core ideas:
- *Differentiable quantization estimator*: a smoother backward gradient
  estimate than the conventional STE
- *Outlier clamping & compensation*: after clipping outliers, recover the
  lost energy

Combined with mixed precision (some layers kept at 8-bit) and vector-wise
quantization, it trained 13B parameters over 100B tokens to near-BF16 quality.

*** Zhou 2025 (Towards Efficient Pre-training)

More engineering-focused: assigns different precision strategies to different
modules (attention vs. MLP) and different training phases, achieving accuracy
comparable to BF16/FP8. In essence: "FP4 alone collapses, so never let it run
alone."

*** Chmiel 2025 (FP4 All the Way)

The most thorough of the three. 200B tokens, 7B parameters. Key contributions:
- NVFP4 format (block size 16, scale in E4M3 rather than power-of-two) beats
  MXFP4
- *Stochastic rounding* in backward, *round-to-nearest* in forward
- A theoretical threshold: when the gradient norm falls below roughly sqrt(3)×
  quantization noise, quantized training breaks down

*** What they left for this paper

Three things:
1. *All experiments run on software emulation* — throughput numbers are
   unrealistic, and training stability results may be contaminated by the
   emulator's extra precision.
2. *No one decomposed FP4 into separate Fprop / Dgrad / Wgrad stages* —
   everyone either enabled all three at once or kept all three off.
3. *Stochastic rounding was accepted as an indispensable stabilizer* — FP4
   All the Way adopted it as the default backward routine without questioning
   whether it truly stabilizes training or merely appears stable on an emulator.

These three points correspond exactly to the three things this paper sets out
to resolve.

** 2026.03 | Cim et al. (same first author): position-sensitivity of FP4 inference

*** What problem it saw

Three months before the present paper, first author Musa Cim published
"Diagnosing FP4 Inference" — a systematic measurement of how *inference
sensitivity* varies across different layers and blocks for both MXFP4 and
NVFP4.

The conclusions were telling:
- MLP up/down projections are consistently the most sensitive positions for FP4
- Gate projections and attention projections are comparatively forgiving
- Early blocks are also anomalously sensitive under MXFP4 (contrary to the
  common wisdom that the last few layers matter most)

*** The thread it handed to this paper

This established that *FP4 fragility is not uniformly distributed but highly
structured — certain paths are simply ill-suited by nature.* If positional
sensitivity is this strong at inference time, the same must be true during
training; one must examine Fprop, Dgrad, and Wgrad independently rather than
treating them as a monolith.

The present paper is almost a training-side sequel — the same divide-and-
conquer logic, transplanted into the training pipeline.

** 2026.05 | This paper: native FP4 + staged enabling + falsifying "add randomness"

*** What problem it saw

Prior FP4 training papers (Wang / Zhou / Chmiel) all claimed success, but this
paper raised three objections:

1. They all ran on emulation. *Has anyone ever measured real hardware-level
   throughput?*
2. They claim full-pipeline FP4 works, but *which of Fprop, Dgrad, and Wgrad
   is actually responsible for the token overhead?*
3. Chmiel treated stochastic rounding as the de facto backward standard. *Does
   it genuinely stabilize training, or does it merely look stable on an
   emulator?*

*** Its solution (the experimental design is the paper's main contribution)

The authors gained access to AMD MI355X — one of the first chips with *native
MXFP4 tensor cores*, requiring no software emulation whatsoever.

Setup: Llama 3.1-8B, C4 dataset, MLPerf standard configuration, target:
validation perplexity 3.3.

They then ran a *progressive ladder*: hold everything else fixed and change
exactly one thing — enable MXFP4 one level deeper at a time:
- FP8 baseline → 0% token overhead
- Fprop only → 8–9%
- Add Dgrad → 10–11%  (still acceptable)
- Add Wgrad → *26–27%*  (nearly triples the overhead)

Conclusion: *Wgrad is the primary instability source in FP4 training.*

*** Then testing stabilizers

For each stage they tested three classes of stabilizer:
- *Stochastic rounding* (the standard endorsed by Chmiel et al.)
- *Randomized Hadamard rotation* (random sign flip + Hadamard matrix)
- *Deterministic Hadamard rotation* (fixed H_16 matrix)

The results are clean:
- The first two make no difference in the Fprop-only or Fprop+Dgrad stages
  (~10% overhead)
- Once Wgrad is enabled, the first two produce *Does Not Converge*
- Only deterministic Hadamard pulls token overhead back to 8–9%, and as a
  bonus improves train-step throughput by 20% (real end-to-end speedup of
  +9–10%)

*** The real insight

The insight is deeper than "use Hadamard to rescue FP4." The real claim is:

#+begin_example
FP4 training instability is *structural micro-scaling error*,
not *insufficient randomness*.
#+end_example

Stochastic rounding — a "add noise" approach — addresses the problem of biased
rounding. But the true killer in MXFP4 is: *when a single channel dominates a
block on the Wgrad path, it monopolizes the shared block exponent and crushes
every other element in that block to zero.* This is structural, not stochastic.
Adding more randomness only amplifies the structural error into noise.

Only deterministic Hadamard rotation, applied before the GEMM, *spreads
activations and gradients within each block toward a near-Gaussian
distribution*, so that no single channel can monopolize the block scale.
That is the genuine stabilizer.

*** What new questions it leaves open (its own acknowledged limitations)

The authors state these in Key Understanding #3:
- This recipe works for Llama 3.1-8B on C4; there is no guarantee it transfers
  to other models, datasets, or fine-tuning scenarios.
- FP4 training recipes are not universal; they should be re-validated per
  setting.
- H_16 is 8% faster than H_32, but the optimal Hadamard size depends on
  hardware tile size.

The biggest open question: *why is the Wgrad path so much more sensitive to
micro-scaling error?* The paper proves in Appendix C that Hadamard rotation
leaves the forward output mathematically unchanged, but it offers no
theoretical explanation of *why structural error on the Wgrad path is
uniquely lethal*. That is work for the next paper.

* What is really happening beneath this line of work

On the surface it looks like bit-width marching downward: 16 → 8 → 4. But
underneath, what is really happening is a *progressive deepening of our
understanding of quantization error*:

1. *Before 2022*: error treated as i.i.d. Gaussian noise; solution: "keep the
   distribution narrow enough."
2. *2022 Wei / 2023 Liu*: error is dominated by *structured outliers*; solution:
   "find the amplifier and eliminate it via equivalent transform."
3. *2023 Rouhani*: the locality of error can be bounded by *per-block scale*;
   the format itself prevents outliers from crossing block boundaries.
4. *2025 Chmiel*: error is asymmetric across forward and backward; use
   *stochastic rounding* in backward to cancel rounding bias.
5. *2026 this paper*: error is also asymmetric *across the three GEMM paths*;
   the Wgrad path error is *structural*, adding randomness makes it worse, and
   only a *deterministic structured transform* can suppress it.

Each generation cuts the object "error" into finer slices — from scalar noise,
to channel-level outliers, to block-level outliers, to path-level sensitivity
differentials. The finer the cut, the more precise the solution, and the
closer it comes to hardware truth.

*Where the field is most likely to go next*: since the Wgrad path is so
uniquely problematic, should it get *its own dedicated quantization format*?
(For instance, NVFP4 for forward and MXFP4-with-larger-block for Wgrad.)
Or more aggressively: *let the training schedule automatically detect which
path should be bumped back to 8-bit*, à la Zhou 2025 but at finer granularity.
A parallel direction: replace the Hadamard rotation with a *learnable
orthogonal matrix* that adapts to the model's own outlier pattern. QuaRot's
successor SpinQuant has done this for inference; the training side is
unexplored territory.

* The spiritual core in one line

#+begin_quote
When bit-width is pushed to its limit, "randomness fixes everything" stops
being true — you must use *deterministic structural transforms* to match the
*structural blind spots of hardware micro-scaling*.
#+end_quote
