* One-liner

When distilling a small model, the teacher says "the next token is probably A," so you pour everything into learning A — but the long tail of low-probability tokens the teacher casually mentioned contains the full color of language, and you threw it all away. This river is the story of how, over a decade, researchers gradually realized that the tail matters more than the head.

* What problem does this river address?

Cramming the knowledge of a large model into a small one is called distillation. The most basic version was proposed by Hinton in 2015: have the small model match the "soft probability distribution" the large model outputs at each position — not just learning the correct answer, but also learning "how likely the teacher thinks each answer is."

That sounds great. But there is a hidden trap: in natural language, the teacher's probability distribution is highly long-tailed. The next token is most often something like /the/, /of/, or a comma, which might carry probability 0.6; but across the remaining 49,999 candidate tokens, each one might have probability 0.0001, yet they collectively account for 0.4 of the total mass — this is the "tail."

The KL divergence used in distillation has a mathematical fact baked in: the magnitude of the gradient is proportional to the magnitude of the probability. This means the token the teacher assigns 0.6 generates an enormous gradient signal for the student; the ten thousand tokens each assigned 0.0001 generate signals so small they are practically invisible. The result is that the student only learns the probabilities of the handful of head tokens, while flattening the tail — discarding exactly the portion of knowledge that shows the teacher truly "understands language."

This river is the story of how, over a decade, researchers gradually identified this trap and tried to climb out of it.

* The evolutionary story

** Act I: Hinton 2015 — "Dark knowledge lives in soft labels"

Before him, training a small model meant showing it the correct answer: the image is a cat, label = 1, everything else = 0. Hinton noticed that a large model outputs more than just 1s and 0s — it says "95% cat, 4% leopard, 1% dog, 0.001% truck."

Those tiny probabilities encode the large model's understanding of the world: it knows cats resemble leopards, not trucks. Hinton called these *dark knowledge*. Having the small model match not just the correct answer but the entire soft probability distribution — using KL divergence as the loss — transfers this knowledge.

This worked impressively for image classification. Then everyone transplanted it wholesale to language models: at each generated token, make the student's distribution fit the teacher's distribution.

The problem left behind: image classification has only 1,000 classes, so the "tail" of a soft label has at most 999 entries. But language model vocabularies run from 50,000 to 250,000 tokens, with extremely long-tailed distributions — and KL divergence is precisely unfriendly to long tails. This tension would not be confronted head-on until many years later.

** Act II: DKD 2022 — "Separate target-class and non-target-class knowledge"

On the vision side, Zhao et al. discovered that the classic KD loss can be mathematically split into two pieces: "knowledge about the correct class" (TCKD) and "knowledge about the relative relationships among all other classes" (NCKD). They showed that NCKD is where KD actually does its work, yet it gets suppressed by TCKD — the more confident the teacher is about the correct answer, the more the NCKD contribution is automatically down-weighted in the loss.

DKD decouples these two pieces, giving NCKD an independent weight β that can be amplified. It works for image classification.

The problem left behind: DKD's decoupling is *label-anchored* — target class vs. non-target class. This is fine for classification tasks where the label is ground truth. But transplanting it to language model pre-training hits a wall: the token the teacher assigns highest probability and the actual next token in the dataset disagree 39–46% of the time. Splitting by label mixes two mutually contradictory signals. This crack would not be sealed until TAD.

** Act III: MiniLLM 2023 — "Switch to reverse KL; let the student learn only the teacher's peaks"

Gu et al. from Tsinghua took a different angle. They argued: the standard (forward) KL is ~E_teacher [log(p_teacher/p_student)]~, which forces the student to spread probability over everything the teacher considers possible — including the tail. The problem is that in long-text generation, collapse into the tail means the student will hallucinate.

So flip it: use *reverse KL* ~E_student [log(p_student/p_teacher)]~. This loss is mathematically *mode-seeking* — it encourages the student to concentrate on the teacher's peaks, preferring to leave parts of the distribution uncovered rather than placing probability where the teacher offers no support.

This requires RL to optimize (PPO-style), because reverse KL involves an expectation under the student distribution. The cost is that training requires sampling from the student on-the-fly — slow, expensive, feasible only at instruction-fine-tuning scale with small datasets.

The problem left behind: MiniLLM chose to *actively abandon the tail*. It engineered around the long-tail problem — since KL favors the head, just learn the head. But this is precisely what discards the dark knowledge Hinton originally emphasized. TAD later shows that models trained with reverse KL have the worst calibration of all — they are overconfident, because they were taught from the start to "only look at the mode."

** Act IV: GKD 2023 — "Use JS divergence; split the difference"

Agarwal et al. from Google felt that both forward KL and reverse KL were too extreme: one learns the whole distribution and risks hallucination; the other learns only the peak and loses diversity. Could they meet in the middle?

They introduced *generalized JS divergence* — essentially a weighted mixture of forward KL and reverse KL, controlled by a parameter β. They further added on-policy training: training samples come not only from the teacher but also from trajectories the student itself generates and the teacher then corrects. This is "Generalized KD."

The problem left behind: JS divergence is just "averaging two KLs" and does not truly confront the essence of the long-tail problem — it is still a divergence that weights by probability value. Meanwhile, on-policy sampling sends compute costs soaring, and like MiniLLM it can only run on small datasets.

** Act V: DistiLLM 2024 — "skew-KL + adaptive scheduling"

Ko et al. from KAIST continued to surgically modify the divergence form. They proposed *skewed KL* — before computing KL, first interpolate the student and teacher distributions (mix 10% student into teacher, and vice versa), so that gradients do not explode when the distributions differ greatly. They also designed an adaptive scheduler that decides when to sample from the teacher and when from the student, achieving a 4× speedup over GKD.

The problem left behind: DistiLLM still runs on instruction-fine-tuning scale — datasets like dollyEval with ~15,000 samples. From the perspective of pre-training distillation (tens of billions of tokens), any method that relies on student sampling is dead. And it does not truly solve "KL is insensitive to the long tail" — it only prevents gradient numerical instability.

** Act VI: MiniPLM 2024 — "Don't change the loss; change the data"

Gu came back with a new approach. This time the logic was: since the KL loss is hard to fix, change the *training data* instead. He used the disagreement between the teacher model and a small reference model to score training samples — high-disagreement samples are valuable hard cases; easy and noisy samples are down-weighted or filtered. Then ordinary KL is trained on this curated corpus.

This is MiniPLM, demonstrated at pre-training distillation scale. Teacher inference can be done offline, so training multiple students does not require repeated teacher forward passes.

The problem left behind: MiniPLM still uses ordinary KL, so the "tail collapse" problem remains intact. It just gives you harder problems to work on. It also requires a fixed small reference model, so switching teachers means redoing data selection — inflexible in multi-teacher settings. It cannot do supervised distillation either. On domains like mathematics, it can even underperform training without distillation.

** Act VII: TAD 2026 (this paper) — "Decouple by rank; pry up the tail gradient"

Dasgupta et al. lay all prior work's problems on the table:

1. Reverse KL (MiniLLM lineage) abandons the tail; calibration collapses
2. On-policy (GKD/DistiLLM) cannot scale to large pre-training datasets
3. Data selection (MiniPLM) does not change the loss; the tail still collapses
4. Label-anchored decoupling (DKD) is unreliable for LMs because teacher and data labels frequently disagree

Their solution strikes at the root — *redesign the divergence itself*, so that the tail gradient is explicitly pried up.

The specific approach:

1. *Decouple by rank* (not by label): treat the top-K tokens by teacher probability as the "head" and the rest as the "tail." Both sets are defined by the teacher alone, with no dependence on data labels, so DKD's mismatch problem does not arise.
2. *Split KL into two terms*: D_KL1 computes KL over the top-K tokens plus one aggregated "remainder" term; D_KL2 computes KL over the *normalized tail distribution*. Normalization is the key — each tail token's raw probability might be 0.0001, but after normalization it could be 0.05, instantly giving the gradient meaningful magnitude.
3. *Use β to pry up tail weight*: the second term is multiplied by the teacher's total tail mass α and a hyperparameter β. Around β=2 works best.
4. *Gradient self-regulation*: when the student's top-K total mass exceeds the teacher's, the tail gradient is amplified, pushing probability back into the tail; once head/tail proportions align, the correction term vanishes, and the final convergence point matches ordinary KL.

Results: TinyLlama-1.1B scores 36.8 on GSM8K, approaching Rho-1 (which used 9× the compute); Llama2-7B scores 56, approaching Llemma-7B (also 9× the compute). The entire training runs on a single H100 for one week.

* What is really happening underneath this river

On the surface it looks like iteration over loss functions and training algorithms. Underneath, there is a cognitive shift:

*Gradually treating "the probability distribution" not as a scalar target to be matched, but as an object with internal structure.*

Hinton's generation treated the whole distribution as a single thing to fit (one KL, one stick to beat everything). MiniLLM began to notice that different parts of the distribution have different natures (head = mode, tail = noise/dark knowledge), but their choice was to discard one part. DKD started splitting, but only dared to split by label. TAD is the first work to cleanly instantiate the insight that "distributions have a head and a tail, and head and tail need separate treatment" directly inside the LM distillation loss.

At a deeper level: this is really about the nature of compression. Distillation is lossy compression. Early methods implicitly assumed "high-frequency signals matter; low-frequency can be discarded" — but language does not work that way. The long tail encodes the diversity and generalization capacity of language. Accepting this means accepting that loss functions cannot simply be weighted by "signal strength."

* Where might this go next?

1. *Adaptive K*: K=5–10 is an empirical choice. The optimal K should vary by token position — low-entropy positions (grammatical function words) need small K; high-entropy positions (content words, highly generative contexts) need larger K.
2. *Distribution-shape awareness*: β is currently a global hyperparameter. In the future it may need to adapt per-sentence or even per-token based on the local distribution shape.
3. *Tail within the tail*: This paper makes a binary split — top-K vs. rest. A natural next step is a three-way head/body/tail split, or even power-law modeling of the tail itself.
4. *Connecting to compression theory*: Current loss design is empirically driven. If one could derive from rate-distortion theory or the information bottleneck why tail normalization is necessary, this river would upgrade into principled science.

* One takeaway

*The high probability at the head is signal in the noise; the low probability in the tail is dark knowledge in the signal. When you learn only the head, you learn the grammar of the language. When you also learn the tail well, you learn the worldview the language encodes.*
