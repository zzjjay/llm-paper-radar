* The River of the Problem

Squeezing a 70-billion-parameter model into the memory a phone can actually run means compressing every weight from 16-bit float down to 4-bit integer. After compression the model still has to "look like its original self" — same input, same output.

Where is the difficulty? You cannot retrain while compressing (too expensive), so you must "compress and move on." But naive rounding destroys the model — precision loss snowballs from layer to layer.

An entire line of research has been working on the same idea: *quantize one weight, then use the remaining unquantized weights to "compensate" for that loss, keeping the layer's output as close to the original as possible.* For more than thirty years, every paper on this line has asked the same question:

#+begin_quote
Is the target I am "aligning to" actually correct? I think I am fitting the original model's output — but am I really?
#+end_quote

Each generation has discovered that its predecessors thought they were aligning to A but were actually aligning to B — a contaminated target. This paper (ResComp) is the latest entry on that line. It shows that GPTAQ, which believed it was aligning to the original model, was still falling short.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1989 | OBD: Using Second-Order Derivatives to Identify Which Weights to Prune

*** What Problem It Saw

In the late 1980s, as neural networks began to grow larger, researchers had already noticed that most weights were effectively useless after training. But how do you decide which ones? The most naive approach: delete each weight one by one and observe how much the loss rises.

That approach is impossibly slow — a network with tens of thousands of weights would require tens of thousands of retraining runs.

*** Its Solution

LeCun's idea: *don't try — compute.* Using a Taylor expansion, the second-order derivative of the loss with respect to each weight (the diagonal of the Hessian) tells you "how much will the loss increase if this weight is removed." One computation suffices; no repeated trials needed.

But a full Hessian matrix is enormous (n weights means n² cross-derivative terms), so OBD simply assumes it is diagonal — in other words, that "removing weight i has no effect on the optimal value of weight j."

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Left)

The diagonal assumption reduces the cost from O(n²) to O(n), making it tractable. But the assumption is wrong — weights in a neural network are coupled. Remove one weight and the optimal values of the others shift. OBD ignores this, so after pruning, the model can degrade more than OBD predicts.

The next generation would ask: can we use the full Hessian without retraining?

** 1993 | OBS: Remove One Weight, Fine-Tune All the Rest

*** What Problem It Saw

OBD assumes all other weights remain fixed when one is deleted. Hassibi pointed out directly: *this wastes every available compensation opportunity.* If you remove weight i but allow the other weights to adjust slightly to "absorb" its original contribution, the increase in loss will be far smaller than OBD estimates.

In other words, OBD prunes "in place," but truly optimal pruning should be "prune one, adjust many."

*** Its Solution

OBS uses the full inverse Hessian. When weight w_i is chosen for removal, *simultaneously* every other weight receives an analytically optimal update Δw that minimizes the impact of this cut on the loss.

Intuitively: each time a weight is pruned, the "responsibility" it carried is redistributed to the remaining weights through the Hessian. This is the origin of the compensation idea.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Left)

The solution is closed-form and optimal at every step. But the computational cost explodes with network size — the n×n inverse Hessian is neither storable nor computable for large models. In the 1990s, networks had only a few thousand weights, so it barely ran.

Looking back at this mathematics twenty years later, a new question emerges: OBS performs "pruning" — setting weights *to zero*. Modern quantization instead *replaces* weights with discrete values (e.g., 4-bit integers) — not deletion but substitution. Can this compensation idea be extended from "removing" to "replacing"?

** 2022 | OBQ: Porting the Compensation Idea from Pruning to Quantization

*** What Problem It Saw

Frantar and Alistarh saw that the mathematics of OBS was elegant but underutilized by being confined to pruning. The step from "set weight to zero" to "round weight to the nearest quantization grid point" is simply a change of constraint: Δw_i is no longer -w_i but rather round(w_i) - w_i.

The rest of the mechanism — using the Hessian to compensate the remaining weights — can be reused wholesale.

*** Its Solution

OBQ generalizes OBS's Lagrange multiplier framework to the quantization setting: at each step, find the weight whose quantization introduces the least error, quantize it, then use a closed-form solution to fine-tune all remaining unquantized weights as compensation. Repeat for the next weight, and so on.

Key detail: both which weight to quantize and how to compensate are driven by the criterion of minimizing the impact on the final output.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Left)

Each step is theoretically optimal. But OBQ cannot run directly on LLMs — for two reasons:
1. *Every row of weights must maintain its own inverse Hessian* (because each row follows a different quantization order).
2. *The inverse Hessian must be updated after every single weight is quantized* (via Gaussian elimination).

For models with hundreds of millions or billions of parameters, both the memory and time requirements of these operations are prohibitive.

Who would translate this mathematics from a paper into engineering that could actually run on LLaMA?

** 2022 | GPTQ: Three Key Moves to Make Compensation Scale to LLMs

*** What Problem It Saw

OBQ's bottlenecks were two:
- One Hessian per row.
- Recomputing the Hessian at every step.

If all rows could share a single Hessian, and if "the inverse-Hessian information needed at every step" could be precomputed all at once, the O(n³) cost could be amortized.

*** Its Solution

GPTQ introduces three engineering-level modifications simultaneously:

1. *Unified quantization order* — all rows are quantized column-by-column from left to right, collapsing n distinct Hessians into 1 shared Hessian.
2. *Cholesky reformulation* — write the inverse Hessian as H⁻¹ = L·Lᵀ; at each step, reading the corresponding row of L provides the required update direction, eliminating the need for online Gaussian elimination.
3. *Lazy batch update* — accumulate a batch of columns before applying the update to the remaining weights, reducing memory thrashing.

Result: a 175B-parameter OPT model can be quantized in under four hours. This is the inflection point that transformed compensation-based quantization from an academic algorithm into an industrially deployable method.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Left)

GPTQ pushes intra-layer optimization to its limit. But it rests on an implicit assumption: *when quantizing any given layer, the input X is clean and identical to that of the original model.*

In reality, layers are chained — the quantized output of layer 1 carries error, which becomes the input to layer 2; layer 2 performs its quantization compensation on a corrupted input. By layer 50, the input has long since diverged from the original model's. GPTQ still treats this corrupted X as the reference.

Error snowballs and accumulates all the way to the end.

** 2025 | GPTAQ: Asymmetric Calibration — Use the Original Model's Input as the Target

*** What Problem It Saw

The authors of GPTAQ identified the crux: *the calibration reference wX is itself wrong.* Because X has been corrupted by preceding layers, even if you perfectly achieve ŵX = wX, there remains a gap from the original model's true output wX̃ (where X̃ is the input along the full-precision forward pass).

GPTQ's "perfect alignment" is actually alignment with a ghost.

*** Its Solution

Replace the calibration target from "corrupted input × original weights" wX with "clean input × original weights" wX̃. This is "asymmetric calibration" — the quantized output uses the real-world (corrupted) input, while the reference output uses the ideal (clean) input:

  min ‖ŵ·X - w·X̃‖²

To integrate this new target into GPTQ's iterative framework, GPTAQ introduces a *residual term* r = w·X̃ - w·X as "the debt owed by the preceding layers," and at each weight update adds a correction term r·Xᵀ·H⁻¹ to repay that debt.

Result: the alignment target genuinely becomes the original model's output, effectively suppressing the accumulation of cross-layer errors.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Left)

GPTAQ resolves the cross-layer contamination. But the authors of ResComp would discover: *it does not resolve intra-layer contamination.*

Specifically: within a single layer, quantization is iterative — quantize column 1 and compensate the rest; quantize column 2 and compensate again; and so on. At step q, the "original weights" remaining are actually those that have already been modified by q−1 compensation steps, denoted w(q) — no longer the true w(0). The "w" appearing in GPTAQ's target is actually w(q), a moving target.

In other words, GPTAQ aligns to the original model across layers, but within a layer it is still aligning to a "version of itself that has already been compensated."

** 2026 | ResComp: Anchoring the Target Firmly to the Original Weights

*** What Problem It Saw

Examining GPTAQ's low-level optimization objective at step q, the "reference weights" are the current w(q), already iterated q times — not the original w(0). This means GPTAQ effectively redefines the "target output" at every single iteration step — the target is moving.

What should truly be aligned to is the output computed once, immutably, by the original full-precision model: w(0)·X̃.

*** Its Solution

ResComp rewrites the objective back to w(0)·X̃ and then, through algebraic derivation, discovers that in addition to the cross-layer residual r already handled by GPTAQ, there is an additional term:

#+begin_example
e_comp = (w(0) - w(q)) · X    <-- "compensation-aware error"
#+end_example

This term captures "how far the weights have drifted from their originals during intra-layer iteration." It is folded into GPTAQ's correction term:

#+begin_example
Δw = standard update                        <-- original GPTQ
   + r · Xᵀ · H⁻¹                          <-- GPTAQ cross-layer correction
   + (compensation-aware correction term)   <-- ResComp addition
#+end_example

Computing e_comp directly is too expensive at LLM scale, so the authors borrow GPTAQ's "neuron decomposition" technique: split the error term into precomputable pieces that require only lightweight updates at each step.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Left)

Mathematically, the entire objective function is now fully consistent with the layer-level optimization goal — inner loop and outer loop, every step and the final target, all aligning to the same thing: the original model's output.

Engineeringly, the cost is almost zero — it amounts to adding a few lines of code to GPTQ or GPTAQ. In experiments, LLaMA-3 8B at 2-bit quantization sees perplexity drop from GPTAQ's 16.45 to 14.32, with consistent gains at 3-bit as well.

What pit remains? By this point, the core question of *"what exactly is the alignment target"* has been substantially resolved within this line of work. The next directions may involve stepping outside the "intra-layer iteration + column-by-column quantization" framework entirely — since every generation of improvement has been patching "the previous generation's target definition wasn't clean enough," perhaps one should avoid this greedy column-wise approach from the start. For instance: true global optimization? Or some other framework altogether?

* Frontier Extensions

ResComp is the ICLR 2026 camera-ready version, uploaded to arXiv in April 2026. As of now (May 2026), no paper has yet cited and extended it.

Possible follow-on directions (speculative):
- *Breaking the column-wise greedy approach*: joint optimization of an entire layer at once, rather than column by column.
- *Extending the compensation idea to activation quantization*: this line has primarily targeted weight quantization; the corresponding ideas for the activation side have not yet been clearly developed.
- *Combining with rotation/Hadamard-based methods (QuaRot, SpinQuant)*: the other mainstream quantization direction first rotates weights into a "quantization-friendly" space before compression; the two lines are currently parallel.

* Insights

Having read the full line of work, three things rise to the surface.

*First, the real through-line of this work is not "algorithms" — it is "target definition."* Over thirty-plus years, the mathematical tools have barely changed — it has always been second-order Taylor expansion plus Lagrange multipliers. What changed every time was "what exactly are we trying to align to." Each generation discovered that its predecessors thought they were aligning to A but were actually aligning to a contaminated A'. This successive purification of the alignment target explains the sustained performance gains far better than any new algorithm does.

*Second, "compensation" is a stronger paradigm than "approximation."* Naive quantization (direct rounding) simply accepts the loss. What the OBS lineage does is *actively redistribute the loss* — this weight is wrong, so let the weights that are not wrong absorb its error. This thinking recurs in many places: gradient compensation in distributed training, activation compensation in sparse inference, residual compensation in low-rank approximation. For any problem involving lossy simplification, it is always worth asking: "Can the parts I haven't simplified yet help me absorb this loss?"

*Third, the further along the line, the more the value of a paper is hidden in the details.* The first two generations (OBD/OBS) were paradigm-creating — the core idea fits in one sentence. The later four (OBQ → GPTQ → GPTAQ → ResComp) all share "compensation" as the core idea; each contribution is buried in "one extra term in the objective function" or "a different decomposition in the implementation." An outsider might think "isn't this just GPTQ with a trick?" — but it is precisely these tricks that re-align the objective function with the true requirement each time.

*Where does it go from here?* This line has essentially exhausted the cleanliness of the target within the "column-wise greedy" framework. Further progress may not come from more patches but from escaping the framework — either through joint optimization (optimize the entire layer at once) or through "rotation preprocessing" (QuaRot-style) combined with the compensation line. It is also possible that this line is approaching convergence, and subsequent engineering effort will shift toward "how to make these compressed models actually run fast on heterogeneous hardware."

* Inspiration

Throughout this river, one action recurs: *pause and ask "what do I think I am fitting — and am I really fitting that?"*

The authors of GPTQ likely looked at their code and asked: "At every step I am achieving ŵX ≈ wX, but by layer 50, can that X still equal the original model's X?" The answer is no — and GPTAQ was born.
The authors of GPTAQ then asked: "The 'w' in my target — at step q inside the layer, is it still the original w?" The answer is also no — and ResComp was born.

This means you can —

*After writing any objective function or loss, scan every symbol in the target: has it moved during execution?* Most performance bottlenecks, training divergences, and alignment failures trace back to "the meaning of a symbol quietly drifted during the process."

More broadly: *if a field is stuck in a loop of engineering improvements, go back and scrutinize its alignment target.* The algorithm may already be mature, but the target definition very likely contains a window-pane no one has yet broken through. Break it once, and the entire performance curve rises another step.
