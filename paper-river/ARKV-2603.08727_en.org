* The River of Problems

In one sentence: when an LLM reaches the hundred-thousandth word of a conversation, the notepad in its head that tracks "what was said before" (the KV cache) can grow larger than the model itself. The question is how to make it — when memory runs out — *forget the unimportant, compress the semi-important, and retain the critical* — rather than applying a blunt, uniform cut.

Why is this problem hard? Why has it resisted clean solutions for three or four years?

- *The future is unknowable*: to decide whether "this token still matters," you need to know what will be asked next. But the next token has not been generated yet — there is no way to know.
- *One-size-fits-all is expensive*: compress everything to 2-bit and math problems collapse immediately; retain everything and a 64 GB GPU cannot hold a long conversation with a 70B model.
- *Every layer is different*: shallow Transformer layers handle syntax, deep layers handle reasoning — their tolerance for "dropping a token" differs by orders of magnitude. Yet the vast majority of methods treat all layers identically.
- *The online vs. offline dilemma*: offline analysis of per-layer sensitivity is accurate, but becomes stale the moment the model or task changes; online decisions are flexible, but recomputing every step burns more overhead than the savings are worth.

Every paper on this river is attempting to move one small step within these four constraints.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

The papers below are not isolated results — each one solves a concrete pain point left by the one before it. Walking this line, you will see how a simple intuition ("keep important tokens, drop the rest") is incrementally pushed toward a far more nuanced view: "importance is layer-dependent, dynamic, and coupled with quantization."

** 2023 | H2O: Discovering That Most Tokens in the KV Cache Are Just Along for the Ride

*** What problem it saw

Before H2O, KV cache management was largely ignored — every token had to be kept, because "what if a later attention query needs it?" The result: LLaMA-70B on long context consumed tens of gigabytes for the KV cache alone, more than the model weights themselves.

*** Its solution

H2O made an observation that was both simple and striking: accumulate how many times each token has been attended to over history, and you find the distribution is extremely long-tailed — *20% of tokens absorb more than 80% of total attention*. The remaining 80% are ignored by everyone and can be dropped. That top 20% are called heavy hitters.

In practice: at every generation step, update cumulative attention scores for all history tokens, retain the top K, and evict the rest from cache.

*** Why it works — and what pits it digs

It works because attention is inherently sparse — most queries focus on a small subset of keys. Dropping the tokens nobody looks at is nearly imperceptible to the model.

But it digs three pits that the rest of the river must eventually fill:

1. Recomputing cumulative scores at every step *is not cheap* (SnapKV inherits this)
2. *The same K is used across all layers*, ignoring that sensitivity varies by orders of magnitude between shallow and deep layers (PyramidKV inherits this)
3. Once a token is evicted it is *gone forever* — if the model suddenly needs it later, that context is simply missing (Q-Hitter and ARKV use quantization as a middle tier to mitigate this)

** 2024 Spring | SnapKV: Can We Compute Importance Once, Before Generation Starts?

*** What problem it saw

H2O updates cumulative attention scores at every decoding step. When the cache is already large, this update itself becomes a bottleneck. SnapKV noticed an interesting phenomenon: *how the last few dozen tokens of your prompt "look at" the preceding context reliably predicts how the entire decoding phase will look at it.*

*** Its solution

So: after the prompt arrives, run a one-shot analysis — take a small trailing window (the "observation window") as a probe, examine its attention distribution over the prefix, select top-K, and evict everything else. During decoding, nothing changes.

*** Why it works — and what pits it digs

It works because LLM attention patterns are fairly stable within a conversation, and the prompt tail's "field of view" genuinely predicts subsequent behavior.

New pit: *the price of prediction is rigidity*. Once decoding begins and the topic shifts, newly generated tokens may need to refer to a token that was judged "unimportant" during the prompt phase and has already been discarded. SnapKV's core limitation is "decide once, execute forever."

ARKV later absorbs the observation-window idea directly (W=32 in the paper), but replaces "decide once" with "decide continuously."

** 2024 Spring | PyramidKV: Different Layers Have Different Appetites — Why Force the Same Budget?

*** What problem it saw

Both H2O and SnapKV treat all Transformer layers as "the same thing" — push budget K down, every layer keeps K tokens. PyramidKV empirically found that *deep-layer attention is highly concentrated while shallow-layer attention is diffuse*. In other words, deep layers need only a handful of key tokens; shallow layers need more. A uniform cut either wastes budget or hurts accuracy.

*** Its solution

Assign different budgets per layer, arranged in a pyramid shape — larger budgets at the bottom (shallow layers), smaller at the top (deep layers).

*** Why it works — and what pits it digs

It works because different Transformer layers genuinely operate at different levels of abstraction, and "attention concentration" is a real signal.

New pit: the pyramid shape is a *hand-crafted prior* — based on the general observation that deep layers are more concentrated than shallow ones, but for a specific model and task the actual curve may look quite different. Forcing a pyramid may not be optimal. ARKV later replaces this prior with per-layer empirical statistics (entropy / variance / kurtosis).

** 2023–2024 | KIVI / KVQuant: A Different Road — Don't Drop, Just Compress Everything

*** What problem they saw

The H2O family's "drop tokens" approach saves memory but carries a fundamental risk: *evicted tokens never come back*. If halfway through generation the model wants to reference a token that was condemned, that context is gone. KIVI and KVQuant reasoned in the opposite direction: keep all tokens, but store them with fewer bits.

*** Their solutions

- KIVI: 2-bit quantization, separate schemes for K and V, no retraining required
- KVQuant: 3-bit + learned scales + grouped quantization, claims support for 10M-token contexts

*** Why it works — and what pits it digs

It works because information is preserved — every token is still there, just slightly blurred. On short tasks the degradation is nearly lossless.

But ARKV's experiments hit this approach hard: at a 512-token budget on GSM8K math problems, *the pure-quantization baseline achieves accuracy 0.010* — essentially random guessing. The reason is that mathematical reasoning is extremely sensitive to numerical precision; small errors at each step multiply into catastrophe. Moreover, keeping all tokens means *attention computation still scales linearly* — quantization saves storage but not compute.

This branch and the eviction branch must be merged — and that is where Q-Hitter enters.

** 2024 | Q-Hitter: The First to Merge Drop and Compress — But Statically

*** What problem it saw

The most naïve merger would be "run H2O to select important tokens, then quantize them." Q-Hitter empirically showed this is a trap — *quantization corrupts the attention scores themselves*, causing the wrong heavy hitters to be selected; simultaneously, *the tokens H2O selects as important are not necessarily quantization-friendly* (they may be precisely the outlier-heavy ones).

In short: chaining two independently optimal solutions gives 1+1 < 1.

*** Its solution

Design a joint score S = attention importance × quantization friendliness. Only tokens that score high on both are retained; they are then quantized to 4-bit. Tokens that are important but fragile to quantization, or quantization-friendly but unimportant, are both discarded.

*** Why it works — and what pits it digs

It works because it genuinely recognizes that "importance" and "quantization tolerance" are two independent dimensions that must be jointly optimized.

New pits — the exact points ARKV directly attacks:

1. *Quantization friendliness is computed offline* — a static value per layer, calculated before deployment and fixed thereafter. This means a different task or different input can render the table stale.
2. Still a *binary decision*: keep (and quantize) or drop — *there is no three-tier structure of "keep at original precision / keep quantized / evict"*.
3. Maintaining a per-layer sensitivity table couples the method tightly to model architecture, adding complexity.

** 2026 Feb | ARKV: Three States + Online Statistics — The Most Fine-Grained Form on This Branch So Far

*** What problem it saw

ARKV's introduction names two core challenges:

1. *Token importance is layer-sensitive* — the same token may be noise in a shallow layer and critical in a deep one. All prior methods assume cross-layer importance is uniform.
2. *Token quantization tolerance is also heterogeneous* — quantizing a token that should not be quantized causes far more damage than mis-quantizing an unimportant one.

PyramidKV solved half the problem with its prior pyramid (layer heterogeneity); Q-Hitter solved the other half with an offline table (quantization heterogeneity). But neither is sufficiently dynamic or fine-grained.

*** Its solution

Upgrade Q-Hitter's binary decision to a *three-state* classification: each token is either Original (full-precision bf16), Quantized (compressed to fp8), or Evicted (dropped).

How is the per-layer budget computed? This is ARKV's cleverest contribution. During the prefill phase it computes three statistics from each layer's attention distribution:

- *Entropy H*: how dispersed is the attention? (high dispersion → every token matters → keep more)
- *Variance V*: how large are the differences among tokens?
- *Kurtosis K*: are the tails of the distribution heavy? (many outliers)

These are combined into an OQ score q_ℓ = H^(1/τ₁) · V^(1/τ₂) · K^(1/τ₃), then normalized to give each layer an OQ ratio ρ_ℓ. High ρ_ℓ → this layer is sensitive → allocate more budget to "original precision"; low ρ_ℓ → allocate more to "quantized," less to original.

During decoding, a lightweight heavy-hitter score S_k = μ_k + γ·σ_k² ranks tokens per layer, and the layer budget splits them into three tiers.

*** Why it works — and what pits remain

Why it works:

- *Three tiers instead of two* — the model gains a "not sure whether to drop" middle option; fp8 preserves secondary information at very low cost
- *Entropy / variance / kurtosis* essentially characterize "how concentrated this layer's attention is" — more faithful to actual data than a pyramid prior
- *14.4% quantization rate* — this figure is telling: ARKV saves memory primarily through eviction (~78%), with quantization accounting for only ~14% and the rest kept at original precision. Quantization is "insurance for semi-important tokens," not the main lever for memory savings

Empirical results: 4× memory compression, ~97% of LongBench accuracy, 86% throughput; GSM8K accuracy 0.789 (base model: 0.791) versus pure quantization at 0.010.

Pits not yet filled:

1. The three temperature parameters τ₁=7.774, τ₂=5.407, τ₃=5.528 were tuned with Optuna — *they may need re-tuning for a different model*
2. Only *fp8* is supported as a quantization precision — no flexible 2/4/8-bit tiers
3. The decision granularity is *token-level*; it does not extend to channel-level or head-level

These three pits are exactly what the relay papers of 2026 are each chewing on.

* Frontier Extensions

ARKV was posted in February 2026. In the months immediately before and after, at least four papers continued pushing the same problem from different angles.

** 2026 Feb | KVTuner (arXiv 2502.04420)

Frames per-layer sensitivity estimation as an *offline Pareto search* — sacrificing online adaptability in exchange for a more accurate inter-layer precision allocation. A direct contrast with ARKV: ARKV is "lightweight online statistics," KVTuner is "heavyweight offline optimality." Both approaches have their market.

** 2026 Feb | "Don't Waste Bits!" (arXiv 2604.04722)

Extends ARKV's "two precision tiers (bf16 / fp8)" to *four tiers: {2-bit, 4-bit, 8-bit, FP16}*, using a small learned controller (conditioned on token frequency / quality score / attention variance / entropy) to select the bit-width. The design is explicitly inspired by Huffman coding — high precision for important tokens, low precision for unimportant ones, with proportions determined by the data.

It directly inherits ARKV's attention-statistic ideas (entropy + variance) but refines the "three states" into a "continuous bit allocation."

** 2026 Dec | MixKVQ (arXiv 2512.19206)

Pushes decision granularity from *token-level down to channel-level*. The observation is that outlier channels exist within the key cache — it is not the entire token that is hard to quantize, but specific dimensions within it. MixKVQ also brings the query into the picture: whichever channels matter for the current query receive higher precision.

This extends ARKV's "token importance" idea into a new dimension — the channel dimension.

** 2026 | PM-KVQ (OpenReview)

A specialized variant targeting *long-CoT reasoning models* (DeepSeek-R1 and similar): block-wise (rather than layer-wise) precision allocation, combined with positional interpolation calibration. Outperforms the state of the art by 8% on 7B–70B long-CoT LLMs.

Can be understood as a task-specialized fusion of PyramidKV and ARKV for reasoning-model scenarios.

* Insights

Reading this river, three genuinely significant shifts emerge — ones that matter far more than "yet another KV cache paper":

** I. Granularity Is Sinking

#+begin_example
  Global K -->  Layer K -->  Token level -->  Channel level
  (H2O)      (PyramidKV)      (ARKV)          (MixKVQ)
#+end_example

Each level down adds one more dimension of modeled heterogeneity, and savings climb accordingly. But *the cost of online decision-making climbs with it*. ARKV's use of three moments (entropy / variance / kurtosis) to compute OQ ratios is effectively finding a sweet spot between "fine-grained enough" and "cheap enough."

** II. The Boundary Between "Drop" and "Compress" Is Dissolving

In the H2O era, eviction and quantization were two independent branches doing their own thing. Q-Hitter first acknowledged that they must be jointly optimized. By ARKV, "drop / compress / keep" has become *three sample points on a continuous spectrum*. In "Don't Waste Bits!" that spectrum becomes a fully continuous bit allocation.

*The ultimate form of the next step: for every token, every channel, every timestep, independently decide a precision value between 0 and 16 bits.* That value will be computed in real time by a lightweight controller.

** III. Attention Statistics Are Becoming the Interface for "Model Self-Awareness"

What is genuinely deep about ARKV is this: it trains nothing, changes no architecture — *using only three scalar statistics derived from the attention matrices the model itself produces* — and from those infers the sensitivity of every layer. This is a kind of built-in health monitor for the model.

This idea is far broader than KV cache management. *Any decision about "which parameters / activations / layers matter more"* — pruning, inference routing, expert selection — can read signals from the higher-order moments of attention without depending on additional calibration data or a training phase.

The most likely next directions:

1. Make ARKV's "statistics → precision allocation" into a *learnable module* (already started: "Don't Waste Bits!")
2. Fuse "online decisions" with hardware kernels — currently ARKV's throughput is 86%, meaning decision overhead still costs 14%
3. Apply to MoE models — expert selection is fundamentally the same problem as "which parameters matter right now," structurally isomorphic to KV cache management

* Takeaways

This river offers several concrete lessons about how to find problems worth solving:

1. *Do not solve compound problems by chaining two independent stages.* Before Q-Hitter, everyone did "eviction first, then quantization" because both subproblems had mature solutions. But optimizing them separately gives 1+1 < 1. Real progress came from acknowledging that they *corrupt each other* and must be jointly modeled.
2. *Intermediate states are often gold mines.* ARKV's biggest design innovation — moving from binary "keep/drop" to ternary "keep/compress/drop" — looks trivially obvious in hindsight, yet the field spent three years on the binary version without anyone adding that middle tier. Whenever you see a field stuck in a binary debate, ask: "Is there a 1.5 option?"
3. *Hand-crafted distribution shapes (pyramid, linear, exponential) will always be displaced by data-driven alternatives.* PyramidKV assumes deep layers need smaller budgets — broadly correct. ARKV directly measures the true statistics of each layer — more accurate. Any "allocate according to shape X" scheme is waiting to be replaced by "allocate according to measured data."
4. *Lightweight statistics beat heavy learned modules.* ARKV trains no retaining head (unlike Locret) and maintains no learned scale (unlike KVQuant) — it just computes three scalar statistics. In online decision settings, "fast to compute" beats "accurate to compute," because decision overhead directly eats into the savings.
5. *The most convincing experiments are not averages but cliff-edge contrasts.* ARKV's 0.789 vs. pure quantization's 0.010 on GSM8K at budget 512 — that single number is more impactful than all the ~97% LongBench results combined. Finding the scenario where prior methods *fall off a cliff* is the most effective way to motivate a paper's contribution.

This means you can take any one of the five points above and apply it to whatever domain you are working in: Is your problem being solved by two chained independent stages? Does your method implicitly assume some prior distribution shape? Does your experiment contain a scenario where the competition falls off a cliff? If the answer to any of these is no, there is a potential next paper waiting.
