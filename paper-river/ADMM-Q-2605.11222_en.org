* The River of the Problem

A single neural-network layer has a hundred million floating-point weights, and you must compress each one into whichever of 8 integers fits inside 3 bits. Which 8? Which weight snaps to which? And after snapping, can the layer's output still match the original?

This is a *discrete combinatorial problem* — every weight must land on exactly one point of a grid, there are hundreds of millions of weights per layer, and the search space is astronomical. At the same time it is a *continuous optimization problem* — you want to minimize the reconstruction error |XW − X·Ŵ|², and the objective is a smooth quadratic.

For over thirty years, everyone has been grinding on the same question: how do you use smooth second-order information (the Hessian) to guide discrete rounding decisions? Every step forward is another bite out of the same bone.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1989 | OBD: Use Second Derivatives to Tell You Which Weights Can Be Cut

*** What problem did it see?

Neural networks had just experienced their first revival in 1989. After training a network, practitioners noticed that many weights seemed "useless" — but *seeming* useless is not a mathematical criterion. Pruning by gut feeling caused accuracy to drop sharply.

What was needed was an *objective*, mathematically grounded criterion that identifies which weight, when removed, causes the least increase in loss.

*** Its solution

LeCun reasoned: if the loss L is already near a local minimum after training, then changing weight w_i by a small amount δw_i produces almost no first-order effect (the gradient is near zero), so the change in loss is dominated by the second-order term:

δL ≈ (1/2) · H_ii · (δw_i)²

Pruning a weight is equivalent to setting δw_i = −w_i, so the "loss increase from pruning it" is:

saliency_i = (1/2) · H_ii · w_i²

Sort by saliency; prune the batch with the lowest scores.

*** Why it works — and what pit it digs

The second derivative H_ii measures how *sensitive* the loss is to that particular weight — a weight that is both insensitive and small is genuinely safe to remove.

But OBD makes a lazy assumption: *the Hessian is diagonal*, meaning every weight's effect on the loss is independent of every other weight's. This is obviously wrong — weights in a network are strongly coupled. After pruning w_3, a small adjustment to w_7 might largely recover the loss. OBD ignores this entirely.

The next paper arrives to clean this up.

** 1992 | OBS: When You Cut One Weight, Compensate All the Others

*** What problem did it see?

Assuming a diagonal Hessian amounts to flattening the full coupling graph into a string of independent points. In reality, after removing one weight, *letting the remaining weights move* can recover a large fraction of the lost accuracy. OBD misses this compensation entirely.

*** Its solution

Hassibi & Stork bring in the full inverse Hessian H⁻¹. The problem becomes:

  Under the constraint that w_q is pruned (w_q = 0), how should the remaining weights move to minimize δL?

A Lagrangian derivation yields a closed-form solution:

  δw = −(w_q / [H⁻¹]_{qq}) · H⁻¹_{:,q}

Meaning: when weight q is removed, all other weights are adjusted along the q-th column of H⁻¹, with magnitude determined by w_q and [H⁻¹]_{qq}. Pruning and compensation happen in one shot.

*** Why it works — and what pit it digs

This is the *locally optimal decision* given full second-order information — if you must prune this one weight, nothing beats this compensation. OBD is simply the diagonal approximation of OBS.

Remaining holes:
1. Only one weight is pruned at a time. After each pruning step, H⁻¹ must be recomputed or rank-1 updated: O(d²) per step, O(d³) overall. Fine on the networks with tens of thousands of parameters common in 1992; completely infeasible at millions or billions.
2. The framework still assumes a binary prune/keep decision. Extending it to "snap to whichever grid point is closest" (quantization) requires a fresh derivation.

Both problems sat unresolved for thirty years.

** 2022.08 | OBC: Rebuild the OBS Framework on Layer-wise Reconstruction Error, with Quantization Support

*** What problem did it see?

OBS's formula was derived against *the entire network's loss* — meaning every time one weight is pruned, the Hessian must be recomputed globally. That is completely impractical for large models.

But Frantar & Alistarh noticed: in practice, LLM compression is done *layer by layer* — feed a small calibration dataset through one layer, and minimize the gap between the quantized layer's output and the original output. This is an independent, much smaller optimization problem:

  min ‖X·Ŵ − X·W‖_F²,  s.t.  W ∈ Q

Under this *layer-wise reconstruction* sub-problem, the Hessian is simply H = XᵀX — independent of the full network loss, fixed, small, and cacheable. The OBS logic can then be applied directly.

*** Its solution

Re-derive the OBS "prune one weight + compensate" formula for this new objective, and simultaneously extend "prune to zero" to "snap to the nearest legal grid point" — giving a single unified framework for both pruning and quantization.

*** Why it works — and what pit it digs

Decomposing the global problem into independent layer-wise sub-problems is the key complexity breakthrough: each layer's Hessian is only n×n (where n is the layer's input dimension), cached once and reused throughout. OBC can therefore scale to hundreds of millions of parameters.

Remaining hole: still processes one weight at a time — which is O(n³) per layer. Once layer widths reach LLM scale (n in the thousands to tens of thousands), it is again too slow.

** 2022.10 | GPTQ: Per-column Processing + Lazy Updates + Cholesky, Scaling to 175B

*** What problem did it see?

The OBC framework was correct, but there was one last engineering mile to close — in a billion- or hundred-billion-parameter LLM, a single layer has tens of millions of weights. Walking through them one by one under OBC would take days.

*** Its solution

Three engineering optimizations stacked together:

1. *Per-column processing*: instead of one weight at a time, quantize an entire column of weights together using the OBS formula, then distribute the column's error to all columns to the right that have not yet been processed.
2. *Lazy batched updates*: accumulate a batch of column updates, then flush them to the remaining weights all at once — fewer memory round-trips.
3. *Cholesky rewrite*: replace the incremental updates to H⁻¹ with a one-time precomputation of the Cholesky factor — numerically stable and free of redundant computation.

Result: a 175B-parameter model can be 4-bit quantized in a few hours with minimal accuracy loss. GPTQ became the default baseline for LLM post-training quantization.

*** Why it works — and what pit it digs

GPTQ is an engineering packaging of OBC — the same second-order compensation idea, but with Cholesky and column-level batching that reduce the constant factor enough to be practical.

The remaining holes — and these are the ones ADMM-Q is actually here to fix:

1. *Column-order sensitivity*: which column is processed first matters. GPTQ defaults to left-to-right with no principled justification.
2. *Error accumulates along columns*: quantizing one column spreads error to the right; later columns absorb this extra error and then spread even more; by the last columns, accumulated error is large.
3. *Only a one-step lookahead*: when GPTQ quantizes a column, it assumes all columns to the right will remain at their original values. But those columns will also be quantized — so the decision is made under a false premise.

Below 4 bits (W3, W2), these defects compound: GPTQ's perplexity degrades sharply at 3 bits and becomes essentially unusable at 2 bits.

** 2026.05 | ADMM-Q: Discard the Greedy Pass; Optimize All Weights Together

*** What problem did it see?

From OBD through GPTQ, every Hessian-based quantization method over 30 years has shared one *structural preference*: move only a small subset of weights at a time (one, one column, one block), treating everything else as a fixed background.

That is *greedy*. The cost of greediness is exactly what GPTQ's failures expose: order sensitivity, error accumulation, and an inability to look ahead.

But the layer-wise quantization problem itself —

  min Tr((W − Ŵ)ᵀ H (W − Ŵ))  s.t.  W ∈ Q

— does not require a greedy solution. It asks for *all weights jointly* to find the point on the grid that is closest in the Hessian-weighted sense.

ADMM-Q asks: can we use a *joint* optimizer that updates all weights simultaneously?

*** Its solution

This is exactly the kind of problem ADMM (Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers) was built for — it excels at separating the "smooth continuous part" of an objective from "ugly discrete constraints."

Introduce an auxiliary twin variable Z, write the constraint W ∈ Q as W = Z with Z ∈ Q:

  min  (1/2) Tr((W − Ŵ)ᵀ H (W − Ŵ))
  s.t. W = Z,  Z ∈ Q

ADMM then alternates between three steps:

1. *W update (continuous)*: solve a Hessian-weighted least-squares problem in W — closed-form solution, all weights updated simultaneously, no ordering.
2. *Z update (discrete)*: project W onto the grid (each element independently rounded to the nearest legal point).
3. *Dual variable update*: accumulate the discrepancy between W and Z; in the next round, pull W toward Z more aggressively.

A penalty schedule gradually increases the coefficient ρ, tightening the constraint from soft to hard until W converges to the grid.

Engineering additions:
- *Diagonal preconditioning*: LLM activations have outlier channels, making the Hessian ill-conditioned. Rescale by Diag(H)^{−1/2} to keep the continuous W update numerically stable.
- *Grid refresh*: during iteration, refit scale/zero-point from the current W so the quantization grid tracks the evolving weight distribution.
- *Local-search refinement*: after ADMM converges, run one pass of discrete local search — check whether moving any weight one grid step in either direction further reduces the objective. A closed-form cost formula makes this step cheap.
- *Convergence proof*: extend the ADMM convergence result of Meng et al. 2024 (proved for sparsity constraints) to quantization constraints (Theorem 1).

*** Why it works — and what pits remain

The difference in one sentence: *GPTQ solves each local subproblem to optimality in a fixed order; ADMM-Q moves all weights simultaneously toward the global optimum, a little at a time, while the discrete constraint is gradually tightened.*

Above 4 bits the two methods are comparable — the quantization grid is dense enough that a greedy pass finds a good solution. Below 3 bits the gap opens wide: on Qwen3-8B at W3A16, GPTQ reaches perplexity 12.85 while ADMM-Q reaches 10.06; at the extreme W2A4KV4 setting, GPTQ collapses to 66.11 while ADMM-Q holds at 19.42.

A particularly appealing property is that ADMM-Q is *orthogonal* — it replaces only the quantizer component and leaves everything else untouched. SmoothQuant's activation migration, SpinQuant's rotation, AWQ's channel scaling — all continue to work. Pull out GPTQ, plug in ADMM-Q.

Remaining new holes:
1. ADMM-Q is slower than GPTQ (multiple iterations rather than a single forward pass). Acceptable for Qwen3-8B scale; cost grows for larger models.
2. The ρ schedule, ADMM iteration count, and local-search neighborhood are additional hyperparameters whose automatic tuning remains unsolved.
3. The Hessian is still assumed to be the layer-local X^T X — cross-layer joint optimization has not been attempted.

* Frontier Extensions

ADMM-Q was posted to arXiv on 2026-05-11, fewer than three weeks ago. No public direct follow-ups exist yet.

But the most likely directions forward are already visible from the remaining holes:

- *Cross-layer ADMM*: the current Hessian is the single-layer X^T X, with no account of how quantization error propagates between layers. Lifting the layer-wise local problem to a block-wise or even global problem is the obvious next step.
- *Integration with KV cache quantization*: in extreme settings such as W2A4KV4, the KV cache is also quantized. Currently each is handled independently; in principle both could live inside the same ADMM framework.
- *Quantization-aware training (QAT) integration*: ADMM-Q is pure PTQ. Embedding the ADMM loop inside the training cycle — coupling weight training and quantization updates — could push further gains at sub-2-bit precision.

* Insight

*The real change is not "a better optimization algorithm" — it is abandoning a 30-year implicit assumption: greedy decision-making.*

OBD → OBS → OBC → GPTQ each improved *the quality of a single local step* — a more accurate Hessian, added compensation, lazy updates, Cholesky. But everyone silently accepted that "move a small subset of weights at a time" is the natural way — initially because the OBS formula only closes for one weight at a time (a hard computational constraint), later out of sheer inertia.

GPTQ polished greediness to its limit, and therefore was the first to hit the wall — its sub-4-bit collapse revealed a fundamental flaw in the greedy strategy itself, not in any particular component.

ADMM-Q's contribution is not "one more grid-point refinement." It is using ADMM as a scalpel to separate "continuous optimization" from "discrete projection" — letting greediness exit, and letting *global soft-to-hard alternating approximation* take over.

The same pattern is playing out elsewhere in compression: in the sparsity domain, ADMM already defeated greedy methods like SparseGPT and Wanda (the Meng et al. 2024 line cited in the paper). Quantization was simply late to the party — discrete projection onto a non-zero grid is harder than projecting onto zero (pruning), so nobody dared try it until now.

*The next conceptual shift is likely: abandon the layer-wise assumption.* Layer-wise reconstruction was an engineering compromise introduced in the OBC era (to make the Hessian cacheable). Once greediness is broken, the next target is layer independence.

* Takeaways

1. *When you see phrases like "per-column processing" or "one-at-a-time," first ask: does the problem truly require sequential treatment, or was the whole thing just computationally infeasible back then?* Thirty years of greedy Hessian-based quantization fall into the latter category. Once a different optimizer (ADMM) makes "move all variables simultaneously" affordable, the old engineering compromise becomes the new attack surface.
2. *Orthogonality is a force multiplier.* ADMM-Q never touches the SmoothQuant/SpinQuant/AWQ layers that handle activation migration and rotation — it only swaps the quantizer kernel. That means it can be dropped in, it inherits all upstream work, and it runs inside vLLM with the same throughput as GPTQ. Replace one component; the whole pipeline benefits.
3. *The assumption that got broken is the title of the next paper.* For four years after GPTQ, "layer-wise + greedy" was accepted as fact, and sub-4-bit collapse was attributed to the fundamental difficulty of quantization. The moment ADMM-Q topples greediness, sub-4-bit becomes usable. Whatever is currently accepted as fact — such as "layer-wise independence" — is the next target.
4. *This means:* if you are working on any research line that treats greedy local decisions as common sense, browse the toolbox of ADMM, operator splitting, and proximal methods. They were designed precisely for "smooth + discrete" problems, and they specialize in dismantling greedy approaches.
