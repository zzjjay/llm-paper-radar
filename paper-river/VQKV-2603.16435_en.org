* The Problem River

As LLM context windows grow longer, every additional token demands more KV cache
in GPU memory. By the time the context reaches 100k tokens, the cache alone can
exhaust an entire A100 — the model hasn't even started computing, yet memory is
already gone.

The problem is simple to state: can we compress this cache without making the
model dumber?

It sounds straightforward. But over three years of effort, researchers found:

- Push for high compression ratios, and model quality drops.
- Hold on to model quality, and you can't compress much.
- Add calibration training, and you've broken the "plug-and-play" promise.

Each generation watched its predecessors hit one wall, then threw itself against
a different one. It took six generations before VQKV said: "You've all been
pushing in the wrong direction — the question is not how much to drop or how
many bits to use; it's that *you've always been treating each dimension
independently*."

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2023.09 | StreamingLLM: Discard the Middle, Keep Head and Tail

*** What Problem It Saw

The earliest response to KV cache overflow was sliding-window attention:
keep only the most recent N tokens and throw away the rest.

Intuitively appealing — humans only remember what was said recently, right?

But the moment the model drops the very first token, performance collapses
catastrophically. Perplexity shoots from 5 to 5000; the model babbles nonsense.

Why? Nobody knew. Answering that question was StreamingLLM's contribution.

*** Its Solution

The authors made a very concrete observation: in the attention matrix, *the
first token always receives disproportionately high attention*, regardless of
its content. Even a meaningless "<bos>" attracts a large share of attention from
every subsequent token.

They named this the "attention sink." The mechanism: softmax forces scores to
sum to 1, so the model must dump excess attention somewhere — the first token
becomes that dumping ground.

The fix is a single sentence: *never touch the first few tokens*; add a sliding
window to retain the most recent ones; discard everything in between.

*** Why It Works (and What New Trap It Laid)

It works because it uncovered an ignored invariant: the mathematical structure
of the attention mechanism requires a "shock absorber." Preserving those few
tokens keeps the model functional.

But the trap is obvious: is the middle segment truly useless? Clearly not. If a
critical fact is buried there (e.g., "the answer is 42"), a position-based cut
destroys it.

*That gap left room for the next paper: can we select by importance rather than
position?*

** 2023.06 | H2O: Find the "Heavy Hitter" Tokens

*** What Problem It Saw

StreamingLLM deletes by position — essentially admitting "we don't know what
matters, so we'll bet on the beginning and the end."

But attention maps give us an explicit signal: which tokens receive heavy
attention and which are consistently ignored. Can that signal be used?

*** Its Solution

H2O stands for "Heavy Hitter Oracle." The idea is almost counterintuitively
simple: *accumulate the attention score each token has received over its
lifetime; keep the high-scorers, drop the rest.*

Think of it as the inverse of LRU: not "keep what was accessed most recently"
but "keep what has been referenced the most."

*** Why It Works (and What New Trap It Laid)

It works because attention distributions are highly sparse — the vast majority
of tokens receive negligible attention most of the time, so dropping them is
essentially lossless; a small minority are referenced repeatedly and truly need
to be kept.

But H2O leaves two traps:

1. The cumulative score must be maintained step-by-step during inference,
   introducing extra computation and memory overhead.
2. The score is a "backward-looking prediction" — historically important does
   not guarantee future importance.

*The next paper needed to be cheaper and more accurate — ideally selecting
everything to keep in a single shot.*

** 2024.04 | SnapKV: Use the Prompt's Tail to Predict What to Keep

*** What Problem It Saw

H2O makes eviction decisions at every step, spreading overhead across the entire
decode phase. Its cumulative scores are also prone to early-context bias.

SnapKV's authors noticed a pattern: *the attention pattern of the last few dozen
prompt tokens already strongly indicates which positions the model will attend to
during generation.*

In other words, at the moment prefill ends, the model already "knows internally"
which tokens are critical — we just need to read that signal out.

*** Its Solution

Take an "observation window" from the end of the prompt (e.g., the last 32
tokens), look at where their attention flows, keep the KV entries at those
positions, and discard everything else — all in one shot.

Take a "snapshot" once prefill is done; make no further decisions throughout
decode.

*** Why It Works (and What New Trap It Laid)

It works because of a statistical regularity: the attention of the final few
tokens is a *strong prior* for the attention pattern during generation.
One-shot selection is both more accurate and has zero runtime overhead.

But at its core, SnapKV is still *evicting tokens*. If a dropped token suddenly
becomes relevant during generation, it is gone for good — this is precisely what
VQKV later criticizes as "irreversible information loss."

*The next paper needed to take a different road: don't delete, just compress.*

** 2024.02 | KIVI: Stop Deleting — Chop Every Number Down to 2-bit

*** What Problem It Saw

The fundamental problem with the eviction path is irreversibility: once
something is thrown away, it cannot be recovered.

What if we kept all tokens but stopped storing each KV vector in 16-bit float,
using 2-bit integers instead? No token is lost, and memory shrinks 8×.

*** Its Solution

KIVI applies *scalar quantization*: each floating-point number is quantized
independently to a 2-bit integer.

A notable finding from careful analysis: *Key cache quantizes better along the
channel dimension* (each channel gets its own scale/zero-point), while *Value
cache quantizes better along the token dimension.* This asymmetric design is
KIVI's core contribution.

*** Why It Works (and What New Trap It Laid)

It works because 2-bit resolution is sufficient to capture the dominant
variation, and the approach is training-free and plug-and-play.

But the trap is clear: *each float is processed independently.* A 128-dimensional
vector's 128 values are correlated — they are not 128 independent scalars but a
single geometric entity. Scalar quantization discards that correlation entirely.

And 2-bit is a hard floor: push to 1-bit and the model collapses.

*The next paper shifted axes: instead of cutting precision per scalar, compress
along the feature dimension.*

** 2024.07 | Palu: Low-Rank Decomposition Along the Feature Dimension

*** What Problem It Saw

KIVI compresses each number to 2-bit and stops there. Could the *dimension* axis
offer further savings?

Observation: a KV vector may be 128-dimensional, but its *distribution* might
concentrate in a much lower-dimensional subspace. Like having 1,000 photos, each
with one megapixel, but all photos are essentially "cats" — the intrinsic
dimensionality is far below one million.

*** Its Solution

Palu applies low-rank decomposition: project KV into a low-dimensional space for
storage and project back at compute time.

This shifts focus from "compress the precision of individual numbers" to
"compress the dimensionality of entire vectors."

*** Why It Works (and What New Trap It Laid)

It works because KV vectors do exhibit low-rank structure, and the compressed
representation stores fewer numbers — each of which can remain high-precision.

But three traps remain:

1. In training-free mode, the approximation error of low-rank projection is hard
   to control — push compression hard and reconstruction quality collapses.
2. Preserving quality requires auxiliary training (the path taken by MLA, CSKV,
   etc.), sacrificing plug-and-play usability.
3. Low-rank decomposition is fundamentally *linear* dimensionality reduction. If
   the KV distribution lies on a curved manifold rather than a linear subspace,
   linear methods cannot capture it.

*The next paper said: you're all doing this linearly and independently. Try a
codebook lookup instead.*

** 2026.03 | VQKV: Compress Entire Vectors Jointly via a Codebook

*** What Problem It Saw

VQKV surveyed the five prior approaches laid out in a row:

- StreamingLLM / H2O / SnapKV: token eviction — *irreversible information loss*
- KIVI: per-channel scalar quantization — *ignores inter-dimension correlations*,
  2-bit is the floor
- Palu / ASVD: linear low-rank reduction — *requires training or sacrifices accuracy*

Its diagnosis: all these methods treat dimensions independently. *Why not
compress entire vectors jointly?*

Vector Quantization (VQ) has long been proven in speech and image compression:
train a codebook of 1,024 representative vectors; for any new vector, find the
nearest entry and store only its index — *thousands of floats become a handful
of integers.*

*** Its Solution

VQKV uses RSimVQ (Residual SimVQ):

- *Residual structure* (Residual VQ): the first codebook finds the nearest
  representative; the remaining "error" is fed to a second codebook for further
  quantization, and so on — multiple small codebooks combine to express an
  exponentially large set of possible values.
- *SimVQ*: a projection matrix precedes each codebook to stabilize training and
  prevent codebook "collapse" (many codewords never being used).

The concrete procedure:

1. Collect ~10 M KV vectors produced by LLaMA3.1-8B processing OpenWebText;
   train codebooks offline (separate sets for Key and Value).
2. At inference time, during prefill: map each KV vector to its codebook index;
   store only the index and discard the original vector.
3. During decode: look up the required entries from the codebook and reconstruct
   them on the fly, integrated with a custom FlashAttention kernel.

Special handling for RoPE: RoPE rotates Key vectors by position, causing the Key
distribution to vary significantly across positions. The residual structure
naturally absorbs these positional variations across successive codebook levels.

*** Why It Works (and What New Trap It Laid)

It works because it upgrades from "process each dimension independently" to
"process each vector as a whole":

- A 128-dimensional vector → 1 codebook index (log₂(1024) = 10 bits)
- Effective bit-width per dimension: ~0.08 bit — far beyond KIVI's 2-bit floor
- Those 10 bits are *jointly* allocated, preserving inter-dimension correlations

Results on LLaMA3.1-8B: 82.8% cache compression, 98.6% performance retention,
and the freed memory enables 4.3× longer generation.

Traps laid:

1. *Training the codebook requires ~10 M tokens and non-trivial compute* —
   strictly speaking it is not "zero-cost," even though the base model itself is
   untouched.
2. *Codebook lookup and residual reconstruction add compute overhead*, even with
   a custom FlashAttention kernel.
3. *Offline-trained codebooks are model-specific* — switching from LLaMA3.1-8B
   to LLaMA3.2-3B requires retraining.
4. The RoPE handling is effective but justified empirically rather than by a
   formal theoretical guarantee.

* Frontier Extensions

VQKV is a 2026-03 paper; as of today (2026-05) it is only two months old.
No papers widely circulating that directly critique or improve upon VQKV have
been identified yet.

But the following directions are predictable targets:

- *Online codebook learning*: the current codebook is trained offline and then
  frozen. Could it be updated continuously during inference, like K-means? That
  would make it truly zero-cost.
- *Cross-model universal codebooks*: can a single set of codebooks work across
  multiple models, eliminating per-model retraining?
- *VQ + eviction hybrid*: drop entirely unimportant tokens; compress everything
  else with VQ. The two historically separate paths converge for the first time.
- *End-to-end distillation with VQ*: abandon the training-free constraint, fine-
  tune the codebook together with the model, and push fidelity one step further.

* Insight

What is really changing across this lineage is the *gradual elevation of the
"unit of processing"*:

1. *Early 2023*: the unit is the *token*. Question: which tokens should be kept?
2. *Early 2024*: the unit is the *scalar* (individual float). Question: how many
   bits per number?
3. *Late 2024*: the unit is the *low-rank subspace of a vector*. Question: which
   direction to project onto?
4. *2026*: the unit is the *vector itself*. Question: which codeword best
   represents it?

Each step up unlocks richer structure to exploit: relationships among tokens,
relationships among scalars, relationships among dimensions, and finally the
geometric structure of the entire vector distribution.

Each generation discarded the last independence assumption that its predecessors
had quietly held onto.

At a deeper level, this is a journey *from discrete decisions (keep vs. drop) to
continuous representation (lookup)*. Token eviction is a 0/1 decision — lost
information never returns. Scalar quantization maps a continuous value into a
discrete bucket — still lossy. Vector quantization asks "which prototype best
resembles me?" and stores the answer as an index — the loss is absorbed into
reconstruction error, and reconstruction error can be further reduced by
residuals.

Every step converts a hard decision into a soft query.

Where will this go next? My bet: *making the codebook differentiable and
updatable online.* VQKV's codebook is trained offline and then fixed — like a
printed dictionary. The next generation will let the dictionary track the
incoming distribution, or even adapt in real time to the specific task at hand.

* Lessons

Three takeaways from this river for the practice of finding research problems:

1. *Watch for the independence assumption the previous paper quietly preserved.*
   Every generation breaks a simplifying assumption its predecessors never
   realized they were making. StreamingLLM assumed "position = importance." KIVI
   assumed "each scalar is independent." Palu assumed "the distribution lives in
   a linear subspace." VQKV broke the "independence" layer. *To find the next
   paper, ask: what am I implicitly assuming right now?*

2. *Borrowing tools across fields is the fastest path to new papers.* VQ has
   been mature in speech and image processing for a decade. What VQKV did was
   carry it over to LLM KV caches — the tool is old, the application is new, the
   results are real. *Keep an eye on neighboring fields and look for tools your
   own field hasn't adopted yet.*

3. *Compression has no "final answer," only a "temporary upper bound."* Every
   generation pushes the trade-off curve a bit further up and to the right, but
   a new impossible triangle always surfaces. VQKV appears to have solved "high
   compression + high fidelity + training-free," yet its codebook must be trained
   offline and is not portable across models — *the new impossible triangle is
   already waiting there.*

This means you can:

- When you see a "breakthrough," first ask what trade-off it pushed and how far;
- Then ask what new trade-off it introduced;
- That new trade-off is where the next paper lives.
