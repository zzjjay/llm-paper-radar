* The River of Problems

A core paradox: the most natural way to train a small model to mimic a large one is to "align the small model's probability distribution with the large model's at every token position." This is the Knowledge Distillation paradigm Hinton introduced in 2015, and the field has relied on it for a decade.

But a decade later, people found: this "supervise every position" approach is consistently outperformed by "supervise only 20% of positions." Stranger still, *which* 20% you choose, and whether you choose well, matters more than which distillation algorithm you use.

The question is this: supervising every token sounds like a more "complete" training signal — so why is it wasteful? Which tokens genuinely carry "knowledge," and which are just noise? And how do you identify them with a cheap signal that doesn't depend on the teacher?

What makes this hard is the catch-22: you need to judge "which positions are worth learning," but the judgment itself cannot be more expensive than the training it is meant to improve. The simpler the signal, the better — but the simpler it is, the more easily noise corrupts it. Generation after generation of researchers has, at bottom, been answering the same question: *Of everything the teacher says, which sentences should the student actually heed?*

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Hinton-KD: The "Dark Knowledge" Hypothesis — Small Models Can Now Stand on Giants' Shoulders

*** What Problem It Saw

Large models are too expensive to deploy. Simply copying weights is infeasible. But small models trained on one-hot labels discard the information encoded in the teacher's softmax — why a token's probability is 0.01 rather than 0.001. Hinton called this *dark knowledge*.

*** Its Solution

Have the student minimize the KL divergence between its output distribution q_t and the teacher distribution p_t at every token position, combined with cross-entropy in a weighted sum:

#+begin_example
loss = lambda * KL(p_t || q_t) + (1 - lambda) * CE(y_t, q_t)
#+end_example

Computed at every position, treating all sequence positions, all vocabulary entries, and all samples equally. This became the paradigm later called *Full KD*.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Buries New Problems)

The intuition: soft labels from the teacher carry richer inter-class similarity information than hard labels — a continuous, information-dense supervisory signal.

But it buried three problems that the entire subsequent line would spend years excavating:
1. *Waste along the position axis* — most tokens in a sentence are things like "the/a/of," which the student can predict correctly with its eyes closed; computing KL on them is pointless.
2. *Waste along the vocabulary axis* — in a vocabulary of 100K tokens, the vast majority have probabilities around 1e-8; storing and computing KL over them is wasteful.
3. *Waste along the sample axis* — on easy samples, the teacher and student already produce nearly identical distributions, so the supervisory signal is effectively zero.

** 2021 | Wang-SelectiveKD: The First Admission That "Not Every Position Is Worth Learning"

*** What Problem It Saw

The Hinton paradigm assumes all positions are equally important. Wang et al. observed that late in training, the student has already converged well on most positions; continuing KL there only increases gradient noise. The positions that actually need the teacher are the ones where the student is still struggling.

*** Its Solution

Use the *student's own cross-entropy CE(y_t, q_t)* as a ranking signal, select the top-k% highest-CE positions, and apply KL only there. To keep k stable across batches, they introduced GLS (Global Level Selection), which maintains a dynamic threshold across batches.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Buries New Problems)

The intuition: high student CE = "I got this wrong" = "I need the teacher here." This is a zero-cost signal, since CE is already computed as part of the forward loss pass.

The remaining hole: *high student CE does not mean "the teacher can rescue you."* The student may be wrong simply because the problem is inherently hard — for instance, the next token is a rare proper noun, and the teacher is also high-entropy and cannot give a confident answer. Up-weighting such positions pushes the student toward directions the teacher itself is uncertain about.

** 2024 | Zhong-ATKD: A Different Perspective — Does the Teacher Actually Have Something to Teach?

*** What Problem It Saw

Zhong et al. revisited Wang's "rank by student CE" approach and argued that the right signal should be *the teacher's probability on the gold token* — if the teacher itself is uncertain about the correct answer (low p_t(y_t)), then the KL signal at that position is intrinsically noisy.

*** Its Solution

AT-KD scores positions by 1 - p_t(y_t) (the teacher's uncertainty about the correct answer) and splits tokens into "easy/hard" buckets:
- *Easy tokens* (teacher is confident): skip the target-class KL, keep only the non-target distribution alignment
- *Hard tokens* (teacher is also uncertain): strengthen both KL terms, prioritize diversity

This builds on Zhao 2022's *Decoupled KD* — only after DKD decomposes KL into target/non-target terms does AT-KD have the room to apply different sub-terms to different positions.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Buries New Problems)

The intuition: the quality of the teacher's signal varies across positions; you cannot pretend the teacher is equally reliable everywhere.

The remaining hole: *it requires the teacher's logits to compute p_t(y_t)* — meaning that if you want to "save the teacher's compute," the ranking signal itself already requires a full teacher forward pass. Position selection saves no inference cost; it only saves backward gradient compute. Moreover, because it re-weights rather than explicitly selects, it cannot benefit from offline caching.

** 2025 | Three Tributaries Converge: High-Entropy Forks, Class-Axis Sparsity, Sample Difficulty

Three parallel threads emerged this year, each addressing a different pain point left by prior work:

- *Wang et al. 2025 — the high-entropy token hypothesis*: Training logs show that learning is actually driven by a small minority of "fork points" — positions where multiple continuations are plausible and the distribution is a sharply multi-modal. Gradient noise on ordinary tokens exceeds the signal. This gives SE-KD a direct theoretical grounding: *high-entropy positions are where real learning happens.*
- *Anshumann et al. 2025 — RS-KD*: Earlier top-k truncation on the vocabulary axis (Raman 2023, Shum 2024) discards the long tail, producing biased gradients and damaging student calibration. RS-KD replaces truncation with *importance sampling* — draw U classes from the teacher distribution and recover an unbiased estimate via importance weights. This is the first approach to make the *class axis* (vocabulary axis) cacheable.
- *He et al. 2025 — DA-KD*: Uses the *CE ratio* r(t) = CE(y_t, q_t) / CE(y_t, p_t) to measure sample difficulty — a large ratio means "the teacher can do it but the student cannot," which is exactly what should be learned; a small ratio means both are competent, or neither is.

*** The Core Open Question Where These Three Lines Meet

By late 2025, researchers had established:
- Selection along the position axis is viable
- Selection along the class axis is viable
- Selection along the sample axis is viable

But *no one had systematically compared which signal is most reliable*. Student CE / teacher CE / teacher entropy / student entropy / KL / reverse KL / CE ratio — which of these signals is stable? And *no one had combined all three axes* — every paper moved only one lever at a time.

** 2026 | SE-KD / SE-KD_3X: Pulling All Axes Together and Discovering an Overlooked Signal

*** What Problem It Saw

Tavor et al. organized every prior method into a single table (Table 1) and identified two things *everyone had missed*:
1. *Student entropy H(q_t)* — superficially similar to "student CE" but fundamentally different. CE measures "did I get it right"; entropy measures "how uncertain am I." Remarkably, no prior work had used entropy as a position-scoring signal.
2. *No paper had ever performed selection on all three axes simultaneously* — position + class + sample. Every paper moved one lever; no one had tested whether all three levers could be pulled together.

*** Its Solution

A two-step approach:

1. *SE-KD*: Score positions by student entropy H(q_t), select the top-k% (k=20% is the sweet spot in experiments). This signal is more stable than CE / teacher entropy / KL because it requires no ground truth and purely reflects "how confused the student is."

2. *SE-KD_3X*: Combine all three axes —
   - Position axis: student entropy top-20%
   - Class axis: RS-KD (importance sampling, U=64 classes)
   - Sample axis: rank full sequences by their mean student entropy; keep only the top-ℓ% of samples

   Key engineering insight: *cache the teacher's logits offline.* Because the class axis uses sparse sampling, each token stores only 64 sampled (index, prob) pairs, compressing storage from 10,000 TB (Full KD over 100B tokens) to 3.84 TB — a *99.96% reduction.*

*** Why It Works (and Where New Problems Remain)

The intuition: student entropy = a pure measure of "I myself am unsure." It is not contaminated by "can the teacher help" (that was AT-KD's target, at the cost of requiring a teacher forward pass), nor by "is the answer correct" (that was the weakness of Wang 2021's CE).

Empirically, SE-KD alone raises accuracy from 64.4 (Full KD) to 64.8, reduces PPL from 7.3 to 6.9, and improves IFEval from 20.5 to 21.4 — *while supervising only 20% of positions.*

Remaining open problems:
- On 80M-token training, SE-KD_3X slightly hurts calibration (ECE rises from 27.3 to 27.9)
- On small task-specific datasets (e.g., GSM8K), pure position selection loses points; sample selection must accompany it to remain stable
- Not yet validated on larger teacher-student pairs (e.g., 70B → 8B) or long-context settings

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026, this paper (released February 2026) represents the latest advance along this line. The code is open-sourced at github.com/almogtavor/SE-KD3x, but no papers citing and extending it have appeared yet.

Plausible next directions (several flagged by the authors in their Limitations section):
- *Feature axis* — prior work (Romero 2015 FitNets, Jiao 2020 TinyBERT) aligned hidden states and attention maps, but how that axis interacts with position/class/sample selection has not been systematically studied
- Extending "student-entropy selection" to *on-policy distillation* (Agarwal 2024), where the student generates its own trajectories and the entropy signal changes dynamically
- Whether, across multiple epochs and larger datasets, the 80/20 split can be pushed further toward 95/5

* Insights

Reading this entire line, the true shift is that *the concept of "distillation" itself has moved from "signal transmission" to "signal sampling."*

- The Hinton generation assumed: the supervisory signal is dense, continuous, and more is always better
- The Wang generation discovered: the supervisory signal has *cardinality* — most of it is redundant, and the critical signal is concentrated in a minority of positions
- The SE-KD generation went one step further: the cheapest detector for the critical signal is the student's own internal state (entropy); no full teacher forward pass required

The deeper conceptual shift is that "distillation" and "pruning" are beginning to converge — *you can not only distill a smaller model, you can also distill a sparser training signal.* SE-KD_3X pushes this sparsification across three orthogonal dimensions simultaneously, which amounts to saying: *the teacher is not a continuous function; the teacher is a sparse, compressible, cacheable lookup table.* Once you accept that, the entire cost structure of distillation flips.

Most likely next directions:
1. *Learning the selection signal itself* — every current method uses a fixed metric (CE / entropy / KL ratio); the next step may be to *meta-learn* a selection network
2. *Integration with RLHF / on-policy training* — in on-policy distillation, samples are generated dynamically, turning "which samples/positions should be supervised" into an RL problem
3. *Cross-task cache reuse* — since teacher logits can be sparsely cached, *a single 100B teacher cache could train dozens of students on different tasks*, driving the marginal cost of distillation toward zero

* Takeaways

The biggest lesson this line offers for doing research: *don't rush to add things — first ask whether you can remove things.*

The real decade-long progress in KD was not the invention of new loss functions; it was the acknowledgment that the original loss function was doing useless work at most positions. Every paper with a genuine breakthrough took Full KD along one dimension, *cut away* part of it, and then showed that performance held or improved. This is a counterintuitive research path — *subtraction is worth more than addition.*

Practical takeaways:
- If you are working on training efficiency or inference efficiency, *first map out "which computations can be sparsified without hurting performance,"* then figure out "what signal to use for the sparsification." SE-KD's Table 1 is the template.
- If you are tuning a distillation pipeline, *stop defaulting to Full KD.* Student entropy top-20% is a one-line code change that can cut wall-clock time by 70%.
- If you are thinking about the value of LLM training data — *student entropy can also be used to select data.* Not just at the token level: the mean entropy over an entire sequence can rank training samples. This line and data selection are two sides of the same problem.

More broadly: *when a paradigm has spent ten years accumulating additions, the next breakthrough usually comes from someone willing to subtract.*
