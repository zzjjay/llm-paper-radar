* The Protagonist: A "Greedy Mistake" Made Over and Over Again

Compressing a large model from 16 bits down to 3–4 bits boils down to one sentence: replace every weight with the "nearest" discrete value while keeping the network's output as unchanged as possible.

It sounds like rounding, but it is actually a combinatorial optimization problem. You have millions of weights to fix simultaneously, and the best choice for each weight affects the optimal choice for every weight that follows. Exhaustive search is impossible, so every method of the past few years has used a different form of approximation to sidestep this NP-hard problem.

But all of these approximations share the same scent: greed. Fix one weight at a time, never look back, and let the accumulated error be absorbed by whatever comes next. The error snowballs, and by 3 bits things fall apart.

This river traces how a group of researchers gradually saw the true cost of "greediness" — and eventually found the cure in pure mathematics.

* Forward Narrative: How the Problem Grew Into What It Is Today

** First Stop: GPTQ — Cutting NP-Hard Down to "Sequential Compensation"

In 2023, Frantar et al. tackled the most immediate obstacle: you cannot simultaneously quantize all the weights of a 4096×4096 matrix to the global optimum — that is NP-hard.

They took a step back: do it column by column instead.

Concretely: arrange the columns of the weight matrix in some order. Quantize the first column and record the error; use that error to update every remaining un-quantized column, so they "know in advance" how much error has been committed and can absorb it when their turn comes. Then quantize the second column, update the rest, then the third, and so on to the end.

Mathematically this is equivalent to maintaining a Cholesky decomposition of the Hessian matrix and "peeling off" the corresponding row/column after each column is quantized.

This approach was remarkably effective. Compressing from 16-bit to 4-bit caused almost no accuracy drop. Open-source, practical, fast — every subsequent piece of work used it as the starting point.

But it planted three time bombs:

1. *It is greedy.* It sees only one column at a time and never revisits. Earlier small errors are "compensated" by later columns, but compensation has a ceiling.
2. *It requires matrix inversion.* The Hessian inverse is numerically unstable, especially at low bit-widths on ill-conditioned matrices.
3. *It produces only one solution.* Given a quantization order, the result is deterministic. There is no second chance.

Bomb number 3 is harmless at 4 bits. At 3 bits, it explodes.

** Second Stop: AWQ — Stepping Outside the Local Per-Weight View

In 2024, Lin et al. looked at GPTQ and felt the entire framework was too "weight-centric." They asked: are all weights equally important?

They are not. Activation distributions are uneven — a small number of channels carry very large activations while the vast majority are small. The quantization error on weights corresponding to those large-activation channels gets amplified.

AWQ's solution is not "a better quantization algorithm" — it reframes the problem entirely. Before quantizing, they multiply the important weight channels by a scale factor and divide the corresponding activations by the same scale (mathematically equivalent), then quantize. This widens the effective range of the important weights so their quantization error shrinks.

This was the first time anyone stepped outside the local question of "how to round each weight better" and treated "which weights deserve protection" as an independent design variable.

But AWQ stumbled into its own trap: it selects scales based on full-precision activations. In actual inference, earlier layers have already been quantized and their activations have drifted — this is the *train-run mismatch*.

** Third Stop: QuIP — If the Distribution Is Unfriendly, Change the Distribution

QuIP (Chee et al., 2024) went further. AWQ was still bargaining inside the original weight space; QuIP simply declared that the original weight distribution is inherently hostile to quantization (heavy tails, large outliers), so it applies a random orthogonal rotation to both weights and activations to make them more "spherical" before quantizing.

Mathematically this is called *incoherence processing* — a random orthogonal matrix spreads the energy of the weight matrix more uniformly across entries. After rotation, the maximum values shrink, the quantization grid is used more efficiently, and 3-bit or even 2-bit quantization becomes feasible.

QuIP's results on small models are striking. But it has a stability problem: the choice of rotation matrix is sensitive, and on certain tasks the method collapses completely (in OJBKQ's experiments, QuIP scores zero on MBPP for Qwen).

At this point, the PTQ river splits for the first time:
- The GPTQ line: do column-wise optimization better within the original space
- The QuIP line: transform the space before quantizing
- The AWQ line: preserve accuracy for the important weights

Each branch's limitations were also becoming increasingly apparent.

** Fourth Stop: OmniQuant and PoTPTQ — Just Train It

OmniQuant (2024) and PoTPTQ (2025) chose a fourth path: since PTQ involves so many hand-designed parameters (scale, shift, clipping boundaries), why not treat them as learnable parameters and gradient-descend on calibration data?

The results are good, but the cost is clear: this is no longer "post-training" — it is lightweight fine-tuning. It requires data, GPU time, and hyperparameter tuning.

OJBKQ explicitly excludes OmniQuant from its experimental comparisons, on the grounds that "it requires training" — making the comparison unfair.

** Fifth Stop: Chen et al. (2025) — Giving the Problem Its True Name

In 2025, something critical and easy to overlook happened: Chen et al. published an analysis of GPTQ's geometric properties and showed that:

*GPTQ is, in essence, Babai's nearest-plane algorithm applied to a lattice.*

The Babai algorithm is a classical 1986 tool from cryptography for solving CVP (the Closest Vector Problem). Given a lattice and a target point, it greedily projects the point onto the nearest lattice hyperplane one dimension at a time, producing an approximate solution called the *Babai point*.

Chen et al.'s insight is that GPTQ's column-by-column quantization — maintaining error compensation via triangular decomposition — is structurally isomorphic to Babai's algorithm. The quantization scheme GPTQ produces is exactly the Babai point of the corresponding lattice.

This paper offered no new method, but it renamed the entire field of PTQ. Once you know "I am doing lattice decoding," decades of tools from cryptography and coding theory suddenly open up to you.

This is the prerequisite that makes OJBKQ possible.

** Sixth Stop: QEP — Seeing the "Over-Penalization" of Cross-Layer Error Accumulation

QEP (Arai & Ichikawa, 2025) noticed something else: what objective should you use to judge whether a quantization scheme is good?

The most intuitive approach (used by GPTQ): use the "actual runtime activations" of the current layer — that is, the activations produced by earlier layers that have already been quantized and have drifted — to compute the error. This objective matches actual inference; call it *runtime-consistent*.

An alternative (implicitly used by AWQ): compute errors against the full-precision activations, on the grounds that error measurement should be absolute and not contaminated by upstream quantization errors.

QEP pointed out that both objectives are problematic. The runtime objective is too sensitive to earlier layers' errors; the full-precision objective "over-penalizes" deeper layers — they are forever chasing a clean reference that is impossible to reach.

QEP did not fully resolve this tension, but it made the binary opposition explicit.

** Seventh Stop: OJBKQ — Bringing the Lattice Toolkit In

Now the stage is set. OJBKQ synthesizes all of the preceding criticisms and delivers a complete answer.

Its three moves correspond precisely to the three unresolved problems above:

*Move one: don't just give one Babai point — give K+1 candidates.*

Since this is lattice decoding, cryptography has long known that the Babai point is not optimal. Klein (2000) proposed a randomized variant: at each dimensional projection, instead of always selecting the nearest neighbor, sample from a probability distribution (Gaussian-weighted over neighbors). Repeat K times to obtain K different candidates, add the original deterministic Babai point, and pick the one with the smallest residual — K+1 candidates in total.

OJBKQ extends the Klein algorithm to *box-constrained* integer least squares (where quantization values are discrete and bounded), with each weight column within a layer sampled independently, and K as a tunable parameter.

This single move directly defuses GPTQ's "single-solution greedy" time bomb.

*Move two: stop inverting the Hessian.*

The entire OJBKQ algorithm operates using the triangular factor from Cholesky decomposition plus back-substitution — no matrix inversion anywhere. Numerical stability improves dramatically for low bit-width, ill-conditioned scenarios.

This defuses the second time bomb.

*Move three: JTA — a dial between the two objectives.*

The binary opposition identified by QEP gets a clean answer from OJBKQ: you do not have to choose. Introduce a continuous parameter alpha, and set the objective = alpha × runtime objective + (1−alpha) × full-precision objective. Alpha sweeps smoothly from 0 to 1, letting you find the most appropriate point for each model and each bit-width.

This move unifies GPTQ's "runtime target is too noisy" and AWQ's "full-precision target is too idealistic" under a single dial.

* Distillation: What This River Is Really Changing

On the surface: quantization algorithms keep getting better. Underneath: a *shift in perspective* is taking place.

- Early work (GPTQ / AWQ / QuIP) treated PTQ as an "engineering optimization problem" — how to make the round-plus-compensate mechanism run more reliably.
- After Chen et al. (2025), PTQ was repositioned as a "lattice decoding problem" — a classical problem with decades of mathematical machinery behind it.
- OJBKQ is the first harvest of this perspective shift: importing cryptographic tools (Klein randomization, K-best search, inversion-free triangular decomposition) to solve LLM quantization.

Once the perspective changes, the road ahead becomes clear:

1. *Stronger lattice algorithms.* Babai dates to 1986, Klein to 2000. The lattice community has LLL, BKZ, and sieving — far more powerful but far slower. Can we find middle grounds suited to PTQ's time budget?
2. *Cross-layer lattices.* OJBKQ is still layer-wise. Under the lattice perspective, the mathematical structure of joint multi-layer optimization is well-defined — it just explodes in scale. Can structured approximations make cross-layer optimization tractable?
3. *Incorporating calibration data into the lattice.* The runtime-vs-full-precision tension identified by QEP is, at root, a training-distribution-vs-inference-distribution tension. Can this tension be expressed geometrically within the lattice framework?

OJBKQ's true contribution is not how many perplexity points it shaves off. It is the proof of a principle: once LLM compression is connected to a *classical mathematical problem*, a decade's worth of tools suddenly becomes available.
