* The River of the Problem

An 80 GB model uses only 20 GB of its parameters on any given inference pass —
the other 60 GB sit in VRAM, summoned only occasionally.
Could we simply throw away those "rarely used" experts and slim the model down?

It sounds straightforward. But a decade of research keeps running into the
same ditch:

Every time you discard something that "looks useless," the model collapses in
some scenario you never tested. It happened with fully connected networks
(Han 2015), with large language models (SparseGPT 2023), and with MoE models
(NAEE 2024). Each generation rediscovers the same truth: "rarely used" and
"unimportant" are not the same thing.

By the time this river reaches MoP in 2025, the question has a sharper edge:
when your *evaluation criterion itself* is biased — calibrated only on general
text, for instance — the experts you prune away are precisely the minority
specialists in math and code. The pruned model still looks fine on general
benchmarks, then craters the moment it meets GSM8K.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Han et al.: Surgery to Slim Down Neural Networks

*** What Problem It Saw

Deep learning had just taken off; VGG and AlexNet were growing ever larger.
But most weights were barely doing any work — their absolute values were tiny,
so even large inputs produced near-zero contributions.
If they weren't working, why keep them around wasting memory?

*** Its Solution

The most naive possible criterion: look at the absolute value of each weight.
Anything below a threshold gets zeroed out, then the remaining weights are
retrained to compensate. This prune–retrain–prune cycle compressed AlexNet
to 1/9 of its original size with almost no accuracy loss.

*** Why It Worked (and Where the New Trap Was Buried)

In small networks, interactions between weights are relatively simple, and
"small weight = small contribution" mostly holds. But this line of thinking
buried two traps that would not be stepped on hard until a decade later.

First, the *retraining assumption* — you assume the cost of training again is
acceptable. In the LLM era this assumption collapses instantly; nobody can
afford to retrain GPT-3 from scratch.

Second, *isolated scoring* — each weight is graded alone, without regard to
what it does in combination with others. That is barely tolerable in small
networks; in large models it is completely insufficient.

** 2023 | SparseGPT: You Cannot Prune a Large Model This Way

*** What Problem It Saw

Frantar and Alistarh applied magnitude pruning directly to BLOOM-176B.
The result was shocking: at just 30% sparsity, perplexity exploded.
A method that had worked on small networks for ten years failed completely
at LLM scale.

Worse, the traditional remedy — retraining — was simply not an option.
Re-training a model with hundreds of billions of parameters? Nobody can pay
for that.

*** Its Solution

If you cannot retrain, you must *immediately* adjust the surviving weights
after each pruning step to absorb the loss. SparseGPT reframes pruning as a
*local reconstruction* problem: using a small calibration set (128 texts), it
computes the input and output of each layer, then solves a sparse regression
— which weights to keep and what values to assign the remaining ones, so that
this layer's output changes as little as possible.

This requires second-order information (the Hessian). The intuition: when you
remove a weight, you don't only ask how important that weight is in isolation,
you ask "can the remaining weights compensate for its absence?" If they can,
the weight may be deleted even if it is large; if they cannot, it must be kept
even if it is small.

*** Why It Worked (and Where the New Trap Was Buried)

For the first time, a hundred-billion-parameter model could be pruned to
50–60% sparsity in one pass with almost no change in perplexity. That was a
qualitative leap.

But it left a very concrete trap: the resulting sparsity pattern is
*irregular* — zeros scattered randomly across each row and column. GPU matrix
multiplication thrives on dense blocks; scatter a bunch of isolated zeros
through the weight matrix and the GPU still has to compute just as much.
You halved the parameters in theory, but wall-clock inference speed barely
changed.

Realizing that speedup requires either dedicated hardware (sparse tensor
cores) or custom kernels. In most practical settings, SparseGPT saves
storage but not inference time.

** 2024 | NAEE: If You're Going to Save, Save in Whole Blocks

*** What Problem It Saw

Lu et al. fixed their sights on a newly prominent architecture: MoE.
In a model like Mixtral 8x7B, each layer contains 8 independent expert
networks and activates only 2 of them per token. The other 6 take no part in
the computation yet must all remain in VRAM — an 80 GB model doing 20 GB of
actual work.

This structure is far friendlier to pruning than the SparseGPT scenario:
you do not need irregular per-weight sparsity. You can drop *an entire expert*
outright. Remove one expert and the layer's pool shrinks from 8 to 6 choices;
parameters genuinely decrease and inference genuinely speeds up — no
"hardware-doesn't-support-this" headache.

*** Its Solution

The question then becomes: of the 8 experts, which 2 do you drop?

NAEE gives a brute-force but effective answer: *enumerate all combinations*.
Keeping 6 of 8 yields C(8,6) = 28 possible configurations. Run each
configuration on the calibration data (C4) and measure the token
reconstruction loss — how closely the pruned model's output matches the
original. Keep the combination with the lowest loss.

Simple, direct, hardware-friendly. Dropping 2 experts from Mixtral 8x7B
reduces the requirement from 2 × 80 GB GPUs to 1, with a 1.2× inference
speedup.

*** Why It Worked (and Where the New Trap Was Buried)

Lifting the pruning granularity from individual weights to entire experts
sidesteps the hardware-acceleration problem SparseGPT left behind.
This is a structural leap — moving from "saving storage" to genuinely
"saving time."

But it left a more insidious trap: *calibration-data bias*.

C4 is a general web corpus, mostly everyday English. When you score experts
on such a corpus, the least-activated experts with the smallest impact on
loss are very likely the minority specialists — the ones that handle
mathematical notation and code syntax.

What NAEE called "unimportant" was really only "unimportant on general text."

** 2025-11 | MoP: Don't Let "General" Become "Overly General"

*** What Problem It Saw

The MoP authors ran a revealing experiment: a Mixtral model pruned with NAEE
showed almost no degradation on ARC, HellaSwag, and MMLU — everything looked
fine. But on GSM8K (elementary school math word problems) and HumanEval
(code generation), performance was cut in half.

Why? Because NAEE's logic is "small output change on C4 = safe to delete."
C4 contains almost no mathematical reasoning or code snippets, so the experts
that activate specifically in those settings are *already idle on C4*, which
means deleting them produces no measurable loss — on C4.

But when users actually deploy the model, they may well ask "solve this
problem for me" or "write me a function." MoP names this phenomenon
*functional collapse*: after pruning, only generalists remain; every
specialist has been cut indiscriminately.

*** Its Solution

The key shift: stop using "how similar is this layer's output to the
original?" as the sole criterion.

MoP proceeds in two steps.

Step 1 — *Clustering*: use an entirely different similarity measure.
Run each expert across 6 distinct domains (math, code, commonsense,
summarization, reasoning, natural-language inference) and record a
"task-performance vector." Two experts whose vectors are highly correlated
(high Spearman correlation across the 6 dimensions) are considered
functionally redundant — they belong to the same cluster.

Cluster all experts by this similarity. Mixtral's 8 experts may fall into
3–4 functional groups: generalists, math specialists, code specialists,
reasoning specialists, and so on.

Step 2 — *Select one representative per cluster*: within each cluster, keep
the expert with the highest activation variance. The intuition: high
activation variance means this expert reacts strongly to different inputs —
it is genuinely making decisions, genuinely discriminative; low activation
variance means it responds similarly to nearly everything, a filler that
could be replaced.

In the end, one maximally discriminative representative survives from each
cluster. The pruning ratio is unchanged, but what remains is no longer
"high-frequency generalists" — it is *a set of functionally complementary
mosaic tiles*, each irreplaceable, together covering all tasks.

*** Why It Worked (and Where the New Trap Was Buried)

MoP converts the *absolute* question "which expert is least important?" into
the *relative* question "which experts are functionally equivalent?" Equivalent
ones can be merged, keeping only one; non-equivalent ones must all survive.

The data validate the intuition: +7.24% on general tasks, +8.92% on
specialized tasks — the largest gains falling precisely where NAEE fell
hardest.

Where are the new traps? At least two.

First, *offline cost*. Computing the Spearman similarity matrix requires
running every expert across multiple domains. More experts and more domains
mean higher offline cost (Qwen1.5-MoE has 60 experts).

Second, *bias in the choice of domains*. MoP's 6 domains were selected by
hand. If your deployment scenario falls outside those 6 — say, medical
dialogue or legal documents — the clustering will still be skewed. The
"general-corpus bias" has been replaced by a "coverage bias."

* Frontier Extensions

While MoP was taking shape, at least two parallel lines of inquiry were
attacking the same problem from different angles.

** 2025-04 | EASY-EP: Let the User Tell You What They Need

Dong et al. proposed a more radical stance: since the problem is
"general calibration → wrongly deleted experts," stop pursuing
"general-purpose pruning" altogether. Give the method a few samples from
the target domain; it uses gating scores plus output L2 norms to identify
which experts are genuinely activated token by token and which ones make
a real contribution — and keeps exactly those.

On DeepSeek-R1, using only half the experts reproduces full-model
performance with 3× throughput. The cost is generality: this pruning is
custom-built for one domain; switch domains and you reprun from scratch.

An interesting contrast with MoP: MoP aims for a single pruned model
that is *good enough across all domains*; EASY-EP abandons that goal and
pursues a model that is *optimal for one specific domain*.

** 2025-07 | MoNE: Instead of Deleting, Use a Stand-In

Gao et al. take the view that "deletion" itself is too drastic. A rarely
used but occasionally critical expert, once deleted, is gone for good —
there is no recovering it.

MoNE's approach: replace the expert to be pruned with a *lightweight
novice* — a tiny module that approximates the original expert's average
output. Frequently used experts are kept intact; cold experts are replaced
by a low-cost surrogate. Instead of "present or absent," the choice is
"full capacity or reduced capacity."

This yields +2.72 points over the baseline across nine zero-shot tasks,
and on Qwen2-57B-A14B a 25% pruning rate drops performance by only 0.14
points.

** A Single Tension Behind All Three Lines

A comparison of MoP, EASY-EP, and MoNE:

| Dimension          | NAEE            | MoP               | EASY-EP        | MoNE              |
|--------------------|-----------------|-------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Calibration data   | C4 (general)    | Multi-domain clus.| Target domain  | Various           |
| Decision granulity | Full deletion   | Full deletion     | Full deletion  | Delete or replace |
| Generality         | Falsely general | Truly general     | Gives up gen.  | General           |
| Offline cost       | Low             | Medium–High       | Very low       | Medium            |

All three have abandoned the framing "find the least important expert"
in favor of "find the most *replaceable* expert" — they simply define
"replaceable" differently.

* Insight

Lay this entire lineage on a single timeline and you realize it is not
really "the evolution of pruning methods." It is *the repeated
overturning of the definition of importance*.

- Han 2015 says: importance = weight magnitude.
- SparseGPT says: wrong — importance = whether others can compensate
  once this weight is removed.
- NAEE says: wrong — the "importance" of a single weight is
  meaningless; what matters is the output determined jointly by
  *a group* of weights.
- MoP says: wrong — even "output" must be qualified: output *in which
  context*? Useless on C4 does not mean useless in general.

Each generation believed it had captured the essence of importance; the
next generation arrived and found that the definition failed
systematically in new settings.

What is really happening underneath: as models have grown from
"small and specialized" to "large and general," the *context* required
to evaluate importance has expanded continually. Early on, a single
weight's importance could be judged in isolation; now, an expert's
importance can only be established across multiple domains and diverse
task distributions.

Where does the next step lead? Two directions already seem to be
surfacing.

First, *online / adaptive pruning* — instead of deciding in advance
"keep this, drop that," dynamically adjust the active set of experts
at inference time based on the domain of each incoming request.
MoP's offline clustering is the refined endpoint of "static pruning";
the next generation may not "prune" at all, but rather "schedule."

Second, a fusion of *pruning + replacement + knowledge transfer* —
MoNE is already exploring this path. A deleted expert does not vanish;
it is inherited by a low-cost module through distillation.
Once the "all-or-nothing" binary is broken, the entire pruning problem
becomes a *continuous* model-compression problem.

* Takeaways

This river leaves two unusually clean hooks for anyone doing research.

First, *someone else's "no benchmark drop" may not be good news*.
Every number in the NAEE paper looked great — because it calibrated
on C4 and evaluated on general benchmarks like MMLU and ARC, whose
distribution nearly matches the calibration set. MoP's contribution
is essentially *using a different evaluation distribution to reveal
what the old method had been hiding*. Every overlooked sub-domain
is a potential entry point for the next paper.

Second, *circular reasoning is the most common and hardest-to-detect
trap in research*. "Use distribution X to select experts, then validate
on benchmarks that resemble X" — this implicit loop exists pervasively
across every field. Whenever you see a paper whose method definition
and evaluation metric draw on similar data distributions, you can be
almost certain there is a hole there.

This has an immediately actionable implication: take any "already-solved
problem," find a real-world scenario that *deviates from the mainstream
calibration distribution*, and test the current SOTA against it.
If it collapses there, you have found the title of your next paper.
