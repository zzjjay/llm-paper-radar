* The River of the Problem

You have a multi-tens-of-GB model you want to squeeze onto a small GPU. Two
things must happen: first, *compression* — cutting each weight from 16 bits
down to 4 or even 3, to make it fit; second, *adaptation* — fine-tuning it
on your task with a small handful of trainable parameters (LoRA).

The industry standard is: compress first, then adapt. Seems obvious. But
generations of researchers have kept attacking this pipeline because it hides
a waste that nobody named explicitly:

*The error introduced by compression cannot be erased by the adaptation that follows.*

You spend your precious LoRA capacity — only a few million parameters — and
most of that energy goes toward "scrubbing stains that won't come out" rather
than "learning the task well." That is the source of this river —
*how should quantization error and low-rank adaptation cooperate?*

Where is the difficulty? At compression time you *do not yet know* what the
downstream task looks like, so you cannot tailor the compression to it. Every
generation of work has therefore been answering the same underlying question:
without knowing the task, how do you *shape* the compression error into a form
that will be easy to clean up later?

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022 | GPTQ + LoRA: Two Rivers Yet to Meet

*** What Problem Did They See

The story starts with two unrelated needs. On one side, large models are too
large — they must be compressed. GPTQ steps in and says: you cannot quantize
blindly; dropping one weight affects others, so you need second-order
information (the Hessian) to calculate precisely "how much does this cut hurt
the output," then quantize column by column with compensation, minimising the
reconstruction error.

On the other side, fine-tuning is too expensive. LoRA steps in and says: leave
those hundreds of billions of original weights frozen, and attach a small pair
of matrices =A*B= alongside them (very low rank, e.g. r=16); during training
only update that tiny pair, and you can still learn the task well.

*** Their Solutions

GPTQ's approach: quantize weights column by column; after each column is
quantized, immediately redistribute the error it caused to the remaining,
not-yet-quantized columns as compensation. Like squeezing toothpaste — squeeze
a little, smooth it out, repeat.

LoRA's approach: original weight =W= is frozen; the output becomes
=W*x + (A*B)*x=. That =A*B= is a very low-rank "patch" — tiny parameter count
yet capable of shifting large-model behaviour.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap Was Left)

Both are elegant in isolation. But the trap is: *they never imagined working
together*. GPTQ's objective is "faithfully reconstruct that frozen, original
weight" — there is no concept in its formulation that "someone will fine-tune
later." It wastes all its correction potential: even though LoRA could later
wipe away some error, GPTQ treats quantization as the final step and minimises
error in isolation.

So the question emerges: if at compression time you already know "LoRA will
come to help," can you compress more cleverly? That is exactly the thread the
next paper picks up.

** 2023 | QLoRA: The First Time "Compress" and "Adapt" Are Connected

*** What Problem Did It See

QLoRA targeted a very concrete pain: you want to fine-tune a 65B model, but
before quantization it simply does not fit in a GPU. LoRA solved "too many
training parameters," but not "the frozen backbone itself is too large."

*** Its Solution

Chain quantization (GPTQ-style) and LoRA together: compress the large model to
4-bit first (using NF4, a data type designed for normally-distributed weights,
plus Double Quantization to save overhead from quantization constants), then
attach LoRA fine-tuning on top of that frozen 4-bit backbone.

This is the pivotal step — *for the first time, a quantized backbone plus a
trainable low-rank patch run in the same pipeline.*

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap Was Left)

Enormously successful in practice — single-GPU fine-tuning of large models
became real. But from the perspective of "cooperation," it is still *two
independent steps*: the quantization stage compresses the model in isolation,
and the fine-tuning stage trains LoRA in isolation. The two stages never
consult each other.

The consequence: quantization error is spread *isotropically* (uniformly in
all directions) across the entire weight space. LoRA's capacity is limited —
it can only repair things within a low-rank subspace. Uniformly spread error
means a significant portion falls in directions LoRA cannot reach; worse,
even the subspace LoRA could repair gets polluted by the uniform noise.

If QLoRA connected the two rivers, it did so by "letting each flow on its own,
merging only at the end." The next question is: can the quantization step,
from the very start, be designed with LoRA in mind?

** 2023 | LoftQ: Making Quantization "Know" a LoRA Is Waiting

*** What Problem Did It See

LoftQ fixed on a detail: after QLoRA quantizes, it initialises LoRA to zero
(or random), which means the starting point of fine-tuning is separated from
the original full-precision model by a "gap" — quantization has already shifted
the model, and LoRA, starting from zero, must first spend its capacity
bridging that gap before it can even begin learning the task.

*** Its Solution

Do quantization and LoRA initialisation *jointly*, rather than sequentially.
Concretely, an alternating procedure: quantize the weights, compute the
residual (original weights − quantized weights), apply truncated SVD to get a
low-rank approximation of that residual, and use it to initialise LoRA; then
factor in this LoRA and re-quantize… repeat for a few rounds.

The intuition: make =quantized weights + LoRA initial value= as close as
possible to the original weights, so fine-tuning *starts in a good position*
from step one, rather than first falling into a pit and then climbing out.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap Was Left)

A genuine cognitive upgrade — quantization "notices" LoRA for the first time.
But LoftQ works entirely in *weight space*, treating all weights *equally*.

The problem is: what the model actually cares about is not "are the weights
accurately restored?" but "are the outputs (activations) correct?" Some weights
affect outputs enormously; others are nearly irrelevant — treating all weights
equally means effort is spread uniformly across important and unimportant ones
alike. Furthermore, LoftQ has no rigorous theoretical guarantee, leaving two
open gaps along this line: *activation awareness* and *theoretical foundation*.

** 2023 | LQ-LoRA: Making the Split Between "How Much to Quantize" and "How Much to Low-Rank" Dynamic

*** What Problem Did It See

LoftQ fixes a quantization scheme and then computes the residual. LQ-LoRA
thinks more dynamically: different layers have different importance, so why
should everyone get the same quantization bit-width and the same low-rank
allocation?

*** Its Solution

Decompose the weight explicitly into two parts: =W = Q + L=, where =Q= is the
quantized integer part and =L= is a low-rank supplement. Then use *integer
linear programming (ILP)* to dynamically decide, under a given memory budget
and guided by each layer's Fisher information (measuring how important that
layer is): how many bits should this layer be quantized to, and how large
should the low-rank part be?

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap Was Left)

It pushes "resource allocation" to the extreme — investing more where it
matters most. But notice: its low-rank part =L= is there to *passively
compensate* quantization error (carved out at decomposition time). It *never,
at the quantization step itself, actively demands "the error must fall into a
low-rank shape that is easy to fix later."*

In other words, everything so far operates in "compress first, then patch up
afterwards." Nobody asked the reverse: can we, during quantization itself,
*shape* the error into a form that is easy to repair? That reversal is
ProjQ's launching point — but before it, one critical piece is still missing:
how should error even be *measured*?

** 2024 | EoRA: Error Should Be Measured by How It Affects Outputs, Not by Weights

*** What Problem Did It See

Everyone before EoRA measured error roughly in terms of "how different are the
weights." EoRA cuts to the point: *we want the outputs to be correct, not the
weights.* Equal-sized weight errors, when multiplied by different activations,
cause wildly different damage to the final output.

*** Its Solution

Minimise *activation-weighted* error. Concretely, introduce the covariance
matrix of activations and project the quantization residual into the
*eigenspace* defined by that covariance for low-rank compensation — that is,
prioritise repairing directions that "are frequently activated on real data and
have large influence on the output."

This gives the entire line a mathematically clean tool: to compute "the most
repairable low-rank directions," one must decompose under the activation
covariance metric (ProjQ later uses this result directly as a cornerstone in
Lemma 4.2).

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap Was Left)

EoRA recalibrated the ruler for measuring error — switching from weight space
to activation space. But it is fundamentally still *post-hoc compensation*:
compress the model first, then add a low-rank term to rescue it. It never
feeds the insight of "activation-aware + low-rank repairability" *back* into
the *quantization decision itself*.

At this point all the pieces are in place: QLoRA merges the two steps, LoftQ
makes quantization aware of LoRA, LQ-LoRA makes allocation dynamic, EoRA
measures error in activation space. All that remains is someone who distils
this into one sentence: *at the moment of quantization, proactively shape the
error into a form that LoRA can erase in one sweep afterwards.*

** 2026 | ProjQ: Welding "Repairability" Directly into the Quantization Step

*** What Problem Did It See

ProjQ's insight is to make the implicit assumption running through the whole
line explicit: everyone has defaulted to "the smaller the error, the better,"
but in fact — *the structure of error matters as much as its magnitude.*

The reasoning is elegant: at compression time the downstream task is genuinely
unknown, so you cannot tailor to it. *But* the *structure* of the error is
something you can control! If you can guarantee that the error falls exactly
within a low-rank subspace, then regardless of what the downstream task turns
out to be, the low-rank LoRA can "absorb" it entirely, leaving its full
capacity for the actual task. Conversely, any error that lands in the
"orthogonal complement" (the irrecoverable direction) is the true loss, and
that must be minimised as hard as possible.

*** Its Solution

An alternating algorithm (block coordinate descent, typically converging in
1–3 rounds), with two steps that alternate:

1. *P step (find the subspace)*: Fix the current quantized weights, compute
   the residual, and use truncated SVD to identify the top r_d directions in
   the error that are "most worth repairing and most easily repaired," assembling
   them into a projection operator =P=. This is "the piece left for LoRA to wipe."

2. *W step (project then quantize)*: Fix =P=, project activations onto its
   *orthogonal complement* (=X_perp = X(I - P)=), then run a standard PTQ
   solver (GPTQ) in this "already-carved-out" space. Because the repairable
   directions have been zeroed out, the quantization bit-budget now *concentrates
   entirely on compressing the irrecoverable tail error.*

In a final phase, EoRA-style activation-covariance-aware decomposition derives
the *optimal initialisation* for LoRA — pointing the low-rank patch precisely
at the subspace identified in the P step.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Gaps Were Left)

It uses every piece from the preceding papers — but reverses the direction: no
longer "compress then rescue," but "shape while compressing." The quantization
budget is no longer wasted on directions LoRA would have erased anyway; it is
fully spent on truly irreversible loss. That is the fundamental reason it can
match others' 4-bit performance at 3-bit (on LLaMA-2-7B, C4 perplexity drops
from GPTQ+SVD-LLM's 26.26 / LoftQ's 28.77 down to 21.50, and commonsense
reasoning average accuracy rises from the mid-40s to 47.82%).

New gaps it leaves: it relies on a preset "repairable rank" r_d, and on an
estimate of the activation covariance (requiring calibration data); the
alternating algorithm converges fast, but the SVD in the P step still carries
cost at very large scale. A deeper open question: this "shape the error for
downstream" framework is currently tailored for LoRA, a *linear low-rank*
adapter — whether it generalises to other adapter structures remains unknown.

* Frontier Extensions

ProjQ was posted in May 2026 and as of now *no* follow-up work has publicly
critiqued or improved upon it. As of 2026-06, this is the latest development
on this line.

Contemporaneous neighbours are worth noting (these are *competitors*, not
successors; their relationship with ProjQ is *inferred* from thematic overlap,
not from a confirmed citation chain):
- *LQER / SLIM / CALDERA* and similar "quantization + low-rank" joint
  compression share the same root idea — balancing how the "quantized part"
  and the "low-rank part" divide the labour. ProjQ's distinction from them:
  the others mostly treat the low-rank component as a *compensation term*;
  ProjQ treats it as a *shaping target* that in turn guides quantization.
- *Low-Rank Quantization-Aware Training (LR-QAT)* takes the quantization-aware
  training route and requires gradient-based training; ProjQ belongs to the PTQ
  camp and requires almost no retraining — they occupy different niches.

* Insights

On the surface, this river is about "ever more precise quantization algorithms."
Underneath, three layers of genuine cognitive shift are happening:

1. *From "reconstruct weights" to "reconstruct outputs."* Early work stared at
   how different the weight values were. EoRA and ProjQ switch to staring at
   how different the activation-weighted outputs are. Change the ruler, and the
   entire optimisation objective changes — what has always mattered is not
   whether the parameters look the same, but whether the behaviour looks the
   same.

2. *From "error is the enemy" to "error is shapeable."* GPTQ treats error as
   an enemy to be eliminated — smaller is always better. ProjQ's reversal:
   error cannot be fully eliminated, so *decide where it lands*. Drive it into
   a room where someone (LoRA) is standing by to mop it up, and it is no longer
   a loss. This is a leap from "fighting noise" to "routing noise."

3. *From "pipeline" to "co-design."* The deepest shift: compression and
   adaptation were two independent operations; this line has progressively
   welded them into a joint optimisation problem. ProjQ makes the quantization
   step "aware" of LoRA's existence and serves its needs — a classic co-design.

Where is the next step most likely to go? Following these three threads:
- If error structure is controllable, can the "repairable rank" r_d be
  *adaptively set per layer or even per task*, rather than fixed as a global
  preset?
- If we are shaping for LoRA, can we shape for *other downstream structures*
  (e.g. MoE experts, or more complex adapters)? Generalising "shape the error
  for the downstream" from LoRA into a universal principle.
- More ambitiously: push the "shaping" idea upstream into *pre-training*,
  so that models are born with weights that are inherently "easy to compress
  and easy to adapt."

* Takeaways

For "how to do research, how to find problems," this line offers three
immediately actionable lessons:

1. *Watch the seams in the pipeline.* The richest problems are rarely inside a
   single step; they live *between* steps, in the junction that "goes without
   saying and so nobody questions it." "Compress then adapt" was treated as
   common sense for years. All of ProjQ's value was found in that seam. *This
   means:* for any "standard pipeline," the first question to ask is "did the
   earlier step do anything to serve the later step?"

2. *Change the metric, not just the algorithm.* The genuine leaps in this line
   all came from switching the ruler (weights → activations) or the viewpoint
   (error magnitude → error structure), not from optimising the same objective
   harder. *This means:* when you are stuck, before tuning hyperparameters, ask
   "is the quantity I'm optimising actually the thing I care about?"

3. *Inject constraints in reverse.* Others "do A first, then use B to fix A's
   problems"; ProjQ feeds B's requirements *back into A's decision.* This is a
   reusable pattern. *This means:* any "two-stage system where the later stage
   rescues the earlier stage" is worth one attempt at "writing the later stage's
   constraints into the earlier stage."
