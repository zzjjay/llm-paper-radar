* The River of Problems

Once an LLM needs to read a long document spanning tens of thousands of tokens, the first thing to blow up GPU memory is not the model weights but the massive pile of K/V tensors that the attention mechanism caches to avoid recomputation — the KV cache. This pile grows *linearly with sequence length*: double the context, double the cache; after dozens of conversation turns, the cache can dwarf the model itself.

A whole research thread has sprung up around one question: *Can we shrink this KV pile without breaking the model?*

But "shrinking" is an art. Not every token, every head, or every dimension in the KV cache is equally important. Compressing uniformly is like throwing treasure and trash away together. Generation after generation of researchers on this river have been doing one thing — *progressively refining the granularity at which they ask "which slots deserve more budget and which deserve less."*

* The Evolutionary Narrative

Along this river, the protagonist is not any single paper — it is *the granularity of KV cache compression*. Each generation has taken the same problem and cut it one level finer, asking "is this unit important, and how much budget does it deserve?"

** 2023.09 | StreamingLLM: Hard Positional Rules

*** The Problem It Saw

After decoding 10,000 tokens, a naïve Transformer's KV cache has already consumed tens of gigabytes of GPU memory. The earliest researchers tried a simple fix: a sliding window — keep only the most recent N tokens. But the model immediately fell apart, producing gibberish.

Xiao et al. traced the root cause and found a counterintuitive phenomenon: *the very first few tokens are disproportionately important*, regardless of whether they carry meaningful content. Attention scores at those positions are abnormally high — they act like "drain sinks" absorbing surplus attention weight. Remove them and the softmax distribution destabilizes, causing the whole model's output distribution to drift.

*** Its Solution

Codify a fixed rule: always keep the first 4 tokens (called attention sinks) plus a recent sliding window; evict everything in between.

This is KV compression's earliest "granularity" — *positional granularity*. The rule is static, content-blind, and attention-blind; a token's fate is determined purely by its index in the sequence.

*** The New Problem It Left Behind

The large middle section is wiped out wholesale, yet obviously not all middle tokens are unimportant. If the user asks "what is the name of the character mentioned around token 3,000?" — that KV was already deleted. The model cannot answer not because it is unintelligent, but because the answer was preemptively erased.

The next step had to look at content and attention.

** 2023.06 | H2O: Picking Tokens by Attention Score

*** The Problem It Saw

StreamingLLM decided fate by position — too blunt. Zhang et al. stared at attention matrices for a long time and found a pattern: *only a tiny fraction of tokens (~5%) are repeatedly attended to*. Remove these "heavy hitters" and performance collapses; remove the tokens that nobody ever attends to, and the loss is nearly zero.

In other words, the attention mechanism has already computed "who matters" — we just need to listen to it.

*** Its Solution

Maintain a *cumulative attention score* — each time a token is attended to, add the received attention weight to its running total. Each time a new token arrives, evict the lowest-scoring token from the cache.

Granularity advances from "position" to *token-level dynamic scoring*.

*** The New Problem It Left Behind

The cumulative score keeps alive tokens that "were attended to long ago but nobody cares about now." What the current query needs, however, is what *will* be used going forward, not who had the highest historical total.

Furthermore, updating cumulative scores across all tokens is computationally expensive.

** 2024.04 | SnapKV: Picking Tokens Based on What the User Is Asking

*** The Problem It Saw

H2O scores using the entire history, but Li et al. ran a simple experiment: use the attention patterns of the last few prompt tokens to predict which earlier tokens will be attended to during decoding — *with remarkable accuracy*.

This means the sentence the user writes at the end of the prompt already reveals what will be needed next. You do not need the full history; the prompt tail is enough.

*** Its Solution

Carve out an observation window at the end of the prompt (e.g., the last 32 tokens) and use their attention scores over the preceding context to rank earlier tokens. High-scoring tokens stay; low-scoring ones are evicted.

This effectively replaces "global historical average" with *"what the user is currently focused on"* — more accurate and more efficient.

*** The New Problem It Left Behind

SnapKV applies the same strategy and the same budget to every layer. But a 32-layer model does very different things at each layer — why should they all get the same treatment?

** 2024.06 | PyramidKV: Different Budgets for Different Layers

*** The Problem It Saw

Cai et al. examined per-layer attention distributions and found: lower layers attend broadly, covering many tokens; higher layers become increasingly focused, until the top layers fixate on just a handful of key tokens.

This leads directly to a conclusion: *lower layers need more KV budget; upper layers need very little.* A uniform budget wastes capacity at the top while starving the bottom.

*** Its Solution

Allocate in a pyramid shape — large budget for lower layers, small budget for upper layers, with the total held constant. Granularity upgrades from "token-level" to *"token-level + layer-level."*

*** The New Problem It Left Behind

Within a single layer, all heads are still treated identically. But head-to-head variation is even larger than layer-to-layer variation — some heads specialize in retrieval, others in local syntax.

** 2024.10 | HeadKV: Different Budgets for Different Heads

*** The Problem It Saw

Fu et al. did one thing: use a needle-in-a-haystack task to assign each head a "retrieval capability score." The results revealed enormous variance — some heads scored perfectly and removing them obliterated long-context capability; others barely contributed to retrieval at all and could be zeroed out without consequence.

PyramidKV distributes budget across layers, but within a layer it is uniform — the same standard for high-scoring and low-scoring heads alike.

*** Its Solution

Compute head importance as weights, and refine budget allocation from layer-level down to *head-level*. Important heads receive more KV slots; unimportant heads receive fewer.

*** The New Problem It Left Behind

At this point, granularity has been refined all the way to token × layer × head. But what about one level finer? *Within the same token at the same head, different dimensions still differ in importance* — that cut has not yet been made.

** Parallel Branch 2024.07 | Palu: A Different Perspective on Compression

A brief detour is necessary here, because MixedDimKV's real elegance lies in welding two previously unrelated lines together.

*** The Problem It Saw

The entire chain above (StreamingLLM → HeadKV) does the same thing — *binary classification*: should this token (or head) stay or go? Chang et al. shifted the lens: instead of asking "keep or not," ask "how many dimensions to keep."

Because the KV cache is fundamentally a matrix, and matrices have *low-rank structure* — you do not need all d dimensions; keeping the r principal components is sufficient.

*** Its Solution

SVD-decompose the K and V projection matrices, and compress all tokens uniformly to r dimensions. This branch (Palu / Eigen Attention / ShadowKV) cares about *dimension-level* granularity, but every token still gets the same dimension count.

*** The Problem It Left Open — the Paper Wall That MixedDimKV Breaks Through

The token-eviction branch only asks "keep or not." The dimension-reduction branch only asks "compress to how many dimensions, uniformly."

But look again: *"evicting a token" is just "compressing it to 0 dimensions." "Keeping a token" is just "compressing it to the full d dimensions."*

Then why can't there be 0.25d or 0.5d in between? Different tokens have different importance — important ones deserve more dimensions, unimportant ones fewer — *that is genuine fine granularity*.

** 2026.03 | MixedDimKV: Unifying "Keep or Not" and "How Many Dimensions"

*** Its Core Move

If you view token eviction as "a special case of dimension allocation (either 0 or full)," then *allowing intermediate values* immediately yields a strictly richer framework:

- StreamingLLM / H2O / SnapKV / HeadKV: binary allocation from {0, d}
- Palu / Eigen Attention: uniform allocation [r, r, r, …, r]
- MixedDimKV: arbitrary non-uniform allocation [r₁, r₂, r₃, …]

Granularity drops in one step from token-level / head-level / layer-level all the way down to *the specific dimension count per (token, head) pair*.

*** The Two Engineering Problems It Had to Solve

1. *How to score the "compression loss" for each (token, head, candidate-dimension-count) triple?*

   Old methods (H2O / SnapKV) score tokens using attention weights. But attention weights only reflect importance on the key side; they ignore value magnitudes and per-dimension compressibility.

   MixedDimKV targets the *attention output* directly (not the attention weight) — because output deviation is what actually affects the model. It derives a *deviation upper bound*: a conservative loss score for each (token, dim) pair that captures the joint effect of three factors — attention saliency, value norm, and per-dimension compressibility.

2. *Given a total budget, how to allocate a dimension count to each token?*

   The candidate dimension set is discrete (e.g., {0, 32, 64, 128}), which looks like an integer program. But the authors prove a key fact: *under long-context conditions, the duality gap between the original problem and its Lagrangian dual is negligible.*

   They therefore use *binary search* to find the Lagrangian multiplier, then monotonically assign each token a dimension count — solved in linear time.

*** Its Two Variants

- *MixedDimKV*: uses only its own loss score for allocation, with no head-level importance. Outperforms all baselines that do not use head information.
- *MixedDimKV-H*: additionally incorporates HeadKV's head-level weights — consistently beats HeadKV when both have access to the same head information.

*** What It Achieves

- LongBench: *a KV cache of only 6.25% (KV size = 512) matches full attention.*
- Needle-in-a-Haystack (50K context): *0.26% of cache (KV size = 128) still achieves 100% accuracy.*
- Decoding latency: reduced to 55% of full attention.

* Insights and Next Steps

** What Is Really Changing Along This River

On the surface, granularity is being refined level by level: position → token → layer → head → dimension. But what is happening underneath is not mere subdivision — it is *the unification of all compression actions into a single framework*.

StreamingLLM, H2O, Palu, and HeadKV were originally four unrelated stories — one group selects tokens, another computes attention, another distributes layer budgets, another does low-rank decomposition. MixedDimKV fits them all into one template with a single sentence:

#+begin_quote
*KV compression = an optimization problem that assigns a 0/1-bit budget to each (token, head, dim) triple*
#+end_quote

All prior methods are special cases of this template — StreamingLLM is the "hard positional constraint" special case; H2O is the "attention-ranked binary allocation" special case; Palu is the "uniform allocation across dimensions" special case.

This is the river's greatest turning point: *not another new algorithm, but placing all prior algorithms inside a continuous optimization space* — predecessors are corner points of that space; this paper moves into the interior.

** Where the River Most Likely Flows Next

1. *Compression-aware training.* All existing methods (including MixedDimKV) are post-hoc; the model itself was never prepared to be compressed. If pre-training or post-training explicitly targets "low-dimensional KV should also work" (along the lines of DeepSeek MLA), the resulting loss will be far smaller.

2. *Absorbing quantization into the same framework.* Quantization (KVQuant, PolarQuant) and dimension allocation are currently two orthogonal lines. The natural next step is to weld them — a token could be assigned "4 dims at 8-bit" or "8 dims at 4-bit," letting the optimizer choose. MixedDimKV has already made dimension count a free variable; making bit-width a free variable too is one more step.

3. *Dynamic budgets.* Current budgets are fixed presets. But in real deployments, query difficulty varies enormously — a simple query may need only 1% of the cache, a hard one 20%. Making "how much cache to allocate per query" itself learnable is what true adaptivity would look like.
