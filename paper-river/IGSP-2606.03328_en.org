* The Problem River

You want to prune away more than half the weights from an 8-billion-parameter model — making it faster and less memory-hungry — without retraining (too expensive). How do you decide what to cut?

The clever approach: grab a small handful of text (a few hundred passages is enough), run the model through it once, identify which weights "barely did any work" during that pass, and delete those. That small handful of text is called *calibration data*.

Here is the question: *what should go in that handful?* Does it matter if you just scrape random articles from the web? If you feed the model math problems versus news articles, does the pruned result differ?

This question lingered for years because the initial consensus was "it doesn't matter" — you're just estimating a statistic, so the source is irrelevant. But once you push for more aggressive pruning (removing 60% of weights rather than 50%), that consensus starts to crack. This river traces how a sequence of papers gradually discovered that calibration data matters enormously — and in ways that are deeply counterintuitive.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2023 | SparseGPT: First to Achieve Training-Free Pruning at Billion-Parameter Scale

*** What Problem It Saw

Before SparseGPT, pruning had an iron rule: prune, then retrain, or the model is broken. For a model with tens of billions of parameters, retraining is absurdly expensive. The existing "prune without retraining" exact methods — the OBS lineage, which uses second-order derivatives / the Hessian to find the most dispensable weights — had only been validated on models one to two orders of magnitude smaller (CNNs, BERT). Applying them at GPT scale was impossible: just computing the inverse Hessian would exhaust GPU memory.

*** Its Solution

SparseGPT reframes pruning as a *regression problem*: after removing a weight, let the remaining weights make a small correction to compensate for the loss. The key trick is that all rows in a layer *share* a single approximate inverse Hessian, processed column-by-column in a fixed order — so only one copy needs to live in memory, and it can be precomputed in one step via Cholesky decomposition. This allows a billion-parameter model to be pruned to 50–60% sparsity in a few hours with negligible accuracy loss.

Computing that Hessian requires the model to "read" some real text so that activation statistics can be estimated — that is the calibration data. SparseGPT follows prior convention and randomly samples 128 passages of 2048 tokens each from C4 (a large web crawl corpus).

*** Why It Works — and What Seed It Plants

It works because "remove one weight, nudge the rest" is far gentler than hard deletion; the loss is continuously compensated. But a seed is planted here: the authors grab C4 data offhandedly, with the implicit assumption that "it doesn't matter what you feed in — you're just estimating a statistic." This "doesn't matter" assumption will be pried open, paper by paper, in the years that follow.

** 2024 | Wanda: Pruning Reduced to a Single Forward Pass — and a Deepened Misconception

*** What Problem It Saw

Wanda targets the *weight* of SparseGPT: that Hessian inversion is both complex and requires weight updates after pruning. Could you decide which weights to delete without ever updating weights or inverting a Hessian?

*** Its Solution

Wanda's insight: a weight's importance ≈ its *magnitude* × the *activation norm* of its corresponding input. Multiply the two together, rank within each output channel, and delete the smallest. The entire process completes in a single forward pass with no weight updates. For a fair comparison, Wanda uses exactly the same 128 C4 passages as SparseGPT.

*** Why It Works — and What Seed It Plants

It works because ‖X‖ is vastly easier to estimate than a full inverse Hessian — Wanda finds that even feeding *one* calibration passage yields a pruned model with perplexity of just 7.66.

But that striking robustness to calibration-data quantity pushes the "calibration data doesn't matter" misconception to its peak. The intuition solidifies: if one passage suffices, then surely the content is irrelevant. The next paper exists to puncture that intuition.

** 2024 | Williams & Aletras: The First Serious Ask — Does Calibration Data Actually Matter?

*** What Problem It Saw

Both prior papers *assumed* calibration data was unimportant, but no one had ever verified this systematically. Williams and Aletras identified the gap: everyone was randomly sampling calibration data on the premise that "compression methods are robust to data distribution" — a belief that had never been rigorously tested.

*** Its Solution

They ran the first large-scale empirical study: swapping data sources, tasks, and models, testing both quantization and pruning, and measuring downstream accuracy. The findings were counterintuitive — downstream performance *varies substantially*. And the split is telling: quantization is relatively insensitive to calibration data, while *pruning is highly sensitive*. In other words, the "doesn't matter" conclusion from the two prior pruning papers fails precisely in the pruning setting those papers were targeting.

*** Why It Works — and What Seed It Plants

It works because it uses data to falsify a belief for the first time. But it leaves two new open problems. First, if real data choices matter so much but vendors won't release pretraining corpora, where do you find the "right" data? Second — and this is what the present paper will ultimately seize on — it still measures quality with *a single average-accuracy number*.

** 2024/25 | Self-Cal: Can't Find the Data? Let the Model Generate It

*** What Problem It Saw

Picking up the first loose end from the prior paper: good calibration data should resemble pretraining data as closely as possible, yet the pretraining corpora of frontier models are almost universally withheld. Random web text may be unrepresentative and harm performance, and the "real" data simply isn't available.

*** Its Solution

Self-Cal's idea is elegant: *stop searching — let the model write its own.* Starting from a beginning-of-sequence token (BOS), the model autoregressively samples text. The linguistic structure, vocabulary distribution, and world knowledge absorbed during pretraining naturally flow into this self-generated text, making it a near-native match to the pretraining distribution. A temperature schedule is used: higher temperature early for diversity, cooling later for coherence. Across five models and four compression methods, self-calibration often matches or exceeds real-data calibration, with pruning benefiting the most.

*** Why It Works — and What Seed It Plants

It works because "the closest approximation to the pretraining data is the model itself." But it remains a *single source with a single generation distribution* — generated in bulk and judged by a single average-accuracy number. Low-quality samples inevitably slip through; that is the opening for the next paper.

** 2025 | SGS: Self-Generated Data Varies — Add a Filter

*** What Problem It Saw

Self-Cal generates text indiscriminately, but not all generated text is equal. SGS (Ji et al., "Beware of Calibration Data") argues that high-perplexity samples indicate patterns the model never learned well — using them as calibration data is actively harmful. This problem is amplified at higher sparsity levels, where calibration data has an outsized influence.

*** Its Solution

Add a *perplexity filter* on top of Self-Cal's self-generation: compute perplexity for every generated sample, discard the top k% (empirically 10–20% works best), and keep only the samples the model "knows best." On strong models such as LLaMA-3, this gives another measurable lift on top of Wanda, OWL, and similar pruning methods.

*** Why It Works — and What Seed It Plants

It works because it operationalizes "resembles pretraining data" as "low perplexity" and filters accordingly. But it still operates with a *single source and a single optimized signal (perplexity)*. The present paper will argue that the perplexity signal is itself *anti-correlated* with certain capability dimensions — so optimizing it is robbing Peter to pay Paul. This is the wall every prior approach runs into.

* Frontier Extension

** 2026 | IGSP: Stop Looking at the Average — It Erases the Trade-offs

*** What Problem It Saw

The paper first reproduces the Williams & Aletras finding of "modest calibration-data impact" — then cuts it open. That "modest" impact is a *statistical illusion produced by averaging*. Decomposing performance into *General / Commonsense / Code / Math* dimensions reveals enormous divergence.

The sharpest finding is the *opposite-sign trade-off*: calibration-data perplexity is *positively* correlated with general-capability retention, but *negatively* correlated with math and code capability retention. In other words, the perplexity-lowering strategy of SGS helps general capabilities while quietly degrading math and code. Any single-source, single-signal approach — no matter how carefully filtered — cannot escape this bind.

The paper also shows that SGS's "vocabulary-level entropy" predictor is weak: phrase-level *Aggregation* achieves |ρ| = 0.79, while vocabulary entropy stays below 0.2.

*** Its Solution

Two empirically validated principles:
- *P1 Aggregation*: minimize 4-gram repetition to prevent repetitive patterns from biasing activation estimates — select samples via greedy unique n-gram coverage.
- *P2 Distortion Balance*: *balance* perplexity across capability dimensions by setting a perplexity band for each dimension rather than uniformly minimizing it.

These principles are instantiated as *IGSP (Information-Guided Self-Calibration for Pruning)*: using only the evaluation taxonomy and the base model itself, IGSP automatically constructs a multi-source calibration set. Two steps — first, build per-capability candidate pools (by slicing corpora or prompting the model to generate domain-specific text); then greedily select samples within each dimension's perplexity band, at complexity O(B·|P|).

Results on LLaMA-3.1-8B with SparseGPT at 60% sparsity: uniform multi-source mixing achieves 58.8% overall capability retention, surpassing the best single source (MetaMath, 50.0%) and the C4 default (40.0%); IGSP improves over Self-Cal by +2.4 and over SGS by +4.8.

Underlying all of this is a broader framework: the authors apply OIT (Objective Information Theory, Xu et al. 2023/2024) to construct a seven-dimensional information portrait of calibration data (Volume, Scope, Diversity, Granularity, Aggregation, Distortion, Mismatch). Empirically, *only Aggregation and Distortion* predict capability retention robustly — P1 and P2 correspond directly to these two dimensions.

Note: This paper is a 2026 preprint currently under review. The above reflects the authors' own reported conclusions and has not been independently reproduced. As of now, this is the most recent development in this line of research.

* Insights

On the surface this river is a technical iteration on "how to choose calibration data for pruning." Beneath it is a *progressive refinement of cognitive granularity*:

1. *From belief to empiricism to dimensional decomposition.* The initial "calibration data doesn't matter" claim was an unverified belief (SparseGPT/Wanda). Williams & Aletras falsified it empirically. This paper then decomposes "matters" down to capability dimensions and discovers that *the same operation has opposite effects on different capabilities*. Each step replaces one coarse judgment with finer, sometimes mutually contradictory sub-judgments.

2. *Averages are a cognitive anesthetic.* The deepest lesson of this entire line of work: when you use an aggregate metric (average accuracy, a single perplexity number) to view the world, trade-offs cancel out silently and mislead you into thinking "roughly the same" or "not sensitive." All the explanatory power of this paper flows from one simple move — *don't average; decompose*. That lesson extends far beyond pruning.

3. *"Optimize a single proxy" is a trap.* SGS compressed "resembles pretraining data" into a single knob — low perplexity — and then discovered that knob turns in opposite directions for different capabilities. When a proxy metric has inconsistent relationships with multiple true objectives, optimizing it inevitably robs one to pay another. The way out is not to find a better single knob, but to *acknowledge multiple objectives and balance among them*.

Where the field is most likely to go next: from "uniform multi-source mixing" toward *task-adaptive mixing ratios* — dynamically determining the proportion of calibration data from each capability dimension based on which capabilities matter most for a given deployment; and extending the "decompose by dimension + information portrait" framework from pruning to quantization, distillation, and other compression paradigms.

* Takeaways

For how to do research and how to find good problems, this river offers three immediately actionable handles:

- *Watch for the default assumptions nobody bothered to validate.* SparseGPT grabbed C4 offhandedly; Wanda said "one passage is enough"; the whole field assumed "calibration data doesn't matter" for years — a belief everyone held but no one rigorously tested. That kind of "treated as common knowledge, never systematically checked" premise is a gold mine. You can deliberately inventory a field's unexamined axioms and go poke them one by one.

- *When a conclusion is "negligible effect," first suspect that trade-offs were averaged away.* The paper's most elegant move is to reproduce "negligible effect" and then demonstrate "large effect" at a finer grain. You can apply this to any "X has no significant effect on Y" claim: ask whether splitting Y into sub-dimensions reveals winners and losers whose effects cancel in the aggregate.

- *Don't trust a single proxy metric until you've verified its correlation direction with every true sub-objective.* Before using perplexity (or any score) as an optimization target, plot its correlation with each sub-objective you actually care about. If any correlation is opposite-sign, you know single-objective optimization will fail and you need to shift to multi-objective balancing. Spending ten minutes building a "proxy metric vs. each real objective" correlation table before you start optimizing can save you from endlessly robbing Peter to pay Paul later.
