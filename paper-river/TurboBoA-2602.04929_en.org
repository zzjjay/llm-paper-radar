* The Problem River

Take a model with hundreds of billions of parameters, compress it down to 2–4 bits, and still have it speak coherently — all without the luxury of backpropagation, using only a small handful of calibration samples, and finishing in a matter of hours.

Where is the difficulty? The same pit has trapped every generation for thirty years: the moment you *round* a weight to the nearest integer, an error appears; that error amplifies layer by layer as it propagates through the network; by the time it reaches the output, the model goes off the rails. So the problem is not "how to round" — it is "after rounding, how do the remaining weights compensate for the error introduced."

Each generation has looked one level deeper into what "compensation" really means — from local compensation of a single weight, to joint compensation within an entire layer, to cross-layer dependency compensation, and finally to *cross-layer compensation that is also fast*. TurboBoA stands at the very end of this line.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1993 | OBS: Remove One Neuron — How Do the Rest Recover?

*** What Problem Did It See?

People in the nineties wanted to prune neural networks down to size. The most naive approach was "delete the weight with the smallest magnitude." But Hassibi and Stork discovered that small magnitude does not mean unimportant. What matters is not how large the weight is, but *how much the overall loss rises when you remove it*.

*** Its Solution

Use a second-order Taylor expansion. Near the optimum, the loss function can be described by the Hessian matrix, which encodes its curvature. Removing a weight corresponds to taking a step in weight space; how much the loss increases equals that step's direction multiplied by the curvature. Therefore:

- Select the weight whose removal causes the smallest increase in loss
- After removing it, *distribute the perturbation that removal introduced across the remaining weights* — again computed optimally via the Hessian

This is the "Optimal Brain Surgeon" — like surgery: cut out a piece, and the surrounding tissue adjusts in concert.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pitfall It Left Behind)

Second-order information captures the coupling between weights. But OBS was designed for pruning small networks; nobody had thought about whether it could generalize to quantization (converting floats to ints), nor about what happens when the network reaches hundreds of millions of parameters and the Hessian matrix simply cannot fit in memory.

Those two pitfalls would not be filled for another thirty years.

** 2022 | OBQ: Turn "Pruning" into "Quantization"

*** What Problem Did It See?

Pruning sets a weight to zero; quantization *rounds* a weight to the nearest integer on a discrete grid. Both are fundamentally "perturbing one weight" — so why couldn't the OBS compensation framework apply to quantization?

At the time, mainstream quantization methods (QAT) all required backpropagation fine-tuning, which was impossibly slow for large models. Frantar asked: could we skip fine-tuning and use the OBS closed-form compensation directly?

*** Its Solution

Replace OBS's "deletion" with "quantization" — at each step, pick one weight, round it to the nearest integer, then use the Hessian to compute how the remaining unquantized weights should be adjusted to absorb the perturbation. Process weights one by one, in order of smallest quantization error first.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pitfall It Left Behind)

OBQ was the first to make "high-accuracy quantization without training" possible. But it had a fatal scalability problem: quantizing one weight requires updating all remaining weights and the Hessian, giving complexity on the order of O(d^4). It could handle BERT-base (~100M parameters) but was helpless against OPT-175B (175B parameters) — the Hessian alone wouldn't fit in memory.

The next paper attacked exactly this scalability problem.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Make OBQ Run on Billion-Scale Models

*** What Problem Did It See?

OBQ's bottleneck was "sorting by error to pick the next weight to quantize." That sorting meant each column was processed in a different order, so Hessian information could not be shared across columns. The Frantar team discovered a counterintuitive fact:

*For large language models, the quantization order simply does not matter.*

Quantizing left to right in a fixed order produced almost identical results to quantizing in the "optimal error order." This single observation untied every knot.

*** Its Solution

Three ideas combined to accelerate OBQ by orders of magnitude:

1. *Fixed order*: all out-channels are quantized in the same order, so the Hessian inverse can be computed once and shared across all channels.
2. *Cholesky decomposition*: the numerically unstable Hessian-inverse updates are rewritten as a stable lower-triangular recurrence via Cholesky.
3. *Lazy batch update*: instead of updating everything immediately after each column, accumulate a batch of columns and then update in bulk, fully utilizing GPU bandwidth.

Result: OPT-175B finished on 4 GPUs in four hours; 4-bit quantization with almost no accuracy drop. GPTQ became the de facto standard for billion-scale LLM quantization.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pitfall It Left Behind)

GPTQ's core assumption is *layer-wise independence* — each layer is quantized independently, with the goal of minimizing that layer's output error. At 4-bit, the per-layer reconstruction error is small enough to be negligible, so this assumption is nearly painless.

But when people pushed toward 2-bit, the assumption broke. Every layer accumulates a small error; those errors pile up inside the attention module — Q multiplied by K, then softmax, then multiplied by V — and the nonlinearities amplify the small errors into a large catastrophe. GPTQ is blind to this *cross-layer coupling* because its Hessian is computed layer by layer.

The next paper lifts its gaze to look at the entire attention block.

** 2024 | BoA: Quantize the Entire Attention Block as One Unit

*** What Problem Did It See?

GPTQ computes a separate Hessian for each of the four projection matrices Q, K, V, and O and quantizes them independently. But these four matrices *are intrinsically coupled inside attention*: Q's error is amplified by K's error (because they form a dot product), then distorted by softmax, then propagated through V.

Optimizing each one independently does not sum to the global optimum. This is why GPTQ "suddenly collapses" at INT2 — it is not that single-layer quantization failed; it is that the coupled error across the entire attention module exploded.

*** Its Solution

Define an *attention-aware Hessian* — this Hessian does not measure "the output error of this one layer," but rather "the second-order sensitivity of the entire attention module's output to each weight." Under this objective, quantizing Q automatically accounts for the reactions of K, V, and O.

But this new Hessian is enormous; storing it naively blows up GPU memory. BoA uses several techniques to make it tractable:
- *Hessian relaxation*: retain the most strongly coupled terms; discard secondary ones.
- *Head-wise simultaneous quantization*: Q/K/V/O within the same head are quantized jointly; across heads they remain independent.

Result: at INT2, BoA decisively outperforms GPTQ; combined with outlier suppression (in the style of AWQ) it achieves SOTA.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pitfall It Left Behind)

BoA correctly "sees the coupling." But the price is a return to OBQ-era scalability problems — for accuracy, it must quantize *one out-channel at a time, serially*, updating the entire attention block's state after each channel.

The batch-update advantage of GPTQ is gone. Quantizing a LLaMA-70B takes tens of hours — accurate, yes, but unusable in engineering practice.

BoA also carries a hidden risk: when quantizing one layer, it *assumes the input is error-free*. In reality, previous layers have already been quantized and the input distribution has already been perturbed. BoA does not account for this accumulated error.

The next paper comes to fix both of these pitfalls.

** 2026 | TurboBoA: More Than 3× Faster than BoA, with Error Propagation Fixed Along the Way

*** What Problem Did It See?

The TurboBoA team (the same group of researchers) profiled BoA and identified:

1. *Serial channel quantization* is the dominant bottleneck — why not batch-process channels the way GPTQ does? BoA is serial because attention-aware compensation is nonlinearly coupled, leaving no closed-form solution.
2. BoA assumes the current layer's input is clean, but *errors introduced by previously quantized layers have already contaminated the input* — none of this upstream pollution is compensated.
3. Throughout the iterative quantization process, the quantization grid (which integer grid points are available) is never updated, missing opportunities for precision improvement.

*** Its Solution

Three corresponding fixes:

1. *Joint multi-channel quantization + closed-form compensation*: replace "quantize one channel at a time" with "quantize a batch at once." The key contribution is deriving a closed-form expression that computes the optimal compensation for an entire batch of channels in one shot — no serial iteration required. This single change delivers more than 3× speedup.

2. *Cross-layer error correction*: when quantizing layer L, explicitly model the input perturbations introduced by quantizing layers 1 ... L−1, so the compensation produces a correction that truly accounts for upstream errors.

3. *Adaptive grid + coordinate descent*: after each joint update, realign the quantization grid (let the step size track the current weight distribution), then apply a small coordinate-descent refinement pass.

Result: same accuracy as BoA (in fact marginally better), but more than 3× faster. Combined with outlier suppression, TurboBoA achieves SOTA on both the weight-only and weight-activation quantization tracks.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pitfall It Left Behind)

At its core, TurboBoA transplants GPTQ's *batching philosophy* into BoA's *cross-layer coupling framework*, and adds the upstream-error compensation that BoA left out. This is engineering maturity — neither accuracy nor speed is sacrificed.

Remaining open pitfalls:
- The closed-form compensation relies on certain approximations (which terms are dropped, and how much error do they introduce?). Can the approximation be tightened further?
- Coupling *between* attention blocks (not just within a block) has not been addressed. In other words, only Q/K/V/O coupling within one block is captured; global cross-layer coupling spanning the full transformer remains untouched.
- This approach has not been validated on non-attention architectures (purely MLP models, Mamba/SSM variants, etc.).

* Frontier Extensions

TurboBoA is a February 2026 paper and currently represents the frontier of this line. A glance at adjacent branches:

- *Block Rotation for MXFP4* (arxiv 2511.04214, 2025): a different philosophy — instead of computing elaborate compensation, apply an orthogonal rotation to the weights first to spread outliers evenly, then quantize. The philosophies differ: BoA/TurboBoA say "accept the coupling and compensate for it"; the rotation school says "prevent the coupling from arising in the first place." The two lines may converge at some future point.
- *AWQ / SmoothQuant* and their descendants (activation-aware) continue to evolve on outlier handling and are routinely combined with BoA/TurboBoA — they are orthogonal, complementary tools.

* Insights

On the surface, this thirty-year evolutionary line looks like "quantization algorithm iteration." Beneath, it is the *progressive expansion of the compensation field of view*.

Every generation has been answering the same question: when I perturb one weight, over *how large a neighborhood of remaining weights* should I distribute that perturbation to absorb it?

- OBS: distribute to other weights within the same layer
- OBQ: the same, but adapted for quantization
- GPTQ: the same, but engineered to run on LLMs at scale
- BoA: expand to the *entire attention module* — Q/K/V/O collectively absorb the perturbation
- TurboBoA: expand further to *input perturbations across attention blocks* — upstream pollution of downstream inputs is also compensated

A prediction follows naturally: the next step will either move toward "global compensation across the entire transformer," or toward "compensation that is no longer a passive post-hoc correction but is jointly optimized with quantization decisions from the start." The former is spatial expansion; the latter is temporal unification.

At a deeper level, all of these methods accept the reality that "quantization error is unavoidable"; they differ only in "how cleverly to redistribute the error once it appears." The rotation school (Block Rotation and its lineage) takes the other road — *rearrange the weights first so that the error is minimized, then quantize*. These two lines represent the split between "compensation" and "prevention," and they will very likely fuse: first rotate to make the pit shallower, then compensate to handle whatever pit remains.

* Takeaways

For research: find an *assumption that has been repeatedly criticized* — such as GPTQ's "layer-wise independence" — and fix your attention on it. When some new application scenario (INT2, a new architecture) causes that assumption to collapse, you have your entry ticket. That is exactly what the BoA team did, and what the TurboBoA team did.

For engineering: the pendulum between accuracy and speed is nearly inevitable. Every time the community swings toward "more accurate but slower" (OBQ, BoA), someone inevitably follows with "equally accurate but much faster" (GPTQ, TurboBoA). If you see an accuracy breakthrough that nobody in engineering dares to deploy, that is your opportunity window.

The practical implication: you can — *within 12 to 18 months after any "accurate but slow" method is published, expect and actively look for its "fast version" paper*. It also means that if you are building a new method yourself, you should think clearly from day one about "how this will eventually be accelerated" — otherwise your method will live only on paper.
