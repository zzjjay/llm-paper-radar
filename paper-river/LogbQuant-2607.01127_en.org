* The Problem River

Neural-network weights are not uniformly distributed — they crowd densely around zero with a few massive outliers sprinkled in. Yet the most common quantization schemes chop the number line into equal-width bins, like casting a uniform net to catch fish of wildly different sizes: the fine mesh fills up with small fish while the big ones simply break through.

This problem grows thornier in the era of large language models: the bigger the model, the more extreme the outliers; the fewer the bits, the coarser the uniform grid; these two bad trends compound, and 4-bit quantization can reduce a model to near-random guessing. The evolutionary thread traced here follows exactly this problem — how the shape of quantization bins has evolved step by step from "one-size-fits-all equal-width grids" into "per-layer adaptive logarithmic grids."

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1993 | OBS: Before Removing a Weight, Calculate the Cost

*** What Problem It Saw

The earlier OBD (LeCun 1990) aimed to prune by importance scoring — using the second derivative of the loss with respect to each weight (the Hessian diagonal) to estimate how much the loss would rise if that weight were removed. The trouble was that it only looked at the diagonal, assuming weights were independent of one another. In practice weights are tightly coupled: remove one and the optimal values of the others shift too. OBD discarded that coupling information, producing large errors.

*** Its Solution

OBS uses the full inverse Hessian matrix. After removing weight $w_q$, rather than leaving the remaining weights untouched, a formula computes a compensation term that simultaneously nudges them to their new optimal positions. Remove one, repair the rest, layer by layer. The key insight: *quantization error need not be borne solely by the quantized weight — it can be distributed among its neighbors*.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because it turned a single-point error into a global optimization problem, "hedging away" the error. But a new pit opened immediately: computing the full inverse Hessian costs $O(n^3)$, where $n$ is the number of weights. Fine for the small networks of the 1990s, completely infeasible for large language models with tens of billions of parameters. So OBS sat largely unused for thirty years, until someone found a way to make it fast enough.

** 2016 | Miyashita et al.: Turn Multiplication into Shifts, and Match the Weight Distribution While You're at It

*** What Problem It Saw

After training, the weights and activations of neural networks almost universally form a bell curve — most values crowd near zero, and extreme large values are rare. Uniform quantization cuts this distribution with equal-width bins, like measuring rainfall with a ruler across a desert: most bins are empty, and the region that actually matters (near zero) is sliced too coarsely. At 3 bits, uniform bins are already rough enough to destroy accuracy.

*** Its Solution

Miyashita et al. said: use powers of 2 as quantization levels. $1, 1/2, 1/4, 1/8, \ldots$ are naturally dense near small values and sparse near large values. Quantizing a weight means taking the log of its absolute value and rounding — the logarithm compresses an exponential span into a linear one, naturally fitting the bell-shaped distribution. Bonus: multiplying by a power of 2 is a binary left-shift; no multiplier needed in hardware. 3-bit logarithmic quantization keeps AlexNet-scale network accuracy nearly intact.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because the bin shape aligned with the distribution shape. The new pit: the base is fixed. Base-2 means adjacent quantization levels always differ by exactly a factor of two — that "factor-of-two step" fits some layers' weight ranges well and is too coarse or too fine for others. There is no knob to turn. Deeper still: 2016 models were tens-of-millions-parameter CNNs; 2022 LLMs have 7 billion to 175 billion parameters, and the shape of parameter distributions and the severity of outliers are on an entirely different scale.

** 2022 | LLM.int8(): The Scale Changed, and So Did the Rules

*** What Problem It Saw

Dettmers et al. ran an experiment: they applied every existing quantization method — tensor-wise absmax, row-wise quantization, zero-point quantization, vector-wise quantization — to progressively larger models and measured the results. A phase transition appeared: once the parameter count crossed roughly 6.7 billion, all methods failed collectively. The cause was an unexpected emergent phenomenon — *systematic outlier features*.

Specifically: a few hidden dimensions had activation magnitudes 3 to 20 times larger than all other dimensions, appearing in nearly every layer and nearly every token. Once these outlier dimensions appeared, they stretched the quantization dynamic range so wide that normal values were crushed into indistinguishable bins — information was effectively drowned out.

*** Its Solution

Mixed-precision decomposition. Those few outlier dimensions (at most 6–7) are separated out and computed in FP16; the remaining 99.9% of dimensions are computed in INT8. The two results are added together in FP16. In effect: outliers get a first-class cabin, ordinary values ride coach. Overall memory is cut by roughly half, with almost no accuracy loss.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because it directly isolated the root cause — outliers no longer pulled the overall quantization range. The new pit: it only reaches 8-bit and does not solve the 4-bit problem. Going from 8-bit to 4-bit is a qualitative leap, because 4-bit uniform bins are so coarse that even without outliers, the quantization error on normal weights is large enough to make the model's behavior collapse. LLM.int8() gave us "usable 8-bit quantization," but said nothing about how to survive at 4-bit.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Borrow OBS's Inverse Hessian, Make It Run on Large Models

*** What Problem It Saw

GPTQ's immediate predecessor is OBC (Frantar et al., NeurIPS 2022). OBC applied OBS's second-order compensation idea to quantization: after quantizing one weight, the inverse Hessian matrix is used to update all remaining weights so they absorb the quantization error. The results were excellent — but OBC greedily selected "which weight to quantize next" at each step and updated the Hessian each time. For GPT-175B this would take six months. Completely impractical.

*** Its Solution

GPTQ's key finding: in large models, the benefit of a greedy ordering is negligible — because of the massive redundancy in large-model parameters, a fixed arbitrary ordering achieves nearly the same result as a carefully chosen one. Once the ordering is fixed, the inverse Hessian update for each column is identical, and the full sequence of updates can be precomputed via Cholesky decomposition and then applied in batches (lazy batching). These three changes together — fixed ordering + Cholesky precomputation + lazy batching — compress quantization time from six months to 4 GPU-hours.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

It worked because it combined the accuracy of second-order compensation with engineering scalability. Large-model 4-bit quantization finally had a practical method. The new pit: everything GPTQ does occurs inside *uniformly spaced linear quantization bins*. It solved "how to compensate for quantization error" but never asked "is the bin shape right?" For large models whose weight distributions are essentially log-normal, uniform bins remain a mismatch at very low bit-widths. Tensor-wise 4-bit linear quantization still collapses on models like Qwen3-14B — accuracy barely above random guessing.

** 2025 | LogbQuant: Let the Bin Shape Follow the Distribution

*** What Problem It Saw

The unresolved threads from two lines converge here:

- Logarithmic quantization has the right bin shape (Miyashita 2016), but a fixed base that cannot adapt to different per-layer distributions.
- GPTQ has the right error-compensation mechanism, but uniform bins.

In the tensor-wise, 4-bit setting — the scenario most relevant to consumer GPUs — linear quantization breaks down completely. Bohn et al.'s starting point: if the wrong bin shape is the core problem, replace it with an adaptive logarithmic grid.

*** Its Solution

Each layer's weight matrix independently computes its own optimal base $b$. The procedure:

1. *Energy clipping*: Sort weights by absolute value; discard the tails that together account for a fraction $\varepsilon$ of total energy, yielding an effective range $[w_{min}, w_{max}]$.
2. *Base derivation*: Solve for the base $b$ such that this range accommodates exactly $q_{max}$ logarithmic levels: $b = (w_{max}/w_{min})^{1/(q_{max}-1)}$.
3. *GPTQ calibration*: Plug this logarithmic grid into GPTQ's per-weight compensation pipeline; error is still absorbed by second-order updates.

Result: each layer's bin shape automatically conforms to that layer's weight distribution, rather than forcing every layer to use the same ruler.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

The intuition for why it works: at 4-bit, there are only 16 bins. A uniform grid distributes these 16 bins including the tails where almost no weights reside; a logarithmic grid concentrates bins in the small-value region where weight density is actually high. Empirical results are striking: 4-bit linear quantization drops Qwen3-14B accuracy to 32.93% (near random), while LogbQuant reaches 66.62%, compresses memory to 10.10 GB (35% of the original), and runs on a 12 GB consumer GPU.

Pits left behind: at 8-bit, the method is actually *worse* than linear quantization — the logarithmic grid's advantage only becomes decisive at very low bit-widths. The method currently covers weight quantization only; activations remain linear. The base is derived from the weight range and does not exploit Hessian information to optimize the base itself.

* Frontier Extensions

As of 2026-07-02, LogbQuant was submitted on 2026-07-01 and is the most recent public work on this thread; no papers citing it directly have appeared yet.

Recent advances in adjacent directions:

- *LogQuant* (arXiv:2503.19950, ICLR 2025 Workshop): same logarithmic-distribution intuition, but a completely different direction — not weight quantization but KV Cache compression. Attention weights follow a log distribution over context positions; this guides which positions retain full precision and which are dropped. 2-bit KV Cache, 25% throughput gain, 60% batch-size increase. The two papers do not cite each other and address different problems.
- *AdaLog* (ECCV 2025, Wu et al.): adaptive logarithmic quantization for vision Transformers (ViT), conceptually the closest to LogbQuant, but targeting image models rather than language models. Full text not yet located; relevance to be confirmed (inferred, not a confirmed citation relationship).

* Insight

What is really happening beneath this thread is not "each generation of technology is stronger than the last" — it is two independent dimensions evolving simultaneously and then converging in LogbQuant.

**First dimension: the shape of the bins.** From 1993 to 2025, researchers gradually came to understand that quantization bins must follow the actual distribution of weights. Uniform bins are a product of engineering convenience, not a law of nature. Logarithmic bins more closely match the true shape of weight distributions, but a fixed base is yet another form of rigidity. The ultimate direction: the parameters of the bins (base, offset) should be a function of the data, not of human prior assumptions.

**Second dimension: the propagation of error.** From simple round-to-nearest (RTN), to scoring each weight's importance with the Hessian (OBS), to real-time compensation of quantization error (OBC/GPTQ). Quantization error went from "borne alone" to "shared among neighbors." This is in essence a local optimization strategy, sharing something in spirit with JPEG's quantization tables: not making every individual value precise, but making the overall output as close to the original as possible.

Where is the next step most likely to go? Two directions:

1. *End-to-end base learning*: LogbQuant's base is derived from the weight range; no gradient has been used to optimize it. If the base (and other quantization parameters) were made learnable variables and jointly trained, the accuracy ceiling could be pushed higher.
2. *Activation quantization*: Only weights have been addressed so far; activations remain linear. During inference, the activation distribution is dynamic. Whether the adaptive advantage of logarithmic bins can be extended to activations is an open question.

* Takeaway

This thread has a very clear research methodology: *each new paper identifies the specific failure mode of the previous method in a specific scenario* — not a vague claim that "accuracy is insufficient."

Miyashita says: "Under this distribution shape, uniform bins give small values too coarse a grid; it collapses at 3-bit."
LLM.int8() says: "Beyond 6B parameters, a few specific hidden dimensions systematically become 20× larger than the rest, and those dimensions wreck the overall quantization."
GPTQ says: "Greedy ordering in OBQ barely helps for large models, but the Hessian compensation itself is extremely valuable — the computation just needs to be redesigned."
LogbQuant says: "In tensor-wise 4-bit quantization, the step size of each linear bin is so large that small values become nearly indistinguishable; accuracy collapses toward random guessing."

This means: *finding a method's failure scenario is more valuable than finding its average improvement*. The failure scenario precisely points to what the next paper should solve. If you are doing research, instead of spending time proving your method beats baselines across various benchmarks, first describe precisely which specific failure mode of the prior method you are resolving. That is what will actually make readers remember your paper.
