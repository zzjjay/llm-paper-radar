* The River of Problems

Can a large model with tens of billions of parameters have half its weights discarded outright — without any retraining — and still lose almost no accuracy?

The difficulty lies in two constraints that pull against each other. First, the model is enormous: retraining costs thousands of GPUs over weeks, and nobody can afford that bill, so you are forced to operate one-shot — prune once and deploy. Second, remove one critical weight and the model may collapse — yet you cannot enumerate all possible combinations when there are tens of billions of weights to consider.

So the story of the past decade is really different answers to the same question: *Without retraining and without exhaustive search, how do you decide which weights are expendable?* Each generation found a smarter importance-scoring function, and the next generation discovered what that score still failed to see. This river is the evolutionary history of importance scoring.

* Evolutionary Narrative

We begin with the oldest question: how do you know a weight can be removed?

** 1989–1993 | OBD/OBS: How Much Do You Lose by Removing One Weight?

*** What problem did it see?

Before OBD, pruning was largely intuitive — "this number is small, so it probably doesn't matter." But small does not mean useless. A weight with a tiny absolute value might sit at a sensitive point in the loss landscape; remove it and the model collapses. Conversely, a large weight might have others that can compensate for its absence; remove it and nothing happens. Judging by magnitude alone is judging by appearances.

*** Its solution

OBD/OBS asked a more fundamental question: *If I remove this weight, by how much does the total loss increase?* It uses the second-order derivative of the loss with respect to that weight — the Hessian, which can be thought of as "how steep the loss surface is at this point" — to estimate that increase. The steeper the surface, the more critical the weight, the less it should be touched. OBS went further: after each removal it would adjust the surviving weights to compensate for the incurred loss.

*** Why it worked (and what trap it left behind)

It worked because it was the first to upgrade "importance" from "look at magnitude" to "look at impact." But it buried a fatal trap: the Hessian is an N×N matrix, where N is the parameter count. Tractable for a network with a few hundred parameters; for a model with tens of billions — the matrix alone cannot fit in memory, let alone be inverted. This "intractable computation" curse sent OBS into exile as networks scaled up.

** 2015 | Magnitude Pruning: Can't Compute It? Fall Back to the Simplest Thing

*** What problem did it see?

As deep networks grew larger, the second-order machinery of OBS became completely infeasible. Engineers needed something that could actually run on real, large networks, even if it was coarser.

*** Its solution

Han et al. returned to the most primitive criterion: *weights with small absolute values are unimportant — just remove them.* Sort by |w|, prune the smallest batch, then retrain to let the network recover.

*** Why it worked (and what trap it left behind)

It worked because it was simple enough to be foolproof and scalable to any size, and combined with retraining it delivered meaningful compression. But it carried two traps: first, it threw away the hard-won "look at impact" insight that OBS had established; second, and more critically, it *depends on retraining* — in the LLM era the cost of a retraining run is simply unacceptable. The question then became: can we prune accurately without computing the Hessian and without retraining?

** 2023 | SparseGPT: Bringing the Soul of Hessian Back to Large Models

*** What problem did it see?

In the GPT era, pruning by |w| alone — with no retraining allowed — caused unacceptable accuracy loss. SparseGPT identified the root cause: Magnitude pruning throws away the OBS wisdom of "repair the damage after each removal." It prunes and walks away.

*** Its solution

SparseGPT narrowed the problem from "global retraining" to "layer-wise reconstruction": forget the whole model; just require that *each layer's output after pruning matches its output before pruning as closely as possible.* At this reduced scope, the Hessian only needs to be computed within a single layer, making it feasible. After each weight is removed, Hessian information is used to adjust the remaining weights in that layer to compensate. One shot, no retraining.

*** Why it worked (and what trap it left behind)

It was the first demonstration that a model at the 10B+ scale can be pruned one-shot to 50% sparsity with almost no accuracy drop — a landmark result. But it inherited a scaled-down version of OBS's old ailment: it still requires computing and inverting a Hessian matrix, taking roughly 90 minutes for LLaMA-2-70B; and it *modifies the weights*. That left an opening — is it possible to prune accurately without even touching the Hessian, without changing any weights at all?

** 2023 | Wanda: What Really Matters Is "Weight × the Signal It Receives"

*** What problem did it see?

Wanda stared at SparseGPT's scoring formula and noticed something interesting: when the complex Hessian-inverse expression is expanded and simplified, the core reduces to *|weight| × |input activation| squared.* In other words, SparseGPT's elaborate Hessian detour is ultimately approximating something far simpler.

*** Its solution

Skip the detour. Wanda uses *|weight| × the norm of the input activation* directly as the importance score. The intuition is clean: a weight's importance depends not only on how large it is, but on *how strong the signal flowing through it is.* A large weight paired with an input channel that almost never activates isn't doing much work and can be removed. This score takes one multiplication to compute, requires no inversion, and leaves weights unchanged.

*** Why it worked (and what trap it left behind)

The results were striking: accuracy on par with SparseGPT, roughly 300× faster. It revealed a deep fact — large models already contain precise sparse sub-networks; you don't need to modify weights to find them, only to identify them. But Wanda compares scores *per output* (within each row). It does not normalize across the input-channel dimension, which means certain input channels can be wiped out column-by-column — the channel corruption problem that the next paper would address.

** 2024 | RIA: Don't Compare Absolute Scores — Compare Relative Standing

*** What problem did it see?

RIA identified a systematic bias in Wanda (and in magnitude pruning): both compare absolute scores. But different rows and different columns have intrinsically different overall magnitudes. A weight that stands out prominently within its column might still have a small absolute score simply because that column is globally small — and so it gets unfairly pruned. Pruning entire important channels this way is precisely *channel corruption*.

*** Its solution

RIA introduces *relative importance*: each weight is divided by the sum of its row and the sum of its column. The comparison then measures not "how large is your absolute value" but "how highly do you rank within your own row and column." A weight with a small absolute value is kept if it dominates its row and column. Multiplying by activation information gives the full RIA score. A companion Channel Permutation step uses the Hungarian algorithm to reorder channels so that N:M structured sparsity retains more of the truly important weights.

*** Why it worked (and what trap it left behind)

It worked because relative standing eliminates the magnitude-imbalance injustice across rows and columns, outperforming both SparseGPT and Wanda in accuracy. But RIA's field of view for each weight is a *cross shape*: the entire row and the entire column. Two things are missed. First, the weight matrix has 2D local structure; diagonal neighbors are completely invisible. Second, RIA implicitly treats rows and columns as equally important (each divided once), but experiments show the contributions are in fact asymmetric — using only row normalization gives PPL 6.83, only column normalization gives 7.49, a substantial gap.

** 2026 | CRePE: Look Beyond the Cross; Weight the Components Adaptively

*** What problem did it see?

CRePE puts RIA's "cross-shaped field of view" on the table and points out two blind spots. First, attending only to the same row and same column implicitly assumes that the only relationships in the weight matrix run horizontally and vertically, ignoring the 2D local neighborhood structure — especially diagonal neighbors. Second, row and column contributions are forced to be equal (each divided once), even though the data clearly shows asymmetry. RIA's view of a weight is like looking through a crosshair scope without seeing the surrounding terrain.

*** Its solution

Expand the cross into a patch. Beyond RIA's row term and column term, CRePE adds a *2D local neighborhood term* — incorporating information from a small region surrounding each weight. Crucially, this neighborhood term can be computed efficiently with *2D convolution* (sweeping |W| with a masked convolutional kernel), which is the origin of the name "convolution-aware." The row, column, and neighborhood contributions are then weighted by *adaptive coefficients* α, β, and γ respectively, letting the method determine which matters most rather than hard-coding equality.

One-sentence relationship to RIA: *Keep RIA's row and column terms, add a neighborhood term, and replace the fixed "divide once each" weighting with tunable α, β, γ — when α=β=1 and γ=0, CRePE reduces exactly to RIA.* RIA is a special case of CRePE.

*** Why it worked (and what trap it left behind)

It works because it restores the two degrees of freedom that RIA missed: spatial (neighborhood) and distributional (adaptive coefficients). But introducing α, β, γ, and the convolution kernel size immediately raises a new problem: how do you find the optimal combination? The naïve approach — hill-climbing search using perplexity (PPL) as the objective — requires repeated evaluations and takes roughly 11 hours.

CRePE therefore introduces a second weapon: *PHO* (Proxy-based Hyperparameter Optimization). Its key finding is that *the Gini coefficient of the post-pruning importance-score distribution — a measure of how unequally the scores are spread — correlates strongly with final perplexity (ρ=0.953).* Gini is extremely fast to compute, so PHO uses it as a proxy metric, evaluates only on the first-layer q_proj, and runs CMA-ES search, compressing 11 hours down to roughly 20 minutes (a 30× speedup). The hyperparameters found transfer across models within the same family: search once, reuse everywhere.

* Frontier Extensions

Looking in the other direction — where does this line go next?

The most direct branch is *PermLLM* (Zou et al., 2025, NeurIPS). It targets not the RIA/CRePE scoring function but RIA's companion *Channel Permutation*. PermLLM argues that RIA's channel reordering is a *hand-crafted heuristic* — greedily distribute important channels across blocks, then refine with linear assignment — and hand-crafted heuristics tend to give suboptimal solutions. PermLLM's answer is to make the *permutation itself learnable*, allowing the model to discover better channel arrangements under the N:M sparsity constraint.

This reveals that the river has now split into two streams: one continues refining "how to score individual weights" (where CRePE lives), and the other optimizes "how to arrange the retained weights into the structures hardware demands" (where PermLLM lives). The two streams are orthogonal and can be stacked.

As of mid-2026, CRePE is the latest advance on the "scoring function" stream, extending the field of view from the 1D cross to the 2D neighborhood.

* Insights

Reading the whole line together, the surface story is "scoring functions keep getting more complex," but three deeper shifts are actually underway:

1. *From "look at the weight itself" to "look at the weight's situation."* OBD examines curvature, Magnitude examines size — both focus on the weight's *intrinsic properties.* From Wanda onward, the focus shifts to *context*: how strong is the signal you receive (activation)? How do you rank among your neighbors (RIA's relativity)? What does the patch around you look like (CRePE's neighborhood)? A weight's importance is increasingly determined by its relationships to its surroundings rather than by what it is in isolation. This is a shift from atomism to relationalism.

2. *Computational cost has driven the rhythm of the entire line.* Nearly every major turn in this river was forced by "can't afford to compute this." OBS exits because the Hessian won't fit. Magnitude is eventually displaced because retraining is too expensive. SparseGPT resurrects Hessian by shrinking it to a single layer. Wanda explodes in speed by discovering that the matrix inversion can be dropped entirely. *"What scoring function is accurate" was never the only question; "what scoring function is affordable" is the real filter.* CRePE chose 2D convolution precisely because convolutions are cheap on hardware; PHO chose Gini as a proxy precisely because it computes quickly.

3. *Complexity oscillates, and the "special-case" rhetoric recurs.* This line is not monotonically growing in complexity — SparseGPT is complex, Wanda actually simplifies, then RIA and CRePE add structure again. CRePE's use of "α=β=1, γ=0 reduces to RIA" to position itself is a rhetorically smart move: *I am not overturning you; I am the more general framework that contains you.* This is almost the standard argumentative posture for each successive paper in the field.

Where does the line go next? Two signals are clear. First, CRePE itself introduced the problem of hyperparameter search, then patched it with PHO (the Gini proxy) — a sign that the "scoring function" path is approaching diminishing returns, and future gains may come more from *automatically learning or searching for the scoring function* than from hand-designing yet another formula (PermLLM making the permutation "learnable" is already this signal). Second, the two orthogonal lines — scoring (what to prune) and structural permutation (how to pack survivors into hardware-required patterns) — will eventually be unified by some work into a single end-to-end learnable objective.

* Takeaways

The most directly actionable lessons this evolutionary line offers for doing research are three, all immediately usable:

1. *Stare at formulas and look for what got "cancelled out."* Wanda's most elegant move was expanding SparseGPT's complex expression and finding that the core was a simple product — the detour others took was precisely the opportunity. This means that whenever you read a state-of-the-art method, you can ask: *Is it using an expensive approximation to compute something that is actually simple?* If yes, that is where the next paper lives.

2. *Make your predecessor a special case, not an opponent.* CRePE's "α=β=1, γ=0 reduces to RIA" grounds its own contribution — which teaches you that rather than arguing "I'm better than you," design your method so "you are a special case of me." In practice this means *actively looking for the parameters current SOTA methods have hard-coded* (e.g., RIA's implicit equal weight on row and column), freeing them as tunable degrees of freedom. That is often a paper.

3. *Confirm "affordable" before pursuing "accurate."* The line repeatedly proves that no matter how good a scoring function is, if it can't be computed it loses. This means that when designing a method, treat "what is the cost at the target hardware scale" as a *first-class constraint*, not a post-hoc optimization — CRePE chose convolution and PHO chose Gini both by asking "is it cheap?" before asking "is it accurate?"
