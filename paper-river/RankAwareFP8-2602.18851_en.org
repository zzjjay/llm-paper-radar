* One-liner

Attention logits blow up — people noticed, patched it (QK-LayerNorm), then used the patch to stabilize low-precision training (FP8 delayed scaling), and now this paper says: "Stop patching. I can compute an upper bound on logits directly from the weight matrices and hand you a prior scale factor."

* Reading backwards: five layers to the root

** Layer 1 — The phenomenon is named: Henry et al. 2020, "QK Normalization"

The earliest observation is straightforward: when training a transformer, the norms of the Q and K matrices grow indefinitely, with nothing to rein them in.

Why nothing? Because softmax has a mathematical property — it is invariant to a constant shift. Add 1000 to every logit and the softmax output is unchanged. Training loss therefore never penalizes logit growth, but large logits drive the softmax output toward a near one-hot distribution: one position close to 1, all others close to 0. Once that happens, gradients nearly vanish and training stalls.

Henry et al. offered a two-line fix: normalize Q and K separately before computing the dot product. Simple — but at the time nobody gave a systematic account of why the fix had to exist.

** Layer 2 — The phenomenon is theorized: Zhai et al. 2023, "Attention Entropy Collapse"

Zhai and colleagues gave the phenomenon an academic name: *attention entropy collapse*. The entropy of the attention distribution shrinks monotonically, eventually collapsing to a one-hot distribution.

Their key contribution was not a new fix but a connection between the phenomenon and the *spectral norms of the Q and K projection matrices*. In other words, "logit explosion" is fundamentally governed by the largest singular values of two weight matrices.

This planted a seed: the right place to intervene is the spectral properties of the weights, not the activations observed after the fact. Zhai's work remained theoretical, however, and did not translate into an engineering recipe.

** Layer 3 — Hitting the wall at scale: Dehghani et al. 2023, ViT-22B

When Google trained ViT-22B (a 22B-parameter Vision Transformer), training collapsed — exactly along the chain identified above: attention logit explosion → entropy collapse → training divergence.

Their fix was *qk-layernorm*: a LayerNorm applied independently to Q and K. This is essentially Henry 2020 re-engineered, but reproducing and validating it at 22B parameters gave it far more weight.

ViT-22B put "attention logit growth" on the mainstream agenda. What had been a small-model curiosity became something you had to solve before you could train at scale.

** Layer 4 — The phenomenon is systematically quantified: Wortsman et al. 2023, "Small-scale Proxies"

ICLR 2024 Oral. Wortsman et al. tackled a meta-problem: "We can't afford to train 22B models — how do we study this instability?"

Their approach was elegant: use high learning rates on small models to reproduce the same instability, then measure systematically. The findings were quantitative:

- *Every run where any attention logit exceeded 1e4 diverged.*
- The maximum logit value can be *extrapolated* across model sizes — a fit on 1.2B predicted that 4.8B would diverge at LR=1e-2; they ran it, and it did.
- qk-layernorm allowed a 1.2B model to train stably even at LR=0.3.

This layer turned "logit explosion" from engineering folklore into a *predictable failure mode*. The work still lived entirely in FP16/BF16, however, and did not touch FP8.

** Layer 5 — Numerical precision enters: FP8-LM (Peng et al. 2023) + NVIDIA Transformer Engine

The H100 GPU arrived with FP8 compute. The price: dynamic range of only ±448 (E4M3) or ±57344 (E5M2). In the FP16 era a single global loss scale sufficed; in FP8 every tensor needs its own scale factor.

Two paths for computing that scale factor:

1. *Just-in-time scaling*: compute FP32 output first, observe amax, then re-scale to FP8. Downside: an extra data pass, erasing FP8's throughput advantage.
2. *Delayed scaling*: record amax history over the past N steps and use it to predict the next step. This is the default in the NVIDIA Transformer Engine.

Delayed scaling rests on an assumption: training statistics are stationary between adjacent steps. That holds most of the time. But there is one lethal scenario: *transients* — phases where activations spike suddenly, such as the activation outliers that appear after ~200B tokens (also observed by Fishman 2024). When a spike hits, the historical amax is still low, the scale factor is computed too large, and the current activations overflow.

That is the precise target Emadi's paper is aiming at.

** Target paper: Emadi 2026, Rank-Aware Spectral Bounds

#+begin_quote
Can I derive an upper bound on logits *without observing activations* — directly from the weight matrices — and use that bound to compute the scale factor?
#+end_quote

That is the paper's core question.

The answer unfolds in three steps.

*** Step 1: Cast attention logits as a bilinear form

An attention logit is q^T k, which can be written as q^T (W^Q W^K^T) x, where W^Q W^K^T is the "interaction matrix" M. Intuitively, the maximum logit is controlled by the spectral norm ||M||_2.

*** Step 2: Exploit low rank to obtain a tighter concentration inequality

This is the central innovation. Classical concentration inequalities say the tail probability decays roughly as exp(-d · α²), where d is the ambient dimension. But M = W^Q W^K^T is typically *low rank* — rank r = d_h (the head dimension), and usually r ≪ d.

Emadi proves that when M is low rank the tail actually decays as exp(-d² · α² / (γ · r)), which is *8–28× tighter* than the classical bound.

This is where "rank-aware" comes from: classical concentration inequalities ignore the low-rank structure and produce bounds that are unnecessarily loose.

*** Step 3: Engineer the spectral norm computation

Computing ||W^Q W^K^T||_2 via full SVD is too expensive. The paper instead uses *implicit power iteration*: two persistent vectors are maintained and updated once per forward pass. At training start or checkpoint load, five warm-up iterations are run.

The method also handles grouped-query attention (as used in Mistral and Llama-2-70B) without physically expanding K, and is compatible with fused kernels such as FlashAttention.

*** Results

On GPT-2 XL, Mistral-7B, Llama-2-13B, and Llama-2-70B, this geometry-aware scaling:

- *Does not overflow during transient scenarios* (delayed scaling does)
- *Matches delayed scaling on MMLU accuracy*
- Requires no activation observation — the scale factor is fully prior

** Follow-on: QuacK (2511.21377) and Spectral Alignment (2510.04202)

Emadi answers the question "how large is the logit in absolute terms?" The newest direction asks a different question: "how much does the logit *change* between two steps?" — QuacK (Controlling changes to attention logits) directly bounds this change using the spectral norm.

This line of work is still developing. From Henry 2020 to today, six years, the direction is consistent: moving from *reactive firefighting* toward *prior geometric guarantees*.

* What is really happening beneath this line of work

On the surface this looks like "a trick for FP8 training." Underneath is a larger cognitive shift:

*From measurement-driven to geometry-driven*

- Measurement-driven: observe activations to compute scale; observe logits and add a norm. Requires data to flow through first, reacts slowly, fails during transients.
- Geometry-driven: derive the dynamic range directly from the algebraic properties of the weight matrices. The scale factor is known before the forward pass, with no lag and no reliance on stationarity assumptions.

Low-precision training is forcing this shift, because FP8's dynamic range is too narrow for a feedback loop of "measure then adjust" to close in time. The paper's real contribution is not the 8–28× tightness figure — it is *providing a geometric path that closes the scale-factor loop without ever touching an activation.*

* Where this line of work might go next

1. *4-bit training.* FP8 is already tight; FP4 (the MX family) is tighter still. Geometry-driven scale factors may become mandatory rather than optional.
2. *Spectral norm + training dynamics coupled.* Emadi currently computes the spectral norm of the current W. If one could predict how ||W||_2 will evolve over the next step (in the spirit of what QuacK is doing), scale factors could be issued K steps ahead.
3. *Extension to MLP/projection layers.* This paper covers only attention QK. The GEMMs inside MLP blocks face the same overflow problem; the same rank-aware reasoning should apply.
4. *Compatibility with MLA (Multi-Latent Attention).* QK-LayerNorm cannot be used with MLA (which never materializes explicit Q and K). Emadi's method depends on the weight product W^Q W^K^T; whether this product can be meaningfully defined under MLA requires investigation.
