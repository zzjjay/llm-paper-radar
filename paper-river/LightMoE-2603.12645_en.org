* TL;DR

In one sentence: MoE scales models up but won't fit in VRAM — this thread traces how researchers went from "dropping experts," to "merging experts," to this paper's "replacing experts," learning step by step to "save memory without discarding knowledge."

The real protagonist is not LightMoE but a persistent problem: *sparse activation saves compute, not memory*. Mixtral costs only ~13B per token, yet still requires all 47B in VRAM. Since 2024, the community has tried compressing experts in waves, and each time the same ghost strikes back — the knowledge you discard looks unimportant until the moment it matters, and by then it can't be recovered.

* Evolutionary Narrative: How the Problem Grew

** 0. Starting Point: How MoE Fell into the "Memory" Trap

To understand this thread, start with one counter-intuitive fact: MoE was not invented to save money — it was invented to scale models up while activating only a small fraction per token. Shazeer proposed sparse MoE in 2017, Lepikhin brought it to industrial scale with GShard in 2020, and in early 2024 Mistral released Mixtral 8x7B — 8 experts, 47B total parameters, but only 2 experts (~13B) activated per token.

From a compute-graph perspective this is genius: you run 13B worth of arithmetic and get 47B worth of capacity. But practitioners quickly hit an unglamorous truth: *VRAM must hold all 47B, not just the active 13B*. Experts sit idle most of the time, but you cannot swap them to disk — the router decides which expert handles which token on the fly, and by the time you page the weights back in, latency has already blown up.

The problem therefore becomes: *Can we keep fewer experts in VRAM without seriously damaging model capability?*

** 1. NAEE: The First Instinct Is to "Cut" (2024.02)

The most natural solution: since not all experts are important, identify the least important ones and delete them outright. Lu et al.'s NAEE at ACL'24 — "Not All Experts Are Equal" — takes exactly this route.

The approach is thoroughly engineering-minded: run a small calibration dataset through the model, enumerate all combinations of retaining r experts per layer, and keep whichever combination minimizes reconstruction error. At the scale of Mixtral 8x7B (choosing 6 of 8 experts), enumeration is perfectly tractable, and accuracy loss is mild.

But NAEE leaves two latent problems:

- *Enumeration does not scale.* When fine-grained MoE architectures such as DeepSeek-MoE and OLMoE arrive (64 experts per layer, 8 activated), the combination space reaches 10^18 and becomes computationally intractable. LightMoE later calls this out directly: "search-based methods like NAEE are ill-suited for fine-grained MoE architectures due to combinatorial explosion."
- *Knowledge is irreversibly lost.* NAEE assumes "unimportant expert = safely discardable," but deletion is permanent. The community gradually realized that low-frequency experts may be activated precisely on rare but critical tasks such as code or mathematics. Once removed, that capability is gone — and cannot be restored by fine-tuning, because no one knows what those experts originally learned.

EEP (evolutionary search) and MoE-I² (intra-layer low-rank + inter-layer pruning) are both evolutionary steps along NAEE's pruning branch: the former addresses the scalability problem, the latter combines pruning with low-rank compression. But neither resolves the fundamental pathology of irreversible knowledge loss.

** 2. MC-SMoE: Don't Cut — "Merge" Instead (2024.03, ICLR Spotlight)

Li et al.'s 2024 MC-SMoE (Merge, then Compress) approaches the problem from the opposite direction: if deletion is irreversible, fuse multiple similar experts into one. At least mathematically, no information is completely discarded — it is merely "flattened."

The procedure: use router activation statistics to identify "dominant experts," cluster less-active experts around them by similarity, and form each group's representative by a frequency-weighted average. Then apply low-rank decomposition to further compress the merged experts. The reported gains are 80% memory reduction, 20% FLOPs reduction, and near-lossless accuracy.

The crucial conceptual shift here is: *recognizing that experts are not discrete all-or-nothing units but points in a continuous space that can be interpolated.*

Yet MC-SMoE introduces its own complications:

- *Representational diversity collapses.* Merging multiple experts into one blends specializations that originally covered different tasks, and the model degrades noticeably on harder tasks. LightMoE later criticizes this: "this approach inherently diminishes the model's representational diversity."
- *Recovery is expensive.* The "progressive low-rank decomposition" stage MC-SMoE uses to recoup accuracy requires computing gradients through the original experts, making the training overhead non-trivial.

** 3. HC-SMoE and MoBE: Two Attempts to Rescue the Merge Camp (2025)

Subsequent work after MC-SMoE is largely devoted to patching the "diversity collapse" wound.

HC-SMoE (Chen et al. 2025a) replaces K-means with hierarchical clustering — the argument being that K-means risks pulling critical experts into a general-purpose group by mistake. This observation later appears in LightMoE as a reverse citation: "K-means risks inadvertently merging critical experts into a general group, leading to the irreversible loss of expert-specific knowledge." Note that "irreversible loss" surfaces again — the merge camp has not escaped the same ghost.

MoBE (Chen et al. 2025b) goes further: it decomposes all expert weights into a set of shared basis matrices plus a small transformation matrix per expert. The idea is elegant in theory — the shared basis retains general capability; the expert-specific transformations retain specialization. In practice, however, *performance collapses as compression ratios climb.* The transformation matrices are too small to carry all of each expert's distinctive information.

At this point the entire compression thread is caught in a dilemma:
- Pruning: decisive, but knowledge is permanently lost and the approach cannot scale.
- Merging: preserves "average information," but erases distinctions, and recovery is costly.

The field needed a new framing.

** 4. LightMoE: Reframe the Question — "Replace" Rather Than "Cut" or "Merge" (2026.03)

LightMoE's key move is not a new algorithm; it is a *redefinition of the problem*. The argument goes: previous work treated this as "compressing a set of experts," and therefore kept cycling between "which ones to drop" and "which ones to merge." But if the task is reframed as "substituting less-active experts with a lighter module and using low-rank adaptation to restore each one's original specialization," the whole picture changes.

Concretely, experts in each MoE layer are split into two classes:
- High-frequency experts (a minority): original parameters are preserved.
- Low-frequency experts (the majority): replaced by a "shared basis" + a small LoRA adapter. The shared basis centralizes general knowledge; the LoRA adapter (borrowed from Hu et al. 2022's low-rank decomposition) is compact yet retains each replaced expert's direction of specialization.

This one maneuver sidesteps predecessors' pitfalls on three fronts simultaneously:

- *Versus pruning (NAEE branch)* — knowledge is no longer permanently lost, because the LoRA adapter can be fine-tuned back; and no combinatorial enumeration is needed, so the approach scales to fine-grained MoE.
- *Versus merging (MC-SMoE branch)* — representational diversity is preserved, because each replaced expert retains its own LoRA adapter (i.e., its own independent direction of specialization).
- *Versus direct LoRA fine-tuning* — LightMoE introduces "annealing recovery," which lets original experts gradually decay toward the shared basis during training, giving the model time to learn to use the new structure rather than switching all at once.

The paper also delivers a theoretically clean insight: "expert merging can be viewed as a special case of our expert replacing paradigm" — merging maps multiple experts to the same parameters with no residual; replacing maps them to the same shared basis but adds a LoRA residual per expert. This single sentence subsumes the entire history of MoE compression into one unified framework.

Results: at 50% compression, LightMoE outperforms all existing methods by an average of 5.6%; on Math tasks it retains 94% of full LoRA fine-tuning capability with half the parameters removed.

** 5. Open Problems Left Behind

LightMoE is not the end of the line either. It still assigns the same rank LoRA to all low-frequency experts without asking:

- Different experts likely need different ranks — a highly specialized expert may require a larger rank to capture its distinctive knowledge.
- The current replacement policy is "high-frequency stays, low-frequency replaced," but activation frequency and "importance for the target task" need not agree — the policy will break as soon as the downstream task shifts.
- The shared basis has fixed capacity; as models grow larger and expert counts increase, the shared basis may become a new bottleneck.

The most probable next steps: *dynamic rank* (each expert's LoRA rank adapts to its importance) and *task-aware replacement* (deciding which experts count as "low-frequency" based on the downstream task rather than on the pretraining distribution).

* Insights

** What's Really Changing Under the Hood Is the Ontology of "Knowledge"

On the surface, MoE compression moved through three steps from 2024 to 2026: prune → merge → replace. But the deeper shift is this: *people's understanding of what an "expert" is evolved from a discrete functional module to a continuously decomposable structure in a high-dimensional space.*

- In NAEE's worldview, experts are black boxes — keep or delete.
- MC-SMoE was the first to acknowledge that experts overlap — they can be averaged with weights.
- LightMoE pushes the decomposition to its logical conclusion: each expert = shared basis (general knowledge) + small residual (specialized knowledge). The underlying idea — basis + residual — is borrowed from LoRA and transplanted into an entirely different problem.

This pattern of "borrowing a mature tool from another domain and applying it in reverse" has precedents in systems research: RAG applies "retrieval" thinking in reverse to generation; speculative decoding applies "branch prediction" thinking in reverse to LLM inference. This MoE compression thread, at its 2026 step, has caught up with the same paradigm: *the problem stays; borrow a proven tool from another field.*

** Why "Replace" Wins and "Merge" Loses

The philosophy of merging is averaging — collapsing multiple experts into a single new point. The problem is that well-trained experts have typically learned specializations in directions that are nearly orthogonal to one another; averaging cancels those directions out. That is why diversity collapses in MC-SMoE.

The philosophy of replacing is decomposition — the shared basis carries "what everyone needs"; the LoRA residual carries "what makes me different from the others." The residual by design preserves diversity, because it exists precisely to represent difference.

This insight generalizes beyond MoE: *in any scenario that requires compressing multiple similar-but-not-identical modules, decomposition (basis + residual) will in all likelihood outperform averaging (direct fusion).* The same principle has been independently validated in model merging and multi-task distillation.

** Where Does the Field Go Next?

Two bets:

1. *Dynamic-rank LoRA.* LightMoE still uses a uniform rank, but clearly different experts warrant different ranks. Once rank is made learnable, the entire compression-performance trade-off curve should shift upward.
2. *Task-aware replacement decisions.* Today, replacement is decided once during the training phase and frozen. But MoE's core advantage is precisely per-token dynamic routing — the natural next step is to decide on the fly, per downstream task or even per batch, which experts should run at full precision and which should run through LoRA substitutes.

Looking further out: *lowering the replacement granularity from experts to neurons.* If each expert is itself decomposed into basis + residual, the entire MoE becomes a continuous low-rank space, and the discrete concept of "expert" may be fundamentally reconceived.
