* The River of Problems

LLMs learning to "think" — solving math problems, writing code, reasoning step by step — has relied almost entirely on reinforcement learning for the past two years. Give the model a problem, let it generate dozens of solution paths, reward the correct ones, penalize the wrong ones, iterate. Every state-of-the-art reasoning model — DeepSeek-R1, OpenAI o1, Qwen3 — was trained this way.

But one thing has never quite made sense: does RL actually *teach* the model new reasoning moves? Or does it merely select from moves the model already knows?

If you get this question wrong, the entire field is burning compute in the wrong place. A single RL training run costs tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of dollars and tens of thousands of GPU-hours. If RL is really just "selecting" rather than "teaching," most of that money is wasted.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | AlphaZero: RL Can Genuinely Invent New Strategies from Scratch

*** What Problem It Saw

Before this, almost every game-playing AI followed the same recipe: first learn from human game records, then fine-tune with RL. Supervised learning poured in human moves; RL was just the finishing touch. This entrenched an implicit assumption: RL cannot independently invent high-quality strategies — it needs humans to teach first.

*** Its Solution

DeepMind threw out all human game records entirely. AlphaZero started from random initialization, used only self-play and RL, and reached superhuman performance at Go, Chess, and Shogi. In Go, it even invented moves that thousands of years of human play had never seen — the 5th-line invasion, the shoulder hit at unexpected angles.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Set)

Board games have enormous search spaces but closed rules and clean reward signals (binary win/loss). RL combined with Monte Carlo Tree Search can explore corners of exponential search spaces that humans never mapped.

The trap it set was significant: the community elevated "RL can invent new strategies" into a universal truth. When the LLM world encountered RLVR — RL with verifiable rewards, where math-problem answers can be checked automatically — the analogy was irresistible: "Isn't this LLM AlphaZero? RL should teach LLMs to invent new reasoning paths." Whether that analogy actually held was never directly tested for years.

** 2024-2025 | GRPO / DeepSeek-R1: Transplanting the AlphaZero Playbook to LLM Reasoning

*** What Problem It Saw

LLMs handle simple questions well via next-token prediction, but they hit a ceiling on multi-step math and coding problems. How do you push the model to the next level?

*** Its Solution

RLVR: have the model generate G solutions (rollouts) per problem, use automated checking to assign binary rewards, then update parameters via GRPO or PPO. DeepSeek-R1 scaled this up and produced the first open-source model capable of long-chain reasoning, matching OpenAI o1. The community went all-in; tens of millions of dollars of compute poured in.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Set)

It genuinely worked — pass@1 jumped significantly. The whole field went all-in, pouring in tens of millions of dollars of compute.

But something quietly went wrong: all these RL algorithms apply gradients equally to every token in every rollout. If only a small fraction of tokens truly need correction and the rest are noise, you are using a sledgehammer to tap a finishing nail. The deeper problem: everyone assumed "RL is inventing new moves like AlphaZero," but no one went to check whether that analogy held on the LLM side.

** 2025 | Yue et al.: Pass@k Counter-Evidence — the Base Model Already Knew

*** What Problem It Saw

If RL genuinely teaches the model new reasoning moves, you should see breakthroughs on problems the base model could never solve — not just higher success rates on problems where the base model was occasionally right. The question demanded a more targeted measurement.

*** Its Solution

They switched the metric: instead of just measuring pass@1 (probability of getting it right on a single sample), they measured pass@k (probability that at least one of k samples is correct). The ranking reversed — at large k, the base model's pass@k exceeded the RL model's.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Set)

This shows that correct solution paths *were already in the base model's sampling distribution*. RL raised the probability of those paths and suppressed others. RL is acting like "selecting from existing answers," not "constructing what wasn't there." The romantic AlphaZero story of inventing new strategies does not hold for LLMs.

But this is still only behavioral evidence. A nagging counter-argument remained: "Maybe RL is still teaching something new, and the new thing just happens to also live in the base model's long tail." Harder evidence was needed.

** 2025 | Davis-Recht & Zhang: Theory and Bounds — RL Is Just Sharpening

*** What Problem It Saw

Yue's results could still be interpreted as a statistical artifact. What was needed was: first, a theoretical *proof* that RL in this setting can only perform "probability reranking"; and second, experimental identification of *when* RL helps and when it does not.

*** Its Solution

- Davis & Recht prove: every mainstream RL algorithm with binary rewards (GRPO / PPO / REINFORCE) is mathematically equivalent to stochastic gradient ascent on some monotone transform of P(correct). In other words, all of them have a single objective: push the probability of correct answers higher. This optimization is *only meaningful when the base model already succeeds occasionally* — on problems the base model never gets right, there is no gradient signal to exploit.
- Zhang et al. confirm experimentally: RL gains occur only at the "capability frontier" — problems the base model sometimes gets right and sometimes wrong. Problems that are too easy (always correct) or too hard (always wrong) see no benefit.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Set)

At this point, "RL does not teach new things; it only sharpens the existing distribution" was essentially proven. But one concrete question remained open: exactly *where* does RL sharpen? Does it nudge every token's probability slightly? Or does it concentrate its force at specific positions? If the latter, which positions?

** 2025 | Wang et al. (Forking Tokens): RL's Force Concentrates at Branch Points

*** What Problem It Saw

If RL really sharpens only a subset of positions, then applying gradients equally to all tokens is wasteful — most gradients are noise. The task was to find "the tokens that actually matter."

*** Its Solution

Sort every token in the generated sequence by the base model's entropy at that position. High-entropy tokens mean the model is "unsure which path to take" at that step — these are *forking tokens*. Wang et al. restricted GRPO's gradients to the top 20% highest-entropy tokens. Result: performance matched full-token RL training.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Set)

The intuition is clear: at low-entropy positions ("the answer is..." followed by "5") the model is already confident — little to learn. At high-entropy positions (deciding whether to use algebra or geometry) the model is choosing the solution path — that is where the real decision happens. Concentrating the gradient signal there delivers a 5× efficiency gain with no performance drop.

But they left an awkward limitation intact: *they stayed inside the RL framework*. They only made RL cheaper. If RL's useful signal is truly this sparse and this locatable, why keep the entire expensive loop of rollouts, rewards, and policy gradients? Nobody pressed that question further.

** 2026 | ReasonMaxxer (This Paper): If the Signal Is That Simple, Drop RL Entirely

*** What Problem It Saw

Assemble the chain built so far:
- Yue: RL does not create new answers; the base model already has them
- Davis-Recht: mathematically, RL is just gradient ascent on P(correct)
- Wang: signal concentrates on 1–4% of tokens, all at high-entropy positions

Every paper was circling the same truth but none dared take the final step — *throwing out RL itself*. This paper takes that step.

*** Its Solution

Three pieces assembled together:

1. *Mechanistic characterization*: Oracle-intervention experiments show that the difference between the RL model and the base model occurs at *only 1–3% of tokens*, and those tokens always fall within the base model's top-5 candidates. Swapping in the RL-preferred token at exactly those positions precisely reproduces the RL model's full performance; random swaps do not.
2. *Teacher-free localization*: The base model's own token entropy is sufficient to locate these positions — no need to train an RL model to tell you where they are.
3. *ReasonMaxxer*:
   - Sample a few hundred rollouts from the base model (offline; no online RL)
   - Use the base model's entropy to identify high-entropy positions
   - Apply contrastive learning at those positions (push up tokens from correct rollouts, push down tokens from incorrect ones)
   - Anchor all other positions back to the base model with a KL penalty to prevent drift
   - Train only a rank-32 LoRA adapter

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Set)

The results are striking: across 3 model families, 6 scales, and 6 math benchmarks, *50 training problems, a few hundred rollouts, minutes of single-GPU training — performance matches or exceeds RL baselines that cost $1,200–$103,000*. Training cost drops by three orders of magnitude.

The trap: this is a mathematical reasoning task. Whether the signal is equally sparse in code generation, long-chain agent tasks, and multi-turn dialogue remains unverified. If RL genuinely teaches new capabilities in those settings, this approach would not apply.

* Frontier Extensions

ReasonMaxxer is the latest development as of May 2026. Adjacent contemporaneous work provides corroborating evidence and complementary angles:

- *TINA (Wang et al., 2025b)*: Reaches the same conclusion from a different direction — RL under LoRA constraints matches full-parameter RL in performance. This shows that RL's corrections are inherently low-dimensional and fit inside 1% of the parameters. Contemporary evidence for ReasonMaxxer's claim that "LoRA can contain all of RL's corrections."
- *Karan & Du (2025) - Reasoning with Sampling*: Improves reasoning purely by changing the sampling strategy at inference time, without touching the model at all. Complementary to ReasonMaxxer: one proves "no RL needed during training," the other proves "you may not even need training — just change decoding."
- *Agarwal et al. (2025) - Entropy Minimization*: Entropy minimization without labels also improves reasoning. Mutual corroboration with the "high-entropy tokens are the key" finding.

The most likely next directions along this line:
1. *Push ReasonMaxxer into code, agent, and long-chain reasoning* — test whether "sparse policy selection" is a universal property of RL for LLMs or only holds for math problems.
2. *Eliminate even the LoRA*: since RL corrections involve 0.3–0.5% of parameters, could you do inference-time activation patching directly?
3. *Revisit RLHF*: does RL training on human preferences — not verifiable rewards — share the same sparse structure? If so, the compute budget for RLHF is also largely wasted.

* Insight

Reading this whole line of work, what is actually happening is not "an iteration of RL methods" — it is *a field in the process of dismantling its own core belief*.

The original belief: "RL, like AlphaZero, can teach LLMs new reasoning moves."

This belief underwrote the entire industry's compute investment — tens of millions of dollars, tens of thousands of GPU cards, tens of thousands of engineer-hours.

Then paper after paper hammered at that belief from different angles:
1. Behavioral blow: base model pass@k is actually higher (Yue)
2. Theoretical blow: RL is mathematically just a probability sharpener (Davis-Recht)
3. Boundary blow: gains only appear at the capability frontier (Zhang)
4. Token-level blow: signal concentrates at 1–4% of positions (Wang)
5. Parameter-level blow: corrections fit into 0.3% of parameters (TINA / this paper)
6. Algorithmic blow: you can get those corrections without RL at all (ReasonMaxxer)

By the time ReasonMaxxer arrives, the original belief is essentially shattered.

The deeper shift is a *methodological* one: from "build a stronger training algorithm" to "first understand what the algorithm is actually doing." The former is engineering-style scale-up; the latter is scientific mechanism research. Sections 3 and 4 of ReasonMaxxer are entirely in the style of mechanistic interpretability — oracle intervention first to establish causality, then LoRA distillation to prove low-dimensionality. Once the mechanism is clear, the efficient new method follows naturally.

Where does this line go next? Two directions:

- *Test sparse policy selection as a hypothesis in other settings*: Do RLHF, code RL, and agentic RL share the same structure? If so, the cost of post-training across the board could drop by one to two orders of magnitude.
- *Mechanism research before method innovation becomes standard practice*: Future post-training papers will be expected to "do mechanistic analysis first, then design the method." Papers that simply say "we ran a bigger RL" will face increasing headwinds.

* Takeaways

What this line of work suggests about how to do research:

1. *The most expensive assumptions are most worth validating once.* This field burned tens of millions of dollars on the assumption that "RL teaches new things," *without anyone formally testing it for years.* If your field has a belief that "everyone takes for granted but no one has actually proved," that is a gold mine.
2. *Don't optimize locally inside the framework — step outside and look.* Wang et al. found the 80-20 result and stopped at "how to make RL more efficient." The critical move in this paper was asking: "If the signal is this sparse, do we even need RL?" *Upgrading "save X" to "eliminate X"* is often where the real breakthrough lives.
3. *Mechanism research is the source of method innovation.* This paper first conducted two sections of hard mechanistic analysis (sparse + low-dimensional), and then the entire method — ReasonMaxxer — fell out almost as a logical corollary. When the mechanism is clear, the method doesn't require guesswork.
4. *"50 examples + one GPU + a few minutes"* is itself a lever. It completely demolishes the belief that "RL is necessary." Any paper still arguing "we must use RL because of X" must now answer: "Then why does ReasonMaxxer get there for $5?"
5. *Concrete advice*: if you are using RLVR / GRPO / PPO for post-training on reasoning tasks, *try the ReasonMaxxer baseline first.* The cost is nearly zero. If it works, the saved budget can be invested in places that genuinely lack data or mechanistic understanding.
