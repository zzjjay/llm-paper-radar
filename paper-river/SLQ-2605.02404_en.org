* The River of the Problem

Can we actually shrink a model with tens of billions of parameters so it runs faster while still being "itself" — and what does "itself" even mean?

Why this is hard: every compression method orbits the same triangle — the harder you compress, the smaller and faster the model, but the worse the output quality. For the past decade-plus, everyone has been hunting for sweet spots along the edges of that triangle, but no one has escaped it. The deeper reason is that "still itself" was never precisely defined. Does it mean "benchmark scores are roughly the same," "every single response is identical," or something in between? Without a definition, how can you know whether you've arrived?

What makes this thread fascinating: its technical roots lie not in the LLM era but in a seed planted by LeCun at Bell Labs in 1990, while working on neural-network pruning. That seed was called "second-order Hessian sensitivity." Thirty-odd years later, a team at IST Austria unearthed it, grew it into GPTQ, and grew it further into today's SLQ. This is the story of a mathematical trick being pushed to industrial deployment over three decades.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1990 | OBD: Using the Hessian to Decide Which Weights to Discard

*** What Problem It Saw

In the early 1990s, neural networks could already recognize handwritten digits, but they had too many parameters, ran slowly after training, and were a nightmare to deploy on hardware. The earliest pruning methods ranked weights by magnitude — anything small got thrown away. The trouble is: a small weight value does not mean a small effect on the network's output. That weight might be doing critical work right along a key decision boundary.

*** Its Solution

LeCun's insight: to ask "how much will the loss increase if we remove weight w_i," we should use a Taylor expansion. First-order derivatives (gradients) are near zero after training, so the dominant term is the second derivative — the Hessian. LeCun assumed the Hessian is diagonal (ignoring coupling between weights), which yields a closed-form score for "the cost of removing w_i": approximately ~w_i^2 * H_ii / 2~. Rank by this score; discard those with the lowest values.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

It worked because it was the first method to connect "importance" to the geometry of the loss landscape rather than relying on intuition.

The buried problem: the diagonal assumption is far too crude. Weights are correlated — when you drop w_i, the remaining weights w_j should shift to compensate, further reducing the loss. OBD ignores that compensation entirely.

** 1993 | OBS: Not Just Pruning — Letting the Survivors Recover

*** What Problem It Saw

Hassibi and Stork directly challenged OBD's diagonal assumption. Their argument: the interactions among weights in a neural network are strong — ignoring them is like performing surgery by cutting out a tumor without suturing the wound, expecting it to heal on its own.

*** Its Solution

Use the full Hessian, not just the diagonal. When you decide to remove weight w_q, you can simultaneously compute exactly which direction and how far every other weight should move to minimize the resulting increase in loss. This compensation update has a closed-form solution that requires the inverse of the Hessian.

Intuitive analogy: OBD saws off a column and accepts that the building collapses; OBS saws off the column while instantly nudging the neighboring columns to brace and stabilize the structure.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

The compensation update made OBS substantially more accurate than OBD at equal pruning rates.

The buried problem: inverting the Hessian costs O(n^3), where n is the number of weights. Barely feasible for small networks with a few thousand weights; completely impossible for modern models with hundreds of millions or billions. The idea sat dormant for nearly thirty years.

** 2022 | OBQ: Extending the OBS Pruning Framework to Quantization

*** What Problem It Saw

Frantar and Alistarh noticed something: pruning (setting a weight to 0) and quantization (rounding a weight to a finite grid) are fundamentally the same kind of operation — both apply a localized perturbation to a single weight, and both ask "how should the other weights optimally compensate?" The OBS mathematical framework should transfer directly, as long as the perturbation is redefined from "reduce to zero" to "reduce to the nearest quantization point."

But it wouldn't budge. OBS's O(n^3) was already untenable at BERT scale (~100M parameters), let alone GPT-3.

*** Its Solution

OBQ did two things. First, it formally generalized OBS into a unified framework for quantization and sparsification — any "local perturbation + Hessian compensation" scheme fits this mold. Second, it applied several engineering optimizations: process the weight matrix row by row (different rows have decoupled Hessians), and greedily select which weight to quantize at each step.

This made OBQ viable for some larger vision models, but LLMs remained out of reach. The paper reported strong results on ResNet-50 quantization, but OPT-175B-scale was still completely inaccessible.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

It worked because it unified the theoretical frameworks for pruning and quantization, elevating post-training compression from heuristics to a mathematically grounded methodology.

The buried problem: greedy + row-level processing was still too slow. Running OPT-175B would have taken days — meaningless for industrial deployment.

** 2023 | GPTQ: Making OBQ Run on Billion-Parameter Models in Hours

*** What Problem It Saw

The root cause of OBQ's slowness was its strict adherence to OBS's weight-by-weight greedy ordering — quantize one weight, update all remaining weights, then find the next optimal one. This serial dependency left GPUs idle.

*** Its Solution

GPTQ made three engineering decisions that make you want to slap your forehead:

First, fix the order. Instead of greedily selecting the next weight to quantize, scan columns from left to right in a fixed pass. This seems like it should hurt accuracy, but experiments showed almost no difference — quantization order matters far less than intuition suggests.

Second, lazy batched updates. Quantizing each column theoretically requires updating the compensation values for all columns to the right, but GPU memory bandwidth is the bottleneck. GPTQ instead accumulates updates for a batch (e.g., 128 columns) and writes them back all at once. This makes the compute-to-bandwidth ratio GPU-friendly.

Third, Cholesky reordering. Direct inversion of the Hessian is numerically unstable; GPTQ instead uses the upper-triangular factor from a Cholesky decomposition as the equivalent information — numerically stable and reusable.

Analogy: OBQ is hand-embroidery, stitch by painstaking stitch; GPTQ converts the embroidery machine into an assembly-line sewing machine — slightly coarser stitching, 100× faster, comparable output.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

GPTQ made it possible to quantize OPT-175B to 4 bits in under 4 hours on a single A100, with very little quality loss. 4-bit LLM quantization became the default in the open-source community (bitsandbytes, AutoGPTQ, ExLlama all use it as their core).

The buried problem: 4-bit is still lossy. Kurtic et al. 2025 ran a systematic evaluation: GPTQ and AWQ drop 3–5 points on complex reasoning tasks (GSM8K, MMLU-Pro). For settings that demand industrial-grade fidelity — say, a paid API service — this is unacceptable. Meanwhile, a separate camp (ZipNN, DFloat11) pursued true lossless compression, but could only reach 11–13 bpp and required decompressing back to BF16 before inference, offering no speedup.

The field was stuck: methods that compress and accelerate are lossy; lossless methods neither compress well nor accelerate.

** 2026 | SLQ: Redefining What "Lossless" Actually Means

*** What Problem It Saw

Helcig et al. pointed to an overlooked fact: modern LLM inference is inherently stochastic — Qwen recommends temperature=0.7; ask the same question twice and the original FP16 model gives different answers, with benchmark scores naturally varying by ~1%. 

So the traditional definition of "lossless" (bit-exact, perfectly identical output distributions) is not what users actually care about. What users care about is "I can't tell the difference." Once this window-pane is shattered, the whole problem changes: instead of chasing mathematical bit-exactness, the target becomes **statistical indistinguishability**.

*** Its Solution

First, define three levels of "lossless," ordered from loosest to strictest:
- Task-Lossless (TL): zero-shot benchmark scores fall within the natural variance range
- Distribution-Lossless (DL): the Expected Acceptance Rate EAR ≥ 0.99 under optimal coupling between the quantized and original model's next-token distributions (99% of token choices agree)
- Byte-lossless: completely bit-exact

The middle level, DL, is a new invention; the accompanying EAR metric is the paper's conceptual contribution — it translates the abstract KL divergence into plain language: "99% of token choices match."

Second, a theoretical proof: symmetric quantization (mapping weights to [-M, M]) inflates the noise variance by a factor of γ^2 relative to asymmetric quantization (mapping to [L, U], allowing a zero-point offset), where γ = M/(R/2). If the weight distribution is off-center, γ is much greater than 1 and variance explodes. Therefore **asymmetric quantization is a necessary condition for achieving DL** — something no one had ever proved before.

Third, engineering: use GPTQ as the underlying quantizer (no need to reinvent the wheel), then search over the bitwidth set {2,3,4,5,6,7,8} using Shapley values to find the optimal per-layer assignment — sensitive layers get high bits, robust layers get low bits.

Results: TL is achievable at 3.3 bpp, DL at 5–6 bpp, with inference throughput 1.7–3.6× faster than FP16.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buries New Problems)

It worked because of a **reframing of the question**: not "how do we compress harder," but "what does the user actually mean by lossless." This is a product-thinking shift, not an algorithm-thinking shift.

Technically, SLQ does not invent a new quantization algorithm (it still uses GPTQ), but it does three things: clearly defines the objective, proves that asymmetric quantization is necessary for DL, and uses Shapley values to find the optimal bit allocation. This is a classic theory-plus-systems combination punch.

New buried problems: DL requires 5–6 bpp, which is much better than the 11 bpp of the truly-lossless camp but larger than GPTQ's 4 bpp. If you are doing edge deployment with extremely tight memory, TL (3.3 bpp) is the sweet spot; if you are running a paid API, DL (5–6 bpp) is worth it. This means there will be no one-size-fits-all quantization recipe going forward — fidelity level must be chosen per deployment scenario.

* Frontier Extensions

SLQ was just published (May 2026), so there are no direct "critique-and-improve" papers yet. But several parallel threads are worth noting:

** Parallel Thread 1: YAQA (Tseng 2025) — Also Chasing Near-Lossless, via a Different Route

YAQA uses full-model KL divergence (not just per-layer) for sensitivity estimation, paired with its own QTIP trellis quantizer and custom kernels. SLQ's experiments show that YAQA still fails to meet the DL standard, and its system complexity is considerably higher. This is a direct comparison of two technical routes to the same goal — SLQ wins with "simpler method + correct definition" over "complex method + vague target."

** Parallel Thread 2: SpQR (Dettmers 2024) — An Early Attempt at Near-Lossless

SpQR's idea is to identify outlier weights and retain them at high precision separately, achieving ~4 bpp near-lossless. SLQ's experiments show SpQR also fails to meet the strict DL standard. SpQR's contribution was the concept of outlier-aware quantization, but it never answered the foundational question of "what does lossless mean."

** Parallel Thread 3: ZipNN / DFloat11 / ECF8 — The Truly-Lossless Camp

These methods use entropy coding and variable-length encoding to achieve genuine bit-exactness, but with limited compression ratios (11–13 bpp) and the requirement to decompress before inference, offering no speedup. SLQ's implicit reply: "Your pursuit of bit-exactness is over-specified. I give you 99% token agreement at 5–6 bpp and 2–3× faster inference."

** Likely Directions Ahead

- Extend SLQ's asymmetry necessity proof to joint weight + activation quantization (W4A4)
- Generalize the EAR metric to multi-turn dialogue settings (currently only next-token is considered)
- Apply the SLQ framework to vector / trellis quantizers, and see whether DL can be pushed below 5–6 bpp

* Insights

Reading through this thread, three deep things emerge:

*1. Across thirty years, there were really only two kinds of move: redefining the problem and doing the engineering*

The technical core from 1990 to 2026 has not changed — it is still Hessian + local compensation. Each generation's real contribution was either extending the mathematical framework (OBD→OBS→OBQ), making the engineering actually usable (OBQ→GPTQ), or redefining the objective (GPTQ→SLQ). Not a single generation invented an entirely new algorithmic principle.

Takeaway: in a mature field, genuinely new theory is rarer than you think. Most progress comes from "transplanting old theory to a new scale" and "articulating a fuzzy objective with precision."

*2. Redefining the problem is worth more than trying harder to solve the original one*

SLQ's cleverest move was not its Shapley search or its asymmetry proof — it was asking what seemed like a trivial question: "What does lossless actually mean?" Before SLQ, the whole field was split into two camps arguing at cross-purposes (compression vs. fidelity), both silently assuming "lossless = bit-exact." Puncturing that assumption immediately opened new territory.

The same pattern recurs throughout ML: BERT punctured "training must go left-to-right"; Transformer punctured "sequences must be processed time-step by time-step." Each was a breakthrough at the level of definition, not technique.

*3. The alignment moment: system layer + theory layer + user layer, all arriving at once*

SLQ works because three things came together simultaneously:
- System layer: vLLM + MARLIN kernels support arbitrary-bitwidth inference
- Theory layer: the γ^2 variance law proves asymmetry is necessary
- User layer: modern LLMs use temperature sampling, so "bit-exact agreement" is no longer what users need

Remove any one of these and SLQ does not hold. Thirty years ago, hardware did not support arbitrary bitwidths; ten years ago, models were still deterministic; three years ago, the γ^2 law had not been proved.

Takeaway: to judge whether the moment is ripe, do not ask how novel the technology is — ask how many layers are simultaneously in place.

*4. Where things will likely go next*

If SLQ's EAR concept gains traction, the whole quantization field will shift from "report W4 baseline results" to "report bpp at EAR ≥ 0.99." This will force many published "4-bit SOTA" methods to be re-evaluated under the new standard. Meanwhile, "distribution-lossless" as a concept is likely to spread to KV cache quantization, speculative decoding, and model distillation — all contexts that involve measuring the similarity between two distributions.

* Takeaways

What this thread suggests about how to find good problems:

*Actionable 1*: Whenever you are doing an engineering optimization — compression, acceleration, memory saving — first ask yourself: "What is the 'thing' I actually need to preserve?" Task scores? Distribution similarity? Bit-exactness? Thinking this through clearly often eliminates 90% of the engineering toil upfront.

*Actionable 2*: When reading papers in a field, trace the reference chain backward — this paper cites GPTQ, GPTQ cites OBQ, OBQ cites OBS, OBS cites OBD. Following that citation chain back to the source shows you how a single mathematical idea gets repackaged by successive generations. This gives you a sharper grip on the field's true spine than reading ten parallel survey papers.

*Actionable 3*: When you are stuck in a two-camp standoff (Camp A wants X, Camp B wants Y, and they talk past each other), the biggest opportunity is usually to invent a new intermediate definition ("I want neither X nor Y — I want Z, defined as follows…"). That is exactly what SLQ did — it invented a "statistically lossless" middle ground and dissolved the opposition between the two camps. Slicing the cake differently is a higher-order move than fighting for a bigger slice of the existing cut.

*Actionable 4*: Do not hastily discard papers that look outdated. LeCun's 1990 OBD appeared in NeurIPS when the conference was still called NIPS and ran small experiments. Thirty-six years later its mathematical skeleton is still holding up SLQ. Genuinely good ideas tend to outlast their era. When you spot an old idea that has never been fully exploited, that is likely your opportunity.
