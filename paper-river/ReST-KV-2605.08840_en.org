* The River of the Problem

During long-context LLM inference, KV caches for hundreds of thousands of tokens devour GPU memory entirely. Can we *evict* some KV entries on the fly during inference, keeping memory manageable — without making the model dumber?

The hard part is not "how much to evict" but *whom to evict*. How do you estimate the importance of a token's past contribution? The most naive approach is to retain tokens with high attention weights — but this ignores something critical: once a token is dropped, the attention weights of the remaining tokens *redistribute*. Tokens that previously had low attention may suddenly matter; tokens that "seemed unimportant" may have been serving as crucial semantic aggregation points. Generation after generation of researchers is essentially answering the same question: *what signal should really decide whether to drop a token?*

* Evolutionary Narrative

The thread below shows each paper answering what the previous one left unanswered — the same question all along: *how do you estimate the importance of a KV pair so that evicting it causes minimal harm to model output?*

** 2017 | Transformer: KV cache was a debt discovered only later

*** What problem it saw

RNNs compute step by step; they simply cannot run on long sequences.

*** Its solution

Discard recurrence and keep only attention. Every position can compute query/key/value in parallel, sending training throughput up a full tier. To avoid recomputing earlier tokens' keys and values during inference, it introduced the KV cache — once computed, store it for later queries to reuse.

*** Why this worked (and what new debt it buried)

Parallel training plus cached reuse is the foundation that lets Transformers scale. But the KV cache is a *linearly growing debt*: every additional token adds one more key and one more value to store. Nobody cared at the time — context windows were only a few thousand tokens. Once GPT-4 and Llama-3.1 pushed contexts to 128k, the debt collector arrived: a 70B model at 128k context can consume hundreds of gigabytes just from the KV cache alone. The problem shifted from "training throughput" to "can inference memory hold up?"

** 2023 | StreamingLLM: If memory can't hold, keep only both ends

*** What problem it saw

The most naive fix is a fixed window — keep only the most recent N tokens' KV. Sounds reasonable; in practice perplexity collapses immediately. Why? Because the model pays surprisingly high attention to the *very first few tokens*, even when those tokens are semantically empty words like "the" or "<s>". Dropping them throws off the softmax normalization, corrupting the entire distribution.

*** Its solution

Accept this fact: permanently retain the first few tokens (naming them "attention sinks"), then add a sliding window covering recent content. Keep both ends; discard the middle.

*** Why this worked (and what new debt it buried)

This was the first approach that made long-context inference viable under limited memory. But the cost was obvious: *the middle is always dropped*. If the answer to a user's question is buried in the middle (say, a specific fact in an 80k-word report), the model becomes goldfish-brained. The simpler the rule, the more critical information it discards.

** 2023 | H2O: The middle has "important tokens" too — select by cumulative attention

*** What problem it saw

StreamingLLM's two-ends rule is too blunt. In practice, a small cluster of past tokens is attended to repeatedly — these are the "heavy hitters." They sit neither at the start nor in the recent window, yet the model circles back to them every few generation steps. A blanket eviction of the middle buries these heavy hitters with everything else.

*** Its solution

Replace fixed patterns with *dynamic* selection: at each decoding step, accumulate the attention weight each token has received so far; when the budget is exceeded, evict those with the lowest cumulative score.

*** Why this worked (and what new debt it buried)

The first time "importance" was turned into a data-driven signal. But "cumulative attention" is *backward-looking* — it says "how much has this token been attended to in the past," not "will future queries still need it?" In multi-turn dialogue or long generation, once the topic shifts, past heavy hitters become dead weight.

** 2024 | SnapKV: Use an observation window to predict the future

*** What problem it saw

H2O's cumulative scores are too "past tense." When pruning in a single pass at the prefill stage, a more sensible proxy is: *which tokens are the queries in this final stretch attending to?* Because "recent" usually means "generation will continue asking around these positions."

*** Its solution

Reserve an observation window at the end of the prompt (e.g., 32 tokens), pool the attention those queries pay to all historical tokens, and keep the top-K KV pairs. Essentially: let "the most recent queries" run a trial, and what they select is "what will very likely be needed next."

*** Why this worked (and what new debt it buried)

More predictive in nature. But fundamentally the same pattern: *using attention weight as a proxy for importance*. Two obscured facts remain unaddressed:
1. *Value vectors play no role*. Two tokens may have identical attention weights, but if one value vector has ten times the magnitude, its contribution to the output is entirely different — SnapKV cannot see this.
2. *Deletion triggers redistribution*. Drop a high-attention token, and the softmax spreads that weight proportionally across the rest; if the remainder can absorb it gracefully, loss is small; if not, output breaks. SnapKV assumes "each token's influence is independent," but softmax does not permit that assumption.

** 2024 | AdaKV: Per-head budget allocation via an output L1 error upper bound

*** What problem it saw

All prior methods conflated "budget allocation" with "which token to keep." AdaKV separates the two: it observes that different attention heads vary enormously in sparsity, so giving each head an equal budget wastes capacity.

*** Its solution

Derive an *upper bound on the output L1 loss* for each head and use that bound to allocate budget — giving more capacity to heads where eviction would hurt more. The actual token selection still uses attention weight ranking.

*** Why this worked (and what new debt it buried)

The first time "output error" was explicitly written into the framework. But it is only used for *budget allocation*, not for *token selection* — selection still relies on attention weight. And the L1 is an upper bound, not the true output error. This left an obvious next step wide open: can "output reconstruction error" be turned directly into the *eviction indicator itself*?

** 2026 | ReST-KV: Make "how much worse will output be after deletion" the indicator itself

*** What problem it saw

The shared blind spot of all prior methods: using *attention weight* as a proxy for *importance*, when the true objective is *output* — you do not care whether the attention distribution looks like the original; you care whether the downstream MHA output still looks like the original. These are not the same thing, because:
- After deleting an entry, attention *re-normalizes*: the remaining entries receive proportionally amplified weights.
- The direction of value vectors determines where "after amplification" the output gets pulled.
- A token with low attention but a large value magnitude can cause the output to collapse catastrophically when evicted.

*** Its solution

Reformulate KV eviction as an *optimization problem*: within budget B, find the KV subset such that the MHA output computed from that subset is *minimized in L2 distance* from the MHA output computed with all KVs retained.

Direct solution is too expensive, so a greedy approach is taken: compute how much output error each KV pair causes when evicted alone, and rank by that error in descending order. After derivation, each token's importance simplifies to a product of two terms (see paper Eq. 7):
1. =A[n] / (1 - A[n])= — a *nonlinear amplifier*. It preserves the intuition that "higher attention means more important," but uses a monotone convex curve that better differentiates between closely ranked high-attention tokens.
2. =||MHA(full) - v_n W_O||_2= — *redistribution sensitivity*. If this token is dropped, can the remaining KVs compensate for it through their weighted average? If they cannot, the token is truly important.

A more accurate indicator alone is not enough. The authors push one step further: they observe that this indicator *fluctuates in both time and space* — the importance of the same token can jump between adjacent decoding steps; spatially, importance patterns shift across neighboring positions. So two additional smoothing steps are added:
- *Temporal*: exponential moving average (EMA), decay-weighting the indicator over recent steps to prevent single-step noise from dominating.
- *Spatial*: adaptive window-based smoothing, averaging over nearby positions with an adaptively adjusted window offset.

*** Why this worked (and what new debt it buried)

For the first time, "which tokens to keep" is elevated from "ranking by proxy signal" to "optimization against the true objective." LongBench +2.58%, RULER +15.2%, decoding latency at 128k context reduced by 10.61× — this validates the approach.

Remaining debts:
- Output reconstruction is computed *per-layer, per-head* without accounting for cascading errors across subsequent layers — a small error in one layer may be amplified in higher layers.
- Greedy selection is a first-order approximation (local linearity assumption); the joint loss of dropping two tokens is not necessarily the sum of dropping them independently.
- EMA and window smoothing introduce new hyperparameters (decay coefficient, window size) whose generality across tasks remains to be validated.

* Frontier Extensions

ReST-KV was published in May 2026 (ICLR 2026), less than a month ago, and there are no formal follow-up critiques yet. Along the same problem thread, the most likely next directions are:

1. *Cross-layer joint optimization*: Since per-layer reconstruction works, the natural next step is to jointly optimize KV selection across N layers, incorporating cascading error into the indicator — at N times the computational cost.
2. *Indicator + quantization co-design*: KV cache quantization (e.g., the KIVI family) and eviction are complementary axes; putting "which to keep" and "how many bits to store what is kept" into a unified optimization framework may further reduce memory.
3. *Training-aware eviction*: All current methods are training-free. The next step may train the model knowing that "some KVs will be evicted at inference time," encouraging representations that are robust to missing entries.

Uncertain items are noted — the above is speculative, not existing papers.

* Insights

On the surface this thread is "KV cache eviction getting progressively better." What is actually happening underneath is a *shift in the cognitive coordinate frame*:

1. *From empirical rules to data signals to objective functions.* StreamingLLM used intuition (start is important + recent is important). H2O/SnapKV used empirical signals (attention weights). ReST-KV finally wrote down "what we actually want to optimize" as an equation: preserve the MHA output. Each step forward is one step closer to "what we actually care about."
2. *Proxy variables have a lifespan.* Attention weight served as an importance proxy for three or four years, until a very plain observation punctured it: deletion causes redistribution, so a static weight is simply not a predictor of post-deletion output. Any research that "uses A as a proxy for B" should take note: *proxies, in long enough use, are forgotten to be proxies.*
3. *Modeling "deletion" as a perturbation* — ReST-KV is equivalent to performing a leave-one-out influence function estimation. This framing (pruning, quantization, and token drop are all perturbations) as a unified perspective may travel far beyond the specific problem of KV caches.

Where is this most likely heading? See "Frontier Extensions" above — *cross-layer joint optimization* is the most natural continuation, but *baking "dropping KVs" into the training objective* would be the true paradigm leap. The former pushes the same idea to its limit; the latter changes the battlefield.

* Takeaways

If you are doing research that "uses a proxy signal in place of the true target" — not only KV cache, but knowledge distillation, feature selection, pruning, retrieval — this thread offers a very direct methodology:

- *Explicitly write down the loss function for what you ultimately care about.* No matter how expensive it is to compute, write it out first. Then ask: how far is the signal I am currently using from it?
- *Find the boundary conditions where the proxy breaks.* When does attention weight fail as an importance proxy? When a token has low attention but a large value magnitude. That counterexample is the seed of the next paper.
- *Using "change in objective after small perturbation" as an indicator* is usually more accurate than "current state." This is something ReST-KV borrowed from the classical Optimal Brain Damage / Molchanov pruning lineage — not new, but applied in the right place, it lets you leap a generation.

Concretely on the engineering side: if you are doing long-context LLM serving, you can now consider replacing SnapKV/H2O-style eviction with reconstruction-based eviction. ReST-KV's code is already open-sourced (=github.com/an-yongqi/rest-kv=); the gains are especially pronounced in low-budget settings (n=64), making it the highest-value switch to make.
