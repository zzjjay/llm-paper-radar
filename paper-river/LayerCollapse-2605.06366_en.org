* The Problem River

What exactly "matters" inside an LLM? Or, flipped around — how much of its computation is wasted work that could simply be cut?

The question is hard because the model is a black box: tens of billions of parameters, dozens of Transformer layers, and no obvious pattern no matter how closely you look. Yet the answer is essential — compression, quantization, pruning, deployment: every engineering decision bets that "this part doesn't matter, we can remove it safely." Bet wrong, and the model collapses.

What makes it worse is that researchers in this line of work have always assumed "LLM" means autoregressive (AR) model, and every insight has been discovered on AR. But starting in 2025, diffusion language models (DLMs) broke onto the scene — same architecture, comparable capability, but an entirely different training objective. Do the rules found on AR still hold for DLMs? This paper's answer is: *not only do they not hold — they reverse.*

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022 | Dettmers et al. (LLM.int8()): Discovering the "Monster Channels" Hidden in LLMs

*** What problem did it see?

In 2022, researchers wanted to compress large models to 8-bit integers to save memory. Methods that worked fine on small models broke catastrophically on 6B+ models — accuracy fell off a cliff.

*** Its solution

Dettmers traced the culprit: a handful of activation channels whose values were tens of times larger than the rest. Because quantization scales by the maximum value, these monsters caused all normal channels to be compressed to effectively 1–2 bits of precision — pure noise. The fix was simple but effective: pull those outlier channels out separately, compute them in 16-bit, and only then quantize the rest to 8-bit.

*** Why it worked (and where it buried a new problem)

It worked because those "outlier channels," though tiny in number (just a few per layer), carried a disproportionate share of information. But Dettmers only routed around them without explaining *why they exist*. The buried problem: without understanding the mechanism, every new architecture would require rediscovering the same lesson from scratch.

** 2024 | Sun et al. (Massive Activations): Outliers Are Not Random — They Live at Fixed Addresses

*** What problem did it see?

After Dettmers, everyone knew the monsters existed — but where did they live? Did they move around between runs? If they were random, only post-hoc handling was possible; if they were fixed, they could be planned for in advance.

*** Its solution

Sun et al. plotted activation magnitudes as a heatmap over the (channel × token position) grid, and the picture was immediately clear: extreme activations were not spread uniformly. They clustered at a small number of specific token positions — beginning-of-sequence tokens, periods, separators — and at those tokens the activations were more than three orders of magnitude above normal. They named this phenomenon "massive activations."

*** Why it worked (and where it buried a new problem)

This elevated "outliers" from a statistical nuisance to a structural phenomenon — the model actively selected certain tokens as containers for extreme activations. But it left one question open: this is a story about activations. What about the static weight matrices produced by training? Do they contain similarly critical individual entries?

** 2025 | Yu et al. (Super Weights): Monsters in the Weights, Not Just the Activations

*** What problem did it see?

If a few tokens and channels carry disproportionate load in the activation space, does the same structure exist in the trained, static weight matrices? Sun left this unanswered.

*** Its solution

Yu et al. ran a simple but brutal experiment: they zeroed out the largest individual scalar weights one at a time and watched how much performance dropped. The finding: models like Llama contain 1–3 "super weights" such that removing any single one immediately causes the model to produce incoherent repetition. Moreover, these weights have a concrete function — they suppress the probability of stop tokens, preventing generation from terminating prematurely.

*** Why it worked (and where it buried a new problem)

At this point the "outlier" picture had three levels: channels (Dettmers) → token positions (Sun) → individual scalar weights (Yu). All three were interpreted as structures carrying critical function. But the *layer* level had still not been studied systematically — which layers are redundant?

** 2026 | Sun et al. (Curse of Depth): Deep Layers Are Redundant in AR Models

*** What problem did it see?

The intuition that some layers are redundant was widespread — deleting certain layers barely hurt performance. But which layers, and why?

*** Its solution

Sun et al. used inter-layer cosine similarity (the similarity between hidden states of adjacent or separated layers) as a diagnostic, and found that in Pre-LN Transformers (now the dominant normalization choice), *deeper layers are increasingly redundant* — their outputs are nearly identical to their inputs, functioning essentially as identity maps. They attributed this to gradients being flattened as they pass through LayerNorm, leaving the deep layers effectively untrained. They named this the *Curse of Depth*.

*** Why it worked (and where it buried a new problem)

This produced a clean engineering heuristic: if you're pruning, prune the deep layers; if you're quantizing, compress the deep layers first — they're not doing much work anyway. But this conclusion was derived from *AR* models. Everyone assumed the rule was a property of the Transformer architecture itself, independent of the training objective.

** 2025 | Rulli et al.: DLMs Have Attention Sinks, But They Seem Not to Matter

*** What problem did it see?

By 2025, diffusion language models were gaining traction — LLaDA, DREAM, and others no longer emit tokens one at a time but instead iteratively denoise an entire masked sequence. The architecture is nearly identical to Llama, but the training objective is completely different. Do the entire accumulated set of "outlier" findings still apply?

*** Its solution

Rulli et al. focused on attention sinks (discovered in AR by Xiao et al. 2024: models consistently dump a large share of attention onto the first few tokens). They found sinks in DLMs as well, but with a striking anomaly: removing sinks from an AR model causes immediate collapse; removing them from a DLM causes almost no harm.

*** Why it worked (and where it buried a new problem)

This was the first paper to explicitly state "the outlier structure of DLMs and AR models differs." But they looked only at the attention level, not the channel level; they observed the phenomenon without explaining it; and they did not translate the finding into engineering conclusions about compression.

** 2026 | Conzelmann et al. (This Paper): A "Super-Outlier Channel" in DLMs, and Layer Redundancy Completely Reversed

*** What problem did it see?

Combining Sun 2026 (deep layers are redundant) with Rulli 2025 (DLMs are different) brings a concrete question into focus: what does the layer-redundancy distribution of a DLM actually look like? Does it match the AR pattern — deep layers redundant — or is it flipped?

*** Its solution

The authors placed LLaDA-8B (the only large-scale DLM trained from scratch at this scale) alongside Llama-3.1-8B (nearly identical architecture) and took X-rays with three instruments simultaneously:

1. *Activation heatmaps* (channel × token position) — In LLaDA, a *single channel* maintains persistently extreme activations across every token position and across all layers in the first half of the network. This is not the Dettmers/Sun pattern of "a few high channels, scattered." It is *one* channel with absolute dominance. The authors call it the "super-outlier." Remove this one channel and LLaDA degenerates into infinite loops of repeated tokens. Llama has nothing like it.

2. *Inter-layer cosine similarity* — LLaDA's early-layer hidden states are nearly identical across layers, showing clear redundancy; the deep layers are actually more differentiated. This is a perfect reversal of Sun 2026's "deep-layer redundancy" finding on AR.

3. *Heavy-tail spectral analysis of weights (HT-SR theory)* — This is the key explanatory instrument. Cosine similarity alone is insufficient, because "layers that look redundant" could arise from two distinct causes: undertraining (the layer learned nothing), or overtraining (the layer learned only one dominant direction, collapsing everything else). Heavy-tail spectra distinguish the two. The result: LLaDA's early-layer weight spectra are *particularly* heavy-tailed, indicating *overtraining*, not undertraining.

Finally, a clean controlled experiment: under identical data, identical architecture, and identical compute, a pair of 160M models — one AR, one DLM — were trained from scratch. The redundancy reversal and the compression-robustness difference were reproduced, and they arose *solely from the training objective*, ruling out confounders.

*** Why it worked (and where it buried a new problem)

The paper delivers directly verifiable engineering conclusions: "The AR heuristic 'compress deep layers more' must be *applied in reverse* for DLMs — 3-bit GPTQ quantization drops LLaDA's performance by only 1.8% but drops Llama's by 64.7%; at 50% average sparsity, allocating more sparsity to early layers yields +8.4% for LLaDA but −8.4% for Llama." The same action, opposite sign.

The new problem buried: *How does the super-outlier channel actually emerge from the diffusion training objective?* Does the denoising process require a "constant reference channel" to function? Is it a form of learning instability? The authors acknowledge they have not answered this, and explicitly list it as a limitation. There is a deeper question too — if the super-outlier is a product of overtraining, could one modify the training algorithm to suppress it? And if suppressed, would the model be better or worse?

* Frontier Extensions

** 2026 (concurrent) | Goel et al.: Early-Layer Redundancy → Layer-Skip Inference

Also uses inter-layer cosine similarity to study DLMs, but examines only adjacent layers (short range), misses the super-outlier, and skips weight spectral analysis. Their engineering output is layer-skip inference (skipping redundant layers for speedup), a different direction from Conzelmann's non-uniform sparsity allocation. The two works independently corroborate the early-layer redundancy phenomenon.

** 2026 | Myrzakhan et al.: Translating the Attention-Sink Insight into DLM Pruning

Picks up from Rulli 2025 and turns "attention sinks are unimportant in DLMs" into a *concrete pruning algorithm*. Still operates only at the attention level without touching the super-outlier in the channel dimension.

** 2025–26 | DLM-Quant (Xu & Yang), Quant-dLLM (Zhang et al.), Lin et al.: DLM Quantization Engineering

Adapt existing PTQ pipelines (GPTQ, AWQ-style methods) to the bidirectional, multi-step inference of DLMs. The approach is essentially "take the AR method and tune it for the new architecture," without explaining from first principles why DLMs can be compressed so aggressively — exactly the gap Conzelmann aims to fill.

As of now, no published work has taken the super-outlier mechanistic observation and used it to drive a new compression algorithm directly. This is conspicuously low-hanging fruit.

* Insight

On the surface this line of work is "finding monsters in LLMs," but the deeper thing happening is a *continuous upgrade of diagnostic instruments*.

- Dettmers used "maximum activation value" — a crude scalar statistic
- Sun used "activation heatmap over the channel–position grid" — capturing spatial structure
- Yu used "weight ablation" — capturing functional causality
- Sun 2026 used "inter-layer cosine similarity" — capturing layer-level redundancy signals
- Conzelmann added "heavy-tail spectral analysis of weights" — and this is a qualitative leap. For the first time it becomes possible to *distinguish* whether a "visually redundant layer" got that way through undertraining or overtraining.

Without that distinguishing power, cosine similarity alone leads to the wrong conclusion: "DLM early layers are redundant → those layers weren't trained well → train them more." Add the heavy-tail analysis and the true picture emerges: "Early layers aren't undertrained — they're *overtrained*, every degree of expressivity sucked into a single channel." The conclusion flips completely.

The broader lesson concerns *how training objectives reshape internal structure*. AR and DLM use the same Transformer architecture, but different training objectives (next-token prediction vs. masked denoising) produce representational structures with *topological* differences — not "different in degree" but "reversed in direction." This implies a more general proposition: architecture sets the ceiling on capability, but *the objective function determines the shape of capability*. The current fashion is to change architectures (MoE, Mamba, SSMs), but changing the objective may be the steeper lever.

Where is the field most likely to go next? Two directions look ripe:

1. *Mechanistic reverse-engineering*: When during training does the super-outlier channel first emerge? Does it appear suddenly or grow gradually? Can a regularizer actively control whether and how large it grows?
2. *Compression algorithm renewal*: Every current DLM compression method is an AR method with patches applied. With this mechanistic understanding in hand, there is now a clear basis for algorithms *purpose-built for DLMs* — for instance, explicitly protecting the super-outlier channel, applying aggressive low-rank decomposition to early layers, and keeping high precision in deep layers.

* Takeaways

The methodological takeaway centers on *instrument pairing*.

No single tool (cosine similarity, activation distribution, weight spectra) is sufficient, but combining them makes it possible to ask questions no single tool can answer: "Two things that look the same — are they really the same cause?" This is a replicable pattern: when your metric shows "X and Y both satisfy P," resist categorizing them immediately; find a second-order diagnostic capable of *distinguishing the two underlying mechanisms*. Conzelmann's use of HT-SR to separate undertraining from overtraining is a textbook example.

The lesson for finding problems: *old rules + new settings = almost always a counterexample somewhere*. Curse of Depth held robustly on AR; nobody felt it needed re-examining. But when DLMs appeared, they provided a clean perturbation — same architecture, different objective. In that gap of "looks like it should be the same, but nobody has actually tested it," large fish tend to hide. Ask yourself: in your own area, what recent perturbation (new data regime, new objective, new scale) has appeared? Which "everyone assumes this holds" rule has never been re-verified in the new setting?

The practical takeaways:
- Write a simple "baseline diagnostic kit": for any LLM you encounter, run an activation heatmap + inter-layer cosine similarity + weight-spectrum Hill estimator; stack the three plots and look together — non-trivial structure almost always appears.
- If you are deploying or compressing DLMs, take the conclusions directly: compress early layers more aggressively, protect the super-outlier channel — the performance-per-cost ratio far exceeds blindly importing AR heuristics.
- If your work involves any "new training objective + familiar architecture" combination (RL-trained models, RLHF fine-tuned models, diffusion vision models), run this diagnostic suite from scratch — reversed rules are probably hiding there too.
