* The River of Problems

A 70B model has weights alone totaling 140 GB. Can we cut every weight down to 1 bit — that is, only +1 or −1 — so it fits on a consumer GPU?

Where is the difficulty? Weights are not uniformly distributed: the vast majority cluster around zero, while a handful of "giant" weights govern the model's critical behavior. A blunt 1-bit cut pushes every near-zero weight to either +1 or −1, and the model collapses immediately. For a decade, successive generations of researchers have wrestled with the same puzzle: *1 bit is not enough, yet I only want to spend 1 bit — what can I do?*

Each generation's answer grew out of the previous generation's deficiency. The lineage is worth tracing in reverse.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1993 | OBS: Using the Hessian to Decide Which Weights Matter Most

*** What problem it saw

1990s neural-network pruning was blunt work — sort weights by magnitude and delete the small ones. But "small weight" does not mean "unimportant": some small weights sit at sensitive positions, and removing them is fatal.

*** Its solution

Optimal Brain Surgeon uses a Taylor expansion to express "the impact on the loss of removing a given weight" as a second-order term that depends on the Hessian matrix. The Hessian captures the coupling between every pair of weights, so this second-order term tells you: "If I remove w_i, by how much does the loss rise?" Pick the one whose removal raises the loss least.

*** Why it worked (and where the new trap lay)

Second-order information reflects inter-weight dependencies far better than first-order gradients. The trap: the Hessian is an N×N matrix where N is the parameter count — computing it even once for GPT-175B blows memory. It would take thirty years before this limitation was circumvented.

** 2022 | OBQ: Porting the Pruning Framework to Quantization

*** What problem it saw

OBS sets weights to zero (pruning). In the LLM era, what we actually need is not to zero out weights but to compress FP16 down to 4 bits, 2 bits, or 1 bit. Can the same second-order logic guide quantization?

*** Its solution

OBQ (Optimal Brain Quantization) quantizes weights one at a time. After quantizing one weight, it immediately uses Hessian information to update all remaining unquantized weights so they "compensate" for the just-introduced quantization error. The process runs row by row, each row independently.

*** Why it worked (and where the new trap lay)

The compensation mechanism prevents local errors from accumulating blindly — the bias introduced by earlier quantization steps is gradually absorbed by later ones.

The trap: *quantizing one weight at a time means 175 billion iterations for a 175B model — an estimated six months of wall time*. This is exactly the problem GPTQ was built to solve.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Running OBQ on a 175B Model in Hours

*** What problem it saw

OBQ processes each weight column in the greedy order that minimizes error, refreshing the inverse Hessian after every single step. This is fine for small models; for large models it is completely intractable.

*** Its solution

Three observations:
1. *Order doesn't matter* — greedy ordering and fixed ordering yield comparable final accuracy, so just use a fixed order and avoid the overhead of dynamic sorting.
2. *Lazy updates* — partition weights into blocks; process each block sequentially, then apply batch-wide compensation between blocks all at once. This makes Hessian access patterns GPU-friendly.
3. *Cholesky rewrite* — use Cholesky decomposition to pre-compute the required Hessian information once, avoiding numerically unstable matrix inversions.

Result: a 175B model quantized in a few hours; 4-bit quality is nearly lossless.

*** Why it worked (and where the new trap lay)

GPTQ engineered the "second-order compensation" framework into a standard tool for the LLM quantization era.

The trap: GPTQ works beautifully at 3–4 bits, but *push the bit count down to 2 or 1, and even Hessian compensation cannot save it — perplexity spikes into the thousands or tens of thousands*. The problem is not the algorithm; it is the 1-bit target itself: with only two codewords (+1/−1), there simply is not enough information to represent the variation in a single row of weights. This leads directly to PB-LLM's mixed-precision idea.

** 2023 | PB-LLM: High Precision Where It Matters, 1 Bit Elsewhere

*** What problem it saw

If 1 bit cannot represent a full row of weights, what if we *use 1 bit only for the unimportant weights* and keep the important ones at 4 bits or even 8 bits? AWQ experiments had already shown that preserving just the top 1% of "salient weights" is enough to maintain model capability under 4-bit quantization. Taken to the extreme: apply 1 bit to 90% of the weights and keep only the 10% "salient weights" in high precision.

*** Its solution

- *Identify salient weights*: use a Hessian-based score (inherited from GPTQ) to rank every weight; keep the top 10%.
- *Mixed-precision storage*: those 10% in high precision, the remaining 90% at 1 bit.
- *Closed-form scale derivation*: instead of searching for scaling factors like AWQ, derive the optimum analytically.

*** Why it worked (and where the new trap lay)

Preserving the handful of giant weights on critical paths prevents immediate model collapse.

The traps:

1. Salient-weight positions are *irregularly scattered* across the whole matrix, so a per-weight mask (1 bit per weight) is needed to record "is this position salient?" 10% salient + mask overhead + 1-bit body = an average of *2.7 bits/weight* — hardly 1-bit quantization; more like general low-bit quantization.
2. An irregular mask is deeply hardware-unfriendly — every weight read requires a mask lookup, severely degrading inference throughput.

Both traps directly gave birth to BiLLM.

** 2024 | BiLLM: The First PTQ Method to Truly Reach ~1 Bit

*** What problem it saw

PB-LLM's unstructured mask is both expensive and slow. To fix this, salient weight locations must be *structured* — e.g., salient weights appear only in certain fixed columns, so instead of storing one mask bit per weight you only need to record "which columns are salient."

Furthermore, PB-LLM's non-salient weights are simply 1-bit quantized, but these weights follow a bell-shaped distribution (dense in the center, sparse at the tails), and a single hard cut introduces large errors.

*** Its solution

BiLLM's two-piece framework:

1. *Structured column selection*: use a Hessian metric to score each column's salience and select entire columns at once. Salient columns use residual binarization — binarize once, compute the residual, binarize the residual — effectively lifting those columns to ~2-bit equivalent precision.
2. *Optimal bell-shaped partitioning*: for non-salient columns, rather than direct ±1 assignment, split the weights by magnitude into two segments (small-value and large-value), compute binary parameters for each segment independently. This fits the bell-shaped distribution much better.

Final average: ~1.08 bits/weight, and the mask is column-level, so it is hardware-friendly.

*** Why it worked (and where the new trap lay)

Elevating "salience" from individual weights to entire columns solved both of PB-LLM's traps in one move: storage shrinks and inference can actually be accelerated.

The traps:

1. BiLLM's binary parameters (mean, scaling factor) are *computed once* using a simple least-squares solution. But binarization is highly non-linear, so the one-shot solution still leaves a significant gap from the true optimum.
2. The bell-shaped partitioning is done along *rows* only, ignoring column-direction offsets entirely. In practice, LLM weight matrices carry *systematic offsets in both rows and columns*.
3. After BiLLM processing, a single row of weights can only represent *8 distinct values* after de-quantization. This is the ceiling.

** 2024 | ARB-LLM: Iteratively Refining BiLLM's Parameters

*** What problem it saw

BiLLM computes its binary parameters once and stops. But *the mean and scale computed at the start are based on the original weight distribution; once binarization has happened, the binarized matrix has a different distribution — recomputing the mean and scale from it yields better parameters; then re-binarize, recompute again...* After a few rounds of this alternation, the error decreases further.

A second observation: LLM weight matrices have *systematic offsets in the column direction too*. BiLLM's row-only scaling is insufficient.

*** Its solution

- *ARB (Alternating Refined Binarization)*: alternately update the triplet (mean, row scaling factor, binary matrix). In each round, fix two of the three and optimize the third; iterate until convergence.
- *ARB-X*: incorporates calibration data into the optimization of binary parameters.
- *ARB-RC*: introduces both row and column scaling to address column-direction offsets.
- *CGB (Column-Group Bitmap)*: a finer column-grouping strategy than BiLLM's.

Result: ARB-LLM_RC is the first binary PTQ method whose quantized model *outperforms a same-size FP16 baseline* — for example, a binarized OPT-13B beats the full-precision OPT-2.7B.

*** Why it worked (and where the new trap lay)

Iterative refinement makes binary parameters genuinely fit the post-binarization distribution, rather than locking in a one-shot solution based on the original distribution.

The trap:

ARB-LLM raises the number of distinct representable values per row to 10 (upper-bounded by the block size of 128). But *at bottom, both BiLLM and ARB-LLM work entirely in the spatial domain of the weights — slicing by position, scaling by position*. Neither ever asked: *if each row of a weight matrix is treated as a signal, what does its frequency structure look like?*

That overlooked perspective is exactly where HBLLM enters.

** 2025 | HBLLM: Splitting Weights into Two Frequency Bands with Wavelets (Target Paper)

*** What problem it saw

BiLLM can represent 8 distinct values per row; ARB-LLM can represent 10. From an information-theoretic standpoint, this is the ceiling. No matter how much you improve parameter refinement or scaling strategies, as long as each weight is still *one binary codeword × one scaling factor*, the representational capacity is stuck.

FrameQuant, QuIP, and SliceGPT recognized this and applied global orthogonal transforms to scatter the weights before quantization, raising representational capacity. But those *global transforms require O(d²) matrix multiplications and cannot be fused into a linear layer*, so they add an inference-time overhead.

HBLLM's central challenge: *Can we find a "local" orthogonal transform that both increases representational capacity and adds zero inference overhead?*

*** Its solution

Apply the *Haar wavelet transform* — a tool from 1980s image compression that had never been used for LLM quantization.

What Haar does is extremely simple: for each row of weights, take every pair of adjacent weights (a, b) and compute two new numbers: the average (a+b)/2 (the "low-frequency" component, capturing the smooth trend) and the difference (a−b)/2 (the "high-frequency" component, capturing local variation). A row of d weights goes through Haar and comes out as d numbers still, but split into d/2 low-frequency coefficients and d/2 high-frequency coefficients.

Why this is elegant:

1. *Representational capacity explodes*: each original weight position is now determined by two terms — "one low-freq codeword × scale + one high-freq codeword × scale." The number of distinct representable values per row leaps from 8 to *1024* — two orders of magnitude.
2. *Zero runtime overhead*: the Haar transform can be written as two fixed 1D convolution kernels of size 2 ([1/2, 1/2] and [1/2, −1/2]). The entire transform is done offline during the quantization phase; the inverse transform constants are hard-coded into the model, so *not a single extra multiply-add is needed at inference time*.
3. *Good locality*: unlike the global rotations in QuIP, Haar is local — each transform coefficient depends only on a few neighboring weights, rather than mixing information from the entire row.

Two additional structure-aware strategies round out the method:
- *Frequency-aware multi-parameter intra-row grouping*: the low-frequency and high-frequency segments of each row are grouped separately, with binary parameters computed independently for each segment.
- *L2-norm-driven column selection*: the L2 norm (rather than BiLLM's fixed threshold) is used to select salient columns, yielding more accurate selection.

*** Why it worked (and where new traps may lie)

HBLLM reaches 1.08 bits on LLaMA2-13B with a perplexity of 6.71 — versus an FP16 baseline of 4.88 and ARB-LLM's 10.98. This is the first time 1-bit quantization has brought perplexity to within 1.5× of FP16.

And it is practical: ARB-LLM_X and FrameQuant cannot finish LLaMA-30B on a single 24 GB GPU; HBLLM can.

Open questions (speculative):

1. Haar is the simplest wavelet, looking only at pairs of adjacent weights. Could higher-order wavelets (Daubechies, Symlets) raise representational capacity further?
2. Haar splits a row into exactly two frequency bands — the number of bands is fixed. Could multi-level wavelet decomposition, as used in image processing, help?
3. The entire scheme is weight-only; activations remain in FP16. DBellQuant and BWLA are already probing that gap in parallel.

* Frontier Extensions

HBLLM appeared at the end of November 2025 (NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight) and is still very new. Yet several parallel and follow-up explorations have already emerged.

** 2025 | DBellQuant: Transforming Single-Bell Weights into a Dual-Bell Distribution

Rather than frequency decomposition, this line takes a different approach: *first apply a learnable orthogonal transform to reshape the weight distribution from unimodal to bimodal, then binarize.* The rationale: binarization only has two codewords ±1; if the weight distribution is already bimodal, quantization error is naturally smaller. The inverse transform is then applied to the activations, conveniently suppressing activation outliers — achieving W1A6, *the first PTQ method to simultaneously push weights to ~1 bit and activations to 6 bits*.

Relationship to HBLLM: HBLLM works on the *spatial structure* of weights (wavelets); DBellQuant works on the *distributional shape* of weights (dual-bell). The two directions may eventually be combined.

** 2025 | PTQ1.61: Reducing Mask Overhead to 0.0002 Bits via Structured Masking

PB-LLM-style masks are expensive; BiLLM/ARB-LLM use structured column selection to reduce the cost; PTQ1.61 pushes this to the extreme — *reducing the 1-bit mask to 0.0002 bits per weight* — and achieves an average of 1.61 bits. It also introduces lightweight LoRA to "pre-align" weights into a channel-quantization-friendly form.

** 2025 | BWLA: 1-Bit Weights + Low-Bit Activations

Orthogonal Kronecker transforms combined with EM optimization compress weights to 1 bit and activations to 6 bits, enabling true end-to-end acceleration — 3.26× faster than OSTQuant on LLaMA2-13B.

** Trends

The main threads after HBLLM: (a) more sophisticated orthogonal transforms (upgrading from Haar to higher-order wavelets or learned transforms), (b) joint weight + activation low-bit quantization, (c) lightweight "pre-alignment" steps that reshape the weight distribution into a quantization-friendly form before quantization begins.

* Insights

Reading this lineage, the real change is not "bit count dropping from 16 to 1" but rather *researchers developing an ever-deeper understanding of what a single row of weights actually is*:

1. *OBS / OBQ / GPTQ* treat weights as a *collection of scalars*, asking "which one matters most?"
2. *PB-LLM / BiLLM / ARB-LLM* treat weights as a *structured matrix*, asking "which rows and columns matter most?"
3. *HBLLM* treats weights as a *signal*, asking "what is its frequency structure?"

Each step forward is not a new algorithm — it is *a new way of looking at the weights*. So-called "innovation" is often just importing a mature tool from another field (Hessian from 1990s pruning, wavelets from 1980s image compression) and connecting it to the problem at hand. The problem stays the same; the perspective changes.

A second pattern: *the ceiling on compression is set by the number of distinct representable values*. BiLLM: 8. ARB-LLM: 10. HBLLM: 1024. Every step-change improvement came from someone realizing "the representational capacity of this row can actually grow." Where is the next step-change? Perhaps multi-level wavelet decomposition; perhaps learned local transforms; perhaps a joint representation of weights and activations as a more compact codebook.

Three directions are already being pushed:
- *Joint weight + activation low-bit quantization* (DBellQuant, BWLA) — compressing weights alone is not enough; end-to-end speedup requires low-bit activations too.
- *Sub-1-bit* (BTC-LLM has reached 0.7 bits) — using learned codebooks so multiple weights share codewords.
- *Pre-alignment + quantization* (PTQ1.61's LoRA pre-alignment, DBellQuant's dual-bell transform) — reshaping the weight distribution into a quantization-friendly form before quantization even begins.

* Takeaways

This lineage offers several concrete lessons for how to do research:

1. *Read introductions in reverse.* Every LLM quantization paper's introduction explicitly names who it is criticizing and what the flaw is. Follow that "chain of criticism" backward three steps and you have the field's core tensions in hand. This is ten times faster than reading a survey, because surveys spread every direction out flat, while the criticism chain follows only the sharpest line of problems.

2. *Look for old tools in new settings.* The Haar wavelet HBLLM uses is 1909 mathematics, a mature image-compression tool from the 1980s. Nobody in the LLM quantization community had tried it — not because it was unsuitable, but because nobody went looking for it. Every emerging field has abundant "old tool + new context" vacancies. Ask yourself: *what mature tools from other fields has nobody tried on my problem?*

3. *Watch the ceiling, not the local optimizations.* BiLLM 8, ARB-LLM 10 — once a number becomes the bottleneck, all subsequent work optimizes *within* that number. HBLLM broke through to 1024, making all the previous local optimizations suddenly irrelevant. What is the ceiling number in your field? Who could raise it by an order of magnitude?

4. *Validate your idea in plain language.* "I split the weights into high-frequency and low-frequency halves, quantize each independently, get 100× more representable values, and add zero inference overhead." A non-expert can understand that sentence. If you cannot state your method that clearly, you probably have not thought it through yet.

The practical upshot: the next time you read a technical paper, do not start with the abstract — *start with the lines in the introduction that say "X method has Y problem"* and trace backward. The next time you look for a research direction, do not just read the latest survey in your field — *find a mature tool from an adjacent field with thirty years of history and see whether it fits anywhere in your problem*.
