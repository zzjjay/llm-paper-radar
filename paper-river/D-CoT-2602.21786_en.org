* One-liner

This river is about: why teaching small models to "think like large models" fails, and how, step by step, we came to understand that the problem lies not in the *content* of thinking but in its *structure*.

* Reading the Story Forward: How One Problem Grew Into Another

** Stop 1: Make the Model Show Its Work (Wei et al. 2022, Chain-of-Thought)

Early language models behaved like quiz-show buzzers: ask "what is 23 times 17?" and they would spit out a number immediately — and frequently get it wrong. Wei et al. discovered a disarmingly simple trick: if you ask the model to write out its calculation step by step *before* giving the answer, accuracy goes up.

That is Chain-of-Thought (CoT). It sounds obvious, but the implication is significant: reasoning ability does not arise from nowhere — the model only reasons effectively when it "lays its working memory out explicitly across the token sequence." From that point on, "think before you answer" became the default for any complex task.

The question it left behind: how is CoT learned? Early on it was induced by prompting. Could a model be trained to do it spontaneously?

** Stop 2: Push "Thinking" to Its Limit with RL (Guo et al. 2025, DeepSeek-R1)

By 2025, DeepSeek-R1 gave a striking answer: you do not need to teach the model *how* to think. Apply reinforcement learning — reward only correct final answers, say nothing about the process — and the model evolves longer and longer traces that look uncannily like human deliberation.

R1's thinking traces are staggering in length: a single problem can produce thousands of tokens filled with "Wait, let me reconsider…", "Actually, I think…", "Let me double-check…" These are not a trained format; they are an *effective strategy* that RL discovered on its own. The result: R1 reached the frontier on math, code, and science.

The question it left behind: this "long thinking" is expensive. A single inference can consume compute that ordinary deployments cannot afford. Can it be compressed into an 8B small model?

** Stop 3: Distillation Becomes the Standard — and a Trap

The community's first response was entirely natural. Since R1's reasoning is written in tokens, take those tokens as training data and feed them to a small model, teaching it to imitate the pattern. That is *CoT distillation*.

Operationally simple; initially promising. But gradually a strange phenomenon appeared: the small model learned to *look* like it was thinking, yet its accuracy fell below the undistilled baseline. It would spiral into endless self-doubt, writing 8,000 tokens about a multiple-choice question without ever committing to an answer.

The problem went unsolved — until someone named it.

** Stop 4: Give the Phenomenon a Name (Wu et al. 2026, Overthinking)

Wu et al. did something crucial: they plotted CoT length against accuracy. The result was an *inverted-U curve* — too short is bad, but past a certain length accuracy starts falling again. They called this "overthinking."

Why does it happen? Intuitively, reasoning is the product of a chain of steps, each carrying its own error probability. More steps mean higher cumulative error. A large model's per-step error rate is low, so long CoTs gain more than they risk; a small model's per-step error rate is high, so cumulative error overwhelms any benefit. The upshot: *directly feeding a large model's long reasoning to a small model is like asking a novice to imitate a master's hesitations — the master's hesitations are productive iteration; the apprentice's hesitations are just getting lost.*

The diagnosis was in hand. Treatment was left to others.

** Stop 5: First Treatment — Prune the Redundancy (Luo et al. 2025, DLCoT)

DLCoT (Deconstructing Long Chain-of-Thought) offered a direct prescription: if long CoT is the problem, cut the redundant parts out of the training data.

Concretely: decompose each long CoT into four components (roughly "factual statements", "exploratory branches", "computation", "conclusion"), then apply rule-based static filtering to remove the "detour" and "repeated-attempt" branches, retaining only the most direct reasoning path.

Reasonable in principle; it works on math problems. But two cracks appear:

1. *Validated only on math.* Math problems have canonical solutions, so "redundancy" is easy to spot. What about science reasoning tasks like GPQA or broad multi-domain tasks like MMLU-Pro? Unknown.
2. *In Luo's own experiments, DLCoT's accuracy on AIME2024 drops from 53.3% to 40.0%.* This is an admirably honest negative result — and a warning: pruning "exploratory branches" as redundancy also prunes the *trial-and-error capacity* that hard problems actually require.

The problem sharpens: you want the small model to stop rambling, but you cannot afford to strip out its exploratory ability. These two goals appear to be in direct tension.

** Stop 6: A Different Angle — Change the Structure, Not the Content (Ubukata 2026, D-CoT)

D-CoT's pivotal move is: *stop trying to judge which thinking segments are redundant.* That judgment cannot be made reliably, because the same passage of iterative reflection is wasteful on an easy problem and indispensable on a hard one.

Instead: annotate the reasoning data with *mode tags*, and teach the model to learn "which mode to enter when":

- ~<TEMP_LOW>~ signals "listing facts, verifying premises, confirming constraints" — conservative, non-divergent
- ~<TEMP_MID>~ signals "following an algorithm or standard procedure" — steady forward progress
- ~<TEMP_HIGH>~ signals "exploring multiple perspectives, proposing alternatives" — divergent, permitted to leap

These tags are not used to dynamically switch inference temperature at runtime (experiments show dynamic temperature switching yields only a modest 0.5–1.5 point gain). They are structural annotations on training data — telling the model "when this is fact-checking prose, write it this way; when this is exploration prose, write it that way."

D-CoT uses ORPO (Hong 2024) rather than standard SFT, because ORPO learns from contrastive signals of both positive and negative examples. The accompanying negative examples deliberately instantiate 31 failure patterns (e.g., "starting to ramble when facts should be listed", "locking onto one branch when exploration is needed"), so the model learns not just to use the tags but to understand the *correct pairing of tag and content*.

The results are clean: Qwen3-8B on GPQA-Diamond rises from 43% to 53%; MMLU-Pro rises from 55.7% to 64.7%; token usage drops by 31–65%.

More tellingly: *removing the tags entirely at inference time yields the highest performance.* This means the model is not merely following tags on the surface — it has internalized the structured, phase-aware approach. It now automatically organizes facts first, performs single targeted checks when ambiguity arises (rather than the base model's infinite "Wait, but…" loops), and skips over arithmetic details when appropriate.

What D-CoT sees that DLCoT missed: *the small model's problem is not "thinking too much" — it is "not knowing what to think at each moment."* Cutting length treats the symptom; teaching structure treats the cause.

** Stop 7: What Comes Next?

D-CoT's own open seams are visible:

- Validated only on Qwen3-8B. For models like Llama and Mistral that have no native ~<think>~ block support, can the same structure be learned? Unclear.
- How does "internalization" actually happen? The authors promise future attention-map analysis, but for now it is a black box.
- Does the effectiveness with only 5,000 training samples stem from Qwen3 already understanding the "temperature" concept from pretraining, or from ORPO's contrastive signal being inherently data-efficient? No ablation.
- The design of the control tags (the "temperature" metaphor) is empirical — why not ~<EXPLORE>/<EXPLOIT>~ or ~<DIVERGE>/<CONVERGE>~? Nobody yet knows which partition best reflects the model's true internal modes.

The likely trajectory: validation on smaller models (3B, 1.5B), cross-model-family transfer, automatic discovery of tag vocabularies, and interpretability research into the internalization process.

* What Is Really Happening Underneath This River

On the surface, this is a technical evolution story about making small model reasoning more efficient. Beneath the surface, it is about a deeper cognitive shift:

*From imitating content to imitating structure.*

The founding assumption of distillation is: a reasoning trace is a token sequence, so copying the token sequence copies the reasoning. That assumption is wrong. R1's long traces are effective not because they are *long*, but because different *modes of cognitive activity* are interleaved within them — some passages verify facts, some probe possibilities, some advance computation — and the large model is powerful enough to switch between these modes fluidly.

Small models lack the capacity to learn this implicit mode-switching. Imitating the surface tokens therefore becomes "attempting every mode simultaneously while executing none of them deeply" — which is the real nature of overthinking: not too much thinking, but *modal confusion*.

DLCoT tried to remove the parts that "looked redundant" — but the parts removed were precisely the high-temperature exploratory branches that a large model uses to navigate hard problems. Strip them out and the model becomes brittle. D-CoT reverses the logic: *let the small model explicitly know which mode it is currently in, and transitions between modes become structure rather than chaos.*

This is actually an insight older than the LLM era: *learning a skill in its early stages requires explicit scaffolding; once internalized, the scaffolding can be removed.* D-CoT embeds this principle from educational theory into SLM training — and the experiments confirm that the scaffolding genuinely is internalized (removing it at inference time actually improves performance).

Looking forward, the next step in this river is probably not "add one more kind of tag." It is more likely: tags as explicit state variables within model architecture (e.g., a dedicated partition of attention heads), or going the other direction and letting the model *discover for itself* which tags it needs — handing the design of the scaffolding back to the model.
