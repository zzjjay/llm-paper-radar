* The Problem River

A large MoE model has hundreds of "expert sub-networks," and for each incoming token it picks a few to compute. The trouble is: every token is forced to pick the same number of experts — a simple token still picks 8, and so does a hard one. The compute spent on simple tokens is pure waste.

Why is this hard to fix? Over thirty years, every generation has tried to let tokens speak for themselves: "I only need 2 experts — don't stuff 8 in." But every attempt to let the model "decide how many to pick" hits a wall — either training and inference fall out of sync (train-inference mismatch), or the count collapses to 0 and everything breaks, or the two tasks of "picking experts" and "deciding how many" fight each other inside a single module and neither is learned well.

So the evolution along this line is, at its core, a question asked over and over: "Can we let the model learn, on its own, how many experts each token should use — and does that number actually save compute, rather than just pretending to?"

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1991 | Jacobs & Jordan: Let a Group of Small Experts Divide the Labor

*** What Problem It Saw

Neural networks of that era were "one network covers everything" — the same weights processed all inputs. But real tasks often contain multiple sub-patterns: when recognizing handwritten digits, "drawing a 1" and "drawing an 8" involve completely different stroke structures. Cramming them into one network causes the weights to interfere with each other, and neither pattern is learned well.

*** Its Solution

Like a small restaurant that hires several cooks: one specializes in pasta, one in sushi, one in burgers. A host at the door (the gating network) looks at each customer and leads them to the right cook. Each cook (expert) learns only one type of dish; the gate only learns "which kind of customer goes to which cook."

*** Why It Worked (and Where the Next Pit Was Buried)

With division of labor, each expert faces less parameter pressure and can focus on one class of patterns. But the pit is: to make the model smarter you need more experts — and with more experts, the gate still has to query every expert to compute weights, so compute explodes again. That is exactly what Shazeer set out to fix.

** 2017 | Shazeer et al. (Sparsely-Gated MoE): Don't Call All the Cooks to a Meeting — Only Summon the Top-K

*** What Problem It Saw

At the time, researchers wanted to stack hundreds or even thousands of experts to expand capacity. But processing every token through all experts means more experts = slower model; scaling capacity becomes self-defeating. The "full-staff vote" of Jacobs/Jordan is simply infeasible at large scale.

*** Its Solution

After the gate computes a score for each expert, only the K highest-scoring experts (e.g., K = 2) are actually computed; the rest go dormant and are not evaluated at all. In one move, compute drops from O(N experts) to O(K experts) — no matter how large N grows.

To prevent the model from lazily reusing the same two or three experts every time, a load-balancing auxiliary loss was added, forcing the gate to distribute work evenly across all experts.

*** Why It Worked (and Where the Next Pit Was Buried)

Sparse activation + load balancing is a brilliant combination: parameters can scale without bound while compute stays flat. But the new pit is: every token is forced to pick exactly K experts — a token like "the" picks the same number as "prove Fermat's last theorem." Simple tokens are wasting compute.

** 2020 | GShard (Lepikhin et al.): Actually Moving MoE into the Transformer Era

*** What Problem It Saw

Shazeer's MoE ran on an LSTM translation model. By 2018–2020, Transformers had taken over everything. Embedding MoE into a Transformer is not a simple copy-paste — it also requires multi-machine parallelism (a single expert may not fit on one GPU), cross-machine communication, and distributed load balancing.

*** Its Solution

Top-2 routing (K = 2, a special case of Top-K) was inserted into the FFN layers of a Transformer, alternating every other layer with a MoE layer. A full "expert parallelism" engineering scheme was defined: each expert lives on one GPU, and tokens are dispatched to the right GPU via all-to-all communication. The load-balancing loss was retained but made stricter — imbalance causes communication bottlenecks.

*** Why It Worked (and Where the Next Pit Was Buried)

Transformer + Top-2 + expert parallelism became the template for all subsequent MoE engineering (Mixtral, DeepSeek-V2, Qwen3-MoE are all built on this foundation). But the assumption of "Top-2" — more precisely, "fixed Top-K" — was left untouched. The pit remains: every token is forced to consult the same number of experts.

** 2024 | MoE-Dynamic / XMoE (Huang et al.; Yang et al.): Simple Tokens Should Pick Fewer Experts

*** What Problem It Saw

The fixed Top-K assumption inherited from the GShard lineage was finally challenged seriously in 2023–2024. Researchers observed that after softmax, routing weights tend to follow a long-tail distribution: "0.7, 0.2, 0.05, 0.03, ...". Those trailing experts at 0.05 and 0.03 contribute almost nothing when computed — they are just filling a quota.

*** Its Solution

Drop the fixed K. Switch to a "cumulative threshold": accumulate routing weights in descending order and stop once the running sum exceeds a threshold (e.g., 0.9). A simple token may cross 0.9 after just two experts; a hard one might need five or six. Each token adapts individually.

XMoE and similar works all share this core idea: use cumulative probability as a proxy for K.

*** Why It Worked (and Where the Next Pit Was Buried)

Tokens finally have differentiated compute budgets. But three new pits open up simultaneously:
1. It assumes "low routing weight = unimportant," but high-weight experts are not necessarily truly important (BEAM's Section 5 uses experiments to challenge this assumption).
2. At least one expert must be selected — otherwise softmax is mathematically undefined — so the sparsity floor is hard-coded.
3. This approach cannot drop an expert that scores high but is actually redundant.

** 2024 | AdaMoE (Zeng et al.); MoE++ (Jin et al.): Add Some "Null Experts" — Even a Placeholder Can Save Compute

*** What Problem It Saw

The "at least one expert" constraint of threshold methods is a dead end. To push some tokens' compute budget close to zero, changing the threshold alone is insufficient — a different approach is needed.

*** Its Solution

Add a few special "null experts" — experts whose compute is zero (they output zero directly, or simply pass the input through unchanged). They participate in Top-K scoring just like real experts; being selected means that slot is effectively "voided." If a token is assigned two real experts and six null experts, only two real experts' work is actually computed.

LongCat (2025) engineered this idea into production. MoE++ went further with three varieties of null expert.

*** Why It Worked (and Where the Next Pit Was Buried)

This sidesteps the "at least one real expert" constraint. But BEAM identifies:
1. The number of null experts is a hyperparameter that is cumbersome to tune.
2. Sparsity control is "passive" — you cannot say "I want 50% sparsity"; you can only hope the model happens to pick null experts.
3. It modifies the model architecture; it is not a pure software change, making integration difficult.

The deeper problem: expert selection, load balancing, and sparsity control are still all crammed into the same router, and their gradients fight each other.

** 2026 | BEAM (Wu et al.): Expert Selection Is One Thing; Expert Pruning Is Another

*** What Problem It Saw

After surveying all the prior approaches, BEAM distills the root cause into three sentences:
1. Using "routing rank" as a proxy for "expert importance" is wrong. A low-ranked expert may be critically important; a high-ranked one may be redundant.
2. Neither cumulative thresholds nor null experts can "actively" drop redundant experts — sparsity is only achieved indirectly.
3. Expert selection, load balancing, and sparsity control are all crammed into one router, and their objectives conflict.

*** Its Solution

Completely decouple "picking experts" from "deciding how many to pick." The original router remains, continuing to select Top-K candidates as before. A lightweight "mask router" is added on top, dedicated solely to emitting a binary mask (0/1) over the Top-K candidates — those with mask value 1 are kept; those with 0 are dropped.

Binary values are non-differentiable? Use Bengio 2013's STE (Straight-Through Estimator): the forward pass uses binary values; the backward pass pretends they are continuous. A sparsity-encouraging auxiliary loss is added, and the whole system is trained end-to-end, so the model itself learns "how many experts to drop for this token to be most efficient."

A custom CUDA kernel integrated into vLLM is also provided — usable in a single line of code.

*** Why It Worked (and Where the Next Pit Was Buried)

All three problems are addressed:
- Active pruning: the mask directly decides fate, without relying on indirect signals.
- Decoupling: expert selection and sparsity control no longer interfere.
- End-to-end: sparsity is learned, not a hand-tuned hyperparameter.

Remaining pits? Training is required (it is not train-free); the CUDA kernel is hand-written and must be rewritten for new hardware; the mask router itself has computational overhead — small but not zero.

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026 (when BEAM was submitted), the most direct follow-on work along this line is still emerging — it typically takes the form of:
- Pushing BEAM's masking idea to more aggressive sparsity (>90% FLOPs reduction);
- Replacing "binary" with "low-bit" (2-bit/3-bit) for finer-grained control;
- Further lightening the mask router, or having it share some parameters with the original router;
- Extending to long-context settings, jointly optimizing sparsity across position and layer (BEAM's own Section 5.2 already begins exploring this).

Most of these directions have not yet crystallized into landmark papers. Honestly: BEAM is currently the most recent well-defined node on this line.

* Insights

On the surface, this thirty-year line is about "how to make MoE more compute-efficient." Looked at more deeply, it is a recurring cognitive shift: *from "a model = a fixed set of operations" to "a model = a set of learnable switches."*

Every generation's advance consists of taking something "decided by humans" and letting the model learn it instead:
- Jacobs/Jordan: the division of labor is learned by the model (rather than tasks being partitioned by hand).
- Shazeer: which experts to pick is learned by the model (rather than querying everyone).
- MoE-Dynamic: how many experts each token picks is learned by the model (rather than a fixed K).
- BEAM: precisely "which candidates to drop from the already-selected set" is learned by the model (rather than passive sparsity).

Each generation's solution looks like "add one more learnable switch." But every time a switch is added, it exposes a "coupling" that nobody had noticed before — the original router was quietly doing several jobs at once. BEAM's real contribution is not the technical binary mask (that trick goes back to STE in 2013), but the diagnosis: "expert selection and sparsity control are two separate things and should be handled separately."

Where is the next step most likely to go? I would bet on two directions:
1. *Keep decoupling.* BEAM splits the router into 2 components; the next generation may split it into 3 (expert selection, load balancing, and sparsity each handled independently). Coupling is the mother lode of engineering progress.
2. *Hardware co-design.* BEAM's binary mask is a "hardware-friendly" signal, but it is still a software-level afterthought. The next generation will know, at training time, how sparse the target hardware can actually run, and will fold hardware characteristics back into the loss.

* Takeaways

For research practice —

*Watch for coupling, not metrics.* BEAM is not a paper that improves a benchmark by 1 point. Its core insight is "these three things were fighting each other," and it separates them. Work that identifies a hidden coupling has more lasting impact than any metric gain. When you see a module doing multiple things at once — load balancing, selection, and sparsity all crammed into one router — there is almost certainly a paper hiding there.

*"Passive vs. active" is a dichotomy worth revisiting repeatedly.* MoE-Dynamic is passively sparse (relying on a threshold to stumble into sparsity); AdaMoE is also passive (relying on null experts being selected by chance); BEAM is active (the mask directly names who gets dropped). The same dichotomy applies across many domains: cache eviction policies (LRU is passive, LLM-judged is active), attention sparsification, quantization, and so on.

Practical implications —

*When tuning MoE inference*, the first line of defense is no longer quantization or KV cache — it is asking "am I giving every token the same compute budget?" If your workload contains a large proportion of simple tokens (conversation, template generation, code completion), dynamic routing may yield larger gains than quantization.

*When designing new methods*, start by drawing out which coupled objectives are crowded into a single module. The more tightly they are packed, the more worthwhile it is to pull them apart.
