* The River of the Problem

In a single sentence: *A large MoE model contains dozens or even hundreds of "experts," all of which must reside in GPU memory—so you have to compress each expert's bits. But how do you know which expert deserves a few extra bits, and which one can be squeezed mercilessly?*

First, let's be clear about why MoE forces this problem into the open. An ordinary large model passes every token through all weights; MoE (Mixture of Experts) is different—it splits each FFN layer into dozens of "experts," and each token is routed to only two or three of them. Only the selected experts are computed, so *computation is cheap*. The catch is this: *all experts' weights must reside in memory at all times*—you don't know in advance which expert the next token will be routed to. MoE is therefore the archetypal "compute little, store much" architecture, and the bottleneck shifts from compute to memory.

The way to reduce memory pressure is quantization (compressing weights from 16 bits down to 4 or 3 bits). But experts are not equal: some are called upon constantly and carry the bulk of the work—compress them aggressively and the model collapses; others are cold backups, and squeezing them to 2 bits is harmless. The smart play is therefore *mixed precision*—assign each expert a different bit-width according to its importance.

That forces the central question of this entire river into the open: *How do you measure an expert's "importance"?*

For over a decade, everyone's answer has circled the same word—*calibration data*: feed a small batch of samples through the model, observe which experts are activated most, measure how much error their compression introduces, and allocate bits accordingly. This path has been walked for a long time and refined to a high degree—but it has a structural dead-end that no one can escape:

*What data do you calibrate on?* The true training data of frontier MoE models (DeepSeek, Qwen-MoE, Mixtral) is proprietary; outsiders simply cannot obtain it. You are forced to use a proxy dataset—but the moment you calibrate on math problems, the model tilts toward math and stumbles on code; calibrate on general web text, and you may never activate certain specialized experts at all, misclassifying them as "unimportant" and crushing them into garbage. *Whatever flavor the calibration set has, the quantized model is locked into that flavor.*

The current endpoint of this river is the answer AlphaQ gives—a cut-at-the-root response: *Don't calibrate at all. Whether an expert was trained well, and whether it deserves more bits, can be read directly from the weight matrix itself—no data required.*

* The Source Map

This river has *two tributaries*, which converge for the first time at AlphaQ. One is the main thread of "how to allocate bits across MoE experts" (perpetually tangled in calibration data); the other is a quieter undercurrent of "how to judge a weight matrix's quality without any data" (heavy-tail self-regularization theory).

* The Evolutionary Narrative

The two tributaries are told separately below; then we watch them converge at AlphaQ. Each section opens not with "what this paper is" but with "what crack it saw in its predecessor."

** 2019 | HAWQ: Let "Sensitivity" Decide Who Gets More Bits

*** What crack it saw

The earliest researchers attempting mixed-precision quantization faced a combinatorial explosion: a network with dozens of layers, each of which could be 2/4/8 bits, yields an exponential number of configurations. Manual tuning was impossible; brute-force search was equally hopeless. What was missing was a *principled signal*—something to look at that would reveal whether a layer deserved more bits.

*** Its solution

HAWQ's answer was the *Hessian* (second-order derivative, essentially a map of "how sensitive the output is to this layer's weights"). Sensitive layers—perturb them slightly and the output explodes—get more bits; inert layers get compressed hard. HAWQ-v2 refined this further: instead of just the maximum eigenvalue, look at the full trace of the Hessian, and automatically select configurations from the Pareto frontier.

*** Why it worked (and what new flaw it buried)

The framework of "allocate by sensitivity" took hold, and every subsequent method kept the same template of "find a signal to rank expert/layer importance." But HAWQ was designed for CNN vision models; the Hessian is expensive to compute. More critically—years later, MC-MoE would run experiments specifically showing that *Hessian sensitivity is simply not a good signal for MoE experts*. That flaw would only be stepped on when the MoE era arrived.

** 2022 | GPTQ: No Bit Allocation, but Became Everyone's "Compression Back-end"

*** What crack it saw

With 175B-scale models, a single GPU could not hold the weights—compression became a hard requirement. But naïve quantization collapsed below 8 bits because it rounded every weight identically, ignoring the fact that weights are deeply unequal: perturb one and the output explodes; perturb another and nothing happens.

*** Its solution

GPTQ uses second-order information to govern the *order of rounding and error compensation*: each time a weight is quantized, the not-yet-quantized neighbors are nudged to absorb the newly introduced error. This allowed 175B models to be compressed to 3–4 bits in a matter of hours with almost no accuracy drop.

*** Why it worked (and what new flaw it buried)

GPTQ acknowledged that weights are unequal. But notice—*GPTQ uses uniform precision; it makes no decisions about who gets more bits*. It solves "given a bit-width, how do you compress accurately," not "how do you allocate bits." In this river, GPTQ plays the role of a *back-end tool*: later, MC-MoE and MoEQuant both use it as their compression engine, stacking their own "allocation strategy" on top. The real bit-allocation problem remained open.

** 2024 | MC-MoE / PMQ: First-ever Per-Expert Bit Allocation—via Calibration Data

*** What crack it saw

The MoE era arrived. MC-MoE identified a concrete waste: MoE experts vary wildly in how often they are called upon—some are activated constantly, others almost never. Assigning every expert the same bit-width (e.g., all compressed to 2 bits) causes a 28.6-point accuracy drop. Differentiated treatment according to importance was clearly called for.

*** Its solution

PMQ (per-expert mixed precision) frames "assigning bits to each expert" as a *linear programming* problem. It needs three signals: each expert's *activation frequency* (how often it is routed to), its activation weight, and the *reconstruction error* (Frobenius norm) that compressing it incurs. All three signals are measured on *calibration data* (the C4 dataset), the LP is then solved for the optimal allocation, and GPTQ performs the actual compression. As a bonus, PMQ demonstrates empirically that the Frobenius-norm signal beats HAWQ's Hessian signal for experts (54% vs 46%)—landing a blow on the HAWQ thread.

*** Why it worked (and what new flaw it buried)

PMQ was genuinely the first to cast "expert-level mixed precision" as a solvable optimization problem—a real advance. But it planted the root cause of every subsequent headache in the river: *every signal it relies on comes from calibration data*. AlphaQ will attack this head-on: a model calibrated on the MATH dataset performs well on math and noticeably worse on code; *whatever distribution the calibration set has, the quantized model is locked into that distribution*. Worse, the true training data of frontier MoE models is proprietary—any calibration set you can obtain is an imperfect proxy that may never activate certain specialized experts, causing them to be misclassified as unimportant and over-compressed. AlphaQ distills this pathology into two phrases: *locally optimal* and *calibration-dependent*.

** 2025 | MoEQuant & MxMoE: More Patches Inside the Calibration Frame

*** What crack they saw

After PMQ, two independent groups each targeted a different weakness—but neither stepped outside the calibration frame.

*MoEQuant* focused on: MoE routing naturally produces unbalanced calibration data—popular experts accumulate many samples, cold experts accumulate few, so cold experts are under-calibrated and their quantization collapses.

*MxMoE* focused on: PMQ's granularity stops at "the whole expert"—too coarse. And PMQ optimizes only accuracy, ignoring whether the resulting mixed-precision layout is fast on actual GPU hardware.

*** Their solutions

MoEQuant proposed *expert-balanced self-sampling* (EBSS), constructing a calibration set that covers all experts uniformly, then applying affinity-weighted quantization error. MxMoE pushed the allocation granularity *down to the linear-block level* (finer than a whole expert) and co-designed hardware-aware mixed-precision GEMM kernels.

*** Why they worked (and what new flaw they buried)

Each made genuine progress—MoEQuant made calibration more balanced; MxMoE made granularity finer and execution faster. But AlphaQ dismisses both with a single observation: *neither of them pulled out the calibration nail*. MoEQuant produces a better calibration set, but "a better proxy is still a proxy." MxMoE's bit-allocation signals (activation frequency + sensitivity) still come from calibration data. Tributary A, having arrived here, had fully exhausted the question of "how to calibrate more precisely"—yet nobody asked the prior question: *Is calibration even necessary?*

The answer was not anywhere in Tributary A. It was on a quiet undercurrent that almost nobody had noticed.

** 2018 | Martin & Mahoney: Without Seeing Any Data, Reading Quality Straight from the Weights

*** What crack it saw

This is the source of Tributary B, and it asks a question that seems unrelated but turns out to be critical: *Can you judge how well a model was trained when you have no training data, no test data, and don't even know the hyperparameters?* Classical generalization theory cannot—it always requires data.

*** Its solution

Take each weight matrix W, compute the eigenvalue spectral density (ESD) of its correlation matrix—essentially a histogram of eigenvalues. Martin & Mahoney discovered that well-trained networks produce spectra with *heavy tails*: not a tidy bell curve but a distribution with a long trailing edge. Fit a *power law* to that tail and extract exponent *alpha*: smaller alpha (closer to 2, heavier tail) means the matrix has learned stronger structure and was trained more thoroughly. Subsequent work published in Nature Communications validated this across hundreds of public models: *alpha predicts model quality without touching any data whatsoever*.

*** Why it worked (and what new flaw it buried)

This introduced an entirely new ruler: *a weight matrix's "quality" is written in its own eigenvalue spectrum—no external data needed to interrogate it*. But for a long time alpha remained purely a *diagnostic metric*—it told you "how well this model/layer was trained," nothing more. Turning it into a tool that could *command allocation decisions* would take a few more years.

** 2024 | AlphaPruning: Upgrading alpha from "Diagnosis" to "Budget Allocator"

*** What crack it saw

Pruning (zeroing out unimportant weights) had long relied on *uniform sparsity ratios*—every layer pruned by the same fraction. But layers vary enormously in how thoroughly they were trained, making uniform pruning obviously suboptimal and setting a ceiling on achievable sparsity.

*** Its solution

AlphaPruning made the decisive leap: *use each layer's alpha to allocate that layer's sparsity budget*. Heavy-tailed layers (small alpha, well-trained) get pruned less; light-tailed layers get pruned more. It uses the more robust *PL_Alpha_Hill* metric (based on the Hill estimator, which is more stable than raw power-law fitting because the original fit is highly sensitive to the choice of tail cutoff—making cross-layer comparisons unreliable). The result pushed sparsity on LLaMA-7B to 80%, decisively outperforming uniform allocation.

*** Why it worked (and what new flaw it buried)

This step is the cognitive turning point of Tributary B: *alpha moved from "reading" to "budget" for the first time*. The mapping "heavy tail = well-trained = disturb it less" was used for the first time to direct a cross-layer resource allocation. AlphaDecay immediately applied the same template to weight decay during training (heavy-tailed modules get less regularization, light-tailed modules get more). By this point, "alpha as a budget allocator" had been validated across *sparsity* and *regularization strength*. One natural slot remained unfilled: *the quantization bit budget*.

** 2026 | AlphaQ: Two Rivers Converge—Using alpha to Allocate Bits, Zero Calibration Throughout

*** What crack it saw

AlphaQ fixed its gaze on the terminal state of both rivers simultaneously. Tributary A (MoE quantization) had exhausted its trajectory without anyone pulling out the calibration nail—and the pathology of calibration is structural: frontier MoE training data is inaccessible, and any proxy calibration set locks the quantized model into that set's distributional biases (calibrate on MATH → skew toward math). Tributary B (HT-SR), meanwhile, had repeatedly demonstrated: *how well a weight matrix was trained can be read from its own spectrum—no data required*.

Once these two facts are placed side by side, the answer leaps out: *if alpha can judge how well an expert was trained without any data, it can directly tell you whether that expert deserves more bits—without any calibration.* A well-trained expert encodes more useful structure; compressing it aggressively incurs large loss—give it more bits. A poorly trained expert—compress it hard.

*** Its solution

Take the "alpha as budget allocator" template from AlphaPruning/AlphaDecay and *transpose it onto the quantization bit dimension*:

- For each expert's weight matrix, compute its *PL_Alpha_Hill* (the heavy-tailedness of its eigenvalue spectrum).
- Heavy-tailed experts (small alpha, well-trained) → assigned higher bit-width; light-tailed experts → compressed aggressively to low bit-width.
- *Zero calibration data throughout*—alpha is computed from the weights themselves; no samples need to be fed through the model, and no activation frequencies need to be observed.

This is where the name AlphaQ comes from: *Alpha* (the HT-SR spectral metric) + *Q* (quantization). It replaces PMQ's complex pipeline of "activation frequency + reconstruction error + linear programming + calibration data" with a data-free procedure: compute each expert's alpha, rank by alpha, allocate bits accordingly.

*** Why it worked (and what new flaw it buried)

It works because it is the first time the two independently evolving rivers have been joined: Tributary A supplied the problem and the setting ("MoE experts need mixed-precision allocation"); Tributary B supplied the data-free ruler ("weight quality is readable from the spectrum"). Prior work either had the problem but no ruler (MC-MoE had to fall back on calibration), or had the ruler but never applied it to this problem (AlphaPruning/AlphaDecay never touched quantization). AlphaQ inserted the "data-free ruler" precisely into the "calibration dependency" wound—the sorest spot in the entire field.

The payoff: on Qwen1.5-MoE, an average of just *3.5 bits per expert* approaches full-precision quality, achieving more than 4× memory compression—and because there is no calibration dependency, *performance is stable across domains (math / code / general)*, unlike PMQ which is pulled in whatever direction its calibration set pointed.

Flaws left behind: ① alpha measures "how thoroughly a weight was trained," but *thoroughly trained ≠ important for downstream tasks*—a well-trained expert that is rarely routed to will receive high bit allocation from alpha, which may not be cost-effective; the pure weight-space view is blind to actual utilization at inference time. ② It discards the calibration signal (activation frequency) entirely, even though frequency genuinely carries useful information. In principle, fusing "weight quality + utilization frequency" should outperform alpha alone—but bringing frequency back in means bringing calibration back in. This tension between "zero calibration" and "using frequency information" is the entry point for the next paper.

* Frontier Extensions

As of the current moment (June 2026), AlphaQ is itself the latest confluence point on this river—it was posted in early June. Following the fuses it has laid, the most likely next steps are:

- *Fusing "weight quality" and "utilization frequency" without reinstating calibration*. In order to shed calibration, AlphaQ also shed the genuinely useful "activation frequency" signal. The natural next question is: can utilization frequency be estimated in a similarly data-free or lightweight online manner—e.g., read from the router weights themselves rather than from calibration samples—and then fused with alpha?

- *The alpha template continuing to expand along other compression axes*. HT-SR has now traversed sparsity → weight decay → quantization bit-width. Remaining blank squares that "allocate by alpha" could directly fill include KV-cache compression, rank allocation in low-rank decomposition, and even MoE expert pruning/merging.

- *Theoretically grounding "heavy-tail = deserves more bits"*. The current chain "heavy-tail → well-trained → sensitive to quantization → give more bits" rests on an empirical link at the step "well-trained → more sensitive to quantization." Connecting this rigorously to quantization error bounds would elevate the whole method from "useful heuristic" to "principled guarantee."

* Insights

Reading the entire trajectory, the surface story is "MoE quantization getting progressively more refined." But what is really happening underneath involves three deeper shifts:

1. *The basis for judging "importance" moves from "ask the data" to "ask the weights themselves."* Every advance in Tributary A was essentially "make calibration more accurate"—more balanced sampling, finer granularity, better signals. But they all shared an unquestioned premise: *importance must be measured from data*. AlphaQ's breakthrough is not making calibration more accurate but *changing who you ask*—not the data, but the weight matrix's own spectrum. This is a shift from "measuring behavior" to "reading structure."

2. *The strongest innovations are often connecting two unrelated rivers.* AlphaQ invented almost no new components: HT-SR's alpha was ready-made, the "alpha as budget allocator" template had been proven by AlphaPruning, and the MoE mixed-precision problem was defined by MC-MoE. Its entire contribution lay in *seeing that these two parallel rivers could be joined*—the data-free ruler from Tributary B fit precisely into the calibration-dependency wound that Tributary A could not close. *What is truly scarce is not new components; it is the vision to see that A's solution is exactly B's pain.*

3. *The constraint shifts from "minimize accuracy drop" to "escape data dependency."* Early on, everyone competed on how aggressively they could compress without losing accuracy. But once accuracy loss has been pushed close to full-precision levels, the axis of competition changes—to "what external conditions does your method depend on?" AlphaQ's greatest selling point is not the number 3.5 bits; it is that this number is achievable *without any calibration data*, making the method naturally stable across domains and naturally suited to frontier models whose training data is inaccessible. *When accuracy has been wrung dry, the next battlefield is "shedding dependencies."*

Where does the next step most likely go? Take the "utilization frequency" signal that AlphaQ discarded, recover it in a data-free way (e.g., by reading from the router weights), and fuse it with alpha—preserving "zero calibration" while closing the blind spot that "weight quality alone cannot see actual inference-time utilization."

* Takeaways

The most actionable lessons this evolutionary line offers for how to do research:

1. *Fix your gaze on the premise the entire field takes for granted but nobody has questioned.* Everyone in Tributary A competed on "whose calibration is more accurate," and nobody stepped back to ask "why do we need calibration at all?" That collective blind spot was AlphaQ's entry point. Practical implication: *the next time you read a stack of papers in a field, don't only look at what each one improves—explicitly search for the shared assumption that no one touches. That assumption is usually the field's ceiling and your entry point.*

2. *A ready-made solution from a neighboring field is worth more than a new invention in your own.* AlphaQ's core weapon (HT-SR's alpha) had been mature for years in a different subfield (model diagnostics / pruning); it was simply transported. Practical implication: *when a problem in your own field is stuck, don't rush to invent—go browse the neighboring field for an off-the-shelf ruler or tool that happens to measure exactly your gap. The greatest leverage is almost always at the seam between fields.*

3. *Distinguish clearly between "doing it more precisely" and "asking a different question."* MoEQuant and MxMoE are both rigorous and solid—but they optimize "calibration done more precisely." AlphaQ said "no calibration at all." The former optimizes along an established axis; the latter switched to a different axis entirely. Practical implication: *when you notice yourself making marginal improvements on an axis where everyone is competing, pause and ask whether the axis itself can be bypassed. Papers that change the axis have an order-of-magnitude larger impact than papers that advance along it.*
