* One-Line Hook

QAT has coasted for a decade on "just let the gradient pass through." JacQuant tears that down — it argues that the Jacobian we have always pretended is the identity matrix can actually be learned.

Note: Fifty et al. 2025 (Rotation Trick) is strictly in the VQ-VAE context (ICLR 2025 Oral, arXiv:2410.06424),
not LLM weight quantization. The JacQuant paper uses it as a representative "departure from STE"

This is precisely how the paper's own Fig. 1 presents the comparison.

* Forward Narrative: How the Problem Grew

** Stop 1: Bengio 2013 — "Don't worry about the gradient, just push it through"

The earliest story is not about large models — it is about Bernoulli neurons and quantized activations.
You want to train a network, but somewhere in the middle sits a non-differentiable operation —
rounding, the sign function, sampling.
Back-propagation cannot proceed.

Bengio's ruling: just treat it as 1.
In the forward pass you compute round(x) and get 0 or 1; in the backward pass
you pretend d(round)/dx = 1 and pipe the downstream gradient upstream unchanged.
That is the Straight-Through Estimator, STE.

It is mathematically wrong, but it works. It gave quantization training a "runnable" starting point.
Every quantization-aware training method that followed inherited this lie by default.

The problem it left behind: when exactly does this lie explode?
The answer is — when there are very few quantization bins, when parameters sit right on bin boundaries,
or when they are clipped, the gap between "pretend identity" and "true zero gradient"
becomes enormous. But nobody cared in 2013, because everyone was still working in floating point.

** Stop 2: DoReFa-Net 2016 — "Bring STE into deep networks"

DoReFa-Net was among the first to push STE from activations to the full *weight + activation + gradient*
triple in low-bit form. They demonstrated that you can indeed train and quantize simultaneously,
end-to-end, on both CIFAR and ImageNet without disaster.

But they operated at 4-bit and 8-bit. At those precisions, the error introduced by STE is relatively small,
because each weight has several bins nearby and "pretending identity" does not diverge much from reality.
The story looked like a happy ending at 4-bit precision.

The problem it left behind: what if you want to compress further?
Pushing to 3-bit or 2-bit introduces a new complication — *clipping boundaries*.
A large fraction of weights get truncated to the maximum or minimum representable value;
their gradients should be zero (they are clipped and cannot contribute),
but STE still feeds the full gradient back, and training begins to oscillate.

** Stop 3: PACT 2018 — "Then learn the clip threshold"

PACT diagnosed the previous stop's pain point precisely: clipping.
Its answer — do not let the clip threshold be set by hand; let it be learned together with the other parameters.
Whether your activation clips at 6.0 or 2.3, let the data decide.

This alleviated the truncation problem; 2-bit activations became usable.
But the gradient of the rounding operation itself — PACT still handled that with STE.

The problem it left behind: if the clip threshold can be learned, what about the bin width (step size)?
Why can't the step size be a parameter too?

** Stop 4: LSQ 2020 — "The step size is a parameter too"

Esser's team pushed PACT's logic one step further with LSQ:
not only should the clip threshold be learned, the quantization *step size* (bin width)
should also be learned. And for how to differentiate through the step size,
they offered a more careful estimate than raw STE —
exploiting the fact that weights closer to bin boundaries are more sensitive to changes in step size.

LSQ pushed 2-bit and 3-bit ImageNet to the state of the art at the time.
LSQ+ subsequently added offset and initialization adjustments and became the standard baseline.

But looking closely: LSQ changed the *gradient with respect to the step size*,
not the *gradient through rounding itself* — the round operation still uses STE,
meaning the Jacobian along the path through rounding is still a lie.

The problem it left behind: if you want to go below 2-bit, or quantize LLMs with billions of parameters,
the bias accumulated by STE through rounding can no longer be suppressed.
You have to touch the backward path through round directly.

** Stop 5: Rotation Trick 2025 — "Not identity anymore — a rotation"

Fifty's team took a decisive step in the VQ-VAE setting.
They observed that STE treats quantization as "gradient pass-through,"
which completely ignores the relative geometry between the original x and the quantized q.
Every point inside a Voronoi cell receives the same gradient,
leading to catastrophically low codebook utilization (as low as 2% on VQGAN).

Their solution, the *Rotation Trick*:
express the mapping from x to q as a fixed *rotation + scale* transform,
and substitute the Jacobian of that transform as a constant during back-propagation.
This encodes the angle and magnitude ratio between x and q into the gradient.

Effect: VQGAN codebook utilization rose from 2% to 27%, reconstruction error dropped by two orders of magnitude.

The problem it left behind: the rotation is *fixed*, computed geometrically from the current x and q.
Nothing is *learned*. If the data distribution shifts, the quantizer changes, or the model drifts during training,
is this fixed transform still optimal?
Moreover, the Rotation Trick is primarily formulated for VQ-VAE-style "vector quantization to a codebook";
its geometric framing does not directly transfer to LLM-style "scalar element-wise rounding to a grid."

** Stop 6: JacQuant 2026 — "Just learn the Jacobian directly"

This is the paper's core leap.

JacQuant argues: STE sets Jacobian = I; Rotation Trick sets Jacobian = a fixed rotation.
Both are providing a stand-in for "the Jacobian we don't actually know."
So why not let the data tell you what that Jacobian should be?

The form is remarkably simple: each quantization group (e.g., 128 weights sharing one scale)
is assigned *one scalar* b_g, which serves as the "gain" on that group's backward pass.
Stacking all b_g values yields a block-diagonal B(W) that replaces the identity in STE.

How is b_g learned? Two estimators:
- *Probe*: add a small perturbation δ to the group's weights,
  observe the quantizer output change Δq, and fit b_g ≈ <Δq, δ>/‖δ‖² by least squares.
  Geometrically, this measures "what the local slope of the quantizer actually is."
- *Dither*: subtractive dithering — add uniform noise and subtract it back,
  constructing a differentiable proxy quantizer from which the Jacobian is estimated.

Key design choice: b_g is not updated at every step but *refreshed periodically*,
and reused across tens to hundreds of QAT steps in between, so the extra cost is negligible.

What does it capture that STE cannot?
- Deep inside a bin (far from boundaries), the quantizer behaves nearly linearly, b_g ≈ 1, matching STE;
- In the *saturation/clip* region, the true Jacobian ≈ 0, and b_g is learned to be ≈ 0,
  automatically *damping* gradient updates along those truncated directions.

This is precisely where STE is most unstable at ≤ 2-bit:
large numbers of weights are pressed against the clip boundary,
yet STE keeps faithfully propagating full gradients back, destabilizing training.
JacQuant multiplies gradients in those directions by a small learned number, and stability is restored naturally.

On the theoretical side, the authors prove that within a "code-preserving" training window
(i.e., when the discrete output of the quantizer does not change),
JacQuant's updates can be reduced to a well-defined objective gradient,
with convergence guarantees in the non-convex case and a linear rate under the Polyak–Łojasiewicz condition.

Empirically: across LLaMA3-1B/3B/8B and Qwen3-1.7B, sub-2-bit QAT consistently outperforms STE.

The problems it leaves behind (i.e., the next stops on the paper river):
- B(W) is block-diagonal with one scalar per group. If the quantizer has cross-weight coupling
  within a group (e.g., more complex codebooks, mixed precision), is this approximation sufficient?
- The "local + periodic refresh" assumption requires that W does not drift too far between refreshes.
  Could this fail under long QAT schedules, or when combined with LoRA or continued pretraining?
- Concurrent ZeroQAT (Tan 2025) goes fully zeroth-order; FOGZO mixes first- and zeroth-order.
  These represent different points on the "extra compute vs. accuracy" trade-off curve.
  Which will survive in industrial deployment remains an open question.

* What Is Really Happening Beneath the Evolution

On the surface this looks like "gradient estimators getting progressively more refined."
But at a deeper level, this entire line of work is doing something more fundamental —

*Moving from "pretend quantization doesn't exist" to "treat quantization as a learnable, structured object."*

- STE: quantization is noise; suppress it however you can.
- LSQ: quantization is a parametric operator (step size); the parameter can be learned, but the operator's own derivative is still fake.
- Rotation Trick: quantization is a geometric transformation; represent its Jacobian using the true geometry of the transform.
- JacQuant: quantization is an operator with a local linear approximation; that local slope is an observable, learnable statistic.

This is a trajectory from *denial* → *parametrization* → *geometrization* → *statisticization*.
Each step makes "quantization" a more concrete entity in the optimizer's eyes.

Where does the next step lie? I would bet on two directions:

1. *Make "sensitivity" something beyond the first derivative* — JacQuant learns a first-order Jacobian.
   The natural successor should be someone starting to learn a low-rank approximation to the
   Hessian-vector product, recovering the second-order curvature of quantization as well.
   At < 2-bit, the loss surface is no longer smooth, and first-order alone will eventually prove insufficient.

2. *Expand Jacobian learning from group-scalar to intra-group low-rank matrix* —
   Currently each group shares a single scalar b_g. If weights within certain groups are highly correlated
   (e.g., along the head dimension of attention), replacing the scalar with a rank-k matrix
   might yield disproportionate accuracy gains at still-acceptable computational cost.

* Honest Annotations

- *Direct citation relationships*: The STE←DoReFa←LSQ chain is textbook QAT consensus; highly reliable.
- *Conceptual lineage*: The LSQ→Rotation Trick→JacQuant connection is not a literal citation chain.
  Rotation Trick operates in the VQ-VAE context; JacQuant invokes it as a representative "departure from STE."
  This is a *conceptual lineage*, not a *citation lineage*. The paper's own Fig. 1 places them side by side for comparison.
- *PACT/LSQ→JacQuant*: JacQuant lists PACT and LSQ as canonical representatives of STE-based QAT,
  but does not frame them as the direct baselines being overturned. The real foil is STE itself.
- *Parallel threads*: PV-Tuning (proximal), ZeroQAT (zeroth-order), and FOGZO (hybrid) are all
  2024–2025 "departure from STE" attempts. JacQuant explicitly names them in its Related Work,
  positioning itself as the variant that "keeps the hard forward quantizer and replaces only the backward rule."
- *Depth count*: 5 layers found + the root + 1 successor layer. The oldest root is Bengio 2013;
  the successors extend forward to ParetoQ / WinQ / ZeroQAT, the most recent 2025–2026 work.
