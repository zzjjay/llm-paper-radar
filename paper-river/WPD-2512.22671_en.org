* The River of Questions

When you compress a large model down to a smaller one, what exactly do you lose?

For a long time, people working on model compression shared a tacit assumption: pruning is like chopping down a tree — the harder you chop, the more the whole tree wilts.
Capability is a pot of soup; cutting parameters means diluting it, so every flavor fades simultaneously.
The standard move in compression papers, accordingly, was: report a perplexity number, report a composite score, see whether the drop is acceptable, and call it done.

But is that soup really uniform?

What if the "facts" a model has memorized and the model's "ability to follow instructions" live in different rooms — so that the same cut collapses one room while actually opening the other to fresh air?
If that's true, then the entire narrative of "compression = uniform degradation" is wrong. Compression could instead be a *selective scalpel*: cutting this, preserving that — and what gets cut might be precisely what you don't want (wrong memories), while what survives is precisely what you care most about (instruction-following ability).

This is the river this paper traces: from "how much does cutting parameters hurt?" all the way to "what does cutting parameters actually change?"

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Han et al. (Deep Compression): The First Proof That Networks Contain Deletable Redundancy

*** What Problem Did It See?

In 2015 neural networks had just grown large enough that fitting them onto phones was a real engineering problem. The pain point was simple: hundreds of megabytes, impossible to download, impossible to run.
But nobody was sure parameters could actually be cut. The intuition was that a network is a tightly coupled whole — pull one thread and everything unravels.

*** Its Solution

Han did something almost disarmingly elementary: sort all weights by absolute value, cut the bottom fraction, then fine-tune for a few rounds to recover accuracy.
That's it. AlexNet lost 9× its parameters and held its accuracy.

*** Why It Worked (and What Hole It Left)

It worked because post-training networks really are sparse — most weights are near zero, so cutting them is like silencing neurons that were never speaking.
But the next ten years of compression research inherited Han's hidden assumption wholesale:
*we care about one aggregate score — perplexity or accuracy*.
A model is like an exam; losing 5 points means losing 5 points, and nobody asked how those 5 points were lost.
That hole would sit unfilled for a decade.

** 2020 | Shazeer (GLU Variants): Remaking the FFN as a Dual-Branch Gate

*** What Problem Did It See?

The standard Transformer FFN looks like: x → Linear → activation → Linear.
Shazeer found that this "expand then collapse" design hands too much work to a single ReLU/GELU, putting a ceiling on expressiveness.

*** Its Solution

Add a parallel branch called the gate. Two Linear projections both lift x to the intermediate dimension: one passes through SiLU and acts as the gate, the other passes through unchanged and acts as the value. Multiply element-wise, then project back down:
=h = (xW_up * SiLU(xW_gate)) W_down=

This is SwiGLU. Llama, PaLM, and Mistral all use it; it is now the de-facto default.

*** Why It Worked (and What Hole It Left)

The gate lets information flow selectively, giving strictly more expressiveness than a single activation function. But the cost is concrete:
*W_up and W_gate must share exactly the same intermediate dimension* because they are multiplied element-wise.
This embeds a very specific engineering constraint into pruning: you cannot trim one branch without trimming the other; you must prune matched pairs of neurons.
When this paper's author later set out to study GLU pruning, this paired-pruning constraint was the first obstacle.

** 2023 | Sharma et al. (LASER): The First Time Anyone Said "Pruning Some Parameters Makes the Model Smarter"

*** What Problem Did It See?

By 2023 the compression community had almost universally accepted "removing parameters = losing performance" as a law of nature.
Sharma's team was skeptical. Their experiment was genuinely counterintuitive: apply SVD to certain layers of a pretrained LLM and deliberately delete the high-order (small singular value) components.

*** Its Solution

LASER (LAyer-SElective Rank reduction) is not conventional pruning — it is layer-by-layer low-rank approximation that specifically discards the low-magnitude directions in each weight matrix.

The result surprised everyone: after the deletions, question-answering accuracy — especially on rare-fact questions — *went up*, sometimes by more than ten points.

*** Why It Worked (and What Hole It Left)

Sharma's interpretation: those high-order components encode "noisy memories" — weak, mutually interfering signals the model absorbed during pretraining. Deleting them is like erasing garbled versions; the correct version that remains is now heard clearly.

This was the first systematic argument that "compression is not uniform degradation — it is selective change."
But LASER is acupuncture — it surgically removes specific singular-value directions — not structural pruning that actually saves compute.
The hole it left: *does this selectivity survive coarser, compute-saving width pruning?*
Nobody answered.

** 2024 / 2025 | Wei et al., Hou et al.: Treating "Different Tasks Lose Differently" as a Research Object in Its Own Right

*** What Problem Did It See?

After LASER, more and more people noticed an awkward fact: at the same compression ratio, MMLU might drop 20 points while HellaSwag barely moves; GSM8K collapses while PIQA holds steady.
Traditional reporting hid this heterogeneity behind a single average score.

*** Its Solution

Wei (2024) and Hou (2025) independently did something similar: bucket benchmarks by capability type (knowledge / reasoning / instruction-following / commonsense) and plot each bucket's compression curve separately.

Hou's contribution is especially important: he directly documented the phenomenon that "instruction-following does not drop — or even rises — after pruning."
This is the immediate predecessor of this paper's dichotomy.

*** Why It Worked (and What Hole It Left)

Breaking the phenomenon apart is far more informative than reading a single average. But both papers share the same gap:
*the phenomenon is not tied to any architectural knob*. They tell you "knowledge drops, instruction-following holds," but not "which weight is responsible for knowledge, which dial controls instruction-following."
The obvious next step announces itself: find the concrete architectural knob, sweep it, and map phenomenon to knob.

** 2024 | Martra (140% Equilibrium): Locking the Crosshairs on the Expansion Ratio

*** What Problem Did It See?

Llama-3.2-1B's GLU intermediate dimension is 8192; its model dimension is 2048, giving an expansion ratio of 4×.
Why 4×? Nobody explained. Every paper simply inherited LLaMA's design.
Martra's earlier work asked the direct question: *what happens when you turn that dial?*

*** Its Solution

Apply PPM-guided width pruning (rank neurons by peak-to-peak magnitude of their weight rows, cut the lowest-scoring pairs) to Llama-3.2 across several expansion ratios.

Finding: at ~1.4× (140%) there is a sweet spot — the model remains usable while saving a substantial fraction of parameters.

*** Why It Worked (and What Hole It Left)

A clean observation, but it stopped there.
Why does the sweet spot exist? Which capability collapses beyond 1.4×? Which capability survives below 1.4×?
The paper itself left those questions unanswered.
That blank is exactly what the present paper sets out to fill.

** 2026 | Martra (This Paper — Width Pruning Dichotomy): Nailing Phenomenon, Knob, and Capability Together

*** What Problem Did It See?

Compress the preceding timeline into one sentence:
LASER showed selectivity exists; Hou showed different capabilities behave differently; Martra's earlier work found a sweet spot — but *nobody had mapped "a specific knob position" to "which capability is sacrificed and which is preserved."*

Worse, the field was still speaking the language of "pruning = uniform degradation."

*** Its Solution

Design a clean 2D experimental matrix:
- One axis: 7 expansion ratios (sweeping from 4× all the way down to 1.07×)
- Other axis: 13 benchmarks grouped into 6 capability categories (knowledge, math, language understanding, language modeling, truthfulness, instruction-following)

Pruning method: *PPM* (Peak-to-Peak Magnitude) — for each neuron, sum the max-minus-min range of the corresponding row in W_gate and W_up as its importance score; cut the lowest-scoring pairs together.

Then examine what each capability curve actually looks like.

*** Why It Worked (and What Holes It Left)

Three findings emerge with striking clarity:

1. *Capability Dichotomy*: Knowledge-class benchmarks (MMLU, GSM8K) and perplexity degrade monotonically, while IFEval (instruction-following) *improves* by +46% to +75% as the ratio is cut from 4× to 1.6×. MUSR (multi-step reasoning) is nearly flat throughout. The "compression = uniform degradation" narrative breaks down completely.

2. *Truthfulness Paradox*: MMLU and TruthfulQA-MC2 show a strong inverse correlation of r = −0.864 — the less the model can recall facts, the better it resists plausible-sounding false statements. In other words, the source of many hallucinations is over-confident wrong information, and PPM pruning clears those out first.

3. *Efficiency Trade-offs Depend on Use Case*: Single-request latency worsens noticeably (up to +18%), but batch=8 throughput holds, and energy consumption falls by −23%. The lesson: pruned models belong in high-concurrency backends, not in interactive on-device deployments.

Holes left behind:
- Why does PPM have this property? Does it selectively target low-quality memories, or were knowledge and instruction-following already layered separately during Llama's training?
- Does the dichotomy survive in MoE, Mamba, or other emerging architectures?
- If selective deletion is possible, can the logic be inverted — *designing a pruning strategy from a target capability profile*?

* Frontier Extensions

This paper is a May 2026 preprint and currently represents the most comprehensive synthesis on the "selective pruning" line of work.
The genuine next step is to convert it from a diagnostic tool into a design principle — for example, letting you declare when deploying a chatbot "I want instruction-following; I don't need factual recall," and having the system automatically select the optimal expansion ratio.

There is also a potential controversy worth flagging. Li et al. (2025) reported an apparently opposite result: their pruning caused MMLU and TruthfulQA to *fall together*, because they functionally suppressed "truthfulness neurons." The present paper explicitly distances itself from Li: "the mechanism by which structural pruning modulates truthfulness is completely different from functional intervention." This puts an open debate on the table — *two methods both called "pruning" can trigger entirely different circuits*. Whoever unifies these two lines first will own the next key paper in this space.

* Insights

Reading this timeline from start to finish, you notice that what actually changed over time is not the technology — it is *the question being asked*.

- Han asked: "Can we cut?"
- Sharma asked: "Does cutting necessarily mean getting worse?"
- Hou asked: "After cutting, what changes and what doesn't?"
- Martra asks: "Which knob we turn determines what gets cut?"

Each generation took the previous generation's foundational assumption, treated it as a variable, and questioned it.
So the real progress in compression research over these ten years is not "compressing harder" — it is
*decomposing the tangled notion of "model capability" into observable, intervene-able sub-capabilities, one layer at a time*.

And the implicit contribution of this paper — also its most interesting feature — is that it turns pruning from an engineering action into a *probe*: use pruning to reveal which capabilities inside the model share a common circuit and which do not.
This converges on the same goal as interpretability research, but via a destructive experiment rather than a read-the-mind-style inspection.

The two most likely next directions:

1. *Interpretability × Compression confluence* — since PPM pruning can selectively delete wrong memories, is the PPM score itself already a proxy for "memory quality"? If so, it hands the interpretability community a new instrument in return.
2. *On-demand compression* — given a deployment profile specifying which capabilities are needed and which are not, automatically generate the corresponding pruning strategy. Move from one-size-fits-all to capability-aware compression.

* Takeaways

For researchers:

- *Don't trust the unverified assumptions embedded in field consensus.*
  "Compression = uniform degradation" circulated for a decade without anyone systematically testing it. One counterexample from Sharma cracked it open; one matrix from this paper knocked down the whole wall. The next time you encounter a "everyone says so" proposition, ask yourself whether you could sweep it.

- *Reinterpret engineering actions as scientific probes.*
  Pruning was originally justified by cost savings, but the moment you treat it as "controlled destruction to observe system structure," it becomes a zero-marginal-cost causal experiment.
  Almost any "optimization task" you work on probably contains an undiscovered "probe" application waiting to be recognized.

- *Discrepancies are the insight.*
  When a number looks strange — like MMLU and TruthfulQA anti-correlating at r = −0.864 — don't average it away or dismiss it as noise. The discrepancy itself is the conclusion. Most breakthroughs in any field begin with "why are these two things that should agree actually disagreeing?"

The practical upshot: the next time you read a paper claiming "our method improves benchmark X by Y%," ask one more question — *by how much did it change on some other benchmark, and are the two directions consistent?*
If they're not, that inconsistency is where the real story lives.
