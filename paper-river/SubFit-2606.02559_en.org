* The Problem River

Can a model with tens of billions of parameters be cut down by a quarter in size — without retraining — and still remain roughly as capable?

It sounds like dieting: you want to lose weight without surgery and without six months in the gym. The hard part is that you cannot see with the naked eye which components are "fat" and which are "bone." Even harder: once you carve out a piece, what do you do with the hole that's left? Stitch it shut naively and the model may fall apart.

This line of research is, at its core, a succession of researchers answering two increasingly fine-grained questions:

1. *Where can we cut?* — From removing individual neurons, to removing entire layers, to removing half a layer inside a single layer.
2. *What do we do with the hole after cutting?* — From "leave it empty, just delete," to "train a patch to fill it," to "derive the patch analytically with a closed-form formula, at zero training cost."

You will see that each generation complains the previous one "cut too coarsely" or "patched too expensively," then sharpens the knife further and builds a smarter patch.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

The entire chain answers a single question: *once you carve out a piece, what do you do with the hole?* We begin in the era of hard deletion.

** 2023 | LLM-Pruner: The First Systematic "Cut-by-Importance" Framework

*** What problem did it see?

Before it, the standard tricks for slimming neural networks had two common flaws: either you needed the original training data to retrain (and LLM training data runs to terabytes you simply cannot obtain), or the pruning was task-specific and left the model able to do only that one thing, losing general capability.

*** Its solution

Its insight was that components in a model are not isolated — pruning one neuron drags along everything that depends on it, so pruning must happen in "coupled groups." It automatically identifies these coupled groups, scores each group with gradient information (first-order Taylor expansion), and removes the low-scoring ones. The model degrades after removal, so it follows up with a LoRA recovery pass — roughly 3 hours and 50 000 samples — to rehabilitate it.

*** Why it worked, and what new problems it created

Group-based pruning with gradient scoring is genuinely more precise than weight-magnitude pruning — 20% compression still preserves roughly 95% of capability. But it opened two new wounds: first, the pruned model has an irregular shape (some layers wide, others narrow), making storage and deployment awkward; second, *it cannot live without recovery* — skip the LoRA pass and you have a broken model, and recovery means data, gradient computation, and compute.

The next paper fixed its eyes on the "irregular shape" problem.

** 2024 | SliceGPT: Reframing Pruning as Dimensionality Reduction

*** What problem did it see?

It objected that LLM-Pruner-style methods produce irregularly shaped models, while the other camp — unstructured sparsity (punching scattered zeros into weight matrices) — yields no real speedup on ordinary hardware because there are no sparse operators to exploit.

*** Its solution

It discovered a "computational invariance" in Transformers: inserting an orthogonal rotation matrix between two layers leaves the model's output unchanged, as long as adjacent weights absorb it. It uses PCA on calibration data to find these rotation directions, rotates each weight matrix so that information energy concentrates in the leading principal components, then simply deletes the lowest-energy rows and columns. Crucially, what remains is still a *dense* small matrix — the embedding dimension is genuinely smaller, and ordinary hardware can accelerate it directly.

*** Why it worked, and what new problems it created

Recasting pruning as dimensionality reduction elegantly sidesteps the hardware obstacle of sparse operators. But cutting "width" (making every layer narrower) has an obvious quality ceiling: perplexity degrades noticeably once you cut beyond 30%. And giving different layers different widths introduces a tangle of dimension-alignment headaches.

This prompted the question: if cutting width has a ceiling, why not cut depth — remove entire layers?

** 2024 | LaCo: Merging Similar Layers by Collapse

*** What problem did it see?

It shared the objection that LLM-Pruner and SliceGPT "alter the internal structure" (matrix shapes and dimensions both change), complicating deployment. It wanted an approach that preserves each layer's shape intact and operates only along the depth axis.

*** Its solution

Its strategy is merging: take the later, mutually similar layers and fold their parameter differences into the preceding layer by a "keep the consensus, absorb the divergence" rule. After each merge it checks layer-to-layer cosine similarity on calibration data and rolls back if the deviation is too large. Layer count decreases; each layer's width and shape remain untouched.

*** Why it worked, and what new problems it created

Choosing to cut depth rather than width was the right direction — 25–30% compression still preserves more than 80% of performance, genuinely outperforming LLM-Pruner and SliceGPT. But "merging = parameter averaging" is a bit crude: why should averaging two layers' weights replicate two layers' function? And because compression ratio emerges from the merging process, you cannot specify "I want exactly 25% off" upfront.

The next paper resolved both problems at once: instead of merging, precisely measure which layers are least useful, then delete them.

** 2024 | ShortGPT: Score Every Layer, Delete the Weakest

*** What problem did it see?

It considered LaCo's merging roundabout and found LLM-Pruner's reliance on gradients impractical. It wanted a layer-importance metric that is simple, training-free, and yields a clean ranking.

*** Its solution

It proposes *Block Influence (BI)*: a layer's importance is how much it changes its input. Concretely, BI = 1 minus the cosine similarity between the layer's input and output hidden states. If a layer's input and output are nearly identical (similarity ≈ 1, BI ≈ 0), the layer has done almost nothing — it merely passed the signal along unchanged — and can be deleted. Sort by BI from lowest to highest and remove entire layers (attention + FFN block together), touching no weights, computing no gradients.

If you take LaCo's "merge + collapse" procedure and replace it with "rank by a cosine score and delete layers outright," you get ShortGPT. It reduces the problem to its bare minimum.

*** Why it worked, and what new problems it created

Simplicity taken to the extreme can still be effective — removing roughly 25% of parameters while preserving roughly 90% of performance. But it planted *the biggest landmine in this entire lineage*: delete without patching. The hole left by the removed layers sits empty, held together only by the residual connection. At high compression ratios, perplexity explodes (a 7B model goes from 8 to 40+) and generation tasks (summarization, QA) drop to near zero.

This "empty hole" pain point gave birth to the entire *replacement-based* compression branch.

** 2024–25 | LLM-Streamline: Replace the Deleted Layers with a Small Network

*** What problem did it see?

It directly addressed the wound left by ShortGPT: if pure deletion causes collapse, why leave the hole empty? It observed that instead of deleting a contiguous span of redundant layers and letting the residual stream bear the burden alone, you could train a lightweight network to approximate what that span originally did.

*** Its solution

Two steps. First, layer selection: cosine similarity between input and output again identifies the single *contiguous* span of least-important layers. Second, replacement: train a lightweight network (experiments showed one MLP is enough — adding Transformer layers provides no further benefit) to fit the "input → output" mapping of that span, and slot it back into the hole. It also introduces a "stability" metric, noting that raw accuracy overstates compression quality because a compressed model may get lucky on questions the original model was already uncertain about.

If you take ShortGPT's "delete and leave empty" and replace the empty hole with "a trained MLP," you get LLM-Streamline.

*** Why it worked, and what new problems it created

"Patching the hole" is the right direction — 25% compression preserves roughly 93% of classification performance. But the patch *costs money to train*: that MLP needs data and compute. It also inherits a hidden assumption: *redundancy is a single contiguous span of layers*, so it only ever removes one contiguous interval.

The next paper targeted the training cost: can we derive the patch analytically, without any training at all?

** 2025 | ReplaceMe: No Training — Derive the Substitute in Closed Form

*** What problem did it see?

It explicitly called out LLM-Streamline: your replacement network has to be trained (MSE loss + LLM loss + LoRA), and that "recovery" pipeline is slow and expensive. It also noted that ShortGPT, despite claiming to be training-free, still recommends fine-tuning afterward to restore quality. It demanded a substitute that is *completely training-free*.

*** Its solution

Its bet: a contiguous span of Transformer blocks can be approximated by *a single linear transform* (inspired by the observation that "your Transformer is approximately linear"). Layer selection still uses cosine distance to identify a contiguous span, but the substitute is a linear matrix T derived in *closed form* via least squares (`T* = (MᵀM)⁻¹Mᵀ(...)`), with no gradients required. Even better, this linear matrix can be *folded directly into the preceding layer's down-projection weight* — no new parameters added, model shape unchanged.

If you take LLM-Streamline's "trained MLP" and replace it with "a closed-form linear matrix that folds into existing weights," you get ReplaceMe.

*** Why it worked, and what new problems it created

Training-free + zero new parameters, yet competitive with fine-tuned SOTA methods — this step drove the cost of "patching the hole" to near zero. But it still carries two inherited burdens. First, *whole-layer granularity + contiguous span*: it assumes the thing to remove is a single contiguous span of layers where attention and FFN are bundled together. Second, the *linear* mapping runs out of expressive power at high compression ratios — it cannot capture the nonlinear behavior of blocks, and at extreme compression some fine-tuning is still needed.

At this point, "how to patch the hole" had been refined to a high degree. But one assumption had gone unchallenged from ShortGPT all the way through ReplaceMe: *the unit of removal is always a whole layer, and it must be a contiguous span*. This paper cuts right there.

** 2026 | SubFit: Sharpen the Knife from "Whole Layer" to "Half Layer," Drop the Contiguity Requirement

*** What problem did it see?

In a single sentence it pierces the shared assumption of all prior replacement-based methods: they all operate at whole-layer / contiguous-block granularity. SubFit argues both constraints are "overly rigid":

- Redundancy *need not be contiguous* — layer 3's attention may be expendable while layer 4's attention is not, and layer 7's FFN is expendable again. Forcing removal to a contiguous span lets the genuinely redundant pieces scattered throughout the network go untouched.
- Within a layer, the redundancy of attention and FFN *is not symmetric* — prior work has long established that attention heads are more redundant than FFN blocks. Bundling them together and removing both means either cutting too much (damaging the FFN) or too little (sparing wasteful attention heads).

*** Its solution

SubFit = *submodule granularity + fitted residual bypass*. Three moves:

1. *Submodule granularity*: instead of removing whole layers, treat each layer's attention and FFN as independent candidate units to be evaluated separately.
2. *Non-contiguous selection*: each submodule is selected on its own score, with no requirement that selected submodules form a contiguous span. Attention submodules are scored and selected in one pass; then, on the updated activations that result from those replacements, FFN submodules are scored and selected in a second pass (sequential, because earlier replacements alter the activation statistics seen by later submodules).
3. *Fitted residual bypass*: each removed submodule receives its own lightweight bypass patch, computed in *closed form* from calibration-data statistics (using the Frisch-Waugh-Lovell theorem to decouple the estimation), with no full-model fine-tuning. The design is deliberately asymmetric: attention submodules use independent low-rank bases per layer; FFN submodules *share* a single low-rank input subspace across layers (borrowing this idea from DeltaLLM) to keep deployment costs in check.

If you take ReplaceMe's "fit one linear transform to a contiguous span of whole layers" and break it into "fit a separate bypass to each attention and FFN submodule independently, with non-contiguous selection," you get SubFit.

*** Why it worked, and what new problems it created

Dropping granularity from whole layers to submodules, and dropping the contiguity constraint, gives the optimizer far more fine-grained freedom — at 25% compression, it preserves 84.6% of dense accuracy with only a 2.42× perplexity increase, versus the best baseline at 81.6% / 4.34×.

The costs (honestly enumerated): it adds roughly 10% extra parameters relative to the removed parameter count, and about 15% extra compute (ReplaceMe's weight-folding approach adds zero); the one-time offline fitting takes roughly 2000–2600 seconds, slower than Streamline; generation/reasoning tasks (GSM8K) remain unstable; and no fairness or safety audit of the compressed model was conducted.

* Frontier Extensions

Several parallel threads, concurrent with SubFit, attack the same "what to do with the hole" question from different angles:

- *LinearPatch (Chen et al. 2026)*: Takes the "minimal patch" route. It identifies a key cause of layer-pruning collapse as *activation-magnitude mismatch at the seam* — the activation scales on either side of the cut differ substantially, and the effect is amplified by outlier values on special tokens ([BOS], separators). It inserts a single matrix only at the seam (Hadamard transform to suppress outliers + channel scaling to align statistics), one GEMM, plug-and-play, training-free. But it only corrects *statistics* at the seam; it does not restore the *function* of deleted layers, and it still operates at whole-layer / contiguous granularity — exactly the constraints SubFit loosens further.

- *DeltaLLM (Mikaelyan et al. 2025)*: Shares one block's weights across layers, storing only a low-rank delta per layer. SubFit explicitly borrows its idea of "sharing a low-rank input subspace across removed submodules" for the FFN bypass. The difference: DeltaLLM requires distillation training and shares at whole-layer granularity without distinguishing the different redundancy profiles of attention and FFN.

- *GRASP (Liu et al. 2025)*: Uses SVD low-rank approximation to replace redundant layers, with gradient attribution to select which singular values to retain. It follows the same template as SubFit — "fit a cheap substitute module on calibration data to replace removed layers" — but remains at whole-layer contiguous granularity with a single importance criterion, and does not account for the different representational needs of attention (suited to low rank) versus FFN (requiring higher rank).

As of now, SubFit's "submodule granularity + non-contiguous selection + closed-form fitting" represents the finest-grained cut in this lineage.

* Insights

On the surface this looks like a technical progression of steadily higher compression ratios and sharper knives. But the deeper cognitive shifts happening underneath operate on two levels:

1. *From "deletion" to "replacement" is a re-understanding of the residual stream.* Early methods (ShortGPT) treated layer removal as "taking out a part," assuming the residual connection could bear the hole. The awakening of replacement-based methods was the realization: the hole is not empty — the removed submodule was writing a structured *increment* into the residual stream, and you must *approximately restore* that increment. From LLM-Streamline training a patch, to ReplaceMe deriving one in closed form, to SubFit crafting a custom patch per submodule — the patch grows ever more precise because the understanding of "what the removed piece was actually doing" grows ever more accurate.

2. *From "the layer is the atomic unit" to "the layer is a decomposable composite" is a liberation of granularity.* From LLM-Pruner through ReplaceMe, "the layer" was an unquestioned unit of operation — keep a whole layer or remove a whole layer, always contiguously. SubFit's real contribution is not those extra percentage points; it is that it *punctures the implicit assumption that "the layer is atomic"*: attention and FFN can be evaluated and treated independently; redundancy can be scattered rather than contiguous. Once this assumption breaks, the space of valid cuts grows combinatorially.

Where is this heading next? Two directions seem most likely. One is *further granularity descent* — from attention/FFN submodules to individual attention heads or FFN channel groups, enabling mixed compression with non-contiguous, heterogeneous granularity. The other is turning the "what to select + how to patch" procedure from *sequential greedy* into *joint optimization* (SubFit currently selects attention first and FFN second in a pipeline, leaving joint search as open territory). Looking further ahead, "compression" and "low-rank adaptation (LoRA)" will increasingly converge — both are ultimately asking "how many effective degrees of freedom does this weight actually have?"

* Takeaway

What this entire lineage teaches about *how to do research* distills to one point: *find the unit of operation that everyone assumes and nobody has questioned.*

From ShortGPT through ReplaceMe, everyone competed on "how to patch the hole" — trained vs. closed-form, parameter-adding vs. weight-folding. SubFit did not compete in that crowded space. Instead it stepped back and asked: "Why does the hole you're patching have to be as large as one whole layer?" Dropping the unit of operation from a full layer to a half layer immediately reopened a problem that seemed nearly solved.

The practical implication: *the next time you enter a field that looks mature — where everyone is tuning the same knob — don't rush to turn that knob. First list every implicit assumption shared by all existing methods: What is the unit? Must it be contiguous? Must it be symmetric? Then ask, one by one, "what happens if I relax this constraint?"* The genuinely new solution is rarely "turn the existing knob better." It is almost always "discover a knob that nobody has touched yet."
