* The River of Problems

A counterintuitive phenomenon: take a reasoning-capable large model and "compress it down" (quantize it), and it not only answers worse — it also becomes more verbose. For the same problem, it insists on producing a longer chain of thought before stopping. Stranger still, in many cases the model has already arrived at the correct answer midway through, yet it second-guesses itself, backtracks, and recomputes, ultimately submitting the wrong one.

The central question of this river is: *what does quantization actually do to a model that can think?* On the surface the explanation is "compression sacrifices precision, so the model gets dumber" — but that is far too convenient. The real difficulty lies here: a reasoning model's capability lives inside a chain of thought hundreds or thousands of tokens long, and any small perturbation at any point in that chain gets amplified and cascaded by every subsequent step. The errors introduced by quantization do not uniformly "weaken" the model; rather, they change its *behavior* at certain critical junctures — nudging it to keep going with "wait, actually, or let me try another approach" precisely when it should be wrapping up.

Why do successive generations of researchers keep charging at this problem? Because this river is actually formed by two tributaries: one is *quantization* — a decade-long battle against "outliers," trying to fit ever-larger models into ever-fewer bits; the other is *overthinking* — reasoning models grow stronger by "thinking longer," yet can also waste compute and even answer incorrectly by "thinking too long." This paper stands at the confluence of both tributaries and, for the first time, pins down the mystery of "why quantization hurts reasoning" at the level of individually observable, individually intervening tokens.

* Evolutionary Narrative

We walk Tributary A first (how quantization fought outliers step by step), then Tributary B (how overthinking went from a strength to a pathology), and finally see how this paper joins the two.

** 2022 | LLM.int8(): Give the "troublemaker" dimensions their own lane

*** What problem it saw

Compressing model weights from 16 bits down to 8 bits was nearly lossless on small models. But once the model crossed ~6.7B parameters, something strange happened: a tiny fraction of activation dimensions would suddenly spike into "outlier features," appearing systematically in every layer. Since the quantization scale is set by the maximum value, a single outlier stretches the scale so wide that the remaining 99.9% of normal values get crammed into just a few quantization levels — precision destroyed.

*** Its solution

If only a handful of dimensions are troublemakers, isolate them. Normal dimensions go through int8 (saving memory); those outlier dimensions get their own FP16 channel; the two results are summed at the end. A 175B model loses zero precision while cutting memory in half.

*** Why it worked (and what new problems it buried)

It was the first work to name and locate the "outlier" ghost — every subsequent step along the quantization line grapples with this same ghost. But the approach only *isolates* troublemakers; mixed precision saves memory yet gains little or no speed improvement (and may even slow things down). It also addresses activations only; the question of how to avoid collapse at the extreme compression of 3–4 bit weights remained unsolved.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Quantize one weight, compensate the rest

*** What problem it saw

The goal was to push weight compression to 3–4 bits (not just 8). But naive "round to nearest" collapsed at this bit-width. Existing exact solvers (OBQ) were accurate but absurdly slow — quantizing a 175B model would take roughly half a year.

*** Its solution

The core intuition: quantization introduces error, but you can make the *not-yet-quantized weights* shift slightly to compensate. Specifically, use second-order (Hessian) information to determine how much each remaining weight should move and in which direction. Three engineering tricks on top (quantize all rows in the same column order; lazy block updates; Cholesky stabilization) compress a half-year job down to 4 GPU-hours.

*** Why it worked (and what new problems it buried)

The "quantize–then–compensate" paradigm made 3–4 bits usable for the first time. But it requires repeated reconstruction and Hessian inversion against a calibration set — computationally heavy, and with a lurking risk: the compensation is tuned to the calibration data and can *overfit*, performing inconsistently on other domains or tasks. That left an opening for the next paper: can we preserve accuracy without reconstruction?

** 2023 | AWQ: Not all weights deserve protection — activations will tell you which ones do

*** What problem it saw

AWQ targeted two weaknesses of GPTQ: the reconstruction machinery is heavy, and calibration-set compensation risks overfitting. It reframed the question — do we even need to touch all the weights?

*** Its solution

The key insight: weights are not equal. Only about 1% of weight channels are "salient," and which ones are salient is determined not by the weights' own magnitude but by the *magnitude of the activations flowing through them*. Scale those 1% salient channels before quantization (making their relative error smaller), and quantize everything else normally. No backpropagation, no reconstruction — faster than GPTQ, better generalization, and for the first time applicable to multimodal models.

*** Why it worked (and what new problems it buried)

It shifted the perspective from "quantize all weights uniformly" to "some weights are more critical, and activations can point you to which ones" — this "activation-aware" viewpoint reappears later in this paper in a different form (looking at the output distribution at the token level). But AWQ still targets weights; outliers on the activation side remain stubborn, and the extreme regime of W4A4 — compressing activations to 4 bits as well — had not been conquered.

** 2024 | The rotation-transform line: Instead of isolating outliers, change coordinates so they vanish

*** What problem it saw

Whether isolating (LLM.int8()) or scaling-to-protect (AWQ), all previous approaches accepted outliers as given and treated symptoms. SmoothQuant took a step forward: activations are hard to quantize, weights are easy — so migrate the difficulty from activations to weights (via a per-channel scaling that is mathematically equivalent), making W8A8 both fast and accurate. But the outliers themselves were still there.

*** Its solution

Rotation. Multiply the hidden states by an orthogonal (Hadamard) rotation matrix, and the "few dimensions extremely large" spiky distribution is smoothed into a near-Gaussian distribution with no outliers — and because the transformation is orthogonal and inverted at the next layer, the model's output is mathematically unchanged ("computational invariance"). QuaRot applied fixed Hadamard rotations and was the first to achieve W4A4 with 4-bit KV cache; SpinQuant found that which random rotation you drew could vary by 13 accuracy points, so it *learned* the rotation matrix; FlatQuant went further still, arguing rotation alone is insufficient — the goal should be "flatness" — and learned a tailored affine transform for each layer.

*** Why it worked (and what new problems it buried)

By this point, quantization had essentially won on "average accuracy": W4A16 and W8A8 were approaching lossless. But precisely because the average scores looked so good, a new problem was hidden underneath them: average losslessness does not mean every capability is lossless. Especially the kind that depends on long chains where errors cascade — multi-step reasoning. The battlefield shifted from "how much average precision did I lose" to "what behavior of the model did I change."

** 2025 | "Does quantization hurt reasoning?": Average scores lie — test reasoning separately

*** What problem it saw

The entire quantization line up to this point had been chasing average benchmark scores. But reasoning models (DeepSeek-R1, QwQ) are a new species: their capability lives inside a long chain of thought. Works like liu2025 and li2025 went and tested multi-step reasoning on its own.

*** Its solution

Systematic benchmarking. The conclusions were consistent: W8A8 and W4A16 are near-lossless, but going further the drop accelerates sharply; *reasoning tasks drop much harder than non-reasoning tasks*; smaller models suffer more; harder problems (AIME far more than GSM8K) suffer more; RL-trained models are more brittle than distilled ones. li2025 went furthest, using step-aligned error attribution to find that quantization disproportionately produces "method/execution errors" (wrong computation, carry errors, rounding mistakes), and that *errors tend to appear very early — one bad first step sends the entire chain cascading into collapse*.

*** Why it worked (and what new problems it buried)

These works confirmed that quantization does specifically hurt reasoning and provided behavioral-level clues that "errors cascade from early missteps." But almost all of them stayed at the benchmark level — telling you "where it drops and by how much" without answering "why." More critically, nobody connected the reasoning collapse to the outlier/representational-geometry story from Tributary A. That mechanistic gap is exactly what this paper aims to fill. But to fill it, we first need to visit Tributary B and understand what overthinking really is — because this paper finds that quantization hurts reasoning precisely by inducing the model to overthink.

** 2024 | Chen et al.: Name "overthinking"

*** What problem it saw

o1-style reasoning models grow stronger by "thinking longer" — this is the test-time scaling faith: spend more tokens thinking at inference time and the answer becomes more accurate. But Chen went and counted carefully: for easy problems, the model generates round after round of nearly identical solutions, and *in >92% of cases the very first round was already correct* — all subsequent rounds are pure waste.

*** Its solution

Name this phenomenon *overthinking* and construct two efficiency metrics to quantify it (the fraction of tokens up to the first correct answer; the diversity of distinct solution strategies). Then use self-training to make the model prefer responses that "take fewer detours and finish sooner," cutting tokens by ~48% on QwQ-32B with no accuracy loss.

*** Why it worked (and what new problems it buried)

It was the first to tear open the belief that "longer chain of thought = better": a substantial portion of "thinking" is actually filler. But it mainly targets redundancy on easy problems, and the remedy requires retraining. Is overthinking a general, more fine-grained phenomenon?

** 2025 | Su et al.: Reasoning length and accuracy are actually non-monotonic

*** What problem it saw

Chen spoke of "easy questions running too long." Su generalized it: for any fixed problem, *the relationship between reasoning length and accuracy is not a monotonic "longer is better" curve* — longer chains frequently introduce cascading errors and perform worse.

*** Its solution

Decompose the problem into two levels: the sample level (for the same question, how length relates to correctness) and the problem level (whether the model adjusts its length according to difficulty). The conclusion is that models are poor at "difficulty calibration": *easy questions overthink, hard questions underthink*. An experiment preferring shorter responses found that most of the saved length came from wrong answers — incorrect responses tend to be much longer.

*** Why it worked (and what new problems it buried)

It elevated "overthinking" from "an easy-question phenomenon" to a general calibration problem, and made a key observation: *wrong answers tend to be particularly long*. This paper will use that observation directly — a quantized model produces responses that are both longer and more often wrong. But Su's concern is the model's own difficulty calibration; nobody had yet asked: if a model has been "tampered with" (e.g., quantized), will its overthinking be systematically amplified?

** 2025 | Zhao et al.: Teach the model to brake itself

*** What problem it saw

Prior approaches to treating overthinking relied on *external control* — hard truncation, length penalties, external controllers. Zhao argued this treats symptoms: if the model itself doesn't know when to stop, what good is pulling it from outside?

*** Its solution

Self-Braking Tuning: teach the model *itself* to learn "when it's time to stop." They define metrics to find the inflection point where reasoning "transitions from productive to redundant," construct data to teach the model to naturally conclude at that point. This work was the first to precisely name those *overthinking marker tokens* — "Wait," "Alternatively," "Let me check" — self-doubt and restart signals that are the harbingers of redundancy.

*** Why it worked (and what new problems it buried)

It contributed a critical asset: a *list of overthinking marker tokens*. This paper's penalty target set, as the authors explicitly state, "is consistent with prior work (i.e., this line)." But Zhao still requires tuning (training). If one day you discovered that some external factor (quantization) specifically causes the model to emit more of these marker tokens — then perhaps you don't need to train at all; just suppress the probability of those few tokens during generation. That step was left for this paper.

** 2026 | OverthinkPenalty: Quantization makes the model "overthink" — suppress it, training-free

*** What problem it saw

This paper joins the two tributaries. It sees the gap in Tributary A (quantization hurts reasoning, nobody answers "why") and the assets of Tributary B (overthinking has a marker-token list; wrong answers are longer). Then it asks a question nobody else asked: quantization makes the chain of thought *longer* — is this the model "trying harder to compensate" (a feature) or a symptom (a bug)? Prior work (e.g., liu2025) even reported "quantized chain-of-thought length is about the same as FP," but this paper is not satisfied with such aggregate observations and decides to look at the token level.

*** Its solution

Three steps, each building on the last:

Step one: *Localize*. For each generation position, compute the KL divergence between the quantized model's and the full-precision (BF16) model's output distributions, to see exactly which token positions quantization "tampers with." Result: high-KL positions are heavily concentrated at *high-entropy* positions — where the model was already uncertain, with probability spread across many choices (correlation ρ=0.92). In other words, quantization's perturbation specifically targets the forks where the model was already hesitating.

Step two: *Attribute*. At these high-KL, high-entropy forks, the quantized model systematically *over-samples* the overthinking marker tokens — "wait," "but," "alternatively." Where it should have wrapped up and output the answer, quantization error nudged it instead to choose "hold on, let me think again." The consequence: overthinking errors climb from 26% in BF16 to 52% in AWQ 3-bit (a 7.3× increase); in as many as *52% of failure cases the model had already computed the correct answer mid-chain, but did not output it as the final answer*. This connects directly to Su's observation — wrong answers are particularly long — but now provides a causal account specific to the quantization setting.

Step three: *Intervene*. Since the root cause is "over-sampling a few marker tokens at the forks," and this paper has an existing list of those tokens from prior work, no training is needed. Simply apply a logit penalty to that small set of overthinking markers during generation (single hyperparameter λ, zero computational overhead).

*** Why it worked (and what new problems it buried)

Intuitively, quantization did not make the model globally dumber — it merely gave it a gentle push toward "let me think again" at a handful of high-entropy forks. Since the perturbation is so local and so concentrated on an enumerable set of tokens, a equally local counter-push (suppressing those tokens' probabilities) can correct it. Results: chain-of-thought length reduced by 12–23%, accuracy maintained or improved, overthinking errors reduced by up to 58%, validated across 5 models (1.5B–32B), 3 quantization methods, and 5 benchmarks.

Ablations verified the specificity of the mechanism: penalizing random tokens has no effect; penalizing low-KL tokens (positions the model was already confident about) is *catastrophic* — which in turn proves the lesion is indeed located at those high-KL, high-entropy positions.

The remaining open problems are clear: this is an *inference-time remedy*, treating the symptom (over-sampling marker tokens) without explaining at the representational level *why* quantization's perturbation falls specifically on high-entropy positions — meaning the paper connects the quantization story (Tributary A's outliers/representational geometry) and the overthinking story (Tributary B) at the *behavioral* level, but the bridge at the *representational geometry* level remains unbuilt.

* Frontier Extensions

As of now (June 2026), this paper is the latest on the "quantization × reasoning behavior" intersection line, and the first to pin "quantization hurts reasoning" to a token-level observable mechanism with a zero-cost intervention. It explicitly advances the question from "how much precision did quantization lose" (the old framing of Tributary A) to "what *behavior* of the model did quantization change."

Two directions worth watching (not directly covered by the paper; speculative):

The first is *reconnecting to representational geometry*. This paper proves behaviorally that "quantization perturbation targets high-entropy positions," but does not explain how this relates to the outlier/rotation/effective-rank story of Tributary A. If high-entropy positions correspond to some kind of geometric fragility in representation space, then once that bridge is built, quantization methods (rotation, scaling) could potentially prevent overthinking at the source rather than suppressing logits after the fact.

The second is *cross-scenario generalizability*. "Overthinking / reasoning collapse" has been independently observed across multiple compression settings — quantization (this paper, li2025), efficient distillation (eRank collapse in the RED line), and others. An open question: does there exist a unified "reasoning health" probe (KL–entropy correlation, effective rank, …) that can predict, across compression methods, "this compression approach will hurt reasoning"?

* Insight

On the surface this line of work is a chance meeting between "quantization technology" and "reasoning efficiency" — two separate fields. Underneath, it is a *descent in the granularity of attribution*.

For ten years, Tributary A fought outliers, but every win or loss was settled with one coarse metric: average accuracy. When the average score approached lossless, researchers concluded the battle was won. Then reasoning models exposed the blind spot of that metric: a model can be average-lossless yet collapse on multi-step reasoning, because long chains amplify and cascade local errors. The question therefore shifted from "how much did it lose" to "what behavior did it change."

The real contribution of this paper is not another quantization trick; it is *tracing the vague phenomenon of "quantization hurts reasoning" down to a token-level, observable, intervenable mechanism*: quantization's perturbation does not uniformly weaken the model — it falls precisely on the high-entropy forks where the model was already hesitating, nudging it toward "let me think again." This is a cognitive descent from "it got dumber" (functional description) to "at which specific positions, pushed toward which specific choice" (mechanistic localization). Once localization reaches this granularity, the solution collapses from "retrain the model" down to "suppress a few logits during generation" — zero cost.

At a deeper level, it reveals a counterintuitive fact: *the damage from compression may be highly structured and localized*. We habitually think of quantization as "adding noise to a signal," but this paper says no — it is more like introducing a systematic bias (toward self-doubt) at specific decision points. A structured disease is the kind that admits a structured, cheap cure.

The most likely next step: reconnect the behavioral findings (KL–entropy correlation, over-sampled marker tokens) back to representational geometry (outliers, rotation, effective rank), and find a geometric explanation for "why quantization perturbation falls on high-entropy positions" — enabling quantization methods to avoid this bias at the source rather than patching it in the logits afterwards.

* Takeaways

For how to do research and how to find problems, this river offers three concrete lessons:

1. *The junction between two mature fields often has unclaimed gold lying in plain sight.* Quantization and reasoning efficiency are each fiercely competitive on their own, but "how quantization changes reasoning behavior" — as a question at their junction — remained vacant because nobody was standing at both riverbanks simultaneously. The key move in this paper was taking the ready-made assets from Tributary B (the overthinking marker-token list; the observation that wrong answers are longer) and using them to solve Tributary A's mystery. The implication: watch for *gaps* between two fields you know reasonably well — the problems there tend to be both novel and solvable without building tools from scratch.

2. *Don't stop at "it got worse" — push attribution down one level of granularity.* Prior work said "quantization hurts reasoning"; this paper asked "at which tokens, at what kinds of positions, pushed toward which choice?" Each time you descend one level of granularity, the cost of the solution may drop by an order of magnitude — from "retrain" to "suppress a few logits." The implication: when you encounter mysterious performance degradation, don't be satisfied with reproducing the phenomenon; find a metric that is observable at a specific position and a specific decision (here: token-level KL divergence).

3. *Use reverse experiments to prove you found the right lesion.* The most convincing result in this paper is not "penalizing marker tokens works" — it is "penalizing random tokens does nothing, and penalizing low-KL tokens is catastrophic." That contrast set locks down the location of the lesion. The implication: when you claim "the problem is at X," construct a few "deliberately off-target" controls (targeting non-X) — if all of them fail, your attribution holds.
