* The River of the Problem

Why does cutting numerical precision from 16 bits to 4 bits so often cause a large language model to collapse during training?

It shouldn't be that hard. Multiplication and addition are multiplication and addition; lower precision just means coarser results. But in practice, the loss curve of FP4 training steadily diverges from the BF16 baseline, and downstream task scores drop sharply. The trouble isn't simply "imprecise arithmetic" — it's that LLMs develop activations with a peculiar geometric shape: a handful of directions carry enormous energy while the remaining dimensions spread into a long, flat tail. This anisotropy collides with block-wise max-based scaling, the standard approach for low-bit quantization, like trying to fit a bowling ball and a handful of sand in the same measuring cup: to keep the bowling ball from overflowing you set the scale so coarse that all the grains of sand collapse into the same bin.

The reason this problem has resisted generation after generation of researchers is that everyone treated it as an "uneven singular-value spectrum" — a high-dimensional spectral problem, naturally addressed by SVD, Hadamard rotation, random projection, and similar tools. Those tools work, but they are far too expensive to run at every training step. Researchers were forced to either sacrifice performance (run spectral correction infrequently) or sacrifice speed (run it every step). The field stalled for years, and no one questioned the basic premise: what is the shape of that "dominant direction" in the first place?

* The Evolutionary Narrative

The thread below follows "what the 'large' values in activations really are, and how to cure them." Each paper identifies what the previous generation left unresolved and offers a new angle.

** 2018 | All-but-the-Top (Mu & Viswanath): Word Vectors Carry a "Common Background" — Just Subtract It

*** What problem did it see?

After training, Word2Vec and GloVe static word vectors have a strange geometry: instead of distributing uniformly over a hypersphere, all vectors are squeezed into a narrow cone. This means the cosine similarity between any two randomly chosen words, which should be close to zero, is systematically positive. In downstream semantic-similarity tasks, the few dominant shared directions drown out the true semantic differences between words.

*** Its solution

Leave training untouched. After training, run one round of principal component analysis and subtract the top D principal components (typically D = 2 or 3) from every word vector. The remaining vectors are geometrically isotropic and immediately more useful for downstream similarity tasks.

This is the *all-but-the-top* trick.

*** Why it works — and what trap it leaves

The intuition is simple: those shared directions carry no word-to-word discriminative information; they are a "global background" pushed out by high-frequency function words ("the," "of," "is"). Remove the background and the foreground becomes visible.

The trap: the method targets *static* word vectors. Transformer contextual representations are dynamic — every token at every layer looks different, and a single post-hoc PCA cannot handle that. The next paper confronts this directly.

** 2019 | Ethayarajh: Contextual Vectors Are Worse, Not Better

*** What problem did it see?

The contextual representations of BERT, ELMo, and GPT-2 are geometrically worse than static word vectors. The cosine similarity between two completely random tokens can exceed 0.3. In other words, the entire Transformer's representation space is crammed into an extremely narrow cone — a phenomenon called *anisotropy*. It grows more severe in deeper layers.

*** Its solution

This paper prescribes no remedy; it issues a diagnosis. Its contribution is establishing "anisotropy" as a universal, inescapable property of Transformers, and proposing a suite of metrics (self-similarity, inter-token similarity) that give future researchers a measuring stick.

*** Why it matters — and what trap it leaves

Its value is epistemological: it prevents the field from thinking anisotropy is a relic of the Word2Vec era, and establishes that Transformers have brought it to a new scale.

The trap: the disease is located but there is no cheap cure. Subtracting principal components manually? With so many layers, doing it per-layer is either expensive or disruptive. This trap lies dormant until the quantization era forces it open — because low-bit precision amplifies the old ailment into a fatal wound.

** 2023 | SmoothQuant (Xiao et al.): Migrate the "Large" Values Channel-Wise at Inference

*** What problem did it see?

When LLMs are quantized to INT8 for inference, researchers noticed a counterintuitive pattern: quantizing both activations and weights to 8 bits tanks performance, but quantizing weights alone is nearly lossless. Diagnosis pointed to a handful of activation channels with extreme magnitudes. A single per-tensor scale factor had to accommodate those outliers, making it too coarse to preserve the fine detail of the remaining channels — the numerical manifestation of what Ethayarajh described geometrically: a few directions carry enormous energy and force the entire dynamic range to accommodate them.

*** Its solution

Weights are "tame" and tolerate quantization error well; activations are wild. So make a trade: shrink an outlier activation channel by a factor s, and scale the corresponding weight column up by s. The matrix product is mathematically identical, but both the activation and the weight matrix become more uniform, allowing their respective scale factors to fit more tightly.

This is the *outlier migration* idea: don't eliminate the "large" values, just move them from a hard-to-quantize location to an easy-to-quantize one.

*** Why it works — and what trap it leaves

INT8 has plenty of bit budget; a little migration is enough. Downstream task recovery is strong.

The trap: when the target precision drops from 8 bits to 4 bits, the available budget is cut in half. The "migration" of SmoothQuant no longer reaches far enough — shifting the outlier to the weights just makes the weights explode instead. Migration treats the symptom; the root cause is untouched. Moreover, SmoothQuant is purely an inference-time method; its scale factors are computed offline and cannot be used during training. The next paper needs to push to 4-bit inference.

** 2024 | QuaRot (Ashkboos et al.): Wash Away the "Large" Values with a Hadamard Rotation at Inference

*** What problem did it see?

At W4A4 inference, the migration approach of SmoothQuant has run out of headroom. Outliers concentrate in a few channels, which is geometrically an asymmetry — can the entire activation be rotated into a basis where all directions are "uniform"?

*** Its solution

Insert a pair of orthogonal matrices Q and Q⁻¹ into the matrix product X·W, giving X·Q·Q⁻¹·W — mathematically identical. But if Q is chosen well (a Hadamard matrix or a random rotation), the transformed X·Q spreads energy uniformly across all dimensions in the new basis: outliers no longer concentrate in a few channels, and the peak value of the whole activation tensor drops significantly. Quantizing X·Q now requires a much smaller scale factor, and 4 bits suffices.

Compared with SmoothQuant, the difference is qualitative: SmoothQuant *relocates* the large values; QuaRot *disperses* them across all directions so they are no longer large.

*** Why it works — and what trap it leaves

The method is mathematically elegant, the Hadamard transform has a fast implementation, and the overhead is manageable. W4A4 inference becomes viable.

Two traps remain. First, QuaRot is still an inference-time solution — activation statistics evolve during training, and a static rotation matrix cannot keep up. Second, the Hadamard rotation washes all directions indiscriminately, implicitly assuming the "large" values are randomly scattered; in reality they have structure, but QuaRot does not investigate that structure. The next paper moves the battlefield to training.

** 2025 | METIS (Cao et al.): Online SVD Spectral Control During Training

*** What problem did it see?

If FP4 is to be used during training rather than only at inference, the rotation matrix must *evolve dynamically with training*. A static Hadamard is insufficient — the dominant singular direction of activations shifts every few hundred steps. METIS accepts this honestly: to stabilize FP4 training, an "online spectral correction" must be applied before each quantization operation, aligning the activation with its current dominant singular direction.

*** Its solution

Dynamically generalize QuaRot: inside the training loop, perform an (approximate) SVD on the tensor to be quantized, identify the current dominant singular direction, apply spectral adjustment along that direction, then quantize. FP4 (W4A4G4) training is stabilized for the first time, with the loss curve tracking close to BF16.

*** Why it works — and what trap it leaves

METIS is the foundational work in FP4 training — it proves that "as long as the spectrum is controlled, FP4 training stays stable."

The trap: SVD on large matrices is expensive, and running it every training step pushes training cost back up substantially. The SVD operator also clashes with modern GPU GeMM pipelines — incompatible memory-access patterns, impossible to fuse into the main compute kernel. METIS acknowledges this cost explicitly. The question then becomes: is there a much cheaper approximation that achieves nearly the same effect? In other words — what exactly is SVD suppressing? Is the full SVD really necessary?

That is precisely the question MeanBias pursues.

** 2026 | MeanBias (Cao et al., this paper): That "Dominant Singular Direction" Is Just the Mean

*** What problem did it see?

Placing the "dominant singular direction v₁" from METIS alongside the "column mean vector μ" of the same activation, the result is startling: their cosine similarity approaches 0.99. In other words, the "dominant direction" that SVD/Hadamard/rotation methods have spent years struggling to suppress is, in essence, something entirely mundane: the average bias shared by all tokens across every feature dimension.

Why is this mean so large? High dimensionality. In an H-dimensional space, even a tiny per-coordinate shared offset μ̄ produces a mean vector whose norm grows as √H · μ̄. At H = 4096, √H ≈ 64 — a negligible per-coordinate bias is amplified into an element-level colossus sixty-four times larger. Self-attention further aggregates shared signals across tokens, and residual connections accumulate this offset across layers. The shared bias is not noise; it is a structural by-product of the Transformer architecture, continually regenerated and amplified.

*** Its solution

Since SVD is suppressing a rank-1 mean direction, there is no need to take the long way around: before each GeMM, compute the column mean of the activation, subtract it from the activation, perform the matrix multiply, then add the mean contribution back (mathematically: X·W = (X − 1·μᵀ)·W + 1·μᵀ·W, where the second term is computed cheaply via μ). The backward pass applies the same "mean–residual" decomposition to the gradient.

The entire operation requires only a reduce (column average) and an elementwise subtraction — the two cheapest operator classes on modern GPUs, fully fusible with or parallelizable alongside the main GeMM. The cost of SVD is replaced by overhead that is essentially negligible.

*** Why it works — and what trap it leaves

The intuitive chain: the enemy of low-bit quantization is extreme activation values; the source of those extremes is the mean direction; the mean direction is rank-1; a rank-1 object can be described by a single vector and removed by a single subtraction. Every link in the chain lines up.

Experiments on a 1B model with FP4 (W4A4G4) training show the loss gap relative to BF16 closes substantially, and downstream task performance recovers. The paper is also honest: removing the mean eliminates the "primary driver," but higher-order anisotropy (rank 2 and above) remains — it has been reduced from fatal to manageable.

New open questions: who handles the higher-order anisotropy that remains after the mean is subtracted? Would a lightweight rank-2/rank-3 correction on top of MeanBias close the gap further? And can the "mean is the culprit" insight make inference-side QuaRot lighter too? These are the openings for the next wave of research.

* Frontier Extensions

As of this writing (May 2026), this paper appeared in March 2026 and no direct follow-up has been published yet. But tracing its two open ends allows some prediction of what comes next:

- *Residual spectral correction*: MeanBias handles rank-1. After the mean–residual decomposition, a cheap low-rank correction targeting rank-2/rank-3 (e.g., lightweight orthogonalization) might close the remaining loss gap further. This is the direction the paper itself quietly gestures toward.
- *Inference-side feedback*: If MeanBias suppresses the mean bias at the source during training, does inference still require the heavy-weight rotation of QuaRot? A jointly optimized training-plus-inference low-bit scheme may be worth redesigning from scratch.

Neither direction has a published paper yet; both are natural extensions of the research line.

* Insight

The deepest feeling after reading this line of work is not "the algorithms keep getting cleverer" — it is that *the diagnosis keeps converging*.

In 2018, Mu & Viswanath said "the top principal components are problematic" — a vague indictment. In 2019, Ethayarajh generalized it to "anisotropy" — even vaguer. In 2023–2024, SmoothQuant and QuaRot made it concrete: "extreme values in a few channels/directions" — now actionable. In 2025, METIS pinpointed it as "the dominant singular direction" — already quite specific. In 2026, MeanBias converges it to a rank-1 object that fits on one line of math: μ = (1/l) · 1ᵀX.

Each generation of researchers was doing the same thing — seeing the thing that makes low-bit training collapse a little more clearly. And once they saw it clearly, *the complexity of the fix actually went down*. From PCA to SVD, the tools grew more expensive; from SVD to "subtract the mean," they got dramatically cheaper. This is deeply counterintuitive: we usually assume scientific progress means increasingly sophisticated tools. But on this research line, progress looks like *turning an SVD — a heavy weapon — into a single reduce operation* — because the field finally saw clearly that what SVD was suppressing was a rank-1 object all along.

One level deeper: what has actually been happening across this entire line is a progressive descent through *phenomenon → geometry → algebraic structure → physical origin*. Mu & Viswanath gave us the phenomenon (vectors squeezed into a cone). Ethayarajh gave us the geometry (a narrow cone). SmoothQuant/QuaRot/METIS gave us algebraic treatments (rotation matrices, SVD). MeanBias is the first to ask "why does this mean direction exist?" — and the answer involves the summation nature of self-attention, the accumulation of residual connections, and the √H amplification effect in high-dimensional space. Once you know the *physical origin*, you know how to intervene *at the source* rather than performing expensive cleanup downstream.

Where does the field go next? Two directions:

1. *The residual level*: now that rank-1 is handled, what is the physical meaning of rank-2? Does it arise from a specific attention pattern? Whoever can give an equally clear source-level explanation will be able to apply an equally lightweight fix.
2. *Cross-stage unification*: the mean bias appears in forward activations, backward gradients, and optimizer second moments. Can a unified "mean-elimination framework" be written that integrates training, quantization, and optimizer into a single abstraction?

* Takeaways

Three concrete research-practice takeaways from this line:

1. *Ask what your heavy tool is actually solving.* The biggest lesson of this line is that the METIS team (very likely the same authors as this paper) spent a year using SVD, then turned around and asked "what is SVD actually suppressing?" — and found it was the mean. If you are using an "expensive but effective" tool, *do not treat it as the final answer*. Analyze the outputs it produces in reverse, and locate the minimal thing it is truly doing. That minimal thing can usually be achieved in a far simpler way.

2. *High-dimensional amplification is a frequently overlooked lever.* The formula √H · μ̄ is trivially simple, yet it explains why "a tiny per-coordinate mean bias" becomes "a visually enormous element-level outlier." This lever applies everywhere in the LLM era — any phenomenon that is small per coordinate but large in aggregate is worth re-examining through the lens of √dim.

3. *Source-level intervention beats downstream cleanup.* Throughout this line, fixes were applied before or after quantization operators as downstream patches — until MeanBias moved the intervention point to the moment the activation is freshly generated, cutting the mean before it even enters a GeMM. The implication: find the earliest point at which a problem appears (even when it is still small), intervene there, and you will be an order of magnitude cheaper than letting the problem propagate and then cleaning it up.
