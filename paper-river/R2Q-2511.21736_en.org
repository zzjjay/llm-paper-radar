* The River of the Problem

Can you compress a large language model's weights from 16 bits down to 2 bits â€” meaning each weight can only take one of 4 possible values â€” and still have a model that speaks coherently?

Why is this hard: the distribution of model weights looks like a mountain, with the vast majority of values crowded near zero and only a scattered few wandering far out. If you slice that mountain into 4 equal-width bands, all the cuts fall in the deserted foothills, while the dense peak gets crushed into a single value and all that information is gone. The difficulty is nonlinear â€” dropping from 4 bits to 3 bits causes mild degradation, but dropping from 3 bits to 2 bits is often a cliff. Generation after generation has asked the same question: at the extreme limit of only 2 bits of expressiveness, how do you keep a model that still recognizes itself?

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2016 | XNOR-Net: Finding an Optimal Recipe for Binarization Without Guesswork

*** What problem did it see?

Before this line of work began, people had already tried compressing neural-network weights down to 1 bit (only +1 or âˆ’1). Early BinaryConnect simply replaced W with sign(W), resulting in catastrophic accuracy loss on ImageNet. The intuition was clear: binarization itself is not the problem â€” the conflict is between "only 1 bit" and "wildly different magnitudes in the original weights." A tiny weight and a large weight both get mapped to +1 or âˆ’1, and the strength relationship between them vanishes.

*** Its solution

XNOR-Net stopped insisting on "pure binarization" and instead allowed a positive real scalar alpha to be multiplied in front of each binary matrix B. It then posed a clean optimization question: given the original weight W, how do you choose (alpha, B) to minimize ||W - alpha*B||^2?

The answer is elegant enough to feel inevitable:
- B = sign(W) â€” the signs must obviously match W.
- alpha = mean(|W|) â€” the average absolute value of the elements.

The key property of this solution: *it requires no assumption about the weight distribution*. Whether W is Gaussian, bimodal, or heavy-tailed, the closed-form solution holds.

*** Why it works (and where it buries a new problem)

It works because it separates two concerns: the sign bit (B) handles direction, and the scalar (alpha) handles overall magnitude. But the limitation is equally obvious â€” the entire layer has only 2 codepoints (+alpha, âˆ’alpha), and the expressiveness ceiling is right there. You can make the model deeper to compensate, but the information bottleneck within each layer remains unsolved.

** 2017 | ABC-Net: If One Basis Isn't Enough, Stack Several

*** What problem did it see?

XNOR-Net is elegant but impoverished. On CNNs, the accuracy loss from a single binary basis exceeds 5%, making it hard to use in practice. Intuitively: give yourself a ruler with only two tick marks and you cannot trace the contours of a mountain.

*** Its solution

ABC-Net said: use M rulers (M binary bases). Write the weight as

#+begin_example
W ~ alpha_1 * B_1 + alpha_2 * B_2 + ... + alpha_M * B_M
#+end_example

Each B_i is a {âˆ’1, +1} matrix and each alpha_i is a positive real number. With M=3, a single weight position has 2^3=8 possible codepoints; with M=5, it has 32. At inference time all B_i use bitwise operations, so speed remains high.

This is where the embryo of the "residual" idea first appears â€” later bases cover the parts that earlier bases missed. ABC-Net's training does not strictly enforce this, since everything is learned jointly, but the structure is already there.

*** Why it works (and where it buries a new problem)

It works because expressiveness grows exponentially from 1 bit to M bits. The remaining problem: this is CNN-era work, never validated on LLMs. And when M bases are learned simultaneously, they can "negotiate" a division of labor (RaBiT later calls this failure mode *inter-path adaptation*), with no mechanism to prevent it.

** 2023 | LLM-QAT: Dragging QAT Into the LLM Era for the First Time

*** What problem did it see?

The binarization story kept circulating inside the CNN community. When large language models arrived, people first took the lazy route â€” PTQ (post-training quantization): GPTQ, AWQ, SmoothQuant. These methods are acceptable at 4 bits and above, but once you cross the 4-bit threshold accuracy collapses, and at 2 bits things are essentially broken. The reason is that PTQ only sees a small calibration set and has no opportunity to let the model learn to adapt to low precision.

QAT (quantization-aware training) is the obvious answer, but nobody dared apply it to LLMs â€” it is too expensive, and the original training data may not be available.

*** Its solution

LLM-QAT resolved the second concern with *data-free distillation*: let the full-precision teacher model generate a batch of prompts, then have the student (quantized model) match the teacher's output distribution on those self-generated data. No original training set required.

As a bonus, it also quantized the KV cache, which is critical for saving memory during long-sequence inference.

*** Why it works (and where it buries a new problem)

It works because it sidesteps the dead-end of "where does the training data come from" and demonstrates that QAT can work on LLMs down to 4 bits. But LLM-QAT itself only delivers good results at 4-bit configurations. At 2 bits it fails â€” the distillation loss is a standard KL divergence, with no special design for the way the model's output distribution warps under very low precision.

** 2024 | BitDistiller: Pushing QAT + KD to the Limit, Touching the 2-Bit Door

*** What problem did it see?

LLM-QAT left two specific pain points: (1) standard symmetric quantization forces the distribution's extreme outliers into the quantization range, wasting precision that should go to the dense center; (2) standard KL divergence asks the student to imitate the teacher uniformly, but for a quantized model, the tokens where the teacher is most confident are exactly the ones the student should prioritize â€” forcing the student to imitate the teacher's uncertain tokens leads to overfitting to noise.

*** Its solution

Two things:

1. *Asymmetric clipping* â€” before quantizing, clip the extreme tails of the weight distribution, freeing more effective precision for the high-density central region.

2. *Confidence-Aware KL (CAKLD)* â€” weight the distillation objective by the teacher's confidence on each token, so high-confidence samples receive a larger loss weight.

Together, at 2 bits BitDistiller was the first to keep the model from completely collapsing (Llama-7B at coarse-grained 2-bit is still poor, but far better than LLM-QAT). The R2Q paper explicitly calls it "the current state-of-the-art in 2-bit quantization."

*** Why it works (and where it buries a new problem)

It works because it aligns both the training objective and the quantization range to "which weights / which tokens actually matter." But BitDistiller *never touches the quantization structure itself*. Its 2-bit representation is still the uniform 4-codepoint grid â€” no matter how cleverly you train, 4 uniformly spaced points placed over a Gaussian mountain are crowded to death in the center and wasted at the tails. In the coarse-grained configuration, R2Q reports BitDistiller's WikiText-2 PPL as 15938 â€” that is not degraded performance; that is a broken model.

In other words, the entire QAT lineage, evolved to this point, *optimized how to train but never touched the codebook itself*.

** 2025-11 | R2Q: Don't Change the Training â€” Change the Codebook. Split 2-Bit Into Two Sequential 1-Bit Steps

*** What problem did it see?

R2Q diagnoses the failure of BitDistiller and similar QAT methods as a structural problem: they all inherit the integer quantization pipeline â€” choose a scale and zero-point, then round-to-nearest. RTN is nearly lossless at 16 bits, passable at 4 bits, but catastrophic at 2 bits because only 4 uniformly spaced points remain. The industry's workaround is *fine-grained grouping* â€” splitting weights into smaller groups, each with its own scale â€” but this means every group must carry extra scale parameters at inference time, substantially eroding the practical storage and compute savings.

R2Q noticed something that had been overlooked: the 1-bit closed-form solution from XNOR-Net has a unique property â€” *it is completely agnostic to the weight distribution*. All RTN-style multi-bit quantization schemes implicitly assume that weights are uniformly distributed.

*** Its solution

Split 2-bit quantization into two sequential 1-bit steps:

#+begin_example
Step 1 (coarse): Binarize the weight group w
                 alpha_1 = mean(|w|)
                 q_1 = sign(w)
                 w_hat_1 = alpha_1 * q_1

Step 2 (fine):   Compute residual r = w - alpha_1 * q_1,
                 then binarize r
                 alpha_2 = mean(|r|)
                 q_2 = sign(r)
                 w_hat_2 = alpha_2 * q_2

Reconstruction:  w_hat = alpha_1 * q_1 + alpha_2 * q_2
#+end_example

Each weight ultimately falls on one of 4 codepoints: {+alpha_1+alpha_2, +alpha_1âˆ’alpha_2, âˆ’alpha_1+alpha_2, âˆ’alpha_1âˆ’alpha_2}. Crucially, these 4 points are *non-uniform*. alpha_1 determines the coarse axis position and alpha_2 determines the fine-grained spacing; both are computed automatically from the weight statistics.

At inference, the matrix multiplication is also elegant: Q_1 X and Q_2 X are both binary multiply-by-add/subtract operations, computed in parallel and then scaled by alpha_1 and alpha_2 respectively before summing.

*** Why it works (and where it buries a new problem)

It works because it simultaneously achieves two things: (a) the distribution-agnostic property of the closed-form 1-bit solution, requiring no assumption that weights are uniformly distributed; (b) the residual structure naturally makes the second bit specialize in correcting what the first bit missed, producing an adaptive codebook organically. It escapes the dead end of the RTN lineage â€” "fixed codebook â†’ desperately tune training" â€” and replaces it with "adaptive codebook â†’ training converges easily."

The problem it buries is quickly caught: if two 1-bit bases are optimized jointly, will they learn redundant representations? Will they truly form the intended coarse-to-fine hierarchy?

* Frontier Extensions

** 2026-02 | RaBiT: Explicitly Fixing the Residual Hierarchy Problem R2Q Left Behind

R2Q's two-step structure is described in the paper as if it "naturally forms a hierarchy," but when QAT actually runs, alpha_1 q_1 and alpha_2 q_2 are back-propagated together and can easily converge to two overlapping bases rather than a "primary + residual" pair. RaBiT (You et al., 2026-02) precisely names this failure mode *inter-path adaptation* â€” parallel binary paths learn redundant features and the error-correction structure degrades.

RaBiT's solution: algorithmically enforce the residual hierarchy â€” each path must be *sequentially* derived from the same shared full-precision weight, with the input to each subsequent path being the residual of the previous one. This transforms the hierarchy that R2Q *implies* in prose into a hierarchy that is *enforced* in the training procedure.

As a result, RaBiT redraws the accuracy-efficiency frontier at 2 bits, reportedly rivaling hardware-heavy vector quantization methods (AQLM, QuIP#, QTIP) while retaining the hardware friendliness of binarization.

Running in parallel, MoBiQuant (2026-02) turns recursive residual quantization into "elastic precision" â€” a single model simultaneously supports 2/4/6-bit, dynamically allocated per token. This generalizes R2Q's two-layer structure to K layers and adds a router that decides how many layers each token uses.

* Insights

On the surface this lineage is about "how to compress LLMs to 2 bits without breaking them," but what is really happening is a *migration of the optimization target*:

1. *XNOR-Net era*: Optimize "how to binarize." The answer is a closed form.
2. *LLM-QAT / BitDistiller era*: Optimize "how to train." The codebook is assumed uniform and untouchable. All innovation concentrates on loss functions, distillation objectives, and clipping strategies.
3. *R2Q era*: Re-optimize "the codebook structure itself." Leverage the distribution-agnostic closed-form 1-bit solution to let a multi-bit codebook *grow* from the data rather than be *cut* from a uniform grid.
4. *RaBiT / MoBiQuant era*: Optimize "the relationship between residual paths." Not just how many bases to use, but whether those bases are peers or form a strict hierarchy.

The deeper shift: early work treated "quantization" as a numerical approximation problem (find the nearest integer). The R2Q lineage re-frames "quantization" as a *representation learning* problem â€” the codebook is learned, not pre-specified. This brings the neural-network spirit of "learn everything end-to-end" into precisely the corner that had most resisted it.

Where things are most likely to go next:
- Adaptive number of residual levels â€” different layers and different tokens use different numbers of residual bases.
- A hybrid of closed-form 1-bit initialization with learned path weights.
- Applying the residual idea in reverse to activation quantization and KV cache quantization.

* Takeaways

Lessons for how to do research:

1. *The things taken for granted are the most worth attacking.* The entire LLM quantization community assumed uniform codebooks and piled all innovation on the training side â€” then hit a ceiling at 2 bits. R2Q stepped back and asked "why does the codebook have to be uniform?" and the entire dead end was instantly bypassed.

2. *Properties forgotten in old papers get rediscovered by new settings.* The closed-form solution in XNOR-Net (2016) was picked up by ABC-Net and then forgotten in the CNN world, only to have its property of "distribution-agnostic closed-form solution" reactivated in 2025 when the LLM world reached a 2-bit impasse.

3. *A mechanism described as hierarchical in prose may not be enforced as hierarchical in training.* R2Q describes the two steps as "coarse first, then fine," but they are learned jointly. RaBiT immediately patched that gap by forcing sequential derivation so that the claimed hierarchy actually holds. This is a productive research pattern â€” when a paper uses "intuition" to imply a certain structure, the next paper's opportunity lies in "turning that structure into a training constraint."

The practical takeaway: the next time you face a task where the training objective has been tuned to its limit and you are still hitting a wall, look first at *the data structure that everyone assumes is fixed* (codebook / grid / classifier head / positional encoding) rather than continuing to stack new loss terms.
