* One-line positioning

D²Quant is an early-2026 weight-quantization paper that tackles the hard
problem of "staying accurate below 4 bits." It does two things: (1) it
quietly inserts a set of equivalent scaling factors into the hardest-to-
quantize layer inside the MLP — the down-projection — without spending a
single extra bit, thereby reducing quantization difficulty; (2) it
discovers that quantizing weights causes a "global shift" in activations,
and adds a mean-correction term inside LayerNorm to cancel that shift.

To understand why the paper is structured the way it is, you have to trace
back an evolutionary line that has been growing since 2022.

* Who is the protagonist

The protagonist is not the paper — it is a problem:

#+begin_quote
LLMs keep growing larger. Can we compress weights down to 3 or even 2 bits
while keeping the model largely intact? And without retraining — retraining
is too expensive.
#+end_quote

This problem has never been cleanly solved since GPT-3 in 2022. Each
generation of methods pushes the ceiling a little lower, but they all
stall around the 4-bit barrier. D2Quant is the latest thrust along this
line.

The sections below follow the timeline to see how this problem has step by
step forced today's solution into existence.

* First stop: GPTQ — treating quantization as a math problem

** What it saw

Before 2022, weight quantization meant "round to the nearest low-bit
number." Simple, but accuracy dropped catastrophically. The GPTQ authors
realized you cannot quantize each weight in isolation: weights are
correlated — compress w1 a little and the activations shift, the entire
layer's output drifts, so w2 should compensate in the opposite direction.

** Its solution

Cast quantization as minimizing the output reconstruction error. For each
layer W with known input X, find Ŵ that minimizes ‖WX − ŴX‖. This is a
second-order problem that requires the Hessian. GPTQ's engineering
contribution was to handle the costly Hessian inversion with a Cholesky
decomposition plus column-by-column processing, making it feasible to
quantize a 10B-parameter model in a few hours.

** What problem it left behind

4-bit works well, 3-bit still holds, 2-bit collapses. One reason: it only
looks at the weights themselves and ignores the activation side.

* Second stop: SmoothQuant — migrating difficulty from activations to weights

** What problem it saw in its predecessors

GPTQ cares about weights, but it quickly became clear that activations
contain outliers. Certain channels can have values tens of times larger
than the average. If you want W8A8 (weights and activations both at 8
bits), those few outlier channels peg the whole layer's scale, squashing
all other channels to near zero.

** Its solution

A very clever equivalent transformation:

#+begin_src text
  Y = X * W
    = (X / s) * (s * W)        <-- mathematically identical
       smoother   a bit harder
#+end_src

Divide a diagonal matrix diag(s) out of the activations and multiply it
into the weights. Activations become smooth (outliers gone), weights
become slightly harder to quantize (but weights are easier to quantize to
begin with). How to set s? Look at the per-channel maximum of the
activations.

** What problem it left behind

It was designed for joint weight + activation quantization. For weight-only
quantization, the equivalent transformation is not exploited to its full
potential. Moreover, s is computed statically and is not optimized against
the actual quantization error.

—— Remember this "equivalent transformation" idea. It is the ancestor of
D2Quant's DSQ.

* Third stop: AWQ — not all weights are equally important

** What problem it saw in its predecessors

SmoothQuant attaches scales to channels based on activation magnitude. The
AWQ authors found that only ~1% of channels in the weight matrix are truly
critical (salient weights). Protect that 1%, let the rest be freely
compressed, and the result is very good. How to find them? Again, look at
the activations — the weight columns corresponding to large activation
values are the key columns.

** Its solution

Find a per-channel scale that "amplifies" the key columns before
quantization (then divides back afterwards), effectively giving them higher
effective bit-width. Other columns are handled as usual.

** What problem it left behind

AWQ is powerful at 4 bits, starts showing stress at 3 bits, and cannot
hold at 2 bits. Moreover, it can only "amplify certain columns" and cannot
address the structurally difficult quantization of certain layers (such as
the down-projection discussed below).

* Fourth stop: QuaRot — outliers are a coordinate-system problem

** What problem it saw in its predecessors

SmoothQuant and AWQ both wrestle with outliers. The QuaRot authors stepped
back and asked: why do outliers appear at all? Could it be that we chose
the wrong coordinate system?

If you multiply both weights and activations by an orthogonal matrix R
(then multiply by R^T to cancel), the math is completely equivalent, but
the outliers get "scattered" across all dimensions. Random Hadamard
matrices are ideal for this — they are dense, orthogonal, and cheap to
compute.

** Its solution

Insert implicit Hadamard rotations at key positions in the LLM (attention
output, FFN input, etc.). After rotation the activations become
"white-noise-like," the per-channel dynamic range shrinks dramatically, and
the entire layer becomes easy to quantize.

** What problem it left behind

The Hadamard rotation mainly targets the activation side. Difficult-to-
quantize weight layers (such as the down-projection, discussed next) are
not necessarily resolved by rotation. Also, Hadamard is a "global
smoothing" and cannot accommodate the specific characteristics of each
individual channel.

* Fifth stop: OmniQuant and the QAT family — just train a little

** What problem it saw in its predecessors

GPTQ / AWQ / QuaRot are all PTQ (post-training): training is finished and
nothing is touched afterwards. OmniQuant, BitDistiller, LLM-QAT and
similar methods argue: make the scale and clipping threshold "learnable,"
fine-tune with a small dataset, and accuracy jumps up a level.

** Its solution

Make the quantizer's scale, zero-point, and clipping threshold differentiable
parameters; let gradients flow through with STE (Straight-Through
Estimator); run a few dozen steps of fine-tuning.

** What problem it left behind

It requires training. It requires data. It requires time. Fine-tuning
OmniQuant on a LLaMA-70B takes dozens of GPUs running for days. Staying
on the PTQ path — how do you keep pushing *without training*?

—— That question hands the stage back to the PTQ line, which is exactly
where D2Quant enters.

* Sixth stop: GPTAQ / BoA — polishing GPTQ

** What they saw

This 2025 wave of work re-polished GPTQ's Hessian estimation:
- *GPTAQ* folds in input error (quantization error from earlier layers
  propagates to later layers)
- *BoA* recomputes the Hessian with attention-structure awareness, without
  needing back-propagation

Both push PTQ's 4-bit performance up another notch and represent roughly
the SOTA just before D2Quant appears.

** What problem they left behind

Two things were left untouched:
1. They assume a uniform per-row quantization strategy for all linear
   layers, missing the special nature of the down-projection
2. They do not correct activation drift — they only minimize weight
   reconstruction error, but wrong weights mean wrong activations

—— These two gaps are the two holes D2Quant drills into.

* Final stop (for now): D2Quant — drilling two new holes

** Hole one: down-projection can be squeezed again (DSQ)

*** What it saw

The MLP in the LLaMA family looks like this:

#+begin_src text
  X --> [W_gate] --\
                    + --> SiLU(.) * (.) --> [W_down] --> Y
  X --> [W_up]  --/
#+end_src

W_up and W_down are paired. Prior work treats these three matrices as
"independent layers" and quantizes each separately. The D2Quant authors
noticed that the same SmoothQuant-style equivalent transformation can be
applied *between* W_up and W_down:

#+begin_src text
  Original:  (...) * W_up * W_down
  Rewritten: (...) * (W_up * diag(η)) * (diag(η)^-1 * W_down)
                      ===========       =================
                      negligible change  column-wise scale reduced
#+end_src

Why is the "negligible change" side truly almost free? Because W_up is
already quantized with a per-row scale; multiplying by diag(η) means η
can be "absorbed" into each row's existing scale — zero extra bits, zero
runtime overhead.

Meanwhile, the W_down side sees its column-wise dynamic range shrink,
immediately reducing quantization error. This is the *Dual-Scale
Quantizer* — each W_down has two scales: the original per-row scale s,
plus a new per-column scale s^c.

*** How it is solved

Minimize ‖W - Q(W) * s^c‖². Fix s^c, solve for Q; fix Q, solve for s^c
in closed form. Alternating for about 15 iterations converges.

*** Difference from SmoothQuant

SmoothQuant computes the scale *statically* (from activation maxima).
D2Quant *dynamically optimizes* it (directly minimizing quantization
error). Furthermore, SmoothQuant transfers difficulty from activation to
weight; D2Quant transfers it from weight to weight (up → down). That is a
new direction.

** Hole two: weight quantization causes activation drift (DAC)

*** What it saw

Quantize the weights and the activations follow — replace W with Ŵ and
Y = XW becomes Y' = XŴ. Nobody is unaware of this, but the D2Quant
authors looked one step further: *ΔY = Y - Y', this drift, looks
different at different locations.*

Specifically:
- *Input to the LayerNorm after attention* — ΔY has a significant mean
  (a directional bias)
- *Input to the next layer's LayerNorm after the MLP* — ΔY is
  approximately zero-mean noise

Measured by SNR = |μ|/σ², the post-attention LN has a much higher SNR.

*** Why this matters

If the drift is *directional* (high SNR), subtracting a constant bias μ
fixes it, reducing the error from μ² + σ² to just σ². If the drift is
zero-mean noise (low SNR), subtracting a constant does nothing useful.

So D2Quant adds a learned bias at *only* the post-attention LN, leaving
all other positions untouched. This is *Deviation-Aware Correction*.

*** Difference from prior work

Others have modified LayerNorm (e.g. Norm Tweaking), but they apply
corrections everywhere indiscriminately. D2Quant is the first to clearly
explain *why certain positions should be corrected and others should not*,
and it provides a simple SNR criterion to make that determination.

* What is really happening along this line

On the surface it looks like "pushing the bit-count lower and lower," but
the deeper shift is one of *cognitive granularity*:

1. *From weights → seeing activations* (GPTQ → SmoothQuant)
2. *From uniform → seeing non-uniform importance* (SmoothQuant → AWQ)
3. *From coordinate axes → seeing the coordinate system* (AWQ → QuaRot)
4. *From individual layers → seeing inter-layer structure* (all prior
   work → D2Quant's DSQ)
5. *From weight error → seeing activation error propagation* (all prior
   work → D2Quant's DAC)

Each step takes something that "wasn't treated as a variable before" and
makes it "an optimizable variable." The compression problem is evolving
from "find a good quantizer" to "find a good *representation*" — first
reparameterize the model into a form that quantizes more easily, then the
most basic quantizer is sufficient.

* Where the next steps might go

1. The *up–down equivalent transform* idea can be generalized. In LLaMA's
   attention, do W_Q, W_K, and W_O also have analogous absorbable scale
   pairs?
2. The *SNR criterion* provides a principled "should this be corrected or
   not" standard. Can it be extended to the attention K/V cache and the
   residual stream?
3. *DSQ and DAC are both PTQ*. If a few steps of "alignment fine-tuning"
   follow PTQ — learning only the equivalent scales and LN biases while
   freezing the main weights — it may be enough to push sub-3-bit over
   the line.
4. *Live inference*: the s^c added by DSQ is absorbed offline with zero
   runtime cost; the bias added by DAC is also a constant. This means the
   whole approach can be dropped seamlessly into existing vLLM / SGLang
   inference stacks without kernel changes.
