* One-sentence summary

MXNorm replaces the "full-tensor sum-of-squares" step in LLM RMSNorm with the block maximums that MXFP8 quantization already has to compute—reusing the same statistics twice—shrinking the normalisation reduction by 32×, saving 1.3% of one Llama3-8B layer's runtime, and 2.6% under NVFP4.

* Tracing the river upstream

The problem did not emerge from nowhere. It is the collision, in 2026, of two decade-long currents: "how to keep neural-network training stable" and "how to squeeze every last FLOP from GPU hardware."

Digging back five layers yields this skeleton:

Notice that *two forces* converge here: one is the evolution of normalisation layers themselves (BatchNorm → LayerNorm → RMSNorm), the other is the march toward ever-lower-precision matrix multiplication (FP16 → FP8 → MXFP8 → NVFP4). MXNorm sits precisely at the intersection—it is neither a smarter normalisation nor a more aggressive quantization scheme, but rather *the seam that joins the two lines*: letting normalisation reuse a by-product of quantization.

The story told forward follows.

* Section 1: BatchNorm's motivation and cost (2015)

Around 2014–2015, convolutional networks could already be stacked dozens of layers deep, but training was fragile. A slightly elevated learning rate blew up the loss; a conservative one stalled learning. Ioffe and Szegedy named the phenomenon *internal covariate shift*: each layer's input distribution was constantly reshuffled by the layers before it, forcing later layers to chase an ever-moving target.

Their fix was direct: before each layer, subtract the batch mean and divide by the batch standard deviation, forcibly snapping activations back to a stable distribution. That was *BatchNorm*. The effect was immediate—large models could be trained with large learning rates—and deep learning entered the "normalise first, then train" era.

But BatchNorm left a loose thread: its statistics are computed *along the batch dimension*. That means:

- The batch must be large enough for the statistics to be reliable;
- Training and inference behave differently (at inference time there is only one sample, so stored running mean/var must stand in);
- In sequence models, is each token an independent sample? Does it even count as "batch"? The question has no clean answer.

In the RNN / Transformer era these loose threads became increasingly painful.

* Section 2: LayerNorm switches the reduction axis (2016)

Ba, Kiros, and Hinton saw exactly this problem. Their counter-question was simple: *"If we cannot reduce over the batch dimension, which dimension should we use instead?"*

The answer: the *feature dimension*. Compute mean and variance over all hidden dimensions of a single sample and normalise against those. The change looks minor but is profound:

- A single sample suffices; inference and training are perfectly symmetric;
- Batch size—even batch = 1—is irrelevant;
- The operation can be applied independently at every time step of an RNN or Transformer.

That was *LayerNorm*. It lacked BatchNorm's dramatic "cures divergence" story, but by freeing normalisation from batch dependence it was immediately embraced by sequence models. Transformers were born with LayerNorm and used it for years.

Yet LayerNorm left its own loose thread: it requires *both* mean and variance, meaning two reduction passes, two subtractions, two divisions—none of them cheap on a GPU.

* Section 3: RMSNorm drops the mean (2019)

Zhang and Sennrich asked a sharper question: *"Is computing the mean actually useful?"*

Their ablations found that removing the "subtract mean" step and keeping only "divide by root-mean-square" cost almost nothing in model quality and was sometimes more stable. The reasoning holds up: what LayerNorm really contributes is *re-scaling* (controlling the magnitude of activations); *re-centering* (shifting to zero mean) is optional for most tasks.

So they wrote a leaner variant—only RMS, no mean. That was *RMSNorm*, with roughly 64% of LayerNorm's compute and negligible quality loss.

The change looks minor, but it landed right as large language models were taking off. The Llama series adopted RMSNorm from its first release; gpt-oss, DeepSeek-V3, and Qwen followed. By 2024 "LLM norm = RMSNorm" had become the default assumption.

The new loose thread was more subtle: RMSNorm looked cheap enough that no one thought to question it any further.

* Interlude: A parallel branch—MXFP quantization (2023–2025)

Cut to 2023 and a different storyline.

GPU throughput growth has always depended on reducing precision. FP16 → FP8 → FP4: each step roughly doubles matrix-multiply throughput. But pure FP4 has too narrow a dynamic range to represent most tensors. The fix?

Rouhani et al. (Microsoft, 2023) proposed the *Microscaling (MX)* format: slice a tensor into small blocks (typically 32 elements), give each block a shared 8-bit exponent scale factor (E8M0), and store intra-block elements at low precision (E4M3 / E2M1). The dynamic range is carried by the scale; the elements are cheap. Fast *and* representable.

Mishra et al. (NVIDIA, 2025) took this to LLM pre-training at scale, delivering a reproducible MXFP8 recipe. Blackwell / Rubin GPUs support the format natively, and by 2025–2026 MXFP8 / NVFP4 had rapidly become the new training baseline.

At this point the normalisation line and the quantization line were still running in parallel. Until—

* Section 4: MXNorm stitches the two lines together (2026.03, this paper)

The authors of MXNorm spotted something nobody had quite said aloud: *the hardware arithmetic has fundamentally shifted.*

Over the past eight years, matrix-multiply throughput has grown 80× (V100 FP16 125 TFLOPS → GB200 FP4 10 000 TFLOPS). But the CUDA cores that handle normalisation, activation functions, and reductions grew only 5.1×, and HBM bandwidth only 8.9×.

Consequence: those "seemingly cheap" non-matmul operations—like RMSNorm—*now account for a steadily larger share of total runtime*.

Concretely, in one Pre-Norm Transformer layer the execution looks like this:

#+begin_src text
   High-precision input X
       |
       v
   [RMSNorm]  <-- sum(x^2) over the full tensor, full-tensor reduction
       |
   High-precision X_bar
       |
       v
   [MXCast]   <-- max(|x|) per block of 32, then scale and quantize
       |
   MXFP8 tensor
       |
       v
   [matmul]
#+end_src

Staring at this picture, the authors asked the key question: *"RMSNorm computes a sum of squares; MXCast computes a per-block maximum. Why are these two statistics computed separately? Can the block maxima that MXCast already produces be used to recover the RMS?"*

They proved the following: if a tensor's elements are approximately i.i.d., then *a suitable generalised p-mean of the per-block maxima differs from the full-tensor RMS by only a constant factor*. That constant depends only on the block size and the element distribution, and can be estimated offline via Monte Carlo.

MXNorm therefore looks like this:

#+begin_src text
   High-precision input X
       |
       +--> [MXCast: compute block scales m_k = max_b |X_kb|]
       |              |
       |              v
       |        Array of block maxima (K values)
       |              |
       |              v
       |        rho_tilde = c * (mean(m_k^p))^(-1/p)   <-- RMS estimate from K values,
       |              |                                    not from all D = K*B values
       v              |
   X * rho_tilde     /
       |____________/
       v
   Normalised + quantized MXFP8 tensor in one pass
#+end_src

The key gain: *the reduction volume drops from D to K = D/B*—a 32× reduction for block size 32. One pass over the statistics; two things accomplished.

Viewed as a transformation: converting RMSNorm + MXCast into MXNorm requires only three changes:

1. Delete the full-tensor ~sum(x^2)~ reduction inside RMSNorm;
2. Reuse the ~max_b |x|~ per block that MXCast is computing anyway;
3. Apply ~(mean(m_k^p))^(-1/p) * c~ to combine those block maxima into an RMS estimate.

* Section 4 (continued): The new problems MXNorm introduces

There is no free lunch. Using block maxima to estimate the RMS rests on two implicit assumptions:

1. *Enough blocks must exist.* The paper shows the r² accuracy curve: for K ≥ 32 (i.e. D ≥ 1024) the estimate is tight, but on small tensors the error grows. LLM hidden dimensions are typically in the thousands—safely in the accurate regime—so the scheme works in practice.
2. *Elements must be approximately i.i.d.* and the distribution must be stable enough for a fixed constant c to correct the estimate. If the activation distribution drifts—late in training, in unusual layers, during outlier bursts—the estimate becomes biased. In the paper, MXNorm(p=1) on an 8B model finishes training at loss 2.175 vs RMSNorm's 2.132—*there is a gap*. Switching to p=2 closes it.

The second issue reveals something deeper: a normalisation layer is not only a mathematical operation—it also quietly provides *outlier control*. RMS is a quadratic mean; outliers are amplified and then suppressed. The p-mean of block maxima has a different response curve to outliers. MXNorm therefore requires the right p to remain stable.

* Section 5: The contemporary alternative—skip normalisation altogether

Worth noting: MXNorm pursues "make normalisation cheaper." A contemporaneous school takes the opposite route: *"Given how expensive normalisation is, can we eliminate it entirely?"*

- *DyT* (Zhu et al. 2025, "Transformers without Normalization"): replaces RMSNorm wholesale with a saturating nonlinearity such as ~tanh~. No reduction needed, but elementwise special functions are not fast on GPUs either.
- *FlashNorm* (Graef 2025): computes the RMS asynchronously, letting the matmul start before the RMS is ready and correcting afterward. Low latency in theory, but using un-normalised values mid-computation risks accumulator overflow.
- *nGPT* (Loshchilov et al. 2025): constrains weights to the unit hypersphere, removing the need for normalisation entirely. Elegant in principle, but the per-step hypersphere projection adds its own cost.

MXNorm's trade-off: *preserve the mathematical semantics and stability of RMSNorm* (it is still "scale by RMS"), and only reuse a statistic at the engineering level. This makes it the most *drop-in* of all approaches—no other training code needs to change; only the Norm layer is swapped.

* What is really happening in this river

On the surface the story looks like "normalisation keeps getting simpler": BatchNorm (expensive) → LayerNorm (batch-free) → RMSNorm (mean removed) → MXNorm (even the reduction volume is cut).

But the deeper shift is that *the design objective of normalisation layers is changing*:

- 2015–2019: the goal was *training stability*. Preventing deep networks from diverging was paramount; compute cost was secondary.
- 2019–2024: the goal became *minimal necessary mathematics*. Keep the essential function (controlling activation scale); strip the inessential (re-centering, batch dependence).
- 2024–2026: the goal is *co-existence with the hardware pipeline*. Normalisation is no longer a self-contained mathematical operation; it must be *fused with quantization, matmul, and memory layout* to matter. A Norm layer's merit is no longer judged only by what it computes, but by *which statistics it shares with its neighbours*.

MXNorm is the clearest specimen of this new phase: it does not invent a new kind of normalisation—it invents *statistic reuse*.

* Where the river might flow next

If this framing is correct, the next step along this line is probably not "a smarter normalisation function" but rather:

1. *The limits of statistic sharing*: beyond RMS derived from MX blocks, are there other quantities—attention softmax maxima, SiLU stabilisation constants, optimiser second moments—that could share the same reduction?
2. *Fused Norm-Quant-Matmul kernels*: MXNorm merges Norm and Cast; the natural next step is to absorb the subsequent matmul as well, turning the entire "Norm + Linear" pattern into a single fused kernel.
3. *Statistical robustness for NVFP4 and lower*: the fact that p=1 fails while p=2 succeeds hints that as distributions narrow and precision drops, the demands on statistical estimation become more exacting. Will the constant c(p,B) of Theorem 1 evolve from a "fixed value under a Gaussian assumption" into a "distribution-aware learnable parameter"?
