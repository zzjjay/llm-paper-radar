* The River of Problems

Squeezing a 72B model onto a single 24 GB gaming GPU while keeping math-benchmark accuracy from collapsing too badly — this problem has been stuck for over thirty years.

Each weight originally occupies 16 bits, giving 65 536 possible values. Compress it to 2 bits and only 4 values remain. In other words, a curve that could once be described in fine detail must now be approximated with four steps. Where do the steps go? How high? For thirty-plus years, generation after generation answered that question. Early researchers pruned neural-network connections and asked "which weights matter least?"; later ones quantized language-model weights and asked "how do we keep the output error small?" The most recent paper in this line says: everyone assumed the *shape of the steps is fixed* — once you release that assumption, four steps can hold far more than you'd expect.

The central thread of this river is: *at ultra-low bit-widths, how do we keep the loss caused by compression confined to a small deviation in model outputs?*

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1989 | OBD: The Network Is Too Fat — Which Finger Hurts Least to Cut?

*** What Problem It Saw

In the late 1980s, neural networks were just beginning to grow large. LeCun and colleagues noticed that after training, many weights were negligible — but it was unclear which ones could safely be removed. The most naive approach was "remove weights with small absolute values," but that is wrong: a tiny weight in a highly sensitive position can be critical.

*** Its Solution

OBD asked a sharper question: *if I set weight w_i to zero, how much does the loss increase?* This amounts to a Taylor expansion of the loss around the optimum. The first-order term vanishes at the optimum; the second-order term involves the Hessian — but the full Hessian is too large (quadratic in parameter count) to compute.

OBD's simplification: *assume the Hessian is diagonal*. Each weight gets its own scalar importance score, and weights are removed in ascending order of that score.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left)

The diagonal assumption reduces computation from quadratic to linear — practical. But it is equivalent to saying weights do not influence each other, which is almost never true in real networks. When one weight is removed, the remaining weights could shift slightly to compensate; OBD does not exploit that freedom at all.

** 1993 | OBS: Remove One, Prop Up the Others

*** What Problem It Saw

OBD's pruning was like letting a single soldier fall while the rest of the formation stands frozen. But when a weight is set to zero, the remaining weights can absolutely reposition themselves to fill the gap. OBD not only overestimated the cost of pruning but also left the network's remaining capacity untapped.

*** Its Solution

Hassibi brought in the full Hessian. Concretely: to prune weight w_q, first solve for the optimal adjustment of all remaining weights that minimizes the output error, then carry out "prune + compensate" as a single step. This is equivalent to an optimal projection onto the surface defined by the Hessian.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left)

It explicitly models coupling between weights — remove one and the others respond. The catch: the Hessian matrix scales quadratically with parameter count. It was barely feasible for the small networks of the time and completely unusable for today's billion-parameter LLMs. That trap sat open for nearly thirty years before someone filled it.

** 2022 | OBC: Merge "Pruning" and "Quantization" into One Thing

*** What Problem It Saw

The OBS idea had only ever been applied to pruning. But Frantar and Alistarh noticed that *quantization* is fundamentally the same operation as pruning: pruning sets a weight to 0; quantization sets it to the nearest legal value. Both replace a continuous value with a point from a restricted discrete set and then try to keep the network's output unchanged.

Researchers working on quantization and those working on pruning had been running separate tracks with disconnected theoretical frameworks.

*** Its Solution

OBC (Optimal Brain Compression) extends the second-order compensation idea of OBS to "any discrete replacement":
- Process weights one at a time
- After each replacement, update all remaining weights using Hessian information to absorb the error
- "Replacement" can be setting a weight to zero (pruning) or rounding it to the nearest quantization grid point (quantization)

This unified the compression problem under a single mathematical framework.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left)

Theoretically elegant, but the experiments at the time were still at ResNet scale. By then GPT-3 had already appeared with 175 billion parameters. OBC's per-weight processing, entangled with Hessian inverse updates, had a complexity that made it completely impractical at that scale.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Making OBC Actually Run on Large Models

*** What Problem It Saw

OBC's bottleneck: after quantizing each weight, the inverse Hessian must be updated. For a layer with hundreds of millions of weights, this cost is prohibitive. The mathematics of OBC was correct — the idea was not wrong, it was simply unexecutable.

*** Its Solution

Frantar et al. did three things:
1. *Cholesky decomposition*: factor H^{-1} once into an upper-triangular matrix U, eliminating the need for repeated inversions afterward
2. *Column-order processing*: quantize column by column from left to right; error propagates only forward, never back
3. *Blocking/grouping*: further reduce peak memory usage

This made OBC's approach run on OPT-175B for the first time. Overnight, "4-bit LLM" went from impossible to standard practice.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left)

It moved OBC from "theoretically correct but engineeringly infeasible" to "theoretically correct and actually runnable." 4-bit with almost no accuracy loss became the new normal. But dropping to 2–3 bpp caused accuracy to fall off a cliff — for example, 2-bit GPTQ on Qwen2.5-72B yields only 1.52% on GSM8K. At the time, people assumed the culprit was a weak optimization algorithm or insufficient calibration data. BPDQ later points out: the problem is not the algorithm; *it is the grid itself*.

** 2025 | The Babai Geometric View + Qronos: Finally Seeing Through GPTQ

*** What Problem It Saw

Three years after GPTQ, people used it happily but could not cleanly explain *why it worked*. Intuitively you could sense it was performing some kind of optimal projection, but a clean geometric interpretation was missing.

*** Its Solution

Chen et al. (2025b) discovered that GPTQ is equivalent to running *Babai's nearest-plane algorithm* on a lattice induced by the Hessian — a classical algorithm from number theory for finding the closest lattice point. Once this connection is clear, everything about "how error propagates" and "why column-order processing is correct" has a geometric explanation.

Zhang et al. (Qronos) then strengthened the Babai framework: both forward-propagated and backward-retained errors are folded into the compensation, and each step hugs the "minimize output deviation" objective more tightly.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left)

This wave fully cemented GPTQ's theoretical foundations and provided error bounds. But it still *accepts the premise that "the grid is fixed"* — all the theory addresses "given a fixed grid, what is the optimal projection?" No one asked: can the grid itself move?

That is exactly where BPDQ stands up.

** 2026 | BPDQ: The Grid Does Not Have to Be Rigid

*** What Problem It Saw

BPDQ revisits the 2-bit failure. It argues that every prior method — GPTQ, AWQ, AnyBCQ — implicitly accepts one thing: *within a quantization group, only 4 values are permitted, and the relative spacing of those 4 values is fixed*. For a uniform grid the values are {0, 1, 2, 3} × scale; for a non-uniform grid they are {a₀, a₁, a₂, a₃} × scale. The scale can vary across groups, but the *shape* {a₀, a₁, a₂, a₃} is shared by all groups.

How severe is this "shape invariance" constraint? Imagine you must build anything out of four Lego bricks whose shapes are locked in place. Different groups may get a "larger" or "smaller" edition, but you cannot change the brick shapes themselves. The optimization objective says "find the point closest to the target output," but the feasible set is frozen by this shape constraint.

*** Its Solution

BPDQ decomposes a weight group into *bit-planes* — writes each weight as a sum of 0/1 planes scaled by learned coefficients:

W = C0 + C1·B1 + C2·B2 + ...

B1, B2 are binary matrices (indicating which positions receive each plane's contribution); C0, C1, C2 are real-valued scalars learned separately for each group. The key insight: *different groups can learn completely different (C1, C2) pairs*, which means every group grows its own grid shape. Two bits still distinguish only 4 combinations (00, 01, 10, 11), but where those 4 combinations fall on the number line — and their relative spacing — is decided freely by each group.

BPDQ then uses Hessian geometry to run GPTQ-style column-order updates on the variable grid, together with closed-form re-fitting of each group's coefficients and delta error compensation. The entire procedure remains aligned with the OBC/GPTQ objective of minimizing output deviation.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left)

Intuitively: enlarging the feasible set cannot make the optimal solution worse. Empirically: at 2-bit on Qwen2.5-72B, GPTQ scores 1.52% on GSM8K and AWQ scores 0%; BPDQ reaches 83.85% — only 7 points below the 16-bit baseline of 90.83% — on a single 24 GB GPU.

Remaining traps: the bit-plane format requires hardware support (bit-parallel kernels); how sensitive the variable grid is to calibration-data distribution is not yet fully understood; whether releasing even more constraints helps at 1-bit is an open question.

* Frontier Extensions

BPDQ itself is the latest advance along this line as of May 2026. It sits alongside several concurrent works: Qronos strengthens the GPTQ solver; AnyBCQ also follows a bit-plane path but lacks an output-aligned objective; VPTQ pursues vector quantization. Predictable directions ahead:

1. *Push variable grids to 1-bit or sub-bit*: when each group allows only 2 values, can shape freedom still save accuracy?
2. *Variable grids × long context*: BPDQ has already shown preliminary robustness on reasoning tasks, but error accumulation in long-context scenarios is the next battlefield.
3. *Hardware co-design*: the parallelism advantage of bit-planes needs LUT kernels; native bit-plane support in future accelerators may become standard.
4. *Fusion with mixed precision*: making "which groups use 2-bit, which use 3-bit" a jointly learnable part of the optimization.

* Insights

On the surface this line looks like "quantization algorithms keep getting more accurate." Look deeper and what you see is *constraints being peeled away one layer at a time*.

- OBD → OBS: released "weights are independent"
- OBS → OBC: released "compression means only pruning"
- OBC → GPTQ: released "theoretically correct methods can only run on small models"
- GPTQ → BPDQ: released "the grid shape is fixed"

No generation invented a new objective — "minimize loss change" in OBD and "minimize output deviation" in BPDQ are mathematically the same thing. What each generation did was discover *a hidden assumption its predecessors had accepted without realizing it*, and then break through it.

This is telling: in a mature field, finding a breakthrough is less about re-optimizing a target everyone is already optimizing and more about finding *a premise everyone takes for granted that actually need not hold*. BPDQ's core insight fits in one sentence: "The grid shape has nothing to do with the optimization objective — why should it be fixed?" Yet no one asked that question for three years.

Where does the next step most likely lead? My guess is *joint optimization of grid structure + quantization precision + hardware instructions* — these three have always been treated separately, but they are really three faces of the same problem. Whoever first puts all three variables inside a single optimization objective will push this river forward.

* Takeaways

Two things stand out from studying this line of work:

1. *Do not trust "obvious constraints."* Premises that every paper in a field obeys are often convention, not truth. Before OBC unified pruning and quantization, the community had two separate camps doing separate things and no one found it strange. Before BPDQ proposed a variable grid, everyone drew quantization grids as evenly spaced steps. These collective blind spots are where opportunity lives.

2. *Geometrizing theory is a huge lever.* GPTQ was used by countless people for three years; it was only when the Babai geometric interpretation appeared that anyone realized "the grid itself is a variable." Translating an engineering method into geometric language very often reveals which constraints can be released.

The actionable point: the next time you pick up a field's SOTA paper, resist the urge to dive straight into the method. First ask: *what premise does this paper share with all its predecessors? Can that premise be dropped?* That question is usually the seed of the next paper.
