* One-liner
The question of "which KVs to attend to" in long-context inference is, for the first time with PrHS, flipped from "score first, then select" to "select without scoring, with a provable accuracy lower bound."

* Evolutionary Narrative: How the Problem Grew

** Chapter 0: Where the Original Sin Was Left (Vaswani 2017)

The attention mechanism of a Transformer boils down to one sentence: every time a new token is generated, it must glance at every preceding token to see who is relevant. "One glance" = one dot product. With N preceding tokens, that means N dot products.

Once sequence length grew from 2K to 128K, this started to break down. Not because the model was wrong, but because that O(N) "look at everyone" routine becomes unbearable — the KV cache (the stored keys and values for all prior tokens) can blow up GPU memory, and the attention compute itself scales linearly.

The root question of this entire lineage sits right here: *Do I really have to look at everyone?*

** Chapter 1: Prior Sparsity (Sparse Transformer / Longformer, 2019–2020)

The first generation's answer was simple: no, you don't. Tell the model upfront which positions need to attend to which — e.g., "only look at the last 512 tokens" plus "look at every 64th token." This is *fixed sparse patterns*.

The problem surfaced immediately: which token matters is *content-dependent*, not position-dependent. The key character in a document might appear in paragraph 3; a fixed window misses it entirely. Fixed patterns work tolerably for regularly distributed data but fall far short on real long documents.

This forced the next step: *sparsity patterns should follow the input, not be hardcoded.*

** Chapter 2: The First Move in Dynamic Sparsity — Select by Historical Scores (H2O, 2023)

H2O gave what looked like a natural answer: since every attention step already produces scores, high-scoring tokens are important ("heavy hitters") — keep them, discard the rest.

This shifted sparsity from "position-driven" to "score-driven," making sparse selection track semantics for the first time.

But it planted a time bomb: *scores are historical*. A token with a low score in earlier steps is not guaranteed to stay unimportant — and once you have evicted it from the cache, it can never come back. This "I didn't look at you before, so I won't look at you ever" logic is especially fragile for multi-hop reasoning.

** Chapter 3: Catching an Anomaly (StreamingLLM, 2023)

StreamingLLM was not written to criticize H2O, but it uncovered a strange fact: the first few tokens ("attention sinks") absorb a large share of attention in nearly every head — even when they carry no useful semantics.

The side effect: H2O's score-driven selection permanently retains those sink tokens, consuming real KV budget. This exposed the deeper problem — *you think scores tell you who matters; in fact they tell you the model's own quirks.*

StreamingLLM's solution is the minimalist combination of sinks + a sliding window — elegant in efficiency but with a hard ceiling on recall (long-range dependencies are simply gone).

Here a critical insight emerged: *posterior attention scores are an unreliable signal.*

** Chapter 4: Changing Granularity from Token to Page (Quest, 2024)

Quest wanted to preserve long-range dependencies (unlike StreamingLLM) while being cheaper than H2O (no need to recompute all scores every step). Its move: partition the KV into pages and represent each page with an upper-bound estimate of "the highest possible score inside this page," then select entire pages at once.

This is a compromise: scores are still computed, but as "approximate upper bounds" rather than true scores, and selection is coarse-grained at page level.

Its advance is being query-aware (re-evaluating each page per query); its cost is a new approximation — *the upper-bound estimate itself is biased*. Quest computes fast, but you cannot tell users "I guarantee the dropped portion is smaller than ε."

** Chapter 5: Sharing and Reuse, But Posterior Is Still Posterior (HShare, 2025)

HShare pushes further along Quest's path: it observes that the "important KVs" selected by different heads and different layers overlap heavily, so one selection can be *shared across heads and layers*, amortizing the cost of selection itself.

This is an elegant engineering optimization, but it changes nothing conceptually — it is still a *posterior selector*: it must first see (or approximately see) attention scores, then decide whom to pick.

The H2O → Quest → HShare evolutionary line grows ever more refined, but all three are trapped under a shared ceiling:

#+begin_src
You want to know which KVs matter -> You must score them first -> Scoring requires seeing the KVs
                                                                         ^
                                                                         |
                                                               A self-referential loop
#+end_src

Whoever breaks out of this loop enters the next era.

** Chapter 6: PrHS's Reversal — No Scoring at All (2026)

PrHS's core move is deeply counterintuitive: *select the KVs before computing any score*. It is called pre-hoc sparsity — in contrast to the post-hoc (or approximately post-hoc) approach of everyone above.

What justifies selecting without scoring? PrHS derives a result from the marginal-to-mutual-information relationship: the "information loss upper bound" from dropping a subset of KVs depends on exactly one quantity — the *dropped mass δ*. That is, as long as you can guarantee "the total mass of what I dropped does not exceed δ," the deviation in model behavior is bounded by an expression in δ.

This transforms the whole problem from "heuristic selection" to "budget allocation with provable guarantees":

| Dimension | Selector      | What it does                                        |
|-----------+---------------+-----------------------------------------------------|
| Time axis | TimeSelector  | Pre-drops sparse time segments by temporal distribution |
| Depth axis| DepthSelector | Decides per-layer retention budget by layer depth   |
| Layer axis| LayerSelector | Coordinates across layers to avoid redundant budget waste |

The three selectors are orthogonal and composable. Results:
- Retrieval overhead on GSM8K / CoQA reduced by *90%+*
- Retrieval sparsity *3× higher* than HShare at comparable or better accuracy
- LongBench average drop *<1%*
- Attention operator *9.9× speedup*, throughput 2.8×

* The Cognitive Shift That Is Really Happening

On the surface this looks like "KV selection getting more accurate and more efficient each generation."

Underneath, two things are shifting:

1. *From heuristic to provable guarantee.* The entire KV-selection sub-field previously resembled alchemy — which strategy works was discovered empirically, and nobody could tell you upfront "here is how much accuracy I will sacrifice." Once PrHS introduces δ, it is the first time this field is pulled onto the track of "algorithm + mathematical guarantee."

2. *From posterior to prior.* Every prior method assumed that "importance must be observed before it can be known." PrHS proves that under certain conditions the *upper bound* on importance does not require observation — you can use structural properties of the distribution (sparsity, shape) to pre-budget, without examining each individual token's score. This is a rather philosophical turn.

* Where This Goes Next

- *Adaptive δ*: Right now δ is user-specified; the next step will inevitably involve "the model learning how to choose δ" — making "how much accuracy am I willing to sacrifice" itself a learnable quantity.
- *Pre-hoc at training time*: Currently applied only at inference, but the same marginal-MI analysis has no reason it cannot be carried into training-time attention dropout or sparse training.
- *Co-design with hardware*: Pre-hoc selection means the selection decision can be made before KVs enter the GPU — this opens an entirely new door for *KV offloading to CPU/SSD with on-demand prefetch*, which may save more than any GPU-internal sparsity trick.

* Takeaway

The entire evolutionary history of KV sparsification is essentially the history of breaking a self-referential trap: to select KVs you must score them first; to score them you must see the KVs. Each generation made "scoring" cheaper within the loop (H2O → Quest → HShare), until PrHS simply stepped outside the loop — no scoring, just use distributional properties to provide a provable upper bound on "how much dropping these costs."

The real weight of that move is not the 9.9× speedup. It is that an alchemy-driven subfield was, for the first time, connected to the track of "algorithm + proof."
