* Opening in One Line

LLMs spitting out tokens one by one is agonizingly slow — people have spent five years trying to make them emit an entire chunk at once, and every attempt has ended in a quality collapse. *Orthrus* is the first solution on this road that genuinely achieves both speed and zero degradation. This river is the story of how that "have your cake and eat it too" problem was finally split open.

* The Real Protagonist: The Problem

"AR models have great quality but are slow; parallel models are fast but have poor quality. Can we have both?"

On the surface this looks like a physical constraint — speed and fidelity, seemingly an inevitable trade-off. This evolutionary line is, at its core, the record of people repeatedly trying to prove over five years that the trade-off is a false dilemma. Each paper answers the same question in a different way, gets knocked back by reality each time, and learns a little something new on the way down.

* Unwinding Layer by Layer

** L5: The Origin — How AR Locked Itself Into Sequential Order

Back to 2019. GPT-2 runs autoregression with a Transformer — predicting the next token strictly conditioned on all previous tokens. This is mathematically elegant: the joint probability is factored exactly as ~p(x1)*p(x2|x1)*p(x3|x1,x2)*...~, with no independence assumptions and no approximations. So AR gets the *true distribution*.

The cost: to produce N tokens, you must do N forward passes. Each forward pass re-reads all previous KV cache. The inference bottleneck is therefore not compute — it is *memory bandwidth*. GPU compute sits idle while bandwidth is saturated. This is exactly what Leviathan 2022 called the "memory-bandwidth-bound bottleneck" in Speculative Decoding.

Remember this: AR's slowness is not because it computes slowly. It is because it *reads* slowly.

** L4: The First Wave of Resistance — Porting Image Diffusion to Text

People looked at image diffusion (DDPM, 2020) with bright eyes — that thing generates an entire image in one shot, naturally parallel. So Austin et al. proposed D3PM (Discrete Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models) in 2021: could we port this framework to discrete tokens?

The idea: add noise to text (replace some tokens with [MASK]), train a network to denoise in one step. Generation runs in reverse — start from a block of [MASK], progressively unmask, with *the entire block predicted at once* at each step.

By 2024, SEDD (Lou) and MDLM (Sahoo) had refined the idea further — finding that the *Masked Diffusion* variant was cleaner in both performance and stability. At its core it is BERT-style fill-in-the-blank wrapped in an iterative denoising shell.

The breakthrough: generation is no longer sequential. The cost: the training objective uses a *conditional independence assumption* — when predicting token y_k, the model does not see the already-revealed y_{k-1}. Mathematically, this tears *a crack* between the learned distribution and the true AR distribution.

** L3: The Scaling Attempt — LLaDA and Dream

By 2025, researchers had scaled diffusion language models to the 7B range — LLaDA and Dream. Trained from scratch on 580B tokens, the explicit goal was to show that DLMs could go head-to-head with AR.

Result: parallel generation, impressive speed. But on reasoning tasks like GSM8K and MATH-500, DLMs *systematically trail AR models at the same scale*. Dream-7B scores 39.6 on MATH-500; contemporary AR models clear 80 with ease.

Why the gap? Two reasons:
1. *The cost of conditional independence* — on long sequences, errors accumulate step by step, a phenomenon called "conditional drift."
2. *Astronomical training cost* — training from scratch on 580B tokens means effectively running a second pretraining. Out of reach for most organizations.

At this point people started asking: why start from scratch? AR models already exist, fully trained. Can't we just "convert" them into DLMs?

** L2: AR → Diffusion Adaptation — Thinking You've Found a Shortcut

In the second half of 2025, Fast-dLLM-v2 (Wu) and SDAR (Cheng) took this path. The idea: take an already-trained AR model like Qwen or LLaMA and fine-tune it with a second stage of *Block-wise Masked Diffusion* training, teaching it to emit an entire block at once.

No training from scratch — it looked attractive. Continuing training for roughly 50B tokens was enough to get things running. But the problems refused to leave:

- *Fast-dLLM-v2 drops 11 points on MATH-500 compared to its AR backbone.* The paper itself says: "adapted models lose the ability to recover the exact predictive distribution of the original baseline."
- To claw quality back, inference requires *multiple iterative refinement passes*. The theoretical parallel speedup gets consumed by these refinement steps; real-world measured speedup lands at 1–2x — better than nothing, but barely.
- Touching the backbone weights is touching the backbone weights. The "strict causality" advantage of AR is gone.

This lesson is critical: *the moment you touch AR's weights, the distribution drifts.* No matter how carefully you train.

Reading this, you have probably already thought of the next move on the authors' behalf — *what if we just don't touch the weights?*

** L1: Orthrus — This Time They Finally Leave AR Alone

Orthrus in one sentence: *freeze the original AR model, attach a diffusion head alongside it, and let both share the same KV cache.*

How it works in practice:

1. *Dual-head shared KV cache* — inside each Transformer layer, alongside the original attention (AR head), a second attention (diffusion head) is added. Both heads share the same K and V. During prefill, only the AR head builds the cache; during generation, the diffusion head produces an entire block of K candidate tokens in one shot.
2. *Only the diffusion head is trained; AR is completely frozen* — only ~16%~ of the total parameters are updated, trained on ~<1B~ tokens, finishing in 24 hours on a single 8×H200 node.
3. *Use AR itself to verify* — after the diffusion head produces K candidate tokens, they are fed back through the AR head in a single forward pass. The AR head computes the correct distribution at every position simultaneously, and a token-by-token comparison is made — tokens matching the greedy AR prediction are accepted; at the first divergence, the sequence is truncated and AR's own answer is substituted.

This verification mechanism is called *intra-model consensus*. Philosophically it is identical to Speculative Decoding — *let the fast one speculate, let the accurate one arbitrate* — but Speculative Decoding uses two independent models (draft + target), while Orthrus uses *two heads of the same model*.

Results: 5.39× average speedup on Qwen3-8B, MATH-500 score of 96.0 (perfectly matching the AR backbone), beating the adaptation-based methods comprehensively.

And because AR is frozen — *Orthrus inherits the exact inference quality of the backbone. A stronger backbone means a stronger Orthrus.* This is highly valuable in engineering.

** Side Branch Comparison: Why Not Just Use Speculative Decoding?

Speculative Decoding (Leviathan 2022) has almost the same idea as Orthrus — a fast model drafts, an accurate model verifies. EAGLE-3 (Li 2025) is current SoTA.

But there is a hard constraint: *the draft model and target model are separate*, meaning two sets of KV caches and two sets of weights. For large models, memory usage roughly doubles. Orthrus's pitch is precisely that it folds the "draft" into the same model's shared KV cache, adding ~O(1)~ memory overhead.

* What Is Really Happening Beneath This Line

On the surface, this looks like a technical iteration in "speeding up LLM inference." Look deeper, and this line is about something more subtle — *a shift in how people think about modifying something that is already very good*.

- L4–L3 worldview: *tear it down and rebuild*. AR won't do, so switch to a diffusion paradigm and train from scratch.
- L2 worldview: *modify and adapt*. AR is good, but tweak its weights just enough to give it parallel capability.
- L1 worldview: *parasitism without invasion*. Touch not a single AR parameter; instead *attach* new capabilities onto it, and use AR itself as the guarantor.

This final paradigm is fascinating — it treats the AR model as an *inviolable oracle*. You may borrow its representations (KV cache) and borrow its judgment (verifier), but you may not touch a single weight. This "trust and parasitize" design philosophy is the same thing as LoRA, Adapters, and Speculative Decoding — *acknowledging that the base model itself is the answer, and your job is to build scaffolding around it, not to demolish the building*.

Zooming out, this reflects a common direction across large-model engineering over the past three years: a shift from "training = modifying weights" to "training = adding capability without touching weights." Orthrus brings this to a clean form on the parallel decoding front.

* Where Does This Go Next

1. *Multi-step parallel generation* — Orthrus currently runs the diffusion head for only one denoising step before handing off to the verifier. Running multiple steps (as traditional DLMs do) would yield higher-quality drafts and higher acceptance rates, at the cost of more forward passes per cycle. This is an obvious design knob to turn.
2. *Cross-modal transfer* — the same "freeze backbone + add parallel head" idea could be applied to AR models in vision and speech (e.g., VAR, AudioLM). Nothing in Orthrus's architecture is text-specific.
3. *Fusion with Speculative Decoding* — Orthrus currently uses the same model to verify, but one could in principle nest an additional smaller draft head, creating a three-stage pipeline.

The biggest open question: *is 5.39× the ceiling for this architectural line?* If so, the next wave of acceleration requires a different route — perhaps MoE-style sparse activation, or non-autoregressive Transformers at the architecture level.

* The Core

#+begin_quote
*True engineering elegance is accepting that you cannot make better judgments than the base model — and so letting the base model be the judge, while you focus solely on guessing faster.*
#+end_quote

This five-year line on parallel decoding is, at its heart, a cognitive shift from "wanting to replace AR" to "wanting to serve AR well."
