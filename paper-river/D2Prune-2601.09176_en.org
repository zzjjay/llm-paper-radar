* The River of Problems

A 70-billion-parameter model has a large fraction of weights that are, in some sense, expendable. The question is: which ones can actually be removed without breaking the model's ability to speak coherently?

That sounds like a narrow problem of picking a pruning threshold. But it has driven researchers for 35 years — from small neural networks in 1989 all the way to LLaMA in 2026. Each generation thought it had found the answer; the next generation pointed out that the ruler used to measure "important vs. unimportant" was simply the wrong ruler.

Why is it so hard? Because the cost of deleting a single weight cannot be computed exactly — only approximated. What you assume, and what you ignore, inside that approximation determines whether the pruned model still works. From OBD using second-order derivatives, to OBS adding a weight-update correction, to SparseGPT porting that spirit to LLMs, to Wanda dropping weight updates entirely — each generation has searched for a cheaper and more accurate ruler. D²Prune argues: every prior method treated the input activations as a fixed constant during the derivation. That assumption is itself wrong.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1989 | OBD: Scoring Every Weight with Its Second Derivative

*** What Problem It Saw

Neural networks in that era were already prone to overfitting. An intuitive fix was to prune unimportant weights, forcing the network to retain only necessary connections and thereby generalize better.

But how do you define "important"? The most naive approach is to look at the absolute value of a weight — small means removable. LeCun et al. argued this has little theoretical justification: a weight with a tiny absolute value might sit in a position where the output is extremely sensitive to it, making its removal catastrophic; a weight with a large absolute value might be completely inconsequential.

*** Its Solution

Replace the definition: a weight is "important" proportionally to how much the loss rises when it is set to zero. A large rise means important; a small rise means removable.

But you cannot afford to actually test every weight. LeCun used a mathematically natural approximation: Taylor-expand the loss around the current weights. At convergence the first-order term vanishes (gradient is zero), so the dominant term is second-order — the Hessian matrix. Simplify further by keeping only the diagonal of the Hessian (assuming weights do not interact), and you get an independent importance score for each weight.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Dug)

It turned a brute-force "try-everything" problem into an analytic "evaluate a formula" problem — the first principled pruning criterion.

But it dug two pits:
1. It assumed no interaction between weights (the diagonal assumption), which is not true in practice.
2. It only removed, never compensated. After removing a single weight, things are fine; after removing thousands, errors spiral out of control.

** 1993 | OBS: Remove One, Immediately Adjust the Others to Compensate

*** What Problem It Saw

After OBD removes a weight, the remaining weights stay unchanged. But once a connection is severed, the optimal configuration of the whole network changes — the remaining weights should shift to minimize the resulting loss increase.

Hassibi and Stork argued: if you don't make that adjustment, you are leaving free compensation on the table.

*** Its Solution

Replace the diagonal Hessian approximation with the full Hessian inverse. When one weight is removed, this lets you simultaneously compute how every other weight should be nudged to recover the loss.

Analogy: OBD lays off one team member and walks away. OBS lays off the same member, then holds a meeting and redistributes the work among the remaining team so that overall output drops as little as possible.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Dug)

The compensation mechanism allowed much higher pruning ratios. At 50% sparsity, OBS loses far less accuracy than OBD.

The new pit: the Hessian inverse. For a network with N parameters, the Hessian is N×N, and inverting it costs O(N³). Small 1990s networks could handle this; networks with millions of parameters could not. A beautiful formula, but unaffordable.

** 2015 | Magnitude Pruning: Forget the Math — Just Prune the Small Weights

*** What Problem It Saw

By 2015, neural networks had tens to hundreds of millions of parameters. Computing the full Hessian inverse was out of the question — you couldn't even store it. The diagonal OBD approximation was slightly more tractable but still strained large models.

Han et al. asked simply: do we really need such a sophisticated criterion?

*** Its Solution

Return to the most primitive idea: prune weights with the smallest absolute values. Then fine-tune on the remaining training data so the network can learn to recover on its own.

The full loop: train → prune small weights → retrain → prune again → retrain again.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Dug)

Large networks genuinely contain vast numbers of near-zero weights whose removal barely affects performance. On AlexNet and VGG, 90% of weights could be pruned with almost no accuracy drop. Han et al.'s "Deep Compression" became the foundation of a long line of model-compression work.

Two new pits:
1. It requires retraining. For LLMs, retraining is prohibitively expensive.
2. On LLMs, magnitude is an inaccurate ruler — the importance of a weight clearly depends on what inputs it receives, and looking only at the weight itself discards half the information.

** 2023 | SparseGPT: Bring OBS Back, but Only Within Each Layer

*** What Problem It Saw

Magnitude accuracy degrades badly on large models; full-model OBS is impossible. The Hessian of an entire LLM is completely out of reach.

But Frantar and Alistarh noticed something: you don't need the Hessian of the whole model. Prune one layer at a time, running a local OBS within each layer, and that's enough. Each layer has only thousands of weights, making its Hessian tractable.

*** Its Solution

Layer-wise pruning. For each layer, run a small calibration dataset (a few hundred samples) through a forward pass to record input activations. Then apply the OBS formula: each time a weight is removed, adjust the remaining weights in that layer to compensate — the goal is to keep the layer's output as close as possible to the original.

This converts the global problem into a collection of small local problems, each solvable.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Dug)

For the first time, OBS-style "remove one, fix the others" ran on LLMs. At 50% sparsity on OPT and LLaMA, perplexity barely moved.

New pits:
1. It still requires computing the Hessian inverse — even if only layer-wise, that is not cheap on a 70B model.
2. It assumes the calibration-data activation distribution equals the test-time distribution. Calibrate on C4, test on WikiText, and the activations already diverge — the compensation computed on C4 may not carry over.
3. It treats all linear layers uniformly, including the attention Q/K/V projections. Attention scores are naturally long-tailed (a few tokens capture most of the attention), but the L2 reconstruction loss used for weight updates is insensitive to that tail — the updates subtly flatten the distribution and quietly degrade model behavior.

** 2023 | Wanda: Drop the Hessian Entirely — One Multiplication Does It

*** What Problem It Saw

SparseGPT was still heavy: it needed the Hessian inverse and weight updates. Sun et al. asked: can we skip weight updates altogether?

The key observation was: whether a weight matters depends critically on the magnitude of the inputs it receives. A large weight paired with near-zero activations contributes nothing to the output and can be pruned. A moderate weight paired with consistently large activations is critical.

*** Its Solution

Importance score: =|W_ij| × ||X_j||_2=. Multiply the absolute weight value by the norm of the corresponding input activation. Rank by this score; prune the smallest.

No Hessian, no weight updates — just one multiplication.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Dug)

Absurdly simple, yet it matched or slightly outperformed SparseGPT on LLaMA. The paper immediately became the standard baseline.

New pits:
1. =|W|·||X||= is an intuition-assembled formula; the authors acknowledge it measures something closer to "output magnitude" than "true pruning error."
2. No weight updates means small errors in Q/K/V layers accumulate across subsequent layers.
3. The same fixed-activation assumption persists.

** 2024 | Pruner-Zero: If the Formula Is Hand-Crafted, Let the Machine Search for It

*** What Problem It Saw

Wanda's =|W|·||X||= was invented by human intuition. Perhaps a better formula exists?

Dong et al. thought: rather than guessing, treat the importance formula as a searchable expression space and let an evolutionary algorithm find it.

*** Its Solution

Define an expression space: variables are W, X, gradients, etc.; operators are +, ×, norms, absolute values, and so on. Use genetic programming to search for the optimal combination.

The search did find formulas that outperform Wanda.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Dug)

It automated the search for importance criteria, acknowledging that human intuition need not discover the best form.

New pits:
1. The discovered formulas are hard to interpret — it's unclear why one combination beats another.
2. Still computationally demanding (the paper did not scale to 70B models).
3. *Most fundamentally*: every method in this lineage, including Pruner-Zero, only manipulates the weight variable W. The activation side has been treated as a fixed input — an assumption that has gone untouched for all 35 years from OBD to now.

** 2026 | D²Prune: Honestly Putting "Activations Change" Into the Formula

*** What Problem It Saw

D²Prune targets the one piece of bedrock that nobody had ever overturned in 35 years: the *constant activation assumption*.

From OBS to SparseGPT to Wanda, every method that derives "the cost of removing a weight" silently assumes that the input activation X is a fixed value taken from calibration data. But in real LLM deployment, activations vary wildly — across datasets, across layer depths, across contexts. Calibrate on C4, test on WikiText, and the input distributions are already different. Any cost estimate derived under "X is fixed" is, by construction, wrong for the test distribution.

The second observation concerns attention. The output of Q/K/V projections follows a long-tailed distribution — a handful of tokens capture the majority of attention weight. SparseGPT-style methods optimize an L2 reconstruction loss when updating Q/K/V weights, and L2 is blind to tail shape. After the update, the attention distribution gets "averaged out," and model behavior subtly degrades.

*** Its Solution

Two components:

1. *Dual Taylor expansion*: Apply Taylor expansion not only to W but also to X. The cost of removing a weight now accounts simultaneously for the perturbation in the weight, the perturbation in the activation, and their cross terms. When the calibration and test activation distributions differ, the formula contains terms that reflect that discrepancy.

2. *Attention-aware update*: Treat attention layers (Q/K/V) separately. The loss includes not just L2 reconstruction but also the KL divergence between the attention distributions before and after pruning. This writes the long-tail shape explicitly into the optimization objective, so the weight update preserves the long tail instead of flattening it.

*** Why It Worked (and What Pit It Dug)

For the first time, "activation distribution can shift" is promoted from a silently assumed constant into an explicitly modeled variable. This is the first expansion of the variable set — rather than a change of formula form — since OBD.

The attention-KL trick is also conceptually tight: since you know attention is long-tailed, make the objective care about distributional shape rather than element-wise differences.

Anticipated pits (not explicitly stated in the paper):
1. The dual Taylor cross terms add computational overhead beyond SparseGPT.
2. It remains a layer-local method; cross-layer distribution shift is not addressed.
3. The KL alignment primarily benefits self-attention layers; it offers little improvement for MLP layers.

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026, D²Prune was published only a few months ago (AAAI 2026) and has not yet attracted explicit follow-up critiques. But the directions it opened suggest several doors for future work:

1. If activation distribution is the problem, someone will ask: can we use *test-time activations* directly during pruning? This leads toward *test-time / online pruning*.
2. D²Prune uses KL divergence to protect the long-tailed attention distribution, but successors may try Wasserstein distance or direct structural constraints on attention patterns.
3. Dual Taylor extends the variable set from "W only" to "W and X." The natural next step is "W, X, and context (KV cache)." This begins to converge with KV compression and sparse-attention directions at inference time.

* Insights

Reading the whole lineage, you notice something counterintuitive: 35 years of pruning research have advanced not primarily by finding better formulas, but by *acknowledging that more and more things are actually variables*.

- OBD treated the loss increment as a variable (previously people only looked at weight magnitude)
- OBS treated the adjustment of remaining weights as a variable
- SparseGPT treated the layer-wise approximation as a variable
- D²Prune treats the activation distribution as a variable

The "new pit" discovered by each generation is, at its core, something the previous generation had silently held constant. So the real question in pruning is not "which weight is unimportant," but "how many things are we secretly treating as constants while we prune?"

A second insight: methodological complexity does not grow monotonically. OBS uses the full Hessian inverse — very heavy. Magnitude retreats to the simplest possible criterion. SparseGPT returns to the Hessian but only layer-locally. Wanda retreats again to a single multiplication. D²Prune adds Taylor terms back. The field has oscillated repeatedly on the precision-vs-speed axis, and each swing happened because someone discovered that the current cheap approach was missing a critical variable.

Where is the field most likely to go next? I would bet on two directions:
1. *End-to-end modeling of activation drift* — D²Prune uses a first-order Taylor term to approximate activation perturbation; future work will likely learn a generative model of the activation distribution directly.
2. *Unified pruning and quantization* — D²Prune already cites SmoothQuant and OmniQuant. Both communities have run into the same wall around activation distributions. A unified framework seems natural.

* Takeaways

For researchers:

1. *Look for things in old pits that have been silently assumed constant.* In a mature field, the most valuable breakthroughs often aren't "I have a better algorithm" but rather "you all assumed X was fixed — it isn't." D²Prune's core contribution fits in one sentence: activations are not constants. That single sentence outweighs dozens of new formulas.

2. *Return to first principles.* OBD's formula has been cited for 35 years because it derives from the mathematical fact of Taylor expansion. Wanda's formula is elegant but assembled by hand — which is precisely why Pruner-Zero's automated search could surpass it. Work grounded in fundamentals ages better.

3. *Don't worship upward complexity.* The most widely adopted methods in this lineage (Magnitude, Wanda) are the simplest ones. A more complex method only wins if it *models a variable that others ignored* — not merely because it has a more elaborate formula.

Practical takeaway: the next time you read a pruning or compression paper, don't be dazzled by the algorithm's sophistication. Flip to its "Assumptions" section first and ask what it is holding constant. Those constants are usually the gold mine for the paper that comes next.
