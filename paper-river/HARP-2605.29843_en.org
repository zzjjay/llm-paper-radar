* The River of the Problem

In one sentence: *How do you compress a model with tens of billions of parameters down to 2–4 bits per weight while barely losing accuracy?*

Where is the difficulty? The numerical values inside a large model are not uniformly distributed. The vast majority of weights and activations cluster near zero, yet every so often a few unusually large "outliers" shoot up. Quantization is fundamentally about stuffing continuous values into a small number of bins — 4-bit gives you only 16 bins, 2-bit gives you only 4. If your data contains a single value of 100 surrounded by a crowd of 0.1s, then to accommodate that 100 you must widen each bin dramatically, and all those 0.1s collapse into the same bin, losing their information entirely.

The central tension of this entire river is therefore: *outliers destroy quantization*. Generation after generation of researchers has fought against them. The most elegant thread of insight is: rather than "suppressing" outliers, *change the coordinate system* — rotate the data so that outlier energy is spread across all dimensions, no single dimension sticks out, and quantization becomes easy.

This river traces how that "rotation" evolved from a fixed, hard-coded matrix into something that can be *learned* — adapted to each layer, each calibration dataset, and each backend quantizer.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022 | GPTQ (and its ancestors OBS/OBQ): Quantization is not independent — errors must be mutually compensated

*** What problem did it see?

The most naïve quantization is "round to nearest" — each weight is individually stuffed into its closest bin. The trouble is that weights are correlated: the error you introduce by rounding weight A could in principle be cancelled by a small adjustment to weight B, but rounding them independently throws that opportunity away. At large-model scale, independently accumulated rounding errors are fatal.

*** Its solution

It borrowed an old 1990s pruning idea — Optimal Brain Surgeon. The core is the Hessian matrix (second-order derivatives), which precisely describes how weights influence one another. OBQ quantizes one weight at a time and immediately uses the Hessian to compute how the remaining unquantized weights should be adjusted to compensate for that round's error. GPTQ scaled this up to tens-of-billions of parameters — through a fixed quantization order, lazy batch updates, and Cholesky decomposition for numerical stability — compressing what would have taken six months of compute into a few hours.

*** Why it works (and where the next pitfall lies)

It works because it acknowledges that weights interact, using second-order information for global error compensation rather than local greedy rounding. But it buries a trap: *it never touches the coordinate system of the data*. If a particular direction is naturally dominated by huge outliers, no amount of clever compensation can save it — the bins still have to accommodate that outlier. The next paper targeted exactly this coordinate-system issue.

** 2023 | QuIP: Instead of compensating for outliers, scramble the coordinate system first

*** What problem did it see?

GPTQ accepted outliers as a given and worked hard to compensate for them. QuIP asked a different question: *why do outliers have to live on those coordinate axes?* It identified that quantization quality depends on the "incoherence" between the weight matrix and the Hessian — informally, whether the important directions happen to align with coordinate axes and whether any single dimension is especially prominent. If the data can be made uniform across all dimensions with no spiky peaks, quantization becomes much easier.

*** Its solution

Multiply the weights by a random orthogonal matrix before quantization, then multiply back afterward (the inverse of an orthogonal matrix is its transpose, so the full-precision result is unchanged). This rotation spreads the energy that was concentrated in a few directions across all dimensions — that lone value of 100 becomes a collection of medium-sized values, none of them standing out. This is "incoherence processing," a principled form of outlier suppression. QuIP also provided the first theoretical guarantees for LLM-scale quantization algorithms and delivered the first practical 2-bit quantization.

*** Why it works (and where the next pitfall lies)

It works because rotation is "free" — full-precision computation is unchanged, but the quantization landscape becomes friendly. This is the turning point of the entire river: from "fighting outliers" to "changing coordinates to eliminate them." The pitfall: QuIP uses a Kronecker product of random orthogonal matrices, which costs O(d^2), is slow, and produces unstable results whose theoretical bounds are not particularly clean.

** 2024 | QuIP#: Replace random rotation with Hadamard — faster and more principled

*** What problem did it see?

QuIP's random orthogonal matrices were expensive and unstable. QuIP# identified a ready-made "perfect rotation" — the Hadamard transform. Its matrix entries are only +1/−1, it can be computed in O(d log d) time using a fast algorithm (analogous to FFT), and it achieves the optimal worst-case incoherence μ = 1/√n.

*** Its solution

Replace the random orthogonal matrix with the Randomized Hadamard Transform (RHT): randomly flip the sign of each dimension, then apply the Hadamard transform. Fast, and theoretically elegant. Paired with lattice codebooks — quantizing several weights jointly onto a carefully designed lattice — compression is pushed even further. Below 3 bits, QuIP# remained the state of the art for a long time. Nearly every subsequent method reuses its fast Hadamard kernel.

*** Why it works (and where the next pitfall lies)

The Hadamard is mathematically a beautiful "universal rotation" that gives good worst-case guarantees for any distribution. But "universal" is also its weakness — *it is fixed and hard-coded, the same for everyone*. It knows nothing about the data distribution in your specific layer, your calibration set, or which quantizer your backend uses. It guarantees the worst case but not the optimum for your specific case. And QuIP# only rotates weights.

** 2024 | QuaRot: Rotation should govern the entire network, not just the weights

*** What problem did it see?

QuIP# only rotated weights. But during inference the real bottleneck is often activations and KV cache — both are riddled with outliers, and because they are generated dynamically at runtime they are even harder to handle. Quantizing only weights while leaving activations in FP16 saves neither compute nor bandwidth.

*** Its solution

Exploit "computational invariance": y = Wx = (WQ^T)(Qx) holds for any orthogonal Q. QuaRot spreads Hadamard rotation insertion points across the entire Transformer compute graph — four standard insertion points R1–R4 covering the QKV projection inputs, attention outputs, Q/K, and MLP. Some rotations can be fused into weights offline; others must be applied online. The result: weights, activations, and KV cache can all be compressed to 4-bit, making QuaRot the first complete 4-bit inference pipeline.

*** Why it works (and where the next pitfall lies)

It works by carrying the "change coordinate system" idea all the way from weights through the entire data flow. But QuaRot itself observed that switching between random orthogonal and random Hadamard could change perplexity by 1.35 — meaning *the choice of rotation is not arbitrary*. Yet it still used a fixed/random Hadamard. Given that the choice matters so much, why not search for the best one?

** 2024 | SpinQuant: Since the choice of rotation matters so much, learn it

*** What problem did it see?

QuaRot had already hinted that the choice of rotation matters. SpinQuant nailed the point conclusively: it measured that different random rotations could differ by *13 points* in downstream task accuracy. A fixed Hadamard is merely "decent on average," far from optimal. Given this, why use a random or fixed rotation instead of directly learning the optimal one?

*** Its solution

Treat the rotation matrix as a trainable parameter and optimize it on the Stiefel manifold (the space of all orthogonal matrices) using Cayley SGD, directly minimizing the end loss of the quantized network. The learned rotations have very low variance and reliably outperform random or fixed Hadamard. R3/R4 are retained as online Hadamard processors for KV cache and FFN.

*** Why it works (and where the next pitfall lies)

It works because it is the first method to genuinely *learn* the rotation, capturing the full 13-point variance dividend. But it buries two traps. First, it learns *dense large matrices*, costing O(n^2) storage and expensive optimization. Second — more critically — SpinQuant's learned rotations are model-level objects welded into the Transformer compute graph; they are *not plug-and-play preprocessing modules* and cannot be directly dropped into a QuIP#-style "two-sided Hadamard preprocessing" pipeline as a drop-in replacement.

** 2025 | ButterflyQuant: Use butterfly structure to make "learning rotations" fast and compact

*** What problem did it see?

SpinQuant proved that learning rotations is valuable, but dense matrices are too expensive and too large. On the other hand, Hadamard's ±1 entries are discrete and non-differentiable — gradients cannot flow through them. Is there something that is both fast and guaranteed orthogonal (like Hadamard) *and* learnable and adaptive (like SpinQuant)?

*** Its solution

Butterfly transforms. A large rotation is decomposed into successive layers of sparse small rotations (just like the butterfly structure of FFT), each parameterized by a continuous Givens angle. Continuous angles are differentiable, so gradients can flow; the butterfly structure guarantees O(n log n) complexity and only (n log n)/2 parameters; Givens rotations are orthogonal by construction. A "uniformity regularizer" encourages the transformed activation distribution to be more uniform and easier to quantize. Different layers learn different rotations, achieving layer-level adaptivity. At 2-bit on LLaMA-2-7B, perplexity reaches 15.4, far better than QuaRot's 22.1.

*** Why it works (and where the next pitfall lies)

It works by bridging "fast/orthogonal" and "learnable/adaptive" through a structured parameterization that obtains both properties simultaneously. But it uses *identity initialization* (starting from the identity matrix and learning from scratch) rather than starting from the RHT baseline that has been validated as the strongest starting point; it does not directly support non-power-of-two dimensions; and its learning objective is not explicitly aligned with *the block quantizer used by the backend at deployment time*. These three gaps are exactly what HARP addresses.

** 2026 | HARP (the target paper): Initialize to RHT, then learn a quantizer-aligned structured refinement

*** What problem did it see?

HARP distills all the preceding gaps into three simultaneously required properties:
1. *Adaptive* — able to fit each layer, each backend, and each calibration dataset (fixed Hadamard cannot do this);
2. *Faithful* — must be an exact orthogonal change of basis, leaving full-precision results unchanged (non-orthogonal data-aware transforms such as WUSH cannot guarantee this; they alter the full-precision computation);
3. *Efficient* — maintain Hadamard-like O(d log d) cost (SpinQuant's dense matrices cannot).
And it must act as a *plug-and-play* two-sided preprocessing module — precisely what SpinQuant's model-level rotations are not.

*** Its solution

A learnable, structured two-sided orthogonal processor that directly replaces the fixed Hadamard mixture while preserving exact full-precision equivalence. Key design decisions:

- *Staged butterfly factors*: each rotation is written as a product of several sparse "stride stages"; each block core has the form Q(θ)·G — where G is a fixed base mixer (Sylvester-Hadamard for powers of two, QR decomposition as fallback otherwise) and Q is learnable (Givens parameterization for radix-2, Cayley map for larger blocks).
- *Initialize to RHT*: at parameter Θ=0, HARP is *exactly equal to the RHT processor* (up to a fixed permutation). In other words, it does not learn from scratch; it starts from the "validated strongest baseline" and learns a structured orthogonal refinement around it. This makes it *natively compatible with all existing Hadamard pipelines*.
- *Mixed-Radix scheduling + Kronecker fallback*: supports non-power-of-two dimensions (which ButterflyQuant does not handle directly).
- *Aligned with the backend quantizer*: the fitting objective uses a diagonally Hessian-weighted reconstruction proxy plus an "off-block Hessian energy regularizer" that aligns curvature with the block structure of the backend quantizer.

Results: improved perplexity and zero-shot accuracy at 2–4 bits across 1B–70B models; efficiency reaches 128 tok/s (vs. 61 tok/s for FP16).

*** Why it works (and where the next pitfall lies)

The deep reason it works is something HARP's own authors honestly acknowledge: the method *does not improve the classical Hessian incoherence score*. Its gains do not come from being better on every classical incoherence metric, but from *aligning the rotation with the specific quantizer actually being used*. In other words — at this point in the river, the objective has changed: no longer to pursue an abstract "perfect rotation" good for all quantizers, but to tailor a rotation specifically for "this layer + this backend."

The remaining open tension (also honestly noted): once the optimization objective is aligned with a specific quantizer, the classical incoherence-theory guarantees are no longer what is being optimized. This means the theoretical "worst-case guarantee" and the empirical "optimum for this case" begin to diverge — future work will need new theoretical frameworks to explain why "quantizer-aligned rotation" works.

* Frontier Extensions

As of June 2026, HARP is the latest advance on the "adaptive rotation quantization" line. Its position is clear: it braids together QuIP#'s RHT (strong baseline, plug-and-play, efficient), SpinQuant's "learn the rotation" (adaptive), and ButterflyQuant's "structured and differentiable" (compact), and adds one dimension none of them emphasized — *alignment with the backend quantizer*.

Likely directions ahead:
- Extend the "quantizer-aligned rotation" idea to more complex backends (e.g., QTIP's trellis codebooks, vector quantization);
- Build a new theoretical framework for "quantizer-aligned rotation" that explains why it remains effective after decoupling from classical incoherence;
- Joint optimization with online rotations for activations/KV cache (HARP primarily addresses the weight-side preprocessing replacement).

* Insights

Reading the whole river, the surface story is "rotation goes from fixed to learnable," but at a deeper level *three cognitive shifts* are taking place:

1. *From "fighting" to "reframing."* In the GPTQ era, outliers were treated as a given fact of life, to be compensated for as best one could. QuIP's revolution was realizing that outliers are an artifact of the coordinate system — change the coordinate system and they vanish. This is a leap from "playing chess on a given board" to "replacing the board."

2. *From "universally optimal" to "specifically optimal."* Hadamard is the mathematically beautiful "universal solution," pursuing worst-case guarantees across all distributions. After SpinQuant, the whole river begins abandoning universality in favor of "tailored to your data and your layer." HARP pushes this to the extreme — even *which quantizer* you are using gets factored in. HARP's honest admission that it does not improve the classical incoherence score is precisely the hallmark of this shift: *the evaluation criterion has moved from "abstract metric" to "end-to-end empirical effectiveness."*

3. *From "free optimization" to "optimization with strong prior."* SpinQuant searches freely across the entire manifold; ButterflyQuant learns from the identity initialization. HARP takes a step back — *it initializes exactly at RHT, the empirically strongest known point*, and only learns a refinement around it. This is a deep piece of engineering wisdom: do not learn from scratch something for which you already know a good starting region. Use the known strong prior as your starting point, and let learning be responsible only for the "extra polish."

Where is the next step most likely to go? My bet is "co-design": rotation, codebook, and hardware backend will no longer be optimized separately but jointly end-to-end. HARP's "quantizer alignment" has already tied rotation to the backend; the natural next step is to pull hardware constraints into the same optimization objective.

* Lessons

This river offers three directly actionable lessons for how to do research:

1. *The strongest innovations often redefine where the problem lives.* The biggest leap in this entire river is not any particular better algorithm, but QuIP's reframing of "outliers are a coordinate-system problem." When you have been stuck on a problem for a long time, first ask: *am I assuming something I don't have to assume?* Replace "fight X" with "eliminate the conditions that produce X."

2. *Use the strongest baseline as your initialization, not as your adversary.* HARP did not start from scratch to prove itself better than RHT; instead it arranged for Θ=0 to *equal* RHT and then learned the increment. When doing improvement-type work, consider making "my method degenerates to the current SOTA at initialization" a hard design constraint — you inherit all of SOTA's guarantees for free, you are only responsible for the increment, and the paper is naturally easy to explain and easy to deploy.

3. *Honestly reporting what you did not improve actually strengthens your case.* HARP explicitly states that it does not improve the classical incoherence score and precisely attributes its gains to "quantizer alignment." When your method works but the mechanism does not fit the mainstream explanation, *do not force-fit the old framework* — honestly saying "the classical metric didn't move; the benefit comes from somewhere else" both pinpoints your true contribution and leaves a clearly marked theoretical gap for future work.
