* The Problem River

When a large model must handle an ultra-long context of 128K tokens, GPU memory
simply cannot hold it all — how do you make the model remember the full text
without running out of VRAM?

The difficulty is that you need both speed and accuracy simultaneously. Speed
means you cannot let memory-saving crush inference throughput; accuracy means
that whatever content gets compressed, it can still be retrieved whenever it is
needed later. Every generation of solutions wrestles with three things: what to
drop, how to drop it, and whether what was dropped can ever be recovered. Each
step forward reveals a new cost. That is why, from 2023 to 2026, this problem
has drawn wave after wave of researchers.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2023 | StreamingLLM: Discovering "Attention Sinks" to Enable Infinite Generation

*** What Problem It Saw

In LLM inference, every newly generated token requires storing its key/value
vectors in a cache. The longer the sequence, the larger the cache — eventually
exhausting GPU memory. A natural idea: keep only the most recent N tokens in
cache, like a sliding window. Xiao et al. found that once this window slides
past the maximum sequence length seen during training, the model's perplexity
collapses abruptly — because the positional encodings jump to states the model
has never encountered, and it no longer knows how to compute attention.

*** Its Solution

They carefully scanned the attention weight distributions across all layers and
heads and noticed something strange: the very first few tokens of a sequence —
even semantically empty ones like periods, commas, or the BOS symbol — attract
extremely high attention scores in almost every layer and head. This is not
because those tokens are important; rather, softmax must force all scores to sum
to 1 — when no token stands out, attention "pours" onto these globally visible
early tokens, like water flowing into a depression. Xiao et al. called these
tokens *attention sinks*.

The fix is direct: permanently pin the KV of the first 4 sink tokens, and add
a sliding window to retain the most recent tokens. Softmax has somewhere to
drain attention, positional encodings no longer jump, and the model can generate
indefinitely — 22× faster than recomputing from scratch.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Digs a New Hole)

It solves the numerical stability problem of softmax and makes arbitrarily long
generation feasible. But the cost is steep: everything in the middle — the body
of a document, the core context of a conversation — is discarded wholesale,
regardless of importance. This is pure position-based eviction with no regard
for content. Whether eviction could instead be guided by each token's
informational value is the question the next paper had to answer.

** 2023 | H2O: Choose What to Keep by Content — Heavy Hitters First

*** What Problem It Saw

StreamingLLM discards middle tokens without asking why. But attention scores
themselves hint at which tokens matter: tokens that are frequently attended to
will likely be attended to again in future decoding steps. Zhang et al.
examined real attention matrices and found a near-power-law distribution — about
20% of tokens accumulate more than 80% of total attention scores. They called
these tokens *Heavy Hitters (H2)*.

*** Its Solution

They framed KV eviction as a dynamic submodular optimization problem: at each
decoding step, retain two categories of token KV — a recent local window (for
local coherence) and the top-H2 tokens by cumulative attention score (for
global relevance). The H2 set is re-evaluated after every decoding step,
allowing dynamic addition and removal. Theoretically this is provably the best
approximation to attention quality. Empirically, compared with StreamingLLM, it
achieves significant accuracy gains on long-document tasks while reducing KV
cache by 5–10×.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Digs a New Hole)

Content-adaptive retention is genuinely better than fixed-position retention.
But there is one glaring blind spot: H2O only compresses the KV cache for the
*decode* phase and never touches the *prompt* KV cache. In practice, user
prompts routinely run to tens of thousands of tokens, far longer than the
generated response — the true memory bottleneck goes completely untouched.

** 2024 | SnapKV: Compress the Prompt Before Generation Begins

*** What Problem It Saw

Li et al. pointed out that in real-world usage, prompts routinely contain
thousands to tens of thousands of tokens while generated responses are only a
few hundred — the compression priority is entirely in the prefill stage, not
decoding. H2O and StreamingLLM never addressed this.

*** Its Solution

SnapKV compresses the prompt's KV cache *before* generation starts. How to know
which prompt tokens matter? Use the query vectors from the final short segment
of the prompt — the *observation window* (typically the last 64 tokens) — to
score the entire prompt prefix via attention, then aggregate the votes. These
queries represent "what the model is most focused on right before it starts
generating." To avoid selecting scattered individual tokens (which would yield
semantically incomplete fragments), SnapKV applies 1D max-pooling over the
voting scores, clustering adjacent high-scoring tokens together. Each attention
head operates independently, ensuring diversity.

If you take H2O's "re-compute heavy hitters after every decode step" and replace
it with "vote once at prefill time based on an observation window to decide who
stays," you get the core idea of SnapKV.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Digs a New Hole)

It moves compression to the right place and gains flexibility through per-head
independent selection. But it still assigns the same KV budget to every layer.
Experimental evidence shows that attention patterns vary enormously across
layers of an LLM — low layers attend broadly (requiring large budgets) while
high layers are already highly focused (small budgets suffice). Uniform budgeting
is a structural waste.

** 2024 | PyramidKV: Wide at the Bottom, Narrow at the Top — Dynamic Per-Layer Budgets

*** What Problem It Saw

Cai et al. systematically analyzed attention entropy across layers in multiple
models and found a clear "funnel" shape — *Pyramidal Information Funneling*:
lower layers distribute attention across many tokens, middle layers concentrate
progressively, and top layers attend to only a handful of critical tokens. By
applying a uniform budget across all layers, SnapKV forcibly flattens this
natural pyramid.

*** Its Solution

Let the KV budget decrease with layer depth, forming a pyramid: large budget at
the bottom, small at the top. The token selection mechanism reuses SnapKV's
observation-window voting, but replaces the fixed top-K per layer with a
function that varies with depth, keeping the total budget constant.

Results: on LLaMA-3-8B, retaining only 12% of the KV cache nearly matches full-
KV performance; even at an aggressive 0.7% retention, it still outperforms
other methods by 20 points.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Digs a New Hole)

Budget allocation is smarter, but the underlying logic is unchanged: eviction
still happens at the token level, discarding individual tokens. The problem is
that semantics are encoded across tokens. Within a continuous semantic fragment,
dropping a single token can shatter the whole fragment — keep "138", drop "+86"
and the rest of the phone number, and what remains in cache is meaningless.
Could semantic units, rather than individual tokens, become the minimum eviction
granularity?

** 2025 | ChunkKV + SentenceKV: Semantic Units as Atoms — Coherence Preserved

*** What Problem It Saw

Liu et al. (ChunkKV) and Zhu et al. (SentenceKV) converged almost simultaneously
on the same root problem: token-level eviction destroys semantic coherence.
Meaning is encoded across multiple tokens; retaining high-scoring tokens in
isolation while discarding their context is like keeping a word root and throwing
away the entire sentence.

*** Their Solutions

*ChunkKV* groups tokens into fixed-size chunks (default: 10 tokens), scores
each chunk as a whole, and retains or evicts entire chunks together. A complete
phrase is either fully present or fully absent — no semantic fragments. As a
bonus, adjacent layers select nearly identical chunk indices, so they introduce
*cross-layer index reuse* to eliminate redundant computation, yielding a
measured 26.5% throughput improvement.

*SentenceKV* raises the granularity to sentences. After prefill, each sentence's
KV is compressed into a single semantic vector stored on the GPU (very compact);
the full KV is offloaded to CPU (large but loaded only when needed). During
decoding, the current query vector is compared against all sentence semantic
vectors by similarity; only the top-ranked sentence KVs are fetched from CPU to
GPU for attention computation.

The relationship between the two: if you replace ChunkKV's "fixed-size chunk"
with "punctuation-delimited sentence" and add "GPU stores summary, CPU stores
full KV, fetched on demand," you arrive at SentenceKV.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Digs a New Hole)

Semantic-unit retention genuinely outperforms token-level selection, and
SentenceKV's CPU offloading dramatically reduces GPU memory. But both share the
same fundamental limitation: *compression is performed once at prefill, and
resolution is fixed from that moment on*. A semantic span judged "unimportant"
at prefill time cannot have its token-level detail retrieved even if it turns
out to be critical during decoding. Moreover, chunk/sentence granularity is
static — there is no mechanism to dynamically decide "zoom into this span,
compress that one," based on the needs of the current decoding step.

** 2026 | SeKV: Resolution-Adaptive, Zoom Into Semantic Spans On Demand (Target Paper)

*** What Problem It Saw

Abaskohi et al. distill all prior shortcomings into a single sentence: "Once
compression is complete, token-level detail is permanently gone, with no
mechanism to recover it on demand during decoding." Whether ChunkKV's chunks or
SentenceKV's sentences, compression is one-way — gone is gone.

*** Its Solution

SeKV combines three components:

1. *Entropy-guided semantic span segmentation*: Information entropy detects
   semantic boundaries and segments the context into semantically complete spans
   — not fixed-length chunks, not punctuation-delimited sentences, but
   content-adaptive cuts based on information density. High-entropy regions
   (information-dense, blurry boundaries) are cut finely; low-entropy regions
   (repetitive or sparse) are cut coarsely.

2. *Two-tier GPU–CPU storage*: Each span stores only a lightweight summary
   vector on the GPU (used for fast routing decisions; extremely GPU-memory-
   efficient). The full KV is stored on the CPU in a compressed low-rank SVD
   basis (far smaller than the raw full KV, and reconstructible losslessly or
   near-losslessly).

3. *Trainable zoom-in mechanism*: During decoding, if a span's summary vector
   exceeds a similarity threshold with the current query, its SVD basis is
   fetched from the CPU and the full token-level KV is reconstructed on-the-fly
   for high-resolution attention. The routing mechanism itself is trainable but
   adds fewer than 0.05% extra parameters; the base model is fully frozen.

If you take SentenceKV's "one-shot sentence selection" and replace it with "GPU
stores only summary vectors, CPU stores SVD-compressed reconstructible full KV,"
then add "adaptively decide during decoding which spans to zoom into at token
level," you get SeKV.

*** Why It Works (and Where It Digs a New Hole)

The key innovation is decoupling *compression* from *recoverability* — the GPU
always stores only summaries, but the "true KV" lives on the CPU in SVD-
compressed form, ready for reconstruction at any time. Unlike outright
discarding (ChunkKV/SentenceKV), recovery is genuinely possible. Experimental
results: at 128K context, GPU memory is reduced by 53.3%; SeKV outperforms the
strongest semantic-compression baseline by an average of 5.9 percentage points
across four benchmarks (LongBench, RULER, Needle-in-a-Haystack, and others).

New costs: CPU–GPU data transfer latency is a real penalty in high-concurrency
or large-batch scenarios; SVD reconstruction itself carries compute overhead;
if the zoom-in trigger threshold is poorly calibrated, either too many spans are
fetched (no memory savings) or critical spans are missed (accuracy drops).

* Frontier Extensions

SeKV was submitted on June 30, 2026, and as of this writing has no citing
follow-up papers (it has been public for less than one week). However, three
closely related concurrent works from the same period attack the same problem
from different angles:

*CompressKV (arXiv 2606.24467, June 2026, one week apart from SeKV)*: Finds
that in GQA architectures a small subset of "Semantic Retrieval Heads" dominate
semantic localization, and applies a heterogeneous eviction strategy specifically
to those heads while using a standard approach for the rest. The direction
matches SeKV — semantic structure over individual tokens — but CompressKV
operates entirely on GPU with no SVD reconstruction. SeKV's zoom-in can recover
token-level detail; CompressKV cannot. The two papers do not cite each other;
they are a genuine case of parallel convergence.

*xKV (arXiv 2503.18893, March 2026)*: Performs joint SVD decomposition across
layers, projecting KV from adjacent layers into a shared low-rank subspace with
selective reconstruction at decode time. SeKV's SVD is decomposed along the
semantic-span dimension; xKV's is along the layer dimension — two orthogonal
compression axes that could in principle be stacked.

*ScoutAttention (arXiv 2603.27138, DAC 2026)*: Runs attention pre-computation
one layer ahead on the CPU, overlapping it with GPU computation in a pipeline so
that CPU–GPU transfer latency is hidden inside compute time, achieving a 2.1×
speedup. SeKV's primary weakness is precisely CPU–GPU transfer overhead;
ScoutAttention's approach is a natural engineering patch.

As of July 2026, the combination of SeKV + ScoutAttention has not yet been
implemented by anyone, but it is an obvious next step.

* Insight

On the surface this evolutionary line is "progress in KV cache compression
techniques." At a deeper level it is a series of redefinitions of *what
constitutes the minimal meaningful unit of memory*:

token → chunk → sentence → semantic span (adaptive)

Each upgrade pushes the "adjustable resolution range" of memory one notch
higher. Coarser granularity saves more memory but loses more information; finer
granularity preserves information but saves little — this is an intrinsic
tension. All five preceding papers resolve it by "making a tradeoff at some
fixed granularity." SeKV is the first to turn the granularity itself into a
dynamically adjustable parameter at decode time.

The real conceptual shift happens with SeKV: from *compression = loss* to
*compression = deferred reconstruction*. This is not an engineering trick but a
paradigm shift — it imports the database-world idea of "storage hierarchy +
on-demand loading" into the attention mechanism. Previous researchers asked
"which tokens can we drop with minimum loss?" SeKV reframes the question as
"how do we design the compression format so that anything can be faithfully
restored on demand at any time?"

*Most likely next directions*:
- Upgrade the zoom-in trigger from a threshold decision to a continuous
  attention-based router (analogous to MoE gating), learning end-to-end "when
  it is worth spending CPU bandwidth on reconstruction"
- Stack SeKV (SVD along the span dimension) and xKV (SVD along the layer
  dimension) to compress along two orthogonal axes simultaneously
- Apply ScoutAttention's pipeline idea to eliminate SeKV's CPU transfer
  latency bottleneck
- Extend the zoom-in mechanism to MoE architectures, where different experts'
  varying need to zoom into different spans can naturally provide routing signals

* Takeaway

This evolutionary line offers a clear demonstration of *how to find research
problems*: no paper's contribution starts from a blank creative slate; each one
advances a *single concrete contradiction* in the prior work to its logical next
step. StreamingLLM solved infinite generation but missed content awareness. H2O
added content awareness but missed the prompt. SnapKV addressed the prompt but
missed layer differences. PyramidKV fixed layer differences but fragmented
semantic units. ChunkKV/SentenceKV preserved semantic units but locked in the
resolution.

This suggests a practice: read any method you find interesting as a "system
with internal contradictions." Do not ask what it does — ask where its logic
hits a wall if followed to its conclusion. That wall is the entry point for the
next paper.
