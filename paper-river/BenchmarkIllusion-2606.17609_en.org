* The River of the Problem

You strip half the parameters from a large model to make it faster and more memory-efficient. You run it through a standard benchmark — the score barely budges. You breathe a sigh of relief: "Compression didn't hurt it." Did it really not?

Everyone on this river, for the past twenty years, has been chasing the same question: *How do you know you haven't broken the model?*

The difficulty is that "broken" has no clean ruler. A model isn't a bridge that collapses when cracked. It is a continuous mass of weights; remove some of them, and certain capabilities quietly decay while others remain perfectly intact. What you measure, and how you measure it, directly determines whether you "see" or "miss" that decay. Generation after generation has pressed forward, essentially sharpening this ruler — and each time they sharpen it, they find that the previous ruler was lying to them in some corner.

What the target paper ("The Benchmark Illusion") does is catch the newest and most hidden lie in that ruler: a model can pick the right answer from four options yet cannot write that answer out.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Han et al.: Prune the Useless Weights Away

*** What problem did it see?

The earliest trouble was physical: neural networks were too large and clumsy, impossible to fit into phones or embedded devices. A network has tens of millions of connections, but intuitively many of them must be "along for the ride" — their weights near zero, contributing almost nothing to the final output.

*** Its solution

So prune them. The rule fits in a single sentence: *weights with small absolute values, delete them.* After deletion the network has gaps (sparsity); you retrain it so the remaining connections make up the loss. Prune — retrain — prune again, repeated several rounds, and you can shrink a network by an order of magnitude with almost no loss in accuracy.

*** Why it worked (and what it buried)

It worked because large networks are inherently over-parameterized; small weights really are redundant. But it planted a time-bomb: *it depended on retraining.* In 2015, retraining a network with millions of parameters was routine. But once models grew to tens or hundreds of billions of parameters — the LLM era — "prune and then retrain" became computationally impossible. That bomb directly forced the next paper into existence.

** 2023 | SparseGPT: Prune Without Retraining

*** What problem did it see?

At GPT-scale, Han's "prune and retrain" approach broke down entirely — the retraining cost alone was enough to deter everyone. The question became: *Can you prune once and walk away, with no retraining at all?*

*** Its solution

SparseGPT reframes "pruning" as "repair." Instead of asking "which weights should be deleted," it asks: after deleting a batch of weights, *how do we fine-tune the remaining weights so that the output of this layer stays as close to the original as possible?* This is a layer-wise reconstruction problem, solved using second-order approximations (Hessian information), layer by layer. One shot, no training data, no backpropagation.

*** Why it worked (and what it buried)

It worked by converting the impossibly large global problem of "retrain everything" into a collection of manageable local "repair" subproblems, each with a closed-form solution. But it also established a convention — *what proves "no harm done"? Perplexity*: feed the model a passage of Wikipedia text and see how "surprised" it is at each next token. If perplexity barely changes, the pruning is declared successful. This ruler is convenient, continuous, and easy to optimize against. It would become the focal point of every debate in the second half of this river. Meanwhile, its second-order reconstruction is heavy and slow — that is what the next paper will attack.

** 2024 | Wanda: One Multiplication, Replace the Whole System of Equations

*** What problem did it see?

SparseGPT no longer requires retraining, but internally it must compute Hessians and solve linear equations — heavy and slow in practice. Wanda zeroed in on one question: *Is it possible to know which weight to delete without solving any equations at all?*

*** Its solution

Wanda's insight is strikingly simple: a weight's importance depends not only on how large it is, but also on *how large the input fed to it is.* A small weight that is always multiplied by a huge activation actually has substantial impact; conversely, a large weight whose input is always near zero is just decoration. So the scoring formula is a single multiplication: *|weight| × |input activation|*. Compute, rank, delete the low-scoring ones. Done. No retraining, no equations.

Transforming SparseGPT into Wanda requires exactly one substitution: *replace "solve the second-order reconstruction equation" with "compute weight times activation"* — and you have Wanda. The results are surprisingly close.

*** Why it worked (and what it buried)

It worked because activation statistics quietly do most of the work that second-order information would do, at a fraction of the cost. Together, Wanda and SparseGPT became the two standard baselines for one-shot pruning. But Wanda inherited SparseGPT's ruler — *still perplexity, still zero-shot accuracy.* By this point in the river, the "surgical knife" for pruning is already very sharp, yet the "post-surgery checkup" still uses the same chart. Starting with the next paper, the critique shifts from the knife to the chart.

** 2024 | LLM-KICK: Stable Perplexity Doesn't Mean the Brain Is Intact (First Crack)

*** What problem did it see?

This is the pivotal paper where the river turns. LLM-KICK directly challenges the ruler that had been in use for years: *If perplexity doesn't drop, is nothing really hurt?* It uncovered a chilling phenomenon — perplexity can remain rock-solid while tasks that require genuine "knowledge retrieval" quietly collapse.

*** Its solution

Its "solution" is actually a harder set of exams: instead of only checking whether the model is surprised while reading text, it forces the model to perform knowledge-intensive tasks that require real output. Switch the ruler, and the true damage of compression becomes visible.

*** Why it worked (and what it buried)

It was the first paper to shift the field's attention from "how well did we prune" to "are we measuring the right thing." But the new hole it left is this: it showed that "perplexity lies," but it did not yet clarify *at which level* the lie occurs — is the knowledge truly gone, or is it still there but unable to come out? That question — "erased vs. unreachable" — is exactly what the target paper will answer.

** 2025 | M-Wanda: One Average Score Hides Unevenly Distributed Damage (Crack Widens)

*** What problem did it see?

M-Wanda continues tearing the same crack, this time from a multilingual angle. It found that pruning damage is *not uniformly distributed*: a single cut may leave English completely unscathed while gutting certain minority languages. If you look only at an aggregate accuracy, this lopsided damage is entirely invisible.

*** Its solution

It showed that the damage pattern depends on what language the calibration corpus is written in — calibrate on English, and pruning will preferentially preserve English. It then improved the pruning process to make damage more balanced across languages. But for the "evaluation river," its more important contribution is diagnostic: *aggregate metrics can conceal structural damage.*

*** Why it worked (and what it buried)

LLM-KICK said "the ruler is wrong"; M-Wanda said "even with the right ruler, averaging the scores still lies." Together, the two papers have pried the equation "benchmark score = model health" until it is wobbling. The last piece still missing: what is the precise mechanism that lets a "good-looking" model actually be broken?

** 2025 | Park / Rahmani: Multiple-Choice and "Truly Knowing" Are Different Things (Precursor in the Opposite Direction)

*** What problem did it see?

These two papers are not about compression; they are about the form of the exam itself. They found that a model's performance on multiple-choice questions and its performance in free generation can diverge substantially. Park found that models sometimes generate the correct answer yet choose the wrong option in a multiple-choice setting; Rahmani found that self-correction behavior is completely different across the two formats.

*** Its solution

Their contribution is to explicitly decompose "multiple-choice score" and "generative capability" into two separate variables for study, revealing that the four-option format itself introduces a bias relative to true generative ability.

*** Why it worked (and what it buried)

Note the direction: they observed *correct generation, wrong choice* — the model knows the answer but is misled by the options. This is precisely the *mirror image* of the target paper's phenomenon. Flip that mirror over and you have the story the target paper tells: in compressed models, the direction is reversed — *correct choice, failed generation.*

** 2026 | The Benchmark Illusion: The Answer Was Demoted, Not Erased (Target Paper)

*** What problem did it see?

Bringing all the previous cracks together: LLM-KICK shows perplexity lies; M-Wanda shows averaged scores lie; Park/Rahmani show the multiple-choice format has its own bias. The target paper turns its sights on the last and most commonly used shield in compressed-model evaluation — *multiple-choice accuracy.* Developers prune a model, run it through a multiple-choice benchmark, see the score hold steady, and declare "performance intact." This paper says: that is a *false certificate of health.*

*** Its solution

The method is a controlled diagnostic: the same question, the same compressed model, *scored once as multiple-choice and once by letting the model greedily generate a free-form answer.* A massive gap emerges — especially on high-sparsity Wanda: the model can pick the correct answer from the options yet cannot write it out when generating freely.

More importantly, the paper pursues the "erased vs. unreachable" question that LLM-KICK left open. Its answer: *the answer is demoted, not erased.* The correct answer is still present in the model's probability distribution, just pushed down from first place. Evidence: switch to beam search, sampling, or provide just one example (one-shot), and the correct answer floats back up. If it had truly been erased, none of these would recover it.

*** Why it worked (and what it buried)

It pins down the vague accusation "scores can lie" into a precise, falsifiable mechanism: *multiple-choice only tests "recognition"; compression damages "generation" first.* A model that can recognize but not generate is indistinguishable from a healthy model on multiple-choice benchmarks — that is the source of the word "illusion."

The new holes left behind: since the correct answer is merely demoted rather than deleted, can we design a "decompression" or lightweight recovery method to push demoted answers back to the top? And what ruler should we actually use — one that is both fast and not fooled by the gap between "recognition" and "generation"?

* Frontier Extensions

As of mid-2026, this paper is itself the latest development on this "evaluation river." It proposes no new pruning algorithm; instead it proposes a new *diagnostic framework* — decomposing a model's relationship to any given answer into three tiers: "erased / still recognizable / still generatable." Foreseeable follow-on directions (hinted at in the paper; no confirmed subsequent work yet):

- *Recovery*: Since the phenomenon is "demotion" rather than "deletion," targeted promotion of demoted answers may be far cheaper than re-pruning.
- *New rulers*: Incorporating "free-generation accuracy" into the standard health check for compressed models, replacing or supplementing multiple-choice accuracy.

* Insights

On the surface this river is an iteration of pruning techniques; at its core it is a *migration of cognitive emphasis* — from "can I compress the model smaller" to "why should I trust that the compressed model still works."

At a deeper level, it exposes an original sin that all benchmarks share: *the format of the exam quietly defines what "ability" means.* Multiple-choice defines "ability" as "being able to recognize the correct answer," while real-world use requires "being able to generate the correct answer." These two normally correlate so strongly that nobody cares. What compression does is precisely *pry them apart* — it preferentially sacrifices "generation" while preserving "recognition." The same ruler that is accurate on a full model thus becomes a funhouse mirror on a compressed model.

The most elegant strike is "demoted, not erased." It converts a qualitative concern ("scores can lie") into a quantitative, falsifiable mechanism (the answer is still in the distribution — how far down the ranking is it?). The ability to recover it via beam search, sampling, or one-shot prompting is the hardest evidence — this is genuine scientific taste: not satisfied with pointing out "there is a problem," but locating "the physical address of the problem."

Where will things go next? Two directions. First, *evaluation* — "free generation" will become a mandatory health check for compressed models, and using multiple-choice accuracy alone will be viewed as untrustworthy. Second, *recovery* — since the phenomenon is only demotion, targeted "re-promotion" will become a new research direction lighter-weight than re-pruning.

* Takeaways

For "how to do research, how to find problems," this river offers three very concrete landing points:

1. *Watch the ruler everyone shares rather than forging yet another new knife.* The most valuable work in the second half of this river — LLM-KICK, M-Wanda, and this paper — proposed no new pruning algorithm; all of them questioned the evaluation. When a field's "knife" is already intensely competed over, the "health chart" is often an unguarded gold mine. In practice this means: in any mature field, find the evaluation metric that everyone silently assumes is correct but that has never been seriously scrutinized, and poke it.

2. *Force a qualitative suspicion into a quantitative, falsifiable mechanism.* "Scores can lie" is something anyone can say, but only when you nail it down to "the answer is demoted to rank k and recoverable by beam search" does it become a hard paper. In practice this means: whenever you have a vague intuition, force yourself to ask "where is its physical location, and what experiment would falsify it."

3. *The mirror image of a phenomenon hides a new problem.* The target paper's inspiration is almost exactly taking Park/Rahmani's "correct generation / wrong choice" and flipping it over. In practice this means: when you observe a phenomenon, actively ask "does its opposite hold? Under what conditions?" — the opposite direction is often a paper nobody has written yet.
