* The River of the Problem

How can long-context LLMs "look things up" both quickly and accurately?

After reading a million-word novel, the model must — in principle — scan everything it
has read every time it generates a new token. Storing all that "previously read content"
is called the KV cache, which exhausts GPU memory; scanning it all is called attention,
which is agonizingly slow.

For seven years researchers have charged at this deadlock one after another:
- Store everything, scan everything: accurate but slow, and memory explodes;
- Discard some of it: saves memory, but drop the wrong tokens and the model can't answer questions;
- Scan only a subset: saves time, but the retrieval granularity is either too coarse or too fine.

Every generation of solutions fixes one pain point of the previous generation and introduces a new one.
CTkvr is the current downstream of this river.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Transformer: Nailing the Attention Formula into the Foundation of LLMs

*** What Problem It Saw

RNNs must process one token at a time; long sequences are slow to train and cannot be parallelized.

*** Its Solution

Drop the recurrence; let every token attend to all other tokens simultaneously (self-attention).
Compute Q·K^T to obtain an n × n attention map, then take the weighted sum over V.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

Training became parallel and quality improved. But O(n^2) is written into the formula: once n
reaches 100K, the KV cache weighs tens of GB, and reading all K and V anew for every generated
token is unavoidable. This is the hole every subsequent paper has been digging at.

** 2023 | StreamingLLM: Chop the Middle, Keep Head and Tail

*** What Problem It Saw

Not enough memory — the most naive fix is a sliding window: keep only the most recent N tokens.
But as soon as the very first tokens are dropped, the model collapses and produces gibberish.

*** Its Solution

It found that the first few tokens act as "attention sinks" — the model dumps surplus attention
mass onto them by default. Keep the first 4 tokens plus the most recent N; discard everything else.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

Memory stabilizes and the model no longer collapses. But ask "what did paragraph 500 say?" —
it genuinely does not know, because paragraph 500 was physically deleted.
"Long-context understanding" is never truly addressed; the model merely survives.

** 2023–2024 | H2O / SnapKV: Pick "Important Tokens" by Attention Score

*** What Problem It Saw

StreamingLLM is too mechanical — it discards by position, ignoring content. A critical sentence
in the middle can simply be slid away.

*** Its Solution

Observe which tokens accumulate high attention scores across history; those are "Heavy Hitters."
Keep them; evict the low-scoring ones.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

Smarter than a pure sliding window: information is no longer lost by position but by "how often
it has been attended to." The problem is — *being unimportant now does not mean being unimportant later*.
A passage that draws no attention in early turns may become key evidence in turn five of a
multi-turn conversation. Once evicted, it is gone for good.
This eviction line collapses most badly on long outputs and multi-turn dialogue.

** Early 2024 | InfLLM: Don't Evict — Store Everything on CPU and Fetch on Demand

*** What Problem It Saw

The fundamental error of eviction methods is "once lost, lost forever." What if we *keep everything*
but simply don't keep it all on the GPU?

*** Its Solution

Offload the entire KV cache to CPU memory; split it into blocks of 8–16 tokens each. Compute one
representative vector per block (mean-pooled). During generation, the new query computes similarity
against all block representatives and pulls the top-k blocks back to the GPU for attention.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

This is a paradigm shift: from "pruning KV" to "retrieving KV." Nothing needs to be permanently
discarded.

But blocks are coarse — a block of 16 tokens needs only one relevant token to be fetched in full,
and the remaining 15 are noise. In long contexts this dilution makes attention computations blurry.
ShadowKV and Quest are both bitten by this problem in experiments at 128K context length.

** Mid 2024 | Quest: Add a Tight Bound on Block Representatives, Miss Fewer Key Blocks

*** What Problem It Saw

InfLLM's mean-pooled block representative gives an inaccurate relevance estimate — a highly
relevant token inside a block can be averaged out by low-relevance neighbors, causing the whole
block to be skipped.

*** Its Solution

Instead of storing a single mean vector, store the *per-dimension min and max* for each block.
Given a query, compute an *upper bound* on the block's similarity to that query using the min/max
envelope, then rank blocks by this upper bound. This prevents missing blocks that have a bright
spot even when the block average is low.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

Block selection is more accurate. But blocks are still blocks — the selected block still contains
8–16 tokens, with irrelevant ones still present. The precision ceiling is capped by block
granularity; the paper reports Quest dropping 5–7 points on 128K Ruler compared to FullKV.

** Mid 2024 | RetrievalAttention: Go Down to Token Level with ANN

*** What Problem It Saw

Block granularity is inherently a compromise — trading accuracy for speed. What if retrieval
operated directly at the token level?

*** Its Solution

Treat every token's Key as a vector in a database and build an Approximate Nearest Neighbor index
(HNSW, etc.) over all of them. When a query arrives, look up the top-k tokens directly, bypassing
blocks entirely.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

Accuracy rises to near full-KV quality — this is the right direction for precision. But building
an ANN index is prohibitively expensive: HNSW takes 800+ ms on a 128K context, KMeans takes
6000+ ms. The paper reports prefill slowing down 3× and decode slowing down 5×.
Accuracy wins; throughput collapses.

** Late 2024 | MagicPIG: Swap the Index — LSH Hashing, Moved to CPU

*** What Problem It Saw

ANN indexes are too heavy to build and query. A lighter hash-based index (LSH) should be faster.

*** Its Solution

Use 9 random hash functions and 120 hash tables to locate tokens that land in the same bucket as
the query. Store the hash tables entirely on CPU; compute attention only on the GPU.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

Queries are faster and CPU/GPU duties are split. But the hash tables themselves consume enormous
memory: 120 tables × context length × number of heads. Once the batch size grows, CPU memory is
exhausted. The paper shows MagicPIG's throughput collapsing at batch=8 and beyond.
LSH is also a probabilistic index — when collision rates are high, accuracy remains unstable,
dropping 7 points on 128K Ruler.

** December 2025 | CTkvr: Don't Build a Complex Index for Tokens — Let Queries Index Themselves

*** What Problem It Saw

Looking back at this whole trajectory: on one side is block-level retrieval (fast but coarse); on
the other is token-level + ANN/LSH (accurate but index overhead is too heavy).
*The entire field has been working on the Key side* — indexing Keys, clustering Keys. But Keys
are voluminous and static; no matter how you organize them, it is expensive. What if the problem
were approached from the other side?

CTkvr ran an experiment: take query vectors from different positions in the same long context and
compute their cosine similarities. The result — *after RoPE, positionally adjacent queries
(within ~1K positions) consistently exceed 0.8 cosine similarity*. A second experiment showed:
*the higher the query similarity, the more their respective top-k Keys overlap (a clear positive
correlation)*.

In other words: you do not need to scan all Keys anew for every query — nearby queries have
already done the scan, and the top-k Keys they found are almost certainly the same ones you need.

*** Its Solution

Two stages:
1. *Query-Centroid IVF (coarse filter)*: take the most recent C query vectors as "centroids";
   pre-store the indices of each centroid's top-rho Keys. When a new query arrives, find the
   nearest C' centroids first, merge and deduplicate their Key lists, and obtain a "recall set"
   L^Recall (roughly 10K entries).
2. *Token-Level Rerank (fine filter)*: compute the exact Q·K^T scores within the recall set;
   keep the top-rho' Keys (roughly 1K) and run attention on those.

Engineering-wise: the coarse recall step stays on the GPU; the fine rerank step is offloaded to
the CPU (where the KV cache already lives), with a custom CUDA kernel keeping both sides
concurrent. A *Dynamic Centroid Update (DCU)* mechanism handles multi-turn settings: a FIFO
queue rolls out the oldest centroids as new ones arrive.

*** Why It Worked (and Where New Pain Remains)

This is a genuine change of sides — *moving the index from the Key side to the Query side*.
Keys are voluminous and static; indexing them will always be expensive. Queries are few, dynamic,
and — thanks to RoPE — carry a natural structure of local similarity. Using queries to index
themselves transforms "build an index over a dictionary" into "borrow the notes of someone who
just looked this up." Index construction is 1000× faster (HNSW: ~6 s; CTkvr: ~0.4 ms); accuracy
loss is under 1%; decode throughput improves 3–4×.

Remaining pain: the centroid strategy depends on RoPE specifically — whether it holds for ALiBi,
NoPE, or other positional encodings has not been verified. After more than 8 turns of dialogue,
centroid drift accumulates, and DCU only partially compensates. The CPU-side attention path
requires sufficient CPU FLOPs, which may be infeasible on smaller machines.

* Frontier Extensions

CTkvr was posted as a preprint on December 17, 2025, and is the latest advance along this line
at the time of writing.

Foreseeable next steps:
- *Generalizing query-side indexing*: since RoPE clusters queries by position, could one go
  finer — learning a separate set of centroids per head, per layer?
- *Cross-request centroid reuse*: similar prompt templates (agent workflows, RAG pipelines) produce
  highly overlapping centroids; caching and reusing them across requests could yield further gains.
- *Orthogonal combination with KV quantization*: CTkvr decides *which* tokens to retrieve;
  quantization decides *how large* each token representation is; stacking the two leaves
  more room to compress.

* Insight

*Real progress often comes not from "optimizing the existing path by another factor of two"
but from "thinking about the problem from the other side."*

Every paper in this river's first seven years — InfLLM, Quest, RetrievalAttention, MagicPIG —
worked on the Key side: chunking Keys, building ANN indexes over Keys, hashing Keys. Keys are
voluminous and static; no matter how sophisticated the index, construction will be expensive.
CTkvr's pivot lies in recognizing that *Queries are few, dynamic, and — courtesy of RoPE —
carry a natural structure of local similarity*. Moving the index to the query side instantly
transforms "build an index over a dictionary" into "borrow the notes of someone who just looked
this up."

At a deeper level, this is a shift from *"optimizing data structures"* to *"exploiting the
intrinsic structure of the problem itself."* Earlier work borrowed ANN and LSH from the database
community — applying off-the-shelf index technology. The observation CTkvr exploits — "positionally
adjacent queries are similar" — is a property unique to the interior of LLMs (RoPE + autoregressive
generation). You can only think of it once you have thoroughly internalized the domain structure.

*Where is this most likely to go next?* Continue in the same direction — "what other internal
structure of LLMs has not yet been exploited?" For instance: query distributions differ
substantially across heads; could one apply distinct centroid strategies per head? Or: agent
workflows repeat prompt templates at high frequency; could query index caches be reused across
requests? Or: reasoning models generating long chain-of-thought traces exhibit stronger semantic
clustering in their query distributions than ordinary generation; KV retrieval designed specifically
for reasoning might compress another order of magnitude.

* Lessons

*When looking for a research breakthrough, start by asking: which side is everyone currently
working on? Has anyone touched the other side?*

The KV cache river spent a full seven years cutting, indexing, and compressing on the Key side.
The reason CTkvr achieved a 1000× speedup in index construction is not that its algorithm is more
clever — it is that they switched sides. When every paper in a subfield is improving by 10–30%
and everyone is arguing in the same vocabulary, it usually does not mean the current path is
exhausted; it means everyone has forgotten there is another path.

The practical takeaway is this: whenever you face a "find top-k of X" problem, pause and ask —
- Are all current methods building the index over X? Does the querying side Y have any internal
  structure that has not been exploited?
- Do Y's queries exhibit locality, periodicity, or template reuse?
- If so, could "Y index itself," offloading the burden entirely from the X side?

Concretely for LLM inference engineering: if you are building RAG pipelines, agent systems, or
long-document QA, the CTkvr approach suggests you could *shift the index granularity from
"knowledge-base chunks" to "query templates."* Users' queries are actually sparsely clustered in
semantic space; caching the mapping from "query template → recall set" may save far more compute
than caching "chunk embeddings."
