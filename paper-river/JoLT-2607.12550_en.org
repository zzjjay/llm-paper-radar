* The River of the Problem

As LLM inference runs longer and longer, what accumulates in GPU memory is not model weights but the KV cache left by every layer and every token — and sooner or later that pile takes up more space than the model itself. How do you compress it without causing the model to "forget and go dumb"?

The difficulty is that the KV cache is not a uniform blob of numbers. It has several axes — how many heads, how many tokens, how many feature dimensions per head — and the amount of redundancy along each axis is completely different. Every previous method started by betting that "redundancy lives mainly in one particular axis" and then hammered that axis alone. The result was either leaving other axes' redundancy on the table, or squeezing the chosen axis so hard that accuracy collapsed. Generation after generation chased the same underlying question: how do you avoid placing that bet up front, and instead exploit the redundancy in every direction at once?

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Vaswani et al.: Replacing RNNs with Pure Attention

*** What problem it saw

RNNs process sequences step by step; training on long sequences is painfully slow and early information is easily lost.

*** Its solution

Turn "reading the whole sequence" into a single parallel attention computation, eliminating the need for sequential recurrence.

*** Why it worked — and what new pit it dug

Parallel computation bought speed, but at a price: to avoid redundant recomputation, inference must cache the Key and Value tensors computed at every layer so that future tokens can reuse them. As context grows longer and the model gets deeper, this cache balloons out of control. That is the seed of the entire "KV cache compression" research thread — a problem Vaswani et al. never intended to discuss, yet inadvertently planted.

** 2024 | Palu: Treating the Hidden Dimension of the KV Cache as Compressible

*** What problem it saw

At the time, compressing the KV cache came down to two tricks: quantization (store each number in fewer bits) and eviction (simply drop less-important tokens). Both operate on the surface and never ask a more fundamental question: are the dimensions of the K/V vectors themselves full of redundancy that can be compressed?

*** Its solution

Apply SVD to the projection weight matrices that produce K and V, mapping the original hundreds-of-dimensions K/V vectors into a low-rank "latent space" for storage and decompressing them on the fly. To prevent excessive accuracy loss, the paper also works out a "group-by-head" decomposition at medium granularity — better accuracy than per-head decomposition, lower overhead than a global one.

*** Why it worked — and what new pit it dug

SVD finds which directions within a single head's K/V matrix carry large weight and which can be discarded — in essence, it discovers low-rank structure inside one head. This also means Palu examines only one layer and one head at a time, blind to whether adjacent layers have similar K/V distributions. If the distributions of neighboring layers are nearly identical, Palu leaves all of that cross-layer redundancy untouched.

** 2025 | xKV: Discovering That Adjacent Layers' Principal Directions Are Highly Aligned

*** What problem it saw

After Palu, some work (e.g., MiniCache) tried cross-layer compression by merging adjacent layers' token representations, reasoning that they look alike. But xKV measured this with Centered Kernel Alignment and found that token-level similarity is actually unstable — MiniCache's approach collapses on certain models, with accuracy drops exceeding 20 %. What is genuinely stable is not "how similar the tokens are" but "how well the leading singular vectors (principal directions) of each layer's K/V matrix align across layers."

*** Its solution

Since the principal directions themselves are aligned across layers, there is no need to run a separate SVD per layer. Instead, jointly SVD several layers' K/V together, extract one shared set of low-rank subspace bases, let all layers share those bases, and store only each layer's individual coefficients.

*** Why it worked — and what new pit it dug

Because the savings come from eliminating the redundant recomputation and re-storage of bases shared across layers, the compression ratio can reach 8× with little accuracy loss. Yet xKV still treats the cache as a 2-D matrix — it picks the "cross-layer" axis as the one to exploit, and, just like Palu, leaves the within-layer redundancy across heads and feature dimensions unmodeled simultaneously. There is also an efficiency pit the authors acknowledge themselves: computing the cross-layer SVD online at an 8 K context length can take longer than the entire prefill stage.

** 2026 | JoLT (this paper): Allocating Capacity Across All Three Axes of a True 3-D Tensor

*** What problem it saw

The authors placed the Palu/xKV line alongside the KIVI/TurboQuant quantization line and found that both commit the same error in two different guises. Low-rank methods always "pick one axis" (Palu picks within-head, xKV picks cross-layer) and never treat heads × tokens × features as a joint whole. Quantization methods are "locked to fixed bit-widths" — either 4 bits or 2 bits — and simply cannot land in the most attractive middle ground of 2–3× compression with near-zero accuracy loss.

*** Its solution

Treat each layer's KV cache as a third-order tensor (three axes: heads, tokens, features). Apply a partial Tucker decomposition to compress all three axes simultaneously, allocating capacity to each axis according to its own actual redundancy rather than applying a one-size-fits-all squeeze or attacking only a single axis. The residual left after decomposition is then passed through a Johnson-Lindenstrauss random projection for additional dimensionality reduction, recovering the portion of error that Tucker alone does not capture.

*** Why it worked — and what new pit it dug

Each axis retains exactly the capacity it needs and sheds what it does not, so the reconstruction error can be an order of magnitude lower than that of xKV's cross-layer SVD or 4-bit quantization. The residual component sidesteps the "fixed bit-width locks out the middle ground" problem from the quantization line — the residual continuously adjusts how much information is preserved rather than jumping between a handful of bit-widths. The remaining pit: the paper acknowledges in its appendix that Tucker is "currently the best among the four classic tensor decomposition formats (Tucker / CP / t-SVD / TT)" but that comparison is only among those four. More aggressive tensor decompositions or schemes that couple quantization and decomposition more tightly have not yet been systematically compared.

* Frontier Extension

As of now (July 2026), no paper explicitly critiquing or improving on JoLT has been found — it was posted as a preprint in July 2026 and has not yet had time to accumulate citations. What can be observed concretely: on the quantization side, QJL (2024) was the first to apply the Johnson-Lindenstrauss transform to KV cache quantization; TurboQuant (2025, Google) built on this by adding a random orthogonal rotation before quantization. JoLT uses the JL transform on the residual after Tucker decomposition — a different angle from QJL/TurboQuant — but whether future work will directly compare or merge these approaches remains to be seen.

* Insight

What truly evolved along this line is not "the decomposition method switched from SVD to Tucker" but the researchers' understanding of what shape of object the KV cache actually is. Early on, everyone treated it as a collection of 2-D matrices — sliced by head, sliced by layer. Only gradually did it become clear that it is a naturally 3-D (or higher-dimensional) tensor whose three axes carry vastly unequal amounts of redundancy, and that using the same ruler on all of them wastes opportunity by construction. The quantization line pursued a completely different dimension (numerical precision rather than structural redundancy), and JoLT's contribution is, in a sense, the first real stitching-together of these two parallel threads: tensor decomposition to tackle structural redundancy, and JL residuals to cover the "last mile" of numerical precision. The next step will most likely move toward "finer-grained joint modeling" — for instance, should the residual itself be handled separately along each of the three axes? Should different layers use different decomposition ranks? Can this framework be integrated with dynamic runtime eviction strategies (the H2O/StreamingLLM line) rather than remaining orthogonal to them?

* Inspiration

Looking at this evolutionary line, every breakthrough began by admitting "last time we oversimplified": Palu admitted that quantization and eviction never touched the hidden dimension; xKV admitted that Palu never touched the cross-layer axis; JoLT admitted that both predecessors were still viewing a 3-D object through a 2-D lens. The takeaway: when you encounter an old problem that has "already been compressed and seems mined out," it is worth asking again — how many dimensions does the current method assume this data structure has? If you restore it to its native, higher-dimensional shape, are there corners of redundancy that have never been squeezed?
