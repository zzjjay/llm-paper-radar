* The Problem River

Fitting a long-context LLM into GPU memory: KV cache is now the number-one killer. It grows linearly with sequence length — tens of thousands of tokens arrive and immediately devour HBM, batch sizes stay small, throughput never takes off.

Can we compress KV cache down to 2 bits, the way we compress images? It sounds simple, but five years of effort haven't produced a clean solution. The difficulty is this: certain "channels" in the Key tensor naturally carry values dozens of times larger than others (channel-wise outliers). Quantizing along the token dimension gets blown up by these channels; quantizing along the channel dimension avoids that pitfall, but at extreme compression ratios like 2 bits another pitfall emerges — within the same channel, the "magnitudes" of different tokens vary wildly (Token Norm Imbalance, TNI), and a shared scale cannot cover all tokens.

Generation after generation of researchers has been jumping back and forth between these two pitfalls: quantize along tokens or along channels? Apply a rotation first to smooth out outliers? Add a residual correction on top? The pipeline grows ever more complex, and runtime grows ever slower. OScaR says: first diagnose the root cause (TNI), then fix it with the fewest possible parts.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022 | LLM.int8(): Discovering "emergent outliers" in LLMs — naive quantization is doomed

*** What problem did it see

Before LLM.int8(), quantizing CNNs and small transformers was mostly painless — cutting FP16 down to INT8 barely hurt accuracy. But when Dettmers scaled models up to 6.7B parameters, something strange appeared: a small number of feature dimensions (channels) carried values that were frighteningly large, often more than 100× the rest. The limited dynamic range of INT8 simply cannot contain them — you either set the scale large enough to preserve the outliers (everything else collapses to zero) or small enough to preserve everything else (outliers overflow). No setting works.

These outliers are not bugs; they emerge naturally once the model reaches a certain scale, appearing in almost every layer and triggered by almost every token.

*** Its solution

Since these outlier channels are a minority (under 1%), just pull them out and handle them separately: compute outlier channels in FP16 at full precision, run the remaining 99% in INT8, then sum the two streams.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfall it buries)

The intuition is direct: separate "the hard-to-treat minority" from "the easy-to-treat majority" and handle each appropriately. But the engineering cost is steep — mixed-precision computation means splicing two kernels together, which is extremely hardware-unfriendly and makes GPU execution choppy. The positions of outlier channels also vary with input, preventing static optimization. This pitfall was inherited by the next paper.

** 2023 | SmoothQuant: Shifting outlier difficulty from activations to weights

*** What problem did it see

Xiao studied the tensor structure of LLMs carefully: outliers appear exclusively in activations (intermediate values flowing through the forward pass); weights are flat and easy to quantize. The reason LLM.int8() needs mixed-precision is that it accepts "these activation outliers cannot be cured." But the weight side is idle — one hand doing two jobs while the other does nothing.

*** Its solution

Introduce a per-channel scale factor s. Divide activations by s (suppressing the outliers) and multiply the adjacent weights by s (transferring the difficulty to the weights). Mathematically the output is unchanged (s and 1/s cancel), but the quantization difficulty is "smoothly" distributed across both sides — making both INT8-quantizable.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfall it buries)

Think of a seesaw: one side is heavy, the other light. Adjust s to balance them and the whole thing quantizes cleanly. SmoothQuant became the standard baseline for all subsequent LLM quantization work. But its view was still limited to the matrix-multiply scenario of "weight × activation." In long-context inference, the dominant memory consumer is neither weights nor activations — it is the KV cache: as the decoder computes new tokens it caches K and V, and that cache grows linearly with context length. SmoothQuant does not specifically address this.

** 2024 | KIVI: First applying "per-channel quantization" to KV cache

*** What problem did it see

Liu turned the lens to KV cache and noticed that Key and Value behave very differently:

- Key tensors have channel-wise outliers similar to activations — a few channels are inherently large;
- Value tensors lack this structure and are relatively uniform.

If you follow conventional practice and quantize per-token (one scale group per token), then any outlier token in a given Key channel blows up the scale for that entire token.

*** Its solution

Treat them differently:
- Keys: per-channel quantization (channels share a scale; outlier channels get their own large scale);
- Values: per-token quantization (preserving incremental-update friendliness for autoregressive decoding).

Together these two choices define the KIVI paradigm — the foundational work in KV cache quantization.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfall it buries)

The intuition: wherever outliers concentrate, slice along that dimension. Key outliers concentrate in the channel dimension, so per-channel naturally solves it. Values have no outlier structure, so per-token is the natural fit. This paradigm runs well at 4 bits.

But at extreme compression — 2 or 3 bits — KIVI starts to collapse. The reason is that per-channel solves "imbalance across channels," but no one handles the variation within each channel across different tokens (token 100's norm in a given channel might be 10× that of token 500). KIVI never clearly diagnosed this; it only noted "accuracy drops sharply at extreme compression."

** 2024 | QuaRot: Using Hadamard rotation to spread outliers flat

*** What problem did it see

Ashkboos followed up on KIVI with a thought: per-channel quantization was forced upon us by outlier channels. What if there simply were no outlier channels? Wouldn't per-token work then?

Outliers are "concentrated in a few channels" — the problem is uneven distribution.

*** Its solution

Borrow an ancient mathematical tool: the Hadamard matrix — an orthogonal matrix filled entirely with +1 and −1. Multiplying Keys by a Hadamard matrix randomly adds and subtracts all channels together, spreading the outlier energy across every channel. After mixing, every channel is roughly the same size, and per-token quantization can proceed.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfall it buries)

Intuition: imagine a bag of flour with all the pebbles clumped in one corner. Shake the bag until the pebbles distribute evenly — the whole bag "looks uniform." The Hadamard is orthogonal, so multiplying by its inverse at the end of the attention computation recovers the original result exactly, preserving mathematical equivalence.

QuaRot was primarily a weight + activation quantization tool; when applied to KV cache it mainly followed a per-token approach. The cost: the rotation must run on-the-fly at inference time, adding an extra operator. More importantly, rotation only redistributes imbalance along the channel dimension; imbalance along the token dimension — precisely what OScaR later calls TNI — remains untreated.

** 2025 | TurboQuant: Going to the extreme — per-element + random rotation + residual correction

*** What problem did it see

QuaRot and KIVI still show substantial loss at 2 bits. Zandieh and collaborators reasoned: the direction is right, but not extreme enough. Can a finer granularity (per-element, one scale per scalar) combined with theoretically guaranteed random rotations push the error down toward the information-theoretic lower bound?

*** Its solution

TurboQuant extends the pipeline to its maximum length:
1. Multiple random rotations (not a single Hadamard, but a sequence of random orthogonal transforms);
2. Quantization;
3. Residual error correction (the quantization error is separately encoded and appended);
4. Corresponding inverse operations at the decode side.

The cost: more parameters, more kernels, additional structures packed into memory, significant extra computation at every step. But the theory is beautiful: it can be shown to approach the optimal rate-distortion curve.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfall it buries)

Intuition: take "things that are hard to compress" and dilute them round after round with statistical tricks (randomization + residual compensation) until every piece is a small, easy-to-quantize slice.

But the problem with this approach became increasingly apparent: in engineering practice, "longer pipeline = slower inference" is nearly an iron law. TurboQuant is SOTA on paper benchmarks, but at deployment its own complexity drags it down; combined with the unavoidable on-the-fly quantization overhead, measured throughput is not always better than KIVI's. It also left a more fundamental question unanswered: at extreme compression, exactly which structural property creates the error? Everyone was piling on methods; no one was clearly identifying the lesion.

** 2026 | OScaR: First name the lesion (TNI), then cure it with the fewest parts

*** What problem did it see

The OScaR authors, having worked through KIVI, QuaRot, and TurboQuant, stepped back and asked: why does per-channel quantization break at extreme compression?

They analyzed token-wise norm distributions across multiple models and modalities, and observed a universal phenomenon: within a single Key channel, the norms of different tokens can vary enormously — some tokens at the 0.1 scale, others at the 10 scale. Per-channel quantization shares one scale across all tokens; that scale must either accommodate the large-norm tokens (crushing small-norm tokens to zero) or the small-norm tokens (overflowing the large-norm ones). Neither choice works.

They named this phenomenon *Token Norm Imbalance (TNI)* and provided a theoretical derivation showing that under the per-channel framework, TNI systematically amplifies quantization error in an unavoidable way.

This is the true reason KIVI collapses at 2 bits. It is also why QuaRot — which flattened the channel dimension — still couldn't save things: TNI lives along the token dimension, and a rotation along the channel dimension cannot cure it.

*** Its solution

With TNI clearly diagnosed, the fix targets token-dimension imbalance directly:

1. *Canalized Rotation*: Apply a Hadamard rotation first. This step is not meant to cure TNI; it prevents the subsequent token scaling from being contaminated by a side effect called "Scaling-Induced Outlier Artifact" — if you scale the raw Key directly along the token dimension, the scale factors interact with existing channel-dimension outliers and generate new spurious outliers. Rotating first flattens the channels so the scaling step remains clean.

2. *Omni-Token Scaling*: Normalize along the sequence (token) dimension so that all token norms land in the same range. This step directly eliminates TNI — since the per-channel scale is shared, first bring all tokens to comparable magnitudes so the shared scale can contain them.

The two steps together are layered on top of KIVI's per-channel paradigm. No additional training, no residual correction, no repeated random rotations. Ablation studies confirm both are necessary: rotation alone or scaling alone both lose accuracy.

Combined with a carefully optimized CUDA kernel (a fused Hadamard-Norm kernel), the system is genuinely deployable:

- INT2 quantization with near-lossless accuracy;
- 3.0× decode speedup over BF16 FlashDecoding-v2;
- 1/5.3 memory footprint;
- 4.1× throughput.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfall it buries)

The intuition laid out as a table:

| Dimension | Imbalance name | Treatment |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|
| Channel dimension | Channel-wise outlier | Hadamard rotation (QuaRot's original move) |
| Token dimension | Token Norm Imbalance (TNI) | Sequence-level normalization (OScaR's new move) |

OScaR does not invent new tools; it pairs two straightforward operations — "rotate then scale" — and clearly diagnoses what each one cures. This is Occam's Razor in practice: prior work kept adding things; OScaR subtracted.

What new pitfalls remain? The paper is honest:

- The TNI diagnosis focuses primarily on the Key tensor; TNI handling for the Value tensor is still relatively simple.
- Omni-Token Scaling normalizes along the sequence dimension; the prefill-phase implementation requires careful engineering (hence the custom CUDA kernel design).
- TNI distribution characteristics across modalities (multi-modal / omni-modal) are only in their early stages.

* Frontier Extensions

OScaR is a preprint posted in 2026-05 ("Under review"), so there is as yet no subsequent work explicitly critiquing or improving upon it. But the directions most likely to follow are already visible in OScaR's own references:

- *TurboQuant+ (Pope, 2026)*: A continuation of TurboQuant, pushing the per-element direction further and seeking to reduce engineering overhead. OScaR's Appendix K provides a dedicated complexity comparison — TurboQuant+ retains a theoretical advantage in certain settings, but its measured cost is far higher than OScaR's. These two lines (minimal per-channel vs. maximal per-element) will coexist in the near term.

- *OTT (Su et al., 2025a)*: An earlier work from the same lab, representing a different optimization direction within the per-channel paradigm. OScaR is its spiritual successor.

- *Multi-modal / omni-modal quantization*: OScaR is the first work to evaluate X-LLMs (text + multi-modal + omni-modal) as a unified framework. The next wave of work will likely refine the TNI diagnosis for visual tokens and audio tokens — the token norm distributions of different modalities are potentially far more divergent from each other, an unexplored corner.

* Insights

Reading the full arc, three things surface:

1. *Diagnosis is more valuable than tools.* From KIVI to TurboQuant, five years of increasingly elaborate tools: rotation, random rotation, residual correction, per-element scale. But no one stopped to ask "where exactly does per-channel quantization break at extreme compression?" OScaR's only genuinely new contribution is giving a name to a phenomenon that already existed (TNI) and providing a theoretical analysis. Once the diagnosis is right, the cure is naturally short — Canalized Rotation is an old tool, Omni-Token Scaling is a textbook normalization. The scarcest resource in research is not new methods; it is new perspectives.

2. *Occam's Razor is especially sharp in engineering.* TurboQuant is mathematically beautiful but collapsed under its own complexity at deployment. A solution like OScaR that "gets it done in two steps" wins because every step corresponds to a clear diagnosis rather than a stack of insurance policies. Simplicity is not an aesthetic preference; it is a cheaper source of engineering robustness — one fewer component means one fewer bug source and one fewer kernel optimization burden.

3. *The essence of imbalance problems is the choice of slice dimension.* Slice along channels or along tokens? This binary framing misses the right question: "which dimension's imbalance is more structural — that one should be treated first." KIVI chose channels because channel imbalance was more visible. OScaR found that once channel-dimension imbalance was cured, token-dimension imbalance (TNI) was the next wall. Research progress often amounts to "looking at the same problem along a different dimension."

Where is the frontier most likely to move next:

- TNI distribution patterns in multi-modal / omni-modal settings (visual and text tokens mixed together, with potentially far more dramatic norm divergence);
- Migrating the "diagnose first, then apply a minimal cure" paradigm from KV cache quantization to other LLM inference bottlenecks (attention sinks, weight outliers, activation outliers);
- The per-element and per-channel lines will not produce a clear winner in the near term, but per-channel — because of its simplicity — will capture the engineering landscape first.

* Takeaways

The most direct lesson this evolutionary arc offers for doing research is: *in a domain where a tool race is at full heat, stepping back to look at the lesion is usually the highest-ROI move.*

In the five years from KIVI to TurboQuant, everyone was adding things — rotation, random rotation, residual correction, per-element scale. OScaR added no genuinely new tool. What it did was:

1. Run extensive experiments examining distributions, and find that "token norms within the same channel vary dramatically" is a universal phenomenon;
2. Give that phenomenon a name (TNI);
3. Derive a theory showing that "per-channel + TNI necessarily explodes";
4. Use the most primitive tools available (Hadamard + normalization) to fix it.

This suggests you can:

- In any domain you care about, stop and ask: "What disease exactly are the tools everyone uses treating? Has that disease ever been defined? Does it have a name?" — if not, defining it is itself a contribution;
- When you see "methods growing ever more complex while gains approach a ceiling," resist the urge to pile in and instead ask "are we treating the wrong disease?";
- Simplicity is not aesthetics; it is the source of engineering robustness. Two components, each with a clear job description, beats five components stacked together to claim SOTA;
- In contexts related to long-context / multi-modal large model deployment, KV cache quantization will continue to evolve rapidly in the near term; tracking TNI follow-up work and quantization benchmarks on X-LLMs is a worthwhile investment.
