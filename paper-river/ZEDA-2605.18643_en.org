* The Problem River

A MoE model has 128 experts, and each token picks 8 of them to do the work. The problem: every token is treated equally — even writing a punctuation mark summons all 8 experts. Could simpler tokens get away with fewer, saving inference compute?

Why is this hard? You cannot simply "chop half off." The routing weights among experts are learned bit by bit during training — who is good at what, who pairs with whom, all of it is encoded in the router. Forcibly reducing the number of active experts is like smashing a well-trained collaboration network. Simple tasks might survive, but math or code — which require several experts working in concert — collapse immediately.

What makes it worse: today's large models don't just stop at pre-training. They go through a full pipeline of SFT, reinforcement learning, and on-policy distillation, carefully sculpting every capability. Tampering with routing on such a "finished product" is equivalent to knocking out a craftsman's inherited skill. The thread running through this whole story is: *how do you make per-token compute dynamic without destroying what has already been trained?*

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Shazeer et al.: Plugging "On-Demand Expert Dispatch" into Neural Networks

*** What problem it saw

In that era there were only two ways to make a bigger model: either add parameters and run every token through all of them (prohibitively expensive), or keep parameters small and accept a less capable model. There was no third option. The bottleneck was the assumption that capacity and compute must scale together linearly.

*** Its solution

Replace one layer of the network with N expert sub-networks, plus a small router that decides which K experts each token should go to. N can be large (hundreds or thousands); K stays small (2–4). Result: parameters grow tens of times over, while per-token computation stays almost constant.

Analogy: A company used to assign the entire staff to every customer; now a receptionist listens for one sentence and routes the customer to just 2 relevant people.

*** Why it worked (and what it left buried)

It worked because different tokens genuinely need different knowledge — "the" and "Heisenberg" use different circuits. Making the network physically separate this saves enormous redundancy.

New trap: The router was hard to train stably, and experts easily became imbalanced (a few star experts overloaded, others idle). And K is fixed — *every token uses exactly the same number of experts.* The next generation of problems grew right there.

** 2020–21 | GShard / Switch Transformer: Porting MoE to Transformers and Stabilizing It

*** What problem it saw

Shazeer's approach worked on LSTMs but broke down on Transformers with distributed training: top-2 routing was complex, loads were severely imbalanced, and training frequently crashed. At scale, the router collapsed first.

*** Its solution

Switch Transformer made a bold move: simplify routing to top-1 (each token goes to exactly one expert). GShard kept top-K but added an *auxiliary load-balancing loss* — forcing traffic to spread evenly across all experts. Layered on top of distributed orchestration, this pushed model scale to trillions of parameters.

*** Why it worked (and what it left buried)

The auxiliary loss trick became MoE's standard recipe: if you want something to happen (uniform expert distribution), write a dedicated loss to encourage it. Problem solved, but one *fundamental assumption went untouched* — *K is always a constant.* That means every token, whether "." or "integral," uses exactly as many experts. That is the window the next paper would smash.

** 2024 | MoE++: Letting Simple Tokens Burn Less Compute on Their Own

*** What problem it saw

Empirical data showed: more than half of tokens can be predicted correctly using only 1–2 experts, yet the architecture forces all of them through K. That portion of compute is wasted.

But you cannot simply tell the router to "adjust K by token difficulty" — dynamic K makes training extremely unstable and expert assignment collapses.

*** Its solution

Elegant: *don't change K, change the expert pool.* Add a set of "zero experts" alongside the N real experts — their output is always 0, they carry no parameters. Top-K still selects K candidates, but if m of them are zero experts, only K−m real experts actually do anything.

Analogy: The front desk still calls 8 staff members for every call, but a few of them are mannequins — if you reach a mannequin the call ends immediately without service. Simple customers naturally get routed to mannequins more often, and compute drops accordingly.

*** Why it worked (and what it left buried)

The elegance lies in recasting "dynamic compute" as "enlarged candidate pool" — the router interface is unchanged, top-K is unchanged, and the auxiliary loss needs only minor tweaks. Zero experts are purely empty slots, with nearly zero engineering overhead.

New trap: MoE++ requires *pre-training from scratch*. Its entire routing distribution is learned with zero experts present from the very beginning. The problem: there are already many well-trained MoE models (Mixtral, DeepSeek, etc.), and retraining from scratch costs a fortune. Can we do this on existing models instead?

** 2024 | AdaMoE / Dynamic Skipping: Bringing Dynamic Routing to Existing Models

*** What problem it saw

Pre-training a large MoE model from scratch costs millions of dollars. MoE++'s requirement to start over made it impractical for most users. Lu et al.'s Dynamic Skipping observed that in trained MoE models many expert activations contribute almost nothing, so skipping them at inference barely changes output.

*** Its solution

Two ideas:
- *AdaMoE*: Add a group of "null experts" (functionally close to zero experts) and fine-tune the base model lightly so it learns to use them.
- *Dynamic Skipping*: No training at all — just set a threshold on routing weights at inference time and skip experts whose weights fall below it.

Both belong to the "low-cost retrofitting of existing MoE" family.

*** Why it worked (and what it left buried)

On base pre-trained models or single-task settings, these approaches can indeed halve compute without much accuracy loss. But ZEDA ran a critical experiment: applying both methods to *fully post-trained* Qwen3-30B-A3B and GLM-4.7-Flash —

- AdaMoE on Qwen: overall average drops from 74.9 to 54.8. AIME 24 collapses from 80.9 to 25.
- Dynamic Skipping: math holds up somewhat, but code generation (HumanEval+ / MBPP+) degrades sharply.

Why? Because post-training (SFT + RL + OPD) carves very fine-grained structure into the router — which experts handle mathematical reasoning, which handle code syntax, how they collaborate. AdaMoE's uniformity-driven auxiliary loss forcibly flattens that structure; Dynamic Skipping may blindly skip exactly the critical nodes for certain tasks. That is where the next paper begins: *how do you reshape routing without erasing the muscle memory that post-training built?*

** 2026 | ZEDA: Teaching the Model to Use Fewer Experts by Teaching Itself

*** What problem it saw

The diagnosis has three specific counts:
1. The routing distribution of a post-trained MoE is *already non-uniform by design* (each expert's role is settled); imposing a uniformity loss is pure damage.
2. Exposing the model to an external teacher or new data is effectively "re-training it" — either expensive or introducing distribution shift.
3. Adaptive-K calibrated on base models breaks when transferred to instruct/think mode — post-trained capabilities are more fragile than pre-trained ones.

*** Its solution

Four moves, each aimed at disturbing the original model as little as possible:

1. *Zero-expert injection*: Reuse MoE++'s zero-expert idea directly (no reinventing the wheel). The routing weights of the original N experts are left completely untouched. The weights for the newly added NZ zero experts are *initialized by sampling from the mean and variance of the original weight distribution* — ensuring that zero experts are neither manically over-selected nor completely ignored from the start.

2. *Self-distillation*: No external teacher. *The original model teaches itself.* The student (the version with injected zero experts) is trained to mimic the output distribution of the original model. In effect the training data is generated by the model itself — there is no external distribution shift. The student is simply learning "how to approximate my old self under constrained compute."

3. *Two stages*: First SFT (student learns from answers generated by the teacher) to let the new architecture settle; then OPD (on-policy distillation: student rolls out its own trajectories, teacher scores them) to stabilize alignment. The reverse-KL objective from the Thinking Machines paper is used as the reward signal inside policy optimization.

4. *Group Auxiliary Loss*: The key innovation. Traditional auxiliary loss flattens all experts and destroys routing structure. ZEDA splits experts into two groups — "real experts" and "zero experts" — and *only balances between groups*, leaving the internal distribution within each group entirely free. A weight w controls the target zero-expert activation rate (w=2 targets 50%).

Analogy: You want "on average half the calls handled by mannequins," but you don't want to disturb the workload split among real staff at all — so you only regulate "total real-staff calls vs. total mannequin calls," leaving the internal distribution within each group alone.

*** Why it worked (and what it left buried)

In practice: Qwen3-30B-A3B zero-expert activation rate 51.2% (halving expert compute), overall average 74.2 vs. original 74.9, less than 1 point drop. +6.1 over the strongest baseline. GLM-4.7-Flash shows similar results. End-to-end inference speedup 1.20×. The whole run takes 31 hours on 8× H200.

A more interesting finding: zero-expert activation frequency correlates *not with token difficulty* but strongly with *the divergence between teacher and student distributions.* In other words, the model is not judging "is this token hard?" but rather "how certain am I about this token?" — when certain, it hands off to zero experts. That insight is worth more than the speedup itself.

Remaining gaps: (1) Only validated at 30B–100B scale; DeepSeek-V4-level 600B+ is untested. (2) Speedup degrades on long sequences (KV cache dominates cost). (3) Not evaluated on long-chain agentic tasks.

* Frontier Extensions

ZEDA only appeared in May 2026, so downstream papers have not yet formed. The paper itself points to three clear directions:

- *Stability on larger models.* Qwen3-30B and GLM-4.7-Flash are 30–100B. At DeepSeek-V4-level sparsity (more experts, sparser activation), the right zero-expert ratio and routing stability are open questions.
- *Long-context / agentic tasks.* Current evaluation is mainly on math, code, and single-turn instructions. Can it hold up across multi-turn tool use and long reasoning chains?
- *Combination with dynamic-K routing.* ZEDA still steals compute within a fixed top-K framework. The next step may fuse Ada-K Routing or Expert Threshold Routing — adding explicit dynamic K on top — to stack both levels of dynamism.

Parallel papers worth reading alongside: DynMoE (auto-adjusts both N and K), Expert Threshold Routing (replaces top-K with a threshold), LongCat-Flash (Meituan pushing the zero-expert idea to industrial scale). The biggest open question on this whole line is: *can dynamic sparsification enter the pre-training pipeline instead of being a post-hoc patch?* If so, the window for "post-processing" methods like ZEDA may be quite short.

* Insights

Three things become clear after reading this line from end to end.

*First, every generation "saves" compute, but what is being saved keeps going deeper.* Early MoE saved "across experts" (only K active at once). MoE++ saved "within K" (some of those K slots are empty). ZEDA saves "the saving process itself" (how to save quickly without damaging existing capability). Optimization is climbing to the meta-level.

*Second, "non-destructive retrofitting" is the increasingly dominant paradigm on this line.* In the Shazeer era everyone trained from scratch and wasn't worried about disturbance. GShard used auxiliary losses to forcibly reshape distributions. AdaMoE began to tread carefully, touching as little as possible. ZEDA goes all the way: let the model teach itself — the *minimum-disturbance principle* has upgraded to "disturbance may only come from the model itself." This is the inevitable consequence of post-trained models becoming precious — a finished product is worth orders of magnitude more than a raw checkpoint, and nobody can afford to melt it down.

*Third, our mental model of "dynamic compute" has flipped.* Everyone assumed dynamic compute allocation was about distinguishing "hard tokens" from "easy tokens." ZEDA's analysis found that what the model actually distinguishes is "how certain I am about this token" — unrelated to the objective difficulty of the input. This means *the criterion for compute allocation is the model's internal state, not the objective properties of the input.* If that holds, future dynamic MoE designs should stop revolving around "token difficulty estimators."

Where next? Two directions seem most likely: (a) *Generalize ZEDA's self-distillation idea to other "post-hoc retrofitting" scenarios* — e.g., converting dense models to MoE, or full attention to sparse attention (the paper itself already mentions RAD/HALO doing exactly this). (b) *Bake "self-distillation-friendly" structure into pre-training directly* — so end users at deployment time only need 30 hours to adapt, rather than re-running ZEDA's full procedure from scratch every time.

* Takeaways

For researchers: *Don't patch the hard flaws of an existing method — find the free lunch hiding in an assumption it never stated but silently made.* MoE++'s zero experts are exactly that: does the K in top-K actually have to be a constant? Nobody said so. ZEDA goes further: does the "teacher" in self-distillation have to be a stronger external model? Nobody said so — the original model itself will do. Both breakthroughs came not from piling on resources but from questioning a boundary everyone had silently accepted.

For engineers: *Respect the existing structure first, then discuss how to modify it.* AdaMoE and Dynamic Skipping failed not because they were technically weak, but because they didn't realize that post-training leaves not just numbers in the router — it leaves *structure.* When modifying any mature system, first ask "which parts are parameters (changeable) and which are structure (untouchable)?" ZEDA's group-level auxiliary loss is precisely that awareness translated into a loss function.

For product builders: *Inference cost is the chokepoint of LLM services. Whoever can cut it in half without dropping accuracy controls pricing power.* This line of work will not slow down in the next three to five years — dynamic K, sparse attention, speculative decoding, quantization — all directions that "save compute without losing accuracy" are converging. The pitch: "You can serve 1.2× the concurrent requests on a single H200, without retraining the model, and with zero visibility to the end user."
