* The River of the Problem

A deceptively simple question: you have trained a large language model with hundreds of attention heads. If you had to cut half of them to save compute, how would you choose?

Cutting the important ones is clearly wrong; cutting the "useless" ones sounds right — but which ones are useless? This question has been chased for nearly a decade. Early answers were "look at weight magnitude," then "look at activation magnitude," then "look at gradient sensitivity." Each generation improved on the previous generation's metric. Yet nobody answered a deeper question: *why does a head become useless in the first place?*

If you do not know why a head is "useless," you can only guess — guess by weights, guess by activations, guess by intuition. Guess right and the model survives the cut; guess wrong and it collapses.

This line of inquiry finally reached a counterintuitive finding in 2026: those "useless" heads are actually doing something concrete and identifiable — they are the model's *garbage bins*. This paper names the garbage bins, turning head pruning from guessing into recognition.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2019 | Voita & Michel: Most of Multi-Head Attention Is Padding

*** What Problem It Saw

Vaswani's 2017 Attention Is All You Need locked in multi-head attention: 8 heads per layer, 12 heads, 16 heads — nobody explained why those numbers. The default assumption was "more heads is better; each head learns a different pattern."

Voita and Michel (independently, nearly simultaneously) found that assumption was wrong. On machine-translation Transformers they ablated heads one by one — drop a head, measure the BLEU drop. The result was striking: removing 38 of 48 encoder heads cost only 0.15 BLEU. On some datasets, 4 heads could carry the full load of 48.

In other words, *the majority of the attention mechanism is redundant*. But this was a post-hoc discovery, not a pre-hoc selection criterion.

*** Their Solution

Each group proposed a scoring method. Voita used a differentiable gate variable with L0 regularization during training, letting the model shut off unimportant heads itself. Michel computed a gradient-based importance score — the gradient of loss with respect to each head's mask — and pruned from lowest to highest.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Pit)

It worked because the redundancy is real: regardless of which score you use to rank heads, the bottom-ranked ones truly contribute little.

The new pit: *both methods require gradients or additional training*. They tell you "this head is unimportant" but not "why it is unimportant." The next generation needed to answer two things: (1) Can you identify redundant heads without retraining? (2) What are those redundant heads actually doing?

** 2023 | Wanda & SparseGPT: No Retraining — Use "Magnitude" to Choose

*** What Problem It Saw

By 2023, in the LLM era, Voita-style "add gates during training" was prohibitively expensive — you cannot retrain a 70B model. A *post-training* method (prune after training, nothing else) was needed.

But simple weight magnitude had already been shown to fail: LLMs contain emergent large-magnitude features whose activation values are enormous; prune them and the model collapses.

*** Their Solution

SparseGPT (Frantar) uses second-order reconstruction: treat each layer as a problem of minimizing reconstruction error, use the inverse Hessian to select which connections to prune. Accurate but slow.

Wanda (Sun et al.) simplifies elegantly: forget weight magnitude alone; use *weight magnitude × input activation norm*. That product is the connection's "true influence." A single forward pass produces the scores — 300× faster than SparseGPT, with comparable quality.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Pit)

It worked because the activation norm captures emergent large features, so connections amplified by large activations are not mistakenly pruned.

The pit remained unfilled: you are still using *magnitude* as a proxy. Large = important, small = useless — this assumption holds most of the time, but you cannot explain *why*. If a class of heads "looks busy" (non-trivial attention scores) yet is actually doing nothing, magnitude-based methods cannot catch them.

That is precisely the crack the next paper pried open.

** 2023 | StreamingLLM: All Attention Drains to the First Few Tokens

*** What Problem It Saw

This paper was not aimed at pruning at all; it was pursuing long-context inference using sliding-window attention to run LLMs over unlimited length. It stumbled on something strange: as soon as the window evicted the very first tokens, the model immediately began hallucinating.

Xiao examined the attention maps carefully and found an anomaly: *from the second layer onward, nearly all attention flows to the first few tokens* — even if those tokens carry no semantics, even if you replace them with newline characters. The model stares at them relentlessly.

They named the phenomenon *attention sink*.

*** Their Solution

They offered a clean explanation: Softmax forces attention distributions to sum to 1. If the current query has no strong match with any historical token, the model still has to put that "leftover attention" somewhere. It deposits it into the first few tokens — because only the initial tokens are *visible to all subsequent queries*, making them natural "public garbage bins."

The practical fix was simple: when using a sliding window, always keep the first 4 tokens; long-context stability is preserved.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Pit)

It worked because retaining "garbage-bin tokens" gives the softmax normalization pressure a safe destination.

The new pit: now you know *certain tokens are garbage bins*, but you do not know *which heads* are responsible for depositing into them. If a head routes 95% of its attention to BOS, what is it actually doing? Redundancy? Structural bias? Something useful that is just invisible?

That question waited three years to be seriously asked.

** 2024 | Massive Activations: Those Tokens Store the Model's Implicit Bias

*** What Problem It Saw

Sun's group (the same Sun behind Wanda) was analyzing LLM internal states and discovered a deeper phenomenon: at the token positions "receiving" large amounts of attention, *the hidden-layer activations are enormous* — up to one hundred thousand times larger than average.

They ran a stark experiment: in LLaMA2-7B, zeroing out four massive activations caused immediate collapse. But setting them to their mean values (a constant) caused almost no accuracy drop.

This means: *these giant activations are not transmitting information — they are constant biases*. During pre-training the model grew several "bias slots" for itself, borrowing the positions of special tokens to store them.

*** Their Solution

Sun proposed adding explicit attention-bias parameters to the model. After training with explicit biases, the massive activations disappeared.

In other words, attention sink, massive activation, and implicit bias are three manifestations of the same underlying thing.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Pit)

It worked because it identified the root cause: softmax normalization pressure + the model's need to "store constants" → borrow special-token positions as storage.

The new pit — and the precise starting point of the 2026 paper — is this: if BOS-type tokens are *public garbage bins and implicit bias stores*, then what is an attention head doing if it *almost exclusively deposits attention into that position*?

The answer should be: *it is not making substantive predictions; it is simply routing excess probability mass into the garbage bin to satisfy normalization*. In other words — it is an empty head.

If this reasoning holds, selecting pruning targets requires no magnitude inspection at all — just look at which head has the highest BOS attention fraction.

** 2026 | BOSSink: Name the Garbage-Bin Heads and Prune Them

*** What Problem It Saw

Looking back at prior pruning generations: Voita/Michel knew redundancy existed but not why; Wanda used magnitude as a proxy but could not explain the principle; StreamingLLM found the sink phenomenon but only used it for long-context; Sun explained that sink is bias but did not apply it to compression.

Sok et al. connected the chain: *the sink phenomenon itself is the functional label of redundant heads*. A head that consistently routes most of its attention to BOS is not doing prediction — it is doing normalization garbage disposal.

So the fundamental flaw of all prior magnitude methods is: they try to *infer* whether a head is useful from "how large are its weights / activations," but there is a class of head whose magnitude is not necessarily small — it looks like it is working, but it is just clocking in.

*** Their Solution

Define a direct metric: each head's *BOS sink score* = the fraction of attention that head allocates to the BOS token, averaged over multiple inputs.

Sort from high to low; high-scoring heads are garbage-bin heads; prune them directly. No gradients required, no retraining, no reconstruction.

Validated on Gemma-3, Llama-3.1, and Qwen3: especially in deep layers, the sparse structures produced by this score preserve quality better than Wanda-style weight/activation-based methods, and even under aggressive pruning remain close to dense performance. Moreover, sink behavior is stable across sequence lengths — no need to re-score.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Pit)

It worked because it provides, for the first time, a *functional* explanation for "why this head can be pruned" rather than a statistical proxy. What is pruned is not "the head that looks small" but "the head visibly doing a specific pointless job."

The new pit — the one the next paper stabs at — is: if a head became a BOS sink head because *it was forced there by structure* (e.g., positional-encoding-induced collapse), then it is not intrinsically useless; it is trapped. Pruning it discards potential capacity.

* Frontier Extensions

** 2026 (Same-Year Follow-up) | Dormant Is Not Redundant

Immediately following Sok's line of thinking, another group performed the counter-argument on the BLOOM model family: the collapse of BOS sink heads is not redundancy — it is the steep distance penalty imposed by ALiBi positional encoding at certain head indices that structurally forces those heads into sink behavior.

Their experiment: instead of pruning these heads, *re-initialize their weights and retrain with gradient masking*. Two rounds of training recover 98.7% of functional capacity — *and produce behavior qualitatively different from the original model*.

The implication: sink heads are dormant capacity, not redundant capacity. Their weights are present, their positions are present, their contribution to the residual stream approaches zero — so the pruning cost is low. But rescuing them can give the model capabilities it never possessed.

These two papers are not contradictory; they address two sides of the same coin: *given no retraining*, the BOSSink pruning criterion is correct; *given willingness to do light retraining*, the head should be rescued rather than removed.

The most current open question along this line is: is sink behavior something the model actively chooses as a normalization mechanism, or is it a byproduct of positional encoding? Is it an inevitable consequence of softmax, or does it only emerge under certain training dynamics?

* Insights

On the surface this line of work is about "how to make LLMs smaller." What is really happening underneath is *compression shifting from reliance on statistical proxies to reliance on functional explanations*.

Seven years ago, pruning heads meant "let gradients tell me who is unimportant." Four years ago it meant "multiply weights by activations and cut the small ones." Today it means "cut whoever whose job is taking out the garbage." Each generation did not invent a new algorithm — it pushed the question "why can this head be pruned?" one layer deeper. Voita could not answer it; Wanda could not answer it; BOSSink answers it for the first time at the *mechanistic* level.

The deeper shift is that the research community has begun to accept that *LLMs contain structurally non-functional components by design*. BOS sink heads are not bugs; they are "infrastructure" the model actively grew — to absorb softmax normalization pressure and store constant biases. Once you accept that this infrastructure exists, you can treat it either as a pruning target (currently idle) or as reactivatable capacity (currently dormant).

The most likely next step: stop asking "can this head be pruned?" and start asking "what force pushed this head into this state?" Positional encoding? Training objective? Data distribution? Only by controlling the causes of collapse can capacity be *allocated* during pre-training rather than surgically removed after the fact.

* Takeaways

Three research-process lessons from this line of work:

First, *phenomenon observation precedes optimization*. StreamingLLM was trying to do long-context inference and accidentally discovered the sink phenomenon. Massive Activations was doing internal analysis and accidentally found the bias slots. Two findings that "seemed unrelated to pruning" became the core pruning criterion three years later. If you focus only on incremental local optimization of the same class of problems on the SOTA leaderboard, this kind of cross-problem grafting will never happen.

Second, *substitution is not the destination — explanation is*. Wanda is 300× faster than SparseGPT at comparable quality — already a top-venue paper. But it did not answer "why magnitude is a good proxy," so its position was always destined to be displaced by a method that could (BOSSink). If what you build is a *faster, better proxy*, the next generation of explanatory methods will overturn it.

Third, *the same phenomenon can yield two papers from opposite directions*. BOSSink says sink heads should be pruned. A same-year paper says they should be rescued. Both are correct — *within their respective constraints*. Two apparently contradictory conclusions in research usually differ only in their implicit assumptions. Learning to unpack assumptions and make conditions explicit is more productive than picking sides.

Practical bottom line: the next time you want to compress a model, prune structure, or find redundancy, do not rush to a magnitude-based method. *Print out each head's BOS attention fraction*, sort by that number, and you may save yourself enormous engineering time — and prune more reliably than Wanda.
