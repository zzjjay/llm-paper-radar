* One-Line Summary

Compressing a model with hundreds of billions of parameters down to 2 bits
without collapse is not the work of any single trick — it is the confluence
of a decade-long problem-evolution thread: *how to compress a non-uniform
matrix*. LoPRo is the most recent knot tied on that thread.

* Evolutionary Narrative: From 1993 to 2026

** Everything Starts with One Question: If You Remove a Weight, What Happens to the Rest?

In 1993, Hassibi and Stork were working on neural-network pruning. Their
question was elementary: given a trained network you want to shrink by removing
weights, which one do you remove?

The naive answer: remove the one with the smallest absolute value.

Their answer was different. They argued that removing any single weight changes
the network's output — but you can *adjust the remaining weights to compensate
for that change*. So the real question is: "Which one should be removed, and
how should everything else be adjusted, so that the overall loss increases as
little as possible?"

This is a second-order problem. They used the Hessian matrix (second
derivatives of the loss with respect to weights) to derive a closed-form
solution. The method is called OBS (Optimal Brain Surgeon).

The one sentence to remember: *weights are correlated; perturbing one demands
compensating the others, and the Hessian tells you how*.

** GPTQ: Bringing That Old Idea to Large Models

Jump to 2022. The Frantar team realized that quantization (compressing fp16
weights to 4 bits) and pruning (zeroing out weights) are actually the same
class of problem — both are "perturb one weight; how do you adjust the others?"

They adapted OBS into GPTQ: quantize a weight matrix column by column; after
each column is quantized, use the Hessian to tell you how the remaining
unquantized columns should be nudged to compensate for the error.

This technique made it possible to quantize a 175B OPT model to 4 bits and
still get usable results. The entire quantization community had a working
starting point from that moment on.

But — push further down to 3 bit or 2 bit, and GPTQ buckles.

Why? Because weight matrices contain *outliers*. Most weights sit in
[-0.1, 0.1], but a small number live in [-3, 3]. If the quantization grid
accommodates the outliers it becomes too coarse, squashing precision for the
majority; if it accommodates the majority, the outliers get clipped. No matter
how clever the compensation, you cannot recover information that has been
clipped away.

** QuIP's Insight: Scatter the Outliers

In 2023, Chee et al. at Cornell proposed QuIP. Their idea is strange but
elegant:

If outliers are concentrated in certain columns and positions, making the
distribution non-uniform — what if you *rotate the matrix* so that the
outliers are spread evenly across all positions?

Concretely: multiply left and right by orthogonal matrices U and V^T to obtain
U·W·V^T. The model's inputs and outputs are rotated in the inverse direction
synchronously, so the overall function is exactly preserved. But the object
being quantized, W, has changed — its distribution is now more uniform.

They call this property *incoherence* — the matrix is, in a precise sense,
"non-concentrated."

The cost: each inference pass must perform these two rotations online, which
is slow.

** QuaRot: Making Rotation Free

In 2024, Ashkboos et al. discovered that if you use a *Walsh-Hadamard matrix*
as the rotation matrix (a special orthogonal matrix whose entries are all
±1/√n), two benefits follow:

1. It can be fused into adjacent RMSNorm or linear layers, requiring no extra
   computation at runtime.
2. It comes with theoretical guarantees that it suppresses outliers.

This dropped the cost of rotation from "one extra pass per inference" to nearly
zero. QuaRot became one of the strongest fine-tuning-free schemes of its time.

But QuaRot left one gap open: *it treats all columns identically*. After
rotation you can no longer distinguish which columns were originally "important,"
because the information has been scattered. In standard quantization this is
fine, but it causes trouble in the branch described next.

** SVD-Quant / LQER: Instead of Compressing Everything, Split into Two Layers

Around the same time in 2024, a completely different line was developing.

Li et al. (SVD-Quant) and Zhang et al. (LQER) argued: *quantization loses
accuracy because weight matrices contain components that simply cannot be
packed into low bits*. So split the matrix into two layers:

#+begin_src text
        W   =   W_r   +   R
        |       |         |
       full   low-rank   residual
       fp16   high-prec  quantized to low bit
#+end_src

W_r is obtained by taking the top few singular values via SVD — it captures the
most "principal" part of W. The remaining residual R is relatively uniform and
more amenable to low-bit quantization.

This decomposition brought significant accuracy recovery at 2-bit quantization.
But the cost is clear: W_r is high precision and must be stored and computed;
the higher the rank, the heavier the burden. The paper notes that *256 ranks
reduces inference throughput by 45%*.

A subtler issue: *the residual R is still non-uniform*. SVD extracts the
largest principal components, but the R it leaves behind still contains
second-order residuals of the outliers.

Can you also rotate R? That is the question LoPRo sets out to answer.

** The Deadlock LoPRo Identified

Laying all the prior techniques on the table, the LoPRo team saw an awkward
impasse:

- Rotation (QuaRot) can make the matrix uniform, but *destroys column
  structure*.
- Important-column protection (AWQ-style) requires *knowing which columns
  are important*.
- Low-rank decomposition (SVD-Quant) needs to quantize the residual, which
  still has outliers.

Just apply QuaRot directly to the residual R? *That eliminates your ability to
protect important columns.* The paper provides numbers: on LLaMA2-7B, applying
a full Hadamard rotation to the residual raises perplexity from 8.4 to 9.49 —
worse than not rotating at all.

This is the deadlock: rotation makes the distribution uniform (good), but
afterwards every column looks the same, so there is no way to protect the
important ones (bad).

** LoPRo's Solution: Rotate Only Part of It

The core of LoPRo is a design that is not complicated but is quite clever:

*Step 1: Sort columns by importance.* Use the Hessian diagonal (a column's
sensitivity to the loss) divided by that column's average magnitude to obtain
a "quantization difficulty" score for each column. Sort by this score and place
the most difficult (most important) columns at the front.

*Step 2: Rotate block-wise, leaving the first block untouched.* Partition the
sorted matrix into several blocks. The first block — the most important columns
— uses the *identity matrix*, meaning *no rotation*. Each subsequent block is
independently rotated with Walsh-Hadamard.

#+begin_src text
        Q =  [ I       0    ...  0    ]
             [ 0    H_wal   ...  0    ]
             [ :       :    \    :    ]
             [ 0       0    ... H_wal ]

       Important columns: preserved as-is
       Secondary columns: scattered block-wise
#+end_src

*Step 3: Combine with the low-rank component.* This partial rotation plugs
seamlessly on top of the SVD-Quant layer. W_r is high-precision low-rank; R
is sorted, block-rotated, then quantized. The two mechanisms no longer
conflict.

*Extra: R1SVD (rank-1 sketch).* Standard SVD on large matrices is slow; they
use a rank-1 sketch approximation to cut the cost roughly in half, making it
possible to quantize a Mixtral 8×7B model in about 2.5 hours.

** Results

- LLaMA2/3 at 2-bit and 3-bit surpasses all fine-tuning-free baselines.
- In some settings it even outperforms methods that require fine-tuning.
- 4× inference speedup; memory overhead < 2%.
- On the MoE model Mixtral, perplexity actually drops by 0.4 and accuracy
  rises by 8%.

* The Deeper Cognitive Shift

If you look only at the technical evolution it reads: OBS → GPTQ → QuIP →
QuaRot → SVD-Quant → LoPRo. But underneath there is a deeper thread:

*Phase 1 (OBS, GPTQ): treat weights as independent numbers.* Compress one,
compensate another; the unit of analysis is the individual weight.

*Phase 2 (QuIP, QuaRot): treat a weight matrix as a single distribution.*
If the whole thing is non-uniform, transform the whole thing. The unit of
analysis rises from weight to matrix.

*Phase 3 (SVD-Quant, LoPRo): treat a weight matrix as the superposition of
multiple layers.* Some parts are naturally suited to high precision; others
are naturally suited to low precision. Apply different tools to different parts.
The unit of analysis rises from matrix to *subspace of the matrix*.

Each step *cuts the object more finely and applies more targeted treatment*.
LoPRo's "block partitioning" and "sort columns by importance" continue that
line — it even distinguishes, within a single matrix, between "columns to
protect" and "columns to scatter."

* Where the Field Might Go Next

Following this cognitive thread, the probable next step is *one level finer*:

- Instead of a binary "important / secondary" split, assign each column a
  continuous score for "how much rotation it should receive."
- Instead of taking the top-r singular values globally with SVD, use adaptive
  low-rank decompositions per channel or per row.
- Make the decomposition process learnable, letting the model itself decide
  where to rotate and where to keep.

The learnable variant has already appeared in part — SpinQuant (Liu et al.
2025) makes the rotation matrix itself a learnable parameter. It is reasonable
to expect that LoPRo-style "block partitioning" and "importance-based splitting"
will also become learnable.

A second direction is *MoE-friendly compression*: LoPRo unexpectedly gains
accuracy on Mixtral rather than losing it, hinting that MoE's sparse activation
pattern and "important-column protection" are naturally aligned. That thread is
worth following.
