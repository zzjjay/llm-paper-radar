* The River of the Problem

A simple question: you have a pile of already-trained dense large models (Qwen, LLaMA), but they won't fit on — or run fast enough for — automotive head units, smartphones, and edge boxes. Can you "retrofit" them, without retraining, into versions that are smaller, faster, and still accurate?

Three things collide to make this hard:
- *Inference is bottlenecked by memory bandwidth, not compute.* Cutting FLOPs doesn't help; what you need to cut is the mass of weights that must be hauled back from memory on every decode step (KV cache for attention is the biggest culprit).
- *Knowledge lives in the MLPs, not in the attention.* Removing entire layers carries that knowledge away with them.
- *Training a sparse MoE from scratch is prohibitively expensive* — millions of GPU-hours per run; but directly "upcycling" an existing dense model copies the same MLP into every expert slot, requiring yet more massive compute to drive specialization.

For a full decade, researchers kept oscillating between two legs: the pruning camp cut aggressively but hurt accuracy; the MoE camp added capacity but added cost. Dense2MoE is the first work to tie both legs together and walk.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Shazeer et al. (Sparsely-Gated MoE): Decoupling "large model" from "compute bill"

*** What problem did it see?

In 2017 everyone was grinding away at one thing: bigger models are smarter. But a dense model's compute bill scales linearly with parameter count — doubling again was unaffordable. The real tension was not "wanting more parameters" but "wanting more parameters without running every token through all of them."

*** Its solution

Split one large FFN into thousands of small FFNs (called "experts") and add a router. When a token arrives, the router picks only the top-k experts to do the work; the rest sleep through that step. Parameter count multiplies by a hundred; per-token compute stays nearly flat.

*** Why it worked (and what trap it buried)

It worked because language is inherently partitioned — tokens about code don't need to share a neural circuit with tokens about poetry.

But it buried a trap: training such a large sparse model from scratch requires thousands of GPUs. This transformed "large model" from a compute problem into a "size-of-engineering-team" problem — still unaffordable for almost everyone.

** 2022 | Komatsuzaki et al. (Sparse Upcycling): Don't train from scratch — salvage what exists

*** What problem did it see?

By 2022 the open-source community already had a pile of trained dense models (T5, PaLM, early LLaMA lineage). Training a MoE from zero meant treating those checkpoints as scrap paper — burning one pretraining budget for nothing.

*** Its solution

Blunt and direct: copy each layer's MLP 8 (or 16) times, insert a randomly initialized router, and continue pretraining. All the original dense knowledge is preserved, and the extra compute is an order of magnitude less than training from scratch.

*** Why it worked (and what trap it buried)

It worked because the copied MLPs start from non-random weights, so the router only needs to learn "which tokens should go to which copy."

The trap is in the copying itself: 8 experts are identical, giving the router no incentive to route any particular token to any particular expert — all paths are equivalent. Making experts specialize still requires feeding hundreds of billions more tokens until they diverge toward different local optima. The money saved got spent again.

** 2024 | Gromov et al. (UIDL): An unsettling discovery, made in passing

*** What problem did it see?

This is a side branch, but it is crucial to the story. The Gromov team didn't touch MoE at all; they were on the pruning side and asked a plain question: in a 32-layer LLaMA, does every layer actually matter?

*** Its solution

Measure the cosine similarity between the outputs of consecutive layer pairs. The result: in the deep portion of the network, adjacent layers produce nearly identical outputs. Deleting those layers entirely, benchmark scores drop only one or two points.

*** Why it worked (and what trap it buried)

Why it worked: deep transformer layers are doing refinement — small changes per layer, so removing a few has limited impact.

But the cost is high: when a layer is deleted, its *MLP goes with it*. Subsequent research (Meng et al.) confirmed that MLPs are the true knowledge store of a language model — cutting them out is burning the library. The small average accuracy drop masks cliff-edge failures on tasks that depend on that specific knowledge (coding, math).

** 2024 | Chen et al. (LLM-Streamline): Insert a stand-in at the deletion site

*** What problem did it see?

UIDL's wholesale deletion was too aggressive. Could the vacated positions be filled with a simplified substitute?

*** Its solution

After deleting those layers, insert a shallow FFN (far fewer parameters than the original MLP) at each vacated position, then use distillation to fit the "deleted segment's" input-output mapping.

*** Why it worked (and what trap it buried)

The stand-in recovers some performance, but its capacity is an order of magnitude smaller than the original MLP; complex tasks still collapse. The fundamental operation is using a new, small, retrained network to mimic an old, large, already-trained network — a classic "throw it away then try to pick it back up."

** 2025 | Gao et al. (ToMoE): Don't copy the MLP — slice it from within

*** What problem did it see?

ToMoE targeted Sparse Upcycling's fatal flaw: copying 8 MLPs means 8× the MLP parameters. That simply won't fit on a phone.

*** Its solution

No copying. Take the intermediate dimension of a single MLP (e.g., 11008) and slice it column-wise into N pieces; each piece becomes one small expert. With a top-1 router, each token passes through only one piece. Total parameter count stays roughly constant; active compute per token drops.

It also did something valuable conceptually: it unified MoE routing and structured pruning — an MoE router is, at its core, a per-token pruning decision.

*** Why it worked (and what trap it buried)

It worked because MLP intermediate dimensions genuinely contain large redundancies, and column-wise slices are naturally heterogeneous (different columns encode different features).

But two new traps opened, both severe:
1. *It only addresses redundancy inside one MLP; it never touches the inter-layer redundancy* that UIDL uncovered. That entire discovery is left on the table.
2. *It does nothing to attention modules.* Yet on edge devices doing autoregressive decoding, the real bottleneck is hauling all attention weights and KV cache from memory for every generated token — the *memory-bandwidth wall*. No amount of MLP optimization breaks through that wall.

The script is complete at this point: Sparse Upcycling knows how to reuse dense knowledge but wastes parameters; UIDL knows about inter-layer redundancy but kills knowledge; ToMoE knows how to slice within a layer but leaves attention alone. Nobody has merged the advantages; the drawbacks coexist independently.

** 2026 | Li et al. (Dense2MoE): Braiding the three lines into one

*** What problem did it see?

The Dense2MoE team (Li Auto, building automotive head-unit AI) faced a very concrete engineering problem: run a 7B model on automotive-grade silicon with low latency. They did a Roofline analysis and the conclusion was unambiguous — *decoding is memory-bandwidth-bound, not compute-bound*.

Measuring prior work with that ruler:
- Sparse Upcycling does not solve bandwidth (parameters actually increase).
- ToMoE's MLP slicing does not solve bandwidth (attention is untouched).
- UIDL / Streamline solve bandwidth (fewer layers = fewer weights to fetch), but kill knowledge.

Who simultaneously cuts bandwidth + preserves knowledge + avoids training cost? Nobody.

*** Its solution

By this point in the story you can already guess — combine UIDL's "inter-layer redundancy discovery" with Sparse Upcycling's "MLP reuse idea," with one critical modification:

1. *Locate redundant layers*: use a dual-similarity criterion (layer outputs + MLP inputs) to identify runs of mutually similar layers.
2. *Prune only the attention, not the MLP*: physically remove the attention module from each redundant layer (breaking the bandwidth wall) while keeping the MLP intact.
3. *Absorb the retained MLPs into the base layer as experts*: each pruned layer's MLP is moved into a neighboring retained layer and joins its MoE expert pool. The router decides which expert each token visits — or whether to skip entirely.

These three steps together form *Layer-Fusion UpCycling (LF-UC)*.

*** Why it worked (and what trap it buried)

It worked because it side-steps every predecessor's dead end:
- Experts are naturally heterogeneous — they were trained in different layers and already encode different things; *no further training is needed to drive specialization*. Komatsuzaki's perpetual debt is cancelled outright.
- Knowledge is preserved — all MLPs are retained; only the bandwidth-expensive attention is removed. UIDL's penalty is avoided.
- Training cost is minimal — 225B tokens of continual pretraining, less than 1% of what training a MoE from scratch would require.

New traps that remain:
- The method relies on the empirical premise that inter-layer redundancy is high. Does that hold for novel architectures (Mamba, hybrid models)?
- The router is newly introduced and still needs training. Can costs be pushed even lower?
- Which layers to fuse, and in what grouping, is still determined by hand-crafted heuristics.

* Frontier Extensions

This paper was posted in May 2026 and has few forward citations yet. But parallel work is already moving in the same time window:

** 2026 | ExpertWeaver (ICLR 2026 submission)

It raises a shared objection against this class of methods (whether ToMoE's intra-layer slicing or Dense2MoE's inter-layer fusion): *all of these "slice or merge" operations destroy the intrinsic activation patterns of GLU gates inside the original MLP*. The gate and up-projection of a GLU naturally form a kind of "native expert structure"; forced reorganization may discard this prior. ExpertWeaver proposes to extract experts directly by following GLU activation patterns, without any structural rearrangement.

This line is worth tracking — if ExpertWeaver's branch demonstrates that GLU patterns are a stronger prior than "inter-layer similarity + attention pruning," the hand-crafted heuristic component of Dense2MoE will be displaced.

* Insights

Reading through this lineage, three things stand out beyond the technical details:

*First: climb one level up in hardware perspective, and the entire research landscape flips.* From 2017 to 2024, the community optimized against "parameter count" and "FLOPs." Dense2MoE's pivotal move is switching to the Roofline model. Once you accept that decoding is memory-bound, the value of every prior technique gets re-appraised: some old approaches (pruning attention heads) turn out surprisingly effective; some new ones (intra-layer MoE) turn out surprisingly useless. *The correctness of a technical decision depends entirely on which performance model you are using as the judge.*

*Second: the MoE story over ten years is really a story about progressively eliminating training cost.* Shazeer wanted more capacity; Komatsuzaki wanted to stop paying pretraining cost; ToMoE wanted to stop paying parameter cost; Dense2MoE wants to stop paying even the "drive-specialization" training cost. The hidden current running through the whole line is: *everyone has been trying to subtract "training" from the MoE cost structure, step by step.* The logical endpoint should be zero-training MoE conversion — take a dense model, run a few analysis passes, get a usable MoE. That is precisely what the ExpertWeaver branch is attempting.

*Third: research progress often takes the form of "two independent lines welded together."* Dense2MoE invented no new components — inter-layer similarity analysis (UIDL had it), MLP-as-expert (Sparse Upcycling had it), routers (Shazeer had it). Its genuine novelty is *seeing that two parallel lines could be braided into one*. This "welding innovation" looks deceptively simple on paper, but it is actually the hardest thing — you have to be an insider on both lines simultaneously before you can see where the joint is.

Where is the field most likely to go next? Two directions seem likely:
- *Hardware-aware automatic structure search* — turning "which layers to fuse, how many experts to keep, how deep to prune attention" from heuristics into differentiable search grounded in the Roofline model.
- *Integrated training pipelines* — Dense2MoE is still a two-stage prune-then-pretrain process; the next generation will likely bake "disassemblable" structure into the original pretraining phase so that MoE conversion requires zero additional training afterward.

* Takeaways

This means you can:

1. *In engineering research, draw the Roofline first.* Before choosing an optimization direction, measure clearly whether the bottleneck is compute, bandwidth, or something else — this single step eliminates 80% of wrong turns downstream. Dense2MoE's counter-intuitive decision to "prune attention, keep MLP" was made possible entirely by that Roofline ruler.

2. *When looking for research problems, focus on the gaps between lines.* Pruning and MoE were pursued in nearly complete isolation before 2024 — yet anyone fluent in both could see that their advantages and disadvantages are perfectly complementary. The next opportunity usually lies not at the frontier of one line but at the junction of two already-mature lines.

3. *Be suspicious of objective-function mismatch.* Sparse Upcycling's "copy MLPs and drive specialization" sounds perfectly reasonable — until you question the premise that specialization itself is the right target. Dense2MoE's counter-move is simply: why not start with experts that are already different? *Challenging a predecessor's objective function has more leverage than improving its implementation.*

4. *Reuse beats retraining.* The entire MoE evolutionary line says the same thing over and over: if you can reuse what was already learned during pretraining, don't make the model learn it again. Any plan that ends with "we'll just fine-tune it a bit" is fundamentally running up debt.
