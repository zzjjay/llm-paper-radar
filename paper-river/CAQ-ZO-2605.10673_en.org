* The River of Problems

Can you fine-tune a 4-bit large model without ever computing a gradient or storing optimizer states?

It sounds like cheating. The standard fine-tuning recipe is: forward pass for loss, backward pass for gradients, keep an FP16 copy of the gradients, keep Adam's first and second moments. For a 7B model, optimizer states alone can consume tens of gigabytes. If the model itself has already been compressed to 4-bit to fit in VRAM, adding all those copies brings you right back to square one.

There is a lazy shortcut: *use only forward passes*. Instead of computing gradients, evaluate the loss twice — once at w and once at w + perturbation — and use (L(w+perturb) − L(w−perturb)) / (2 · perturbation radius) as a gradient surrogate. This is a trick Kiefer–Wolfowitz played seventy years ago, known as *zeroth-order optimization (ZO)*. MeZO brought it to large models and showed it actually works.

But when you combine ZO with quantization, something strange happens: you throw a perturbation ±μu in *high-precision* weight space, yet the hardware can only land on a *low-precision* 4-bit grid. It first *rounds* both endpoints to the nearest codeword, then computes the loss difference. You think you are measuring "how much does loss change if I move a small step in direction u," but the hardware is actually measuring "the loss difference along some wildly bent chord."

Worse still: 4-bit neural-network weights use *non-uniform* quantization (NF4, μ-law, and similar). Codewords are dense near zero and sparse in the tails. For the same perturbation radius μ, in dense regions the two endpoints may be rounded to the same codeword — and the difference collapses to zero; in sparse regions they may leap across several codewords, turning into a "long-range secant." Your "gradient estimate" is neither local nor consistent.

The river asks: when ZO collides with low-precision quantization, should we *switch to a smarter codebook*? Or should we *keep the codebook and change the way we ask questions*?

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 1952 | Kiefer–Wolfowitz: No Gradient? Then Pull Out a Ruler

*** What Problem Did It See?

You want to minimize a function f(x), but you cannot access ∇f — perhaps because f is a simulator, or because f is an experimental pipeline that cannot be differentiated. All you can do is ask: "Give me an x and I will tell you f(x)."

*** Its Solution

Then measure with a ruler. Along each coordinate i, ask twice: f(x + h·e_i) and f(x − h·e_i). Subtract and divide by 2h, and you have an estimate of ∂f/∂x_i. This is *finite differencing*. Wrap it in the shell of SGD and you have the first optimization machine that needs no gradient.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfall It Buries)

It works because, as long as f is smooth enough, f(x+h) − f(x−h) ≈ 2h·f'(x); as long as h is neither too large nor too small, the error is O(h²).

But there is an obvious pitfall: each step requires *2d* function queries. When x is a neural-network weight vector with d = one million dimensions, a single step requires one million forward passes — this machine crawls on the ground.

** 1992 | SPSA: One Random Punch That Hits All Coordinates Simultaneously

*** What Problem Did It See?

The 2d evaluations of Kiefer–Wolfowitz stem fundamentally from measuring each partial derivative *one by one*. Spall asked a counterintuitive question: if I do not care about individual partial derivatives and only want an unbiased estimate of the *overall gradient direction*, do I really need that many queries?

*** Its Solution

Sample a random vector u (each component drawn independently and uniformly from ±1), and compute (f(x + μu) − f(x − μu)) / (2μ) · u.

This term can be shown to point in the gradient direction in expectation. Each step requires *only 2* function evaluations, regardless of whether d is 100 or 10 billion. The dimensionality is absorbed into the "random direction."

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfall It Buries)

The price it pays: variance increases. Instead of precisely measuring each partial derivative, you use "the difference along a random direction" as an unbiased estimate — correct in direction, but noisy. To recover precision, you must either shrink μ or average over multiple directions.

The pitfall it leaves: this 1992 framework was developed in control theory and was not ready for the modern neural-network setting of "non-convex + high-dimensional + stochastic mini-batches." The next relay leg must upgrade it into a modern, analyzable optimizer.

** 2013–2017 | Nesterov–Spokoiny / Ghadimi–Lan / Flaxman: Recasting "Random Dice Throws" as the Gradient of a Smooth Function

*** What Problem Did It See?

SPSA works in practice but is not clean in theory — especially in the *non-convex* setting, where there is no bridge connecting it to classical SGD convergence frameworks. You cannot directly apply SGD's non-convex convergence proofs.

*** Its Solution

*Reinterpret* the SPSA difference estimator from scratch. Define the smooth function F_μ(x) = E_u[f(x + μu)] — the average of f over a ball of radius μ centered at x. One can then show:

#+begin_example
SPSA estimate  =  an unbiased estimate of ∇F_μ(x)   (in expectation)
#+end_example

So ZO is not optimizing f — it is optimizing the μ-smoothed version F_μ of f. As μ → 0, F_μ → f. The remaining non-convex convergence proofs are all recycled from classical SGD. Nesterov–Spokoiny turned this into the standard paradigm for Gaussian smoothing; Ghadimi–Lan provided convergence rates for non-convex stochastic ZO; Flaxman's branch handled the single-point bandit version.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfall It Buries)

It works because "random direction + ball averaging + smoothing" is a mathematically tractable object that allows all the tools of continuously differentiable optimization to apply.

The pitfall it leaves: all of this analysis assumes *function evaluations are exact* — f(x + μu) is the true f, perhaps corrupted by Gaussian white noise at most. The next leg must confront evaluations that are *corrupted by quantization*.

** 2023 | MeZO: Truly Bringing ZO into LLM Fine-Tuning

*** What Problem Did It See?

The LLM fine-tuning memory ledger: model weights (FP16) + gradients (FP16) + Adam first/second moments (FP32 × 2) ≈ 4× the model size. For a 30B model, optimizer states alone approach one hundred gigabytes. LoRA alleviates some of this, but *full fine-tuning* remains intractable.

*** Its Solution

Apply SPSA directly to LLM fine-tuning, with two engineering tricks:

1. *In-place perturbation*: never store a second copy of the weights. First apply `w += μu` and evaluate loss, then `w -= 2μu` and evaluate the other loss, then `w += μu` to restore. A *fixed RNG seed* guarantees that all three accesses reconstruct exactly the same u.
2. *No gradient copy, no Adam state*: each step needs only two scalar losses and one RNG seed. The update is simply `w -= η · (loss_diff / (2μ)) · u`, written back in-place.

The memory ledger shrinks to roughly inference-level. A 30B model can be fully fine-tuned on a single 80 GB GPU.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfall It Buries)

It works due to a surprising finding: a pretrained large model, once activated by a prompt, has a loss landscape that looks *locally* very well-behaved. SPSA's high variance is offset by the low effective dimensionality. MeZO approaches backprop accuracy on downstream tasks such as SST-2 and RTE, using one-thousandth of the memory.

The pitfall it leaves: MeZO assumes the weights are still in FP16 — *you need the original model in FP16*. If the model is already a 4-bit deployment artifact (which is the realistic norm for phones and edge devices), MeZO cannot help: it needs to perform in-place ±μu on FP16 weights, and there are no FP16 weights to modify in a 4-bit model.

** 2024–2025 | QuZO (and the QZO/ZOQO Family): The First Head-On Collision of Quantization and ZO

*** What Problem Did It See?

MeZO solved the "gradient copies are expensive" problem but not the "the model itself is expensive" problem. To truly achieve *inference memory = fine-tuning memory*, you must not only avoid storing gradients — *the model weights themselves must be in low bit-width*. Once weights are compressed to 4-bit, the forward pass is low-precision: when querying f(x + μu), the hardware first *rounds* the FP16 point (x + μu) to the 4-bit grid, then computes the loss.

The QuZO line (including QZO, ZOQO, QES, and other variants) tackles this directly.

*** Its Solution

Several approaches emerged:

- *QuZO*: Make the perturbation itself quantized — the u you throw is low-bit — and pair it with *stochastic rounding* so that endpoint rounding errors cancel in expectation, keeping the difference estimate pointing toward the true gradient direction on average.
- *QZO*: Do not touch the weights at all — perturb only the *quantizer's scale parameters*. Replace "optimizing discrete codebook indices" with "optimizing a continuous scale."
- *ZOQO / QES*: Use sign-based quantization noise with error feedback, accumulating updates too small to land on the integer grid until they are large enough not to be erased by rounding.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfall It Buries)

It works because these methods explicitly acknowledge that "endpoints will be rounded" and use various tools (stochastic rounding / changing the optimization variable / error feedback) to keep the difference estimate *unbiased in expectation*.

But a deeper pitfall is buried: all these approaches implicitly assume that "the perturbation direction is correct" — they handle *endpoint rounding errors* by averaging them away statistically. When quantization is *uniform*, this is sufficient; when quantization is *non-uniform* (NF4 / μ-law — which every 4-bit LLM uses), the problem is not merely that *endpoints are rounded* but:

- For the same weight-space radius μ, in *dense-codeword regions*, both endpoints round to the same codeword — the difference collapses to zero and directional information is completely lost.
- In *sparse-codeword regions*, the two endpoints leap across many codewords — the difference becomes a "long-range secant," not approximating a derivative at all.
- The difference is computed along a *rounded chord*, but you still *attribute it to the original direction u* — you are measuring a wildly bent chord while pretending its direction is still u.

The "statistical unbiasing" of QuZO/QZO is powerless against this *directional distortion introduced by the quantizer*.

** 2026 | CAQ-ZO: Don't Change the Codebook — Change *How You Ask*

*** What Problem Did It See?

CAQ-ZO renames the problem inherited from the previous leg: this is not a codebook problem; it is a problem of *query geometry*.

Its core insight is that every non-uniform quantizer can be written as Q = ϕ^{-1} ∘ U ∘ ϕ — first apply a monotone function ϕ to compress weights into a new coordinate z = ϕ(x), perform *uniform* quantization U (step size Δ) in z, then map back with ϕ^{-1}. This ϕ is called the *compander*. NF4 works this way, μ-law works this way, A-law works this way too.

So "non-uniform quantization" is actually *uniform quantization viewed through the lens of ϕ*. The problem becomes clear: previous ZO methods throw perturbations in *weight space x* and then let ϕ bend the endpoints into z-space — which is exactly why the difference ends up being computed along a "bent chord."

*** Its Solution

Then throw the perturbations *directly in z-coordinates* — and not at an arbitrary radius, but at exactly *one grid step Δ* via a Rademacher step:

#+begin_example
Query points = ϕ^{-1}( z ± Δ · r ),    r ∈ {-1, +1}^d
Update       = take one step in z-coordinates, then quantize back to the z-grid
#+end_example

The key magic: because z ± Δr *lands exactly on the uniform grid of z*, the quantizer U is the *identity map* at both endpoints — it does nothing. In other words, *query-time rounding error = 0*. The difference estimate measures the true directional derivative of the clean composite function F ∘ ϕ^{-1}.

CAQ-ZO is not a new codebook, not a straight-through estimator, not an optimizer wrapper — it *leaves the quantizer untouched, leaves the model untouched, and only changes the geometry of how perturbations are thrown*.

*** Why It Works (and What Pitfall It Buries)

It works because it upgrades *measurement error elimination* from "average it away statistically" to "cancel it algebraically." The QuZO line can only achieve E[bias] → 0; CAQ-ZO achieves bias ≡ 0 (at query time).

Theoretically, CAQ-ZO proves a *grid-span theorem*: no matter what weight-space radius μ you choose, weight-space ZO will inevitably collapse at some points and over-span at others — *no single μ can globally calibrate a non-uniform grid*. CAQ-ZO transforms this "globally unsolvable problem" into a "trivially local one" by changing coordinates — every step is exactly one grid step.

The pitfalls it leaves (acknowledged by the authors): 1) Updating in z-space enlarges the *smoothness constant L_z* (the compander warps the loss landscape), representing a trade-off between convexity and quantization alignment. 2) This framework applies only to *scalar* companders — APoT, k-means codebooks, and vector quantizers are out of scope. 3) Experiments only cover NF4 up to Qwen-2.5-1.5B / Llama-2-7B; larger scales, more tasks, and wall-clock comparisons are left for future work.

* Frontier Extensions

The target paper appeared on 2026-05-11; as of the time this river was written (2026-05-28), no formally published follow-up work has appeared. Likely next directions include:

1. *Vectorized / multi-dimensional companders*: generalizing ϕ from scalar to block-wise, covering group-wise quantization as used in GPTQ / AWQ.
2. *Coupling with codebook search*: currently ϕ is fixed; if ϕ also participates in the ZO update, one can do *query-aware codebook learning*.
3. *Merging with the QZO line*: CAQ-ZO optimizes z while QZO optimizes the quantization scale — both can be updated jointly, forming a complete "ZO inside the quantizer's internal space" paradigm.
4. *Hardware co-design*: on-grid queries mean low-precision inference kernels can be called directly with no dequantize–requantize round trips — an immediate engineering cost saving.

* Insights

On the surface this river traces the technical evolution of "zeroth-order optimization + quantization," but what is really happening underneath is a shift in *the relationship between an optimizer and the arithmetic system on which it runs*.

- 1952–2017: Optimizers assume arithmetic is *ideal* (real numbers + exact function evaluations); quantization is just "a final post-processing step before saving to disk," irrelevant to optimization.
- 2023 (MeZO): Arithmetic is still ideal, but the optimizer begins *yielding to hardware budgets* — in-place updates, fixed seeds, discarding Adam state.
- 2024 (QuZO family): Arithmetic is no longer ideal; quantization has invaded the *measurement loop*. The first response is "use statistical tools to average it away."
- 2026 (CAQ-ZO): The second response is "don't treat quantization as noise — treat it as a *coordinate system*. Do your work in its internal coordinates, and it disappears."

This cognitive shift from "fighting quantization" to "aligning with quantization" has historical precedent: when floating-point arithmetic first appeared, all numerical algorithms treated rounding error as an *enemy*; later, Kahan summation, interval arithmetic, and stochastic rounding *rewrote algorithms in the language of the arithmetic system* rather than fighting it. CAQ-ZO is the counterpart of that thinking, applied to *optimization*.

Where is the most likely next step? I predict two directions:

1. *Rewriting the entire fine-tuning pipeline "in low-precision coordinates"* — not just the perturbation, but also optimizer momentum, Adam state, and learning-rate schedules, all redefined in grid language. This would give rise to a class of "lattice-native optimizers."
2. *Pushing this thinking back into training, not just fine-tuning* — QAT (quantization-aware training) has long relied on *using STE in the backward pass to pretend the quantizer is differentiable*; the CAQ-ZO approach of *replacing backpropagation with forward passes and aligned queries* may offer a new path on the training side.

* Lessons

Three concrete takeaways for "how to find research problems":

1. *When everyone in a field is racing to improve the same object, ask whether that object is the wrong choice in the first place.*
   In the ZO + quantization space, every 2024 paper was improving the *codebook* or the *perturbation distribution*. CAQ-ZO asked instead: "Is query geometry a design dimension that everyone has been ignoring — one that can be tuned independently?" This turned something silently assumed as background into the main character. The implication: *in your field, list the "latent variables nobody ever tunes." A paper is probably hiding there.*

2. *When a solution is unbiased in E[·] but still unstable in practice, don't add more statistical averaging — check whether each individual call is structurally wrong.*
   QuZO uses stochastic rounding to cancel rounding bias in expectation, but CAQ-ZO observed that on a *single evaluation*, the quantizer distorts the very *direction you threw*, and no amount of averaging can recover that. The implication: *when you see an algorithm that is "unbiased in expectation but high-variance and slow to converge," suspect that its single-instance behavior carries a bias that can be eliminated algebraically.*

3. *Changing the coordinate system is far cheaper than changing the algorithm.*
   CAQ-ZO invents no new optimizer, changes no architecture, modifies no hardware — it only changes one coordinate system (from x to z), and the entire error-channel structure transforms. This is a lesson taught by Fourier, PCA, and log-coordinates, but one that must be rediscovered every few decades in each new domain. The implication: *before you brainstorm a new algorithm, first get clear about which coordinate system you are currently working in, and whether there is a more natural one available.*
