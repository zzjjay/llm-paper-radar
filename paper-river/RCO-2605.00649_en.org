* One Line

"Pick a compression level for each layer, total cost within budget" — this problem has been worked on for seven years.
The first three generations relied on "per-layer scoring + dynamic programming"; the fourth argued that scoring breaks down entirely at high compression ratios and switched to evolutionary search,
but evolutionary search has no gradient and requires hundreds of forward passes. RCO identifies the budget constraint as a clean manifold in logit space
and lets Adam walk on that manifold — gradients used exactly, budget satisfied exactly, 3–16× faster than evolutionary search.

* The Core Problem of This Line

LLMs keep growing larger; compression is necessary. But compression has an unavoidable sub-problem:

*Not every layer should be compressed equally hard.* Early layers are sensitive, later ones are not; certain attention heads cannot be touched,
while certain FFN experts can be dropped entirely. So each layer needs its own "compression level" — this layer in 4-bit,
that layer in 8-bit, this expert deleted, that expert kept.

The constraint is: *the total cost of all chosen levels (model size, memory, inference latency) must not exceed the budget.*

The objective is: *after selection, the model's actual loss should be as small as possible.*

The difficulty for seven years boils down to two points:
1. *Loss is not the sum of per-layer terms* — layers interact with one another. The impact of layer 3 at 4-bit and layer 5 at 4-bit cannot simply be added together, so you cannot decompose this into "score each layer independently and sum."
2. *The budget is a hard constraint* — total parameter count must strictly equal (or not exceed) the target. "Close enough" is not acceptable.

The tension is here: to satisfy the constraint strictly, traditional methods use dynamic programming — but DP requires the objective to decompose per layer; to optimize the true loss directly, there is no way to guarantee the budget. This river is the story of researchers oscillating between "accuracy of the decomposition assumption" and "exactness of the constraint" across successive generations.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2019.05 | HAQ: The First Paper to Treat "Bit-Width per Layer" as a Standalone Problem

*** What Problem It Saw

Before mixed-precision quantization, the industry defaulted to a uniform bit-width across all layers.
The problem with a one-size-fits-all approach was obvious: sensitive layers (e.g., the first conv, the final classifier) suffer badly at 4-bit,
while insensitive layers waste capacity at 8-bit. Early practitioners tuned manually — 8-bit here, 6-bit there — but with tens or hundreds of layers,
manual tuning is impractical.

HAQ was the first paper to say explicitly: "how many bits per layer" should be *learned*, not hand-tuned.

*** Its Solution

Reinforcement learning: an agent iterates over each layer sequentially, "picking" a bit-width; after the picks, the whole model is quantized,
run on real hardware, and the resulting latency and accuracy are fed back as reward; the agent updates its policy from the reward.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problems It Buried)

The core contribution was not "using RL" but *formalizing* the problem: N layers, K candidate levels (bit-widths) per layer,
one global budget constraint (model size or latency), one global objective (accuracy). This (N, K, budget) triple
became the standard formulation for the entire river.

But RL was simply too slow — the quantize-evaluate loop per candidate combination took hours to explore only dozens of configurations,
and the reward signal was noisy and non-reproducible. The next paper needed a way to *estimate* per-layer sensitivity *without any learning*.

** 2019.10 | HAWQ: Stop Learning — Second-Order Derivatives Are the Sensitivity

*** What Problem It Saw

HAQ used RL precisely because it didn't know "how much accuracy a layer would lose if compressed by a given amount."
But trial-and-error is expensive. If there is a quantity that *mathematically* characterizes each layer's sensitivity to quantization, no trials are needed.

*** Its Solution

Borrowing from optimization theory: quantization is equivalent to adding a perturbation ΔW to the weights. The effect of this perturbation on loss, via Taylor expansion:

#+begin_example
ΔL ≈ g^T · ΔW  +  (1/2) · ΔW^T · H · ΔW
#+end_example

At convergence g ≈ 0, so the *Hessian H* is the intrinsic sensitivity measure — specifically, the larger the maximum eigenvalue
λ_max(H) of a layer, the more sensitive that layer is.

With per-layer sensitivity in hand, the rest is a classic *0/1 knapsack problem*: N layers, K candidate bit-widths per layer
(each with a cost and a sensitivity increment), total cost within budget. Solved in seconds by integer linear programming (ILP) or dynamic programming (DP).

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problems It Buried)

It replaced "online learning" with "offline computation." The Hessian eigenvalue is a mathematically grounded scalar — no trials, no hyperparameter tuning,
one pass to get per-layer sensitivity. This "sensitivity + DP/ILP" paradigm dominated from 2019 all the way to 2025.

But the problems are clear, and they operate at two levels:
1. *Surface problem*: the Hessian top-eigenvalue is a single number and may not fully capture "sensitivity."
   Hence HAWQ-V2 (2020) switched to trace-weighted, HIGGS (2025) to MSE-to-perplexity linearization,
   MxMoE (2025) to per-(expert, weight-block) second-order terms — all *refining the score*.
2. *Deep problem*: all of these methods assume *loss decomposes per layer*, which is what makes DP/ILP applicable.
   The cost of "layer 3 at 4-bit" and "layer 5 at 4-bit" are assumed to be additive. This holds roughly at moderate compression,
   but at high compression ratios — the next paper will *prove* it collapses.

** 2025.06 | EvoPress: The Decomposition Assumption Collapsed — Drop Scoring Entirely

*** What Problem It Saw

Sieberling et al. ran an experiment: at high compression ratios (e.g., LLaMA compressed to 2–3 bit),
they compared configurations produced by the HAWQ/HIGGS sensitivity-allocation approach against near-brute-force search —
*the gap was enormous.* In other words, six years of improving "how accurate the sensitivity is" had been treating symptoms, not the disease.
The root cause was *the decomposition assumption itself*:

At low compression, inter-layer coupling is weak, and per-layer independent scoring approximates true loss adequately.
At high compression, each layer's perturbation shifts the input distribution of subsequent layers; inter-layer coupling *dominates*,
and the sum of independent scores diverges systematically from the true loss.

*** Its Solution

Since the decomposition assumption doesn't hold, abandon *any* proxy objective — evaluate the true loss directly.

But with N layers and K levels per layer there are K^N combinations, so brute force is infeasible. EvoPress uses an *evolutionary algorithm*:
maintain a candidate pool; at each step, randomly mutate a configuration (change one layer's level),
run a full model forward pass to measure loss, keep the low-loss configurations, discard the rest. The constraint is satisfied passively by accepting only budget-compliant candidates.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problems It Buried)

For the first time, configurations at high compression ratios were the product of *measured* loss rather than estimated sensitivity.
EvoESAP (Liu et al. 2026) later applied this framework to MoE expert pruning.

But the cost was:
1. *Zero-order* — no gradient signal; each step can only "try a new configuration and see whether loss went up or down."
   Convergence requires hundreds of full forward passes over the entire model. On an LLM, a whole night disappears.
2. *Passive constraint satisfaction* — many candidates are generated and then discarded for exceeding the budget; sample efficiency is poor.
3. *High variance* — evolutionary algorithms are inherently noisy; repeated runs give inconsistent results.

At this point the tension across the whole river becomes clear:

** 2026.05 | RCO: Turn the Constraint Itself into an Optimizable Manifold

*** What Problem It Saw

EvoPress solved "the objective must be real," but gave up gradients. Yet *gradients are not truly unavailable* —
an LLM's forward pass is differentiable and the gradient of loss with respect to weights always exists.
The real problem is *what to do with the constraint*:

- Lagrangian penalty (ProxylessNAS, FBNet, SDQ): add λ·(C(α)−B)² to the loss.
  λ is a hyperparameter — too small and the constraint is violated, too large and the penalty swamps the gradient. Hard instances oscillate.
- Frank-Wolfe (HardCoRe-NAS): projection-free updates on a linearized convex polytope; the constraint is hard.
  But updates are confined to FW steps and cannot exploit Adam's momentum or adaptive learning rate.

EvoPress implicitly conceded that "gradient methods struggle with hard constraints." But the authors asked: does this constraint
*itself have undiscovered geometric structure*?

*** Its Solution

Parameterize level selection as logits α ∈ R^(N×K), obtain distributions p_i = softmax(α_i).
Expected total cost C(α) = Σ_i w_i · ⟨p_i, c⟩.

The constraint C(α) = B defines a *set* M = {α : C(α) = B}. The authors prove:

1. M is a smooth (NK−1)-dimensional Riemannian submanifold of R^(NK).
2. The normal vector ∇C has a *closed form*: (∇C)_{ik} = w_i · p_{ik} · (c_k − E_{p_i}[c]).
3. Shifting logits along the cost vector c changes C monotonically (derivative = Σ w_i · Var_{p_i}[c] > 0) —
   so any point that drifts off the manifold can be pulled back by *a one-dimensional binary search*.
4. The manifold has codimension 1, so vector transport (adjusting Adam's momentum to the new tangent plane) = a single inner product.

Wrapping these three primitives around Adam:

#+begin_example
Each Adam step:
  g  = ∇L(α)
  n  = ∇C(α)                          # Normal vector, closed form
  g_tan = g - (⟨g,n⟩/‖n‖²) · n        # Tangent projection
  α' = Adam_step(α, g_tan)            # Standard Adam update
  α  = retract(α')                    # Binary search to pull back onto M
  m  = m - (⟨m,n⟩/‖n‖²) · n           # Transport momentum to new tangent plane
#+end_example

Additionally: Gumbel-STE handles discrete choices in the forward pass; a budget-constrained DP ensures the forward phase also satisfies the budget.

*** Why It Works (and What Remains Open)

The elegant part: the constraint is no longer a *penalty* (soft), nor *passive filtering* (as in evolutionary search),
but a *geometric structure* — the entire optimization trajectory is forced to stay on the manifold. The budget is always exactly satisfied, *with no constraint-related hyperparameters* (no λ to tune, no schedule to set).

The authors also prove (Proposition 6): the tangential projection eliminates the component of STE gradient bias that lies along the normal direction, so even if the gradient itself is biased, the constraint remains exactly satisfied.

Empirically: on a synthetic knapsack, RCO reaches within 1% of the DP optimum while plain Lagrangian gets stuck 37.6% away; on LLM compression, RCO is 3–16× faster than evolutionary search with matching or better accuracy.

Remaining open problems:
- The forward-phase DP is O(N·K·B'), a bottleneck for very large option spaces.
- STE bias means convergence guarantees for the *full algorithm* are only empirical, with no theoretical proof.
- Non-linear costs (e.g., real inference latency) break the monotonicity of retraction; iterative root-finding is needed.

* Frontier Extensions

RCO is a May 2026 paper — the newest development in this line. Foreseeable directions:

- *Manifolds for non-linear costs*: when cost is not linear (e.g., inference latency varying non-linearly with batch / sequence length),
  M is no longer a simple level set, and retraction requires more complex root-finding. This is an open problem the paper itself raises.
- *Multi-constraint manifolds*: currently there is only one constraint ("total parameter count"), but real deployments impose multiple constraints: peak memory, KV cache, prefill/decode latency, etc. Multiple constraints lower the manifold dimension and complicate its geometry. Appendix A.8 of the paper has already made a start.
- *Integration with NAS / MoE routing*: RCO's setting (N groups, K levels each, hard budget) is naturally applicable to NAS and MoE expert routing. The paper already validates expert pruning on OLMoE / Qwen3-MoE, but has not yet touched routing itself (dynamic token-to-expert assignment).

* Insights

The deepest pattern after reading this entire line:

*When a field repeatedly refines a proxy with diminishing returns, the problem is usually not that the proxy isn't accurate enough — it is that the proxy paradigm itself is wrong.*

- HAWQ → HAWQ-V2 → HIGGS → MxMoE: six years of improving the sensitivity formula,
  from top-eigenvalue to trace-weighted to MSE-to-perplexity linearization to second-order Shapley values.
  Each generation claimed "our proxy is more accurate," but Sieberling 2025 showed directly:
  at the high compression ratios where accuracy matters most, every proxy collapses.
- EvoPress's contribution is not "evolutionary algorithms" — those date to the 1960s — but *the first acknowledgment*:
  the proxy route is dead; you must evaluate the true loss directly.
- RCO goes one step further beyond EvoPress: abandoning the proxy *does not mean* abandoning gradients.
  The gradient of loss with respect to weights is always there; the only question is how to handle the constraint.
  The authors discover that the constraint itself has clean geometric structure, so *constraint handling regresses from a hyperparameter (λ) to a geometric object (a manifold)*.

A second pattern: *Riemannian optimization has always been a niche tool in ML* — aside from Bonnabel 2013's SGD on the Stiefel manifold and Bécigneul 2019's hyperbolic embeddings, mainstream ML rarely uses it.
The reason is that most constraint manifolds lack clean geometry (normals have no closed form, retractions have no analytic solution).
RCO's key contribution is *discovering* that the budget constraint under softmax parameterization has "unusually clean" manifold geometry —
the normal vector, retraction, and transport are all a single inner product or a one-dimensional binary search. This *recognition of geometric insight*
matters more than "using Riemannian methods" as such.

Where is this most likely to go next? Two directions seem most probable:

1. *Applying manifold geometry to other "apparently non-decomposable" constraints* — MoE load balancing,
   multi-constraint deployment at inference time, sparsity pattern constraints during training.
2. *Borrowing the RCO paradigm for non-compression settings* — any problem of the form "N independent choices + global budget" can fit this framework:
   resource allocation in multi-task learning, joint hyperparameter optimization in AutoML, even chunk-count budgets in RAG systems.

* Takeaways

Lessons for how to do research:

- *Identifying geometry is more valuable than stacking hyperparameters* — RCO introduces no new proxy function, no new loss term, no new hyperparameter.
  It simply *discovers* a geometric structure that already existed but had never been exploited. This kind of "discovery" paper is increasingly rare in an engineering-heavy ML community, but yields the highest return.
- *When everyone in a field is improving along the same axis, step back and look for a branch* — everyone in this river was refining the sensitivity formula; nobody asked "can we avoid sensitivity altogether?" Sieberling stepped back once and opened the EvoPress branch; Helcig stepped back again, found structure in the constraint itself, and opened the RCO branch.
- *Acknowledging "the assumption collapsed" is a valuable negative result* — Sieberling 2025's core contribution is not the EvoPress algorithm itself, but *proving* that the decomposition assumption from the past six years fails in the critical regime. This kind of "clearing the field" research consistently opens new directions for others.

Concrete landing points for what you can do:

- If you work on *model deployment* (quantization, pruning, MoE serving),
  RCO's code is already open-sourced (github.com/IST-DASLab/RCO) and ready to try —
  especially at high compression ratios (below 4-bit, MoE expert pruning > 50%),
  where it is several times faster than evolutionary search with no hyperparameters to tune.
- If you work on *optimization algorithms*, scan the constraints in your own problem —
  is there a "clean undiscovered geometry" hiding there? In particular, any constraint of the form
  "Σ_i w_i · ⟨softmax(α_i), c⟩ = B" (resource allocation, attention routing, probabilistic mixing coefficients)
  may directly admit RCO's manifold framework.
- If you work on *AutoML / NAS*, "penalty with a tuned λ" should be on your list of anti-patterns —
  Frank-Wolfe (HardCoRe-NAS) or Riemannian methods (RCO) are more principled alternatives.
