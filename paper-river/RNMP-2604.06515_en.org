* One-liner

The real challenge in MoE quantization is not "how many bits" but "which experts deserve aggressive compression and which deserve protection." The line runs from Hessian-MP (2020) through GPTQ (2023), QMoE (2024), BSP (2024), and PMQ (2025). Along the way, inter-expert variation is either dodged or resolved by brute-force search. This paper (RNMP, 2026) uses the *change in router L2 norm during training* as a nearly zero-cost signal for per-expert bit allocation, and accompanies it with a generalization error upper bound.

* Evolutionary Narrative (How the Problem Grew)

** Stop 1: Hessian-MP / HAWQ (Dong et al. 2020) — Who Gets How Many Bits?

The story begins with a basic question: a deep network has dozens or hundreds of layers; applying a uniform INT4 across all of them is prone to collapse. Can different layers use different bit-widths?

HAWQ's answer: use *second-order information*. Layers with large Hessian eigenvalues are sensitive to perturbation and should receive higher bit-widths. Elegant — but the hidden cost is steep: computing the Hessian trace or top eigenvalues is practically nightmarish at LLM scale.

Problem left behind: *Hessian is unaffordable, and this is layer-level granularity — variation within a single layer remains invisible.*

** Stop 2: GPTQ (Frantar et al. 2023) — PTQ for LLMs

The LLM era introduced an awkward constraint: even quantization-aware training (QAT) is out of reach. Fine-tuning a round costs several A100s for several days; tens of billions of parameters simply cannot afford it. The field pivoted to post-training quantization (PTQ): use a small calibration set, keep weights frozen, and compress directly from FP16 to INT4.

GPTQ's trick is *column-wise greedy quantization + Hessian error compensation* — quantize one column at a time, then redistribute the resulting error via the Hessian to the remaining unquantized weights, correcting itself on the fly. LLaMA-65B in INT4 loses almost no accuracy, opening the practical door to large-model quantization.

But GPTQ assigns the *same bit-width to every layer*. It simply sidesteps layer-level bit allocation — for dense models there is no urgency to do otherwise.

Problem left behind: *For MoE, where experts vary enormously, a single bit-width is obviously wasteful.*

** Stop 3: QMoE (Frantar & Alistarh 2024) — First Get the MoE Into GPU Memory

MoE has a distinctive awkwardness: only a sparse subset of experts is activated during inference, yet all experts must reside in memory. Mixtral-8x22B has 140B parameters and requires 280 GB in FP16 — ten times the activated compute (~22B). The bottleneck is memory, not arithmetic.

QMoE directly applies GPTQ to each expert individually, still with a uniform bit-width. It is an engineering victory: it proves MoE can be compressed to around 2 bits, fitting a near-1 TB model into a single GPU.

But this is precisely where the next story begins: *Is giving every expert the same bit-width truly optimal?*

** Side Branch: Chowdhury et al. (2024) — The Router's L2 Norm Speaks

This branch has nothing to do with quantization — it is an *expert pruning* paper, but it plants a deep seed.

The authors observe that during MoE training the *router vectors* corresponding to different experts change their L2 norm by varying amounts.
- Large change: frequently activated, handling high-frequency task features.
- Small change: rarely activated, capturing low-frequency but critical features.

Their conclusion: prune experts with small changes — the reasoning being low activation frequency.

But there is a counterintuitive undercurrent: these "rarely activated" experts are *not necessarily* unimportant. They carry low-frequency critical patterns. Pruning them away is one choice — but what about *quantization*?

(This paper seizes exactly that undercurrent.)

** Stop 4: BSP (Li et al. 2024) — Different Bit-Widths for Different Blocks

Back to the main thread. BSP (Block-wise Sensitivity-aware Precision) notices that within a single MoE layer, MLP sub-blocks and attention sub-blocks differ in sensitivity and should receive different bit-widths.

This is progress — from layer-wise to block-wise granularity. But it still stops at *structural granularity* and never touches the core of MoE: *the experts themselves*. Whether there are 8 or 256 experts, BSP treats them as one undifferentiated category.

Problem left behind: *The variation among experts is the essence of MoE — why has no one exploited it?*

** Stop 5: PMQ (Huang et al. 2025a) — The Most Direct Competitor

PMQ (Pre-loading Mixed-precision Quantization) is the first to answer this question head-on: *assign a distinct bit-width to each individual expert.*

Its method is *brute force*: for each expert, quantize at every candidate bit-width (1/2/3/4), run a forward pass on the calibration set, measure the Frobenius error between pre- and post-quantization outputs as a sensitivity proxy, then weight by activation frequency and gating score.

PMQ does demonstrate that expert-wise > layer-wise/block-wise. But the cost is severe:
- Mixtral-8x7B: 110 GB GPU memory, 2227 seconds
- Mixtral-8x22B: 350 GB GPU memory, 6000 seconds

Worse still, PMQ tends to assign high bit-widths to *frequently activated* experts — which actually increases inference cost (high-bit experts are called most often).

Problem left behind: *Can we know each expert's sensitivity without ever running the model?*

** Destination: RNMP (Chowdhury et al. 2026, This Paper) — Connecting the Side Branch

The paper's insight in one sentence: *Take the router L2 norm signal discovered in the 2024 pruning side branch, apply it to quantization, and reverse the direction.*

Three concrete steps:

1. *Free signal*: For each expert, compute Λ_s — the change in the router vector's L2 norm during training. This is already recorded. Rank all experts by Λ_s; this ranking is, in theory, a provably valid sensitivity ordering.

2. *Counterintuitive allocation*: Experts with *small* Λ_s (rarely activated, carrying low-frequency critical features) should receive *higher bit-widths*. The naive intuition — "rarely activated = unimportant = low bit" — is inverted here: rare activation means the signal is weak, so once quantization noise enters there is *nowhere to recover from*, making precision preservation more critical.
   - This is not guesswork — the paper derives a *generalization error upper bound for a two-layer MoE on a classification task* and rigorously proves this allocation is optimal.

3. *MaxVar patch*: Some experts contain individual neurons with extremely large intra-neuron variance (MaxVar). Even if their Λ_s is large, they must be bumped to a higher bit-width, or quantization noise explodes. On Mixtral-8x7B, only 11 out of 256 experts need this adjustment, but applying it yields a significant improvement.

Results vs. PMQ:
- Accuracy: higher at ≥ 2.0 avg bits/expert (on both Mixtral-8x7B and 8x22B).
- Computation overhead: just sorting — no GPU required.
- Inference speed: faster, because high bit-widths go to rarely-activated experts, which are called infrequently.

* What Is Actually Changing

On the surface this looks like *progressively finer quantization granularity*: layer → block → expert. But underneath lie two deeper shifts.

** First Layer: From "Post-hoc Metrics" to "Training Traces"

Both the Hessian and PMQ's output error are measurable *only after a model snapshot is taken* — they require a forward pass. The router L2 norm change used here is a *byproduct of training*; the signal leaks out while training is still happening.

This is a paradigm shift: *from "probe the model" to "read the training log."* The next generation of quantization methods may require no calibration set at all — every signal is already embedded in training dynamics.

** Second Layer: "Frequent = Important" Flips to "Rare = Fragile"

Intuitively, frequently activated experts seem more important (PMQ implicitly assumes this). This paper argues the opposite: rarely activated experts carry low-frequency critical features; they are *more fragile*, because once quantization noise enters there is nowhere for it to be absorbed.

This reversal was already latent in the pruning side branch (Chowdhury 2024) — but there "rare = expendable." Here it becomes "rare = must be protected." *The same observation, the exact opposite use.* This kind of "signal already known, usage inverted" secondary development is the hardest gold to find in the literature.

* Where Things Might Go Next

1. *Joint optimization during training*: Since router L2 norm is a training signal, one could directly incorporate a "to-be-quantized" constraint into the training loss — quantization-aware MoE training rather than a post-hoc allocation.
2. *Dynamic bit-widths*: Different inputs activate different experts; could bit-widths be adjusted online per input? Currently the allocation is static.
3. *Generalize from MoE to sparse structures broadly*: The router L2 norm is fundamentally a statistic of how gating weights respond to inputs; it could plausibly extend to Mixture-of-Depths and conditional computation architectures.
4. *Tightening the theoretical bounds*: The paper proves generalization bounds for a *two-layer* MoE. Applicability to large models is empirical extrapolation. Theoretical guarantees for deep MoE remain an open gap.
