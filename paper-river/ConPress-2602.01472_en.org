* One-liner

Pack several problems into one prompt and the model naturally shortens its thinking for each — ConPress takes this "squeeze and it shrinks" side effect and flips it into a self-supervised compression signal that needs neither a teacher nor RL.

* How the Problem Grew

** Starting point: Chain-of-Thought — "make it show its work"

Before 2022, large language models answering math or reasoning problems amounted to one word: guessing. Hand them a word problem and they spat out a direct answer — almost always wrong.

Wei et al. proposed something almost too simple to be a paper: embed a few "think step by step" demonstrations in the prompt and let the model learn to write out its reasoning process. That was Chain-of-Thought (CoT).

The results were striking. The same model, prompted with "let's think step by step," could jump from low double-digit accuracy to fifty or sixty percent on math problems.

This established one *unquestioned axiom*: showing work > answering directly. Every story that followed ran on that track.

** Second leap: Long-reasoning models — "if writing helps, write harder"

Over 2024–2025, OpenAI shipped o1, DeepSeek shipped R1, Qwen shipped the thinking mode of Qwen3 — this wave became known as *Large Reasoning Models* (LRMs).

Their shared recipe: use reinforcement learning to train the model to run a long internal monologue before answering — self-doubt, self-verification, backtracking, restarting with a new approach. Accuracy climbed another notch; competition-grade problems like AIME and GPQA became tractable.

But the cost became visible:

- A single AIME problem could generate ten thousand tokens of thinking
- At a thousand tokens per second, waiting for one answer took over ten seconds
- In production, per-problem costs started at several cents

Writing out the process was good — and had been pushed to the extreme, and beyond.

** Third leap: Overthinking named — "most of it is filler"

In early 2025, Chen et al. landed a decisive blow with a paper titled *"Do NOT Think That Much for 2+3=?"*: in long-reasoning models, most of the thinking on easy problems is redundant — repeated checks, detours, reassuring "let me confirm once more" loops.

Cut that filler out and accuracy stays the same, or ticks up slightly.

Sui et al. followed with a survey called *Stop Overthinking* that formally named the phenomenon: *overthinking*.

At this point the problem was crisp:

#+begin_src
Original problem: long-reasoning models are accurate but verbose, expensive, and slow
Real problem:     most of that verbosity is redundant and should be cut
New problem:      how to cut it? who tells the model "this part is filler"?
#+end_src

** Fourth leap: The RL camp — "use a reward function to tell it"

The most direct approach: bring back the same RL machinery used to train long reasoning, but reward short-and-correct trajectories and penalize long-and-correct and short-and-wrong ones.

Representative works:

- *O1-Pruner* (Luo+): PPO-style training with length as part of the reward signal
- *L1* (Aggarwal+): constrain the model to finish reasoning within a specified token budget
- *ThinkPrune* (Hou+): prune progressively during training, shortening step by step

The approach works, but the barrier is high:

- Reward function design (how do you trade accuracy against length?)
- Rollout generation (sampling many trajectories at each step)
- Compute (RL training costs far more than SFT)

For smaller teams, this infrastructure is simply out of reach.

** Fifth leap: The SFT/DPO camp — "fall back to supervised learning"

If RL is too heavy, drop one gear: use SFT (supervised fine-tuning) or DPO (direct preference optimization). Representative works: Arora+, Cheng+ *Incentivizing Concise Reasoning*, Yi+ *ShorterBetter*, Zhang+ *AdaptThink*.

The shared idea: collect short-and-correct trajectories as training targets, or build short-vs-long pairs as preference data, and train the model to write like the short ones.

Simpler, cheaper, reproducible.

But there is an unavoidable question: *where do the short-and-correct trajectories come from?*

The mainstream answers:

1. Distill from a stronger "teacher model" that generates concise answers
2. Write demonstrations by hand (expensive)
3. Truncate long reasoning chains (easy to break the content)

Every answer has a dependency: a teacher model, human labor, or a heuristic.

** Sixth leap: ConPress's pivot — "it turns out the model already compresses itself"

And that brings us to this paper.

Deng et al. made an observation that looks incidental but is actually pivotal:

#+begin_quote
Pack several independent problems into one prompt and ask the model to answer all of them — it will automatically write shorter reasoning for each problem.
#+end_quote

They call this *Self-Compression*, driven by *multi-question contextual pressure*. The intuition is clean: when the context is already crowded with multiple problems, attention is split and the model stops investing in a lengthy internal monologue for any single one — it moves more directly toward the answer.

This leaps over the dead-end that stopped the previous camp:

#+begin_src
SFT/DPO camp:  "short-and-correct trajectories must come from a teacher / human / heuristic"
ConPress:      "the model generates short-and-correct trajectories itself in a multi-problem setting"
#+end_src

What follows is straightforward:

1. Construct a batch of multi-problem prompts and let the model generate
2. Parse out the reasoning segment for each individual problem
3. Filter out incorrect answers and abnormally long outputs
4. Use the remaining short-and-correct trajectories to SFT the same model

The entire pipeline *needs no teacher, no human annotation, no RL*. Eight thousand samples suffice.

Result: 59% token reduction on MATH500, 33% on AIME25, with accuracy essentially preserved.

** What actually changed along this line

On the surface: long → short, CoT getting progressively leaner.

The deeper shift is really about *where the compression signal comes from*:

#+begin_src
  External teacher   ->  Reward function   ->  Preference data   ->  *The model itself*
  (distillation)         (RL)                  (SFT/DPO)             (ConPress)
#+end_src

Each step lowers the cost of knowing "what counts as short and correct." By ConPress, that cost is nearly zero — the model *itself* is the provider of concise answers; you just need the right prompt mode to surface that capability.

This is an elegant migration: from relying on external judgment to excavating an internal capability that was already there.

* Core: one takeaway

ConPress seized a counter-intuitive side effect — *applying pressure to the model (cramming multiple problems together) actually makes it leaner* — and turned that side effect into a self-compression loop that requires no external supervision at all. This is a clean instantiation of self-supervised thinking in the reasoning-compression domain: the capability you want is already inside the model; you just need the right prompt to force it out, then use those outputs to train the model on itself.

* Possible Next Steps

- *Stronger contextual pressure*: scale from multi-problem to multi-document, multi-agent, and multimodal settings — how far can self-compression go?
- *Pure inference-time prompting*: if packing multiple problems already compresses, could you skip the training stage entirely and rely on inference-time prompt engineering alone?
- *The boundary of self-compression*: on which problem types and difficulty levels does self-compression hurt accuracy? When does the model "over-compress"?

The first direction is most likely to take off — LongGenBench and batch-prompting research are already laying the groundwork. ConPress effectively opens a new exit on that line: batch inference is not only cheaper in API calls but can also be recycled as a training signal.
