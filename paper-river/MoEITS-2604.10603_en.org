* The River of Problems

Mixtral, an MoE model with 8 experts, only uses 2 per token — so why can't the other 6 simply be discarded?

The question sounds simple, but in practice it is full of pitfalls:
- Which two get activated is *decided dynamically per token* — an expert that looks "unused" on one batch can become the dominant one on the next
- There genuinely is redundancy among experts — the 8 experts in the same layer behave similarly on many inputs, because training never forced them to differentiate from one another
- Identifying "which ones can be merged" has traditionally required either activation statistics (needing a calibration set and a full forward pass over the dataset), behavioral similarity (needing output-distance computation), or evolutionary search (taking hours)
- The cost of getting it wrong is high — dropping one expert that looks expendable can cause a direct double-digit drop on some benchmarks

The whole research thread keeps asking: how much redundancy is actually hiding inside the MoE architecture? Can we expose it without expensive methods? MoEITS is one attempt along this thread to find the most redundancy with the fewest tools — it argues that you do not even need activation statistics; looking at *Normalized Mutual Information* (NMI) between expert outputs is enough.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1991 | MoE: Instead of One Brain Handling Everything, Let Specialists Divide the Work

*** What Problem It Saw

Early 1990s neural networks faced an awkward tension: you wanted a single network to simultaneously learn several very different tasks (say, recognizing letters and recognizing digits), but the parameters fought each other — improving one task corrupted what had been learned for another. This is called "catastrophic interference."

*** Its Solution

Jacobs et al. proposed an intuitive scheme: instead of one large network, use N small expert networks plus a *gating network*. The gate examines the input and assigns a weight to each expert. The final output is a weighted sum of all expert outputs.

During training, the gate automatically learns "which inputs go to which expert," and the experts naturally specialize — one for letters, another for digits.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Created)

It turned "one monolithic model" into a *cooperating team of specialists*. But at the time all experts were activated; the gate only distributed weights — so from a compute standpoint, N experts meant N times the work. Capacity went up; the compute bill was not settled. MoE spent the entire 1990s stuck in this hole.

** 2017 | Sparsely-Gated MoE: Activate Only the Top-K, Decoupling Capacity from Compute

*** What Problem It Saw

By 2017 the deep-learning community was chasing scale. Shazeer et al. realized that "taking the weighted sum over all experts" was wasteful — most inputs only need a handful of experts. If only the top-K are activated, the model can be enormous in total parameters while the per-inference compute depends only on K.

*** Its Solution

Replace the gate with *sparse top-K gating*: each token is sent to only the K highest-scoring experts. Add a *load-balancing loss* to prevent training from collapsing into "always pick the same one or two experts while the rest starve."

Inserting this mechanism between LSTM layers produced a 13.7-billion-parameter language model — the ceiling at the time.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Created)

It was the first time "total parameter count" and "inference compute" were mathematically decoupled — the foundation of every MoE-LLM that followed. But: first, it was built on RNNs, out of step with the Transformer revolution; second, expert redundancy was entirely ignored — as long as load balancing was satisfied and every expert received gradients, nobody asked whether two experts had learned the same thing.

** 2022 | Switch Transformer: K=1 Is the Ultimate Simplification

*** What Problem It Saw

Sparsely-Gated MoE's top-K routing carried several engineering headaches: K=2 training was unstable and needed careful tuning; the routing itself required a softmax plus a sort; communication overhead scaled with K.

*** Its Solution

Fedus et al. pushed to the extreme: K=1. Every token goes to exactly one expert. The routing code could hardly be simpler. With a carefully designed capacity factor and auxiliary loss they scaled Switch Transformer to 1.6 trillion parameters.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Created)

It proved that MoE training efficiency could be taken to the limit. But at this point MoE was still a "research demo" — a 1.6T model that ordinary researchers could not even download, let alone deploy. For MoE to become practical, one final step was missing: an open-source version that was good enough in quality yet small enough (relatively speaking) to actually run.

** 2024 | Mixtral 8x7B: The First MoE-LLM You Could Actually Deploy

*** What Problem It Saw

Switch Transformer was too large for anyone to use. Yet MoE's "large in total parameters, small in activated parameters" property was exactly what the consumer-GPU era needed — in principle, it could preserve quality while keeping the activated compute very low.

*** Its Solution

The Mistral team built Mixtral 8×7B: 8 experts, 2 activated per token, decoder-only Transformer. Total parameters: 47B; effective compute per token: equivalent to ~13B parameters. On multiple benchmarks it matched or exceeded the dense LLaMA-2 70B.

Most importantly — it was open-sourced.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Created)

It became overnight the *de facto benchmark* for MoE-LLM research. Nearly every subsequent paper on MoE compression, pruning, or quantization runs experiments on Mixtral.

But the deployment reality was harsh: all 47B total parameters still had to *fit in VRAM simultaneously* — the experts you are not activating still have to sit there waiting to be routed to. A consumer GPU could not hold it. So *MoE expert pruning* emerged as a hot sub-field in the second half of 2024, with one central question: out of 8 experts, how many are truly "necessary"?

** 2024 | Not All Experts Are Equal (Lu et al.): Just Enumerate Them

*** What Problem It Saw

Intuitively, Mixtral's 8 experts must contain redundancy — but which ones, specifically? There was no theoretical basis. The most direct approach: enumerate every combination of k experts, measure its loss on a calibration set, and keep the combination with the lowest loss.

*** Its Solution

Lu et al. proposed task-agnostic (global selection) and task-specific (select per downstream task) variants, both fundamentally *combinatorial search*. Choosing 6 from 8 gives 28 combinations — brute-force is fine. Choosing 4 from 8 gives 70 — also manageable.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Created)

It conclusively demonstrated that Mixtral has significant redundancy — dropping 2 experts caused almost no performance loss. But the approach has three implicit ceilings: first, *it requires calibration data*; second, with more experts (e.g. DeepSeek-V2-Lite has 64) the combinations explode; third, the task-specific variant means re-running the search for every new task — undermining the generality of the MoE model.

** 2024 | STUN (Lee et al.): No Enumeration — Cluster by Behavior

*** What Problem It Saw

Lu et al.'s method works but is expensive. Lee et al. observed that many experts are already highly similar in *behavior* on calibration data — their output distributions nearly overlap. If that is the case, why enumerate? Just cluster behaviorally similar experts and keep one representative per cluster.

*** Its Solution

STUN (Structured-Then-Unstructured Pruning): first apply structured pruning — cluster experts by "behavioral similarity" (quantified by cosine distance or KL divergence) and retain one representative per cluster; then stack conventional unstructured pruning to remove fine-grained weights.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Created)

It reframed "whom to prune" from a search problem into a *clustering problem*, reducing complexity from exponential to polynomial. But on closer inspection: what similarity metric do you use? Cosine? KL? JS divergence? Every researcher invents their own, with no unified theoretical grounding. And *calibration data is still required* — you must first run the experts forward to obtain outputs before you can cluster them.

** 2026 | MoEITS (This Paper): Back to the Information-Theoretic Root

*** What Problem It Saw

By early 2026 the MoE pruning landscape looked like this: every paper was competing on "which similarity metric is better" — MoE-Pruner combined weight magnitude, activations, and routing; MoE-I² used a genetic algorithm search; ToMoE did dynamic structural pruning. The metrics kept getting fancier, but nobody stepped back to ask:

#+begin_quote
What does "two experts are redundant" really mean, at its core? It means their outputs are statistically determined by each other. That is precisely the definition of *mutual information*.
#+end_quote

Cosine distance, KL divergence, behavioral similarity — all of these are approximations or proxies for that same quantity. Why not use the canonical information-theoretic measure directly?

*** Its Solution

The core of MoEITS can be described in one sentence:

1. For each MoE layer, compute the *Normalized Mutual Information* (NMI) between every pair of experts — NMI lies in [0, 1]; values close to 1 mean the two experts' output distributions are mutually determined
2. Set a threshold τ; treat every expert pair with NMI above τ as redundant, and eliminate one from each such pair
3. Adjust the routing weights connecting to that layer so that the "knowledge" of the eliminated expert is inherited by the surviving one through the weight matrices
4. A single hyperparameter τ controls pruning strength — larger τ means less pruning, smaller τ means more aggressive pruning

Experiments are run on Mixtral 8×7B, Qwen1.5-MoE-2.7B, and DeepSeek-V2-Lite, evaluated on BoolQ, HellaSwag, WinoGrande, and ARC-e/c, consistently outperforming the prior methods.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pitfall It Created)

Its strength is not "NMI is a little more accurate than cosine" — it is in *returning to first principles*. Once you define "redundancy" as mutual information in the information-theoretic sense, the entire method gains a unified theoretical narrative: the threshold τ has an information-theoretic interpretation, the pruning process is *minimizing total redundancy*, and the computational complexity has a provable upper bound.

The remaining pitfall is also clear: NMI is *static* — it is measured on a fixed calibration set. Yet the whole point of MoE is *dynamic routing* — the same pair of experts may have completely different redundancy levels under different input distributions. MoEITS does not address this. That is precisely the opening for the next round of research.

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026, two visible directions are emerging:

- *Dynamic pruning*: ToMoE (Gao et al. 2026) attempts to decide per-token at inference time which parameters to activate, effectively fusing "pruning" and "routing." More aggressive than MoEITS's static pruning
- *Joint pruning + quantization*: Work such as Chen et al. (2026) optimizes pruning decisions and quantization error together — because low-bit quantization introduces *expert-selection bias* (the router preferentially selects certain experts after quantization), and purely static pruning can be undone by this bias

MoEITS's "static, information-theoretic, single-hyperparameter" approach is most valuable for its *clarity and interpretability* — it will serve as a new baseline, but is likely to be superseded within one to two years by hybrid methods that incorporate dynamic behavior and quantization awareness.

* Insights

Reading this timeline in reverse reveals a rhythm that keeps repeating: *each generation of MoE solves the bill left by the previous generation, but always leaves a new one behind.*

- 1991: Solved "insufficient capacity," left behind "the compute bill"
- 2017: Solved "the compute bill," left behind "training instability + RNNs becoming obsolete"
- 2022: Solved "training instability," left behind "impossible to deploy"
- 2024: Solved "impossible to deploy (relatively)," left behind "wasteful redundancy"
- 2026: Begins solving "wasteful redundancy" — but with a method that is still *static, offline, and calibration-dependent*, leaving behind "the tension between dynamism and compressibility"

At a deeper level, there is a cognitive shift worth noting: *all early MoE papers were about adding things* — more experts, smarter gating, load-balancing losses, more sophisticated routing strategies. MoEITS represents *the first systematic effort to take things away*. This is not merely a methodological change; it is a marker of MoE moving from an "ambitious expansion phase" into a "cost-efficiency optimization phase."

The most likely next direction is to merge MoEITS-style "information-theoretic measurement" with ToMoE-style "dynamic structural pruning" — producing an algorithm that *dynamically* determines the redundant expert set based on the current input. Theoretical grounding plus dynamism: those are the respective strengths of the two current threads. Whoever combines them first will own the next benchmark.

* Takeaways

- *Going back to the root is often worth more than adding a new metric.* MoEITS invented nothing new — NMI is a textbook quantity, standard since Shannon in 1948. Its contribution is *putting the standard answer that had been bypassed back on the table*. When a field starts competing on "whose heuristic is more elaborate," that is often the signal to step back and ask "where is the first principle?"

- *"Pruning surges" follow a predictable timeline.* After any new architecture appears (Transformer, Diffusion, MoE), the first 2–3 years are an expansion phase of "adding things." When models grow large enough that deployment becomes painful, "removing things" research explodes. You can read a field's maturity stage from whether it is currently adding or subtracting.

- *A one-hyperparameter solution tends to spread further than a ten-hyperparameter one.* MoEITS has only τ. An interface where "tune one number to control strength" will make engineers love it — even if it is marginally outperformed on some benchmarks by more complex methods.

- *Practical recommendation:* If you have an MoE model to lighten, start by running MoEITS to get a baseline — plot the accuracy-versus-parameters curve across different values of τ. If that is not good enough, escalate to heavier methods like STUN or MoE-I². Use information theory for the baseline first, heuristics second.
