* The River of Questions

Can a "thinking" large model lose half its parameters and still keep thinking?

This line of questions starts ten years ago with "pruning small weights doesn't hurt ImageNet," and winds all the way to today's "after pruning, can the model still solve AIME math problems?" Along the way, three successive barriers emerged. The first: once models hit tens of billions of parameters, using weight magnitude to judge importance simply stops working. The second: the methods that do work require computing the Hessian matrix, which is intractable at that scale. The third: even after solving the tractability problem, feeding the wrong calibration data causes the model's reasoning ability to be pruned away. Each generation thought it had cleared the previous barrier, only to take two steps forward and fall into a new pit — and the newest pit SSGR runs into is this: once a model graduates from "answering questions" to "thinking step by step," the old approaches can't even clear the third barrier anymore.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Magnitude Pruning: Cut the Small, Keep the Large

*** What Problem It Saw

Once a neural network is trained, a large fraction of its weights are "nearly zero." Han et al. noticed that zeroing these small weights out caused almost no drop in ImageNet accuracy — a remarkable finding. A 60M AlexNet could lose 90% of its parameters and survive.

*** Its Solution

Bluntly simple: sort by absolute value, remove the smallest X%. This approach dominated CNN-era pruning for nearly a decade.

*** Why It Worked (and Where a New Pit Was Buried)

In CNNs, most weights truly do contribute little. But the underlying assumption is that "a weight's importance depends only on its own magnitude." Once Transformers arrived and models scaled to billions of parameters, this assumption began to crack. LLMs exhibit what are called "emergent outlier features": a small number of activation values are large enough to dominate an entire layer's output. A weight that is numerically tiny can still be critically important if it is multiplied by a massive activation. Pruning the small and keeping the large removes not the irrelevant weights, but the keys that unlock singular behaviors. The SparseGPT paper makes this explicit: traditional magnitude pruning fails on LLMs.

** 2023 | SparseGPT: Use Data to See "What Goes Wrong After Pruning"

*** What Problem It Saw

Frantar & Alistarh faced a concrete scenario: applying magnitude pruning to OPT-175B at 50% sparsity turned the model into a nonsense generator. The problem wasn't how much to prune but by what criterion to judge importance — looking at weights alone is insufficient; you need to see "how far does this layer's output shift if this weight is removed."

*** Its Solution

Introduce a small "calibration dataset" (128 random passages from C4 suffice). Run the data through the model, recording each layer's inputs; then solve, independently per layer, a second-order problem: subject to keeping 50% of weights, find a pruning mask plus a weight correction so that the pruned layer's output stays as close as possible to the unpruned output. Mathematically this involves the inverse Hessian; they apply the OBS (Optimal Brain Surgeon) recursive trick to bring complexity down to O(d^3) per layer, making 175B feasible in a few hours.

*** Why It Worked (and Where a New Pit Was Buried)

This redefined "importance" from a property of a single weight to a joint property of "weight + data." Calibration data became a new hidden variable. Yet the SparseGPT paper barely discusses what data to calibrate on — C4 is used by default, as though data were water and the method were a tea bag: any water will do. The pit was buried right here. If the model's capability distribution does not match C4 (say the model excels at mathematics but C4 is all web chatter), then the "important weights" identified via C4 may be exactly the weights that carry mathematical reasoning. Nobody noticed this in 2023. A second pit: the per-layer cubic solve plus iterative weight updates make the procedure slow even at feasible scale.

** 2024 ICLR | Wanda: One Multiplication Wins the Battle

*** What Problem It Saw

Sun et al. stared at SparseGPT and asked: why compute the second-order Hessian at all? Ultimately, you want to know "how much does the output shift if this weight is removed." But since LLMs have "a few large activations that dominate everything," can the activation magnitude serve directly as a proxy for output sensitivity?

*** Its Solution

Throw away the entire Hessian derivation and replace it with a single multiplication:

  importance(W_ij) = |W_ij| * ||X_j||_2

Each weight's importance equals its absolute value times the ℓ2 norm of the corresponding column of input activations. Sort per output channel, remove the smallest X%. No weight updates, no Hessian, one pass.

*** Why It Worked (and Where a New Pit Was Buried)

The importance definition is now directly interpretable: large weight × large activation = important. Wanda is 300× faster than SparseGPT while achieving nearly the same accuracy (LLaMA-13B at 50%: Wanda 59.33% vs. SparseGPT 58.61%). But Wanda also inherited the calibration-data problem — to keep comparisons fair, the authors simply reused SparseGPT's C4 calibration set without asking "what if we changed the data?" Two consecutive generations treated calibration data as inconsequential background; the pit grew deeper.

** 2025 ICLR | Beware of Calibration Data: Calibration Data Is the Protagonist, Not the Background

*** What Problem It Saw

Ji et al. (the same lab that would later produce SSGR) did something simple: they took Wanda, SparseGPT, and OWL and ran each method with C4, Wikipedia, Common Crawl, synthetic data, and other calibration sources. The results were striking — the same method with different calibration data could differ by several percentage points on downstream benchmarks, with gaps widening at higher sparsity. The sharper finding: the closer the calibration data is to the model's pretraining distribution, the better the pruning outcome. This amounted to declaring that the assumption shared by the previous two generations — "calibration data doesn't matter" — was simply wrong.

*** Its Solution

Pretraining data is almost never available (GPT-4's and LLaMA-3's training corpora are not public). Ji et al. proposed a workaround: let the model generate its own calibration data. Concretely, use the model itself as a sampler to produce text it finds "natural," then use that text as the calibration set. The intuition: self-generated text reflects the model's current activation distribution and therefore approximates its "natural working state" better than any external corpus.

*** Why It Worked (and Where a New Pit Was Buried)

Self-generated calibration improved Wanda/DSnoT/OWL by up to 2.68%. But the entire line of experiments ran on ordinary LLMs (DCLM, LLaMA-3) — models without a reasoning process, where both input and output are a single span of text. The new pit: at the end of 2024, OpenAI o1 and DeepSeek-R1 appeared, and "thinking models" went mainstream. These models operate in a fundamentally different regime — they emit thousands of tokens of internal monologue (<think>...</think>) before producing a final answer. For such models, what should "self-generated data" even mean? Only the answer? The full chain of thought? No one knew.

** 2025.11 | SSGR: Feed Thinking Calibration Data to Thinking Models

*** What Problem It Saw

Xiang et al. (Ji's labmates) ran a first experiment: take DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B, calibrate with C4 as usual, apply SparseGPT at 50% sparsity. Result: AIME math competition score dropped from 50+ to 24.2%. The model didn't "get slightly dumber" — it nearly stopped reasoning altogether. After pruning, output either looped infinitely or delivered a bare wrong answer with no reasoning trace whatsoever. A finer diagnostic compared several calibration choices:
  - Pretraining-style text (C4, Wikipedia, DCLM): catastrophic
  - Math problems with answers only, no reasoning process: still broken
  - Math problems + full chain of thought ("think-then-answer" format): most capability recovered

Conclusion one: for reasoning models, calibration data must include complete reasoning chains; questions with answers alone is not enough. But that single insight was still insufficient. They probed further:
  - What difficulty level works best?
  - What reasoning length works best?

*** Its Solution

SSGR (Selective Self-Generated Reasoning) = the self-generation idea from "Beware," plus two filters.

Filter one: difficulty selection. For each problem, sample the model 16 times (temperature 0.6, top-p 0.95) and compute the pass rate c. If c > 0.75 (too easy), discard. Intuition: on easy problems the reasoning degenerates into mechanical recitation and no longer reflects the model's genuine reasoning activations; hard problems are required to engage the full reasoning circuit.

Filter two: length selection. Among correct responses, sort by token length and select the response at the *median length*. Not the longest, not the shortest — ablations showed that short responses indicate insufficient deliberation while very long ones contain repetitive rambling; median-length responses carry the highest information density per token.

The data source is mathematics alone (Liu et al. found that activation distributions across different reasoning domains are similar, so one domain is representative enough).

*** Why It Worked (and Where a New Pit Was Buried)

The intuitive explanation: during pruning the model is "listening" to calibration data to decide which weights are dispensable. If you only let it hear easy problems and short answers, it concludes that it only needs to handle easy problems and short answers — and prunes away precisely the weights that sustain long reasoning chains. SSGR lets it hear "problems hard enough to require full effort, paired with a complete, density-optimal reasoning trace," so the model naturally preserves the weights supporting that behavior. Results: 7B model at 50% sparsity, average score 62.0% (C4 baseline 50.9%, plain math problems 55.9%). The cliff-edge loss of reasoning ability was arrested. The new pit: SSGR's approach is fundamentally still "select better calibration data" — the pruning objective function itself is unchanged, still "make post-pruning activations match pre-pruning activations." On reasoning models where the prompt-to-decode ratio is highly asymmetric, this objective may not be optimal at all.

* Frontier Extensions

** 2025.09 Concurrent | RAC: A Different Angle — Change the Objective, Not the Data

At roughly the same time (actually two months before SSGR), Cliff et al.'s RAC (Reasoning-Aware Compression) attacked the identical problem from a completely different angle. Their observation: reasoning models operate with short prompts and long decodes — a few hundred input tokens trigger thousands of tokens of thinking. But SparseGPT's reconstruction objective targets activations on the prompt, barely touching the decode-phase activations. This amounts to calibrating the "input end" while discarding the "output end."

RAC's approach: during calibration, feed the model's own CoT traces back into the process, so that SparseGPT simultaneously reconstructs both "prompt activations" and "on-policy CoT decode activations."

The effect is equally striking: DeepSeek-R1-Distill-1.5B at 50% sparsity goes from 35.6% under C4 calibration to 76.4% under RAC (numbers may vary slightly by benchmark).

**The contrast between SSGR and RAC is the most interesting fork on this line today:**
  - SSGR: data is the lever. "Feed the right data and the old pruning method is sufficient."
  - RAC:  the objective is the lever. "Change the reconstruction target and even C4 calibration becomes workable."

Both paths are valid, yet they point in opposite directions. This implies that LRM pruning has at least two independent sources of degradation — data mismatch and objective mismatch — and fixing either one yields a large gain. Where the ceiling lies, however, depends on what happens when both are fixed simultaneously. As of this writing (2026-05), no work combining the two has appeared.

* Insight

Reading across this line, one thing stands out: **what we have called "pruning" for the past decade is, at its core, an epistemological tug-of-war over who gets to define "importance."**

  - In 2015, importance was a property of the weight itself — one number, case closed.
  - In 2023, importance became a joint property of "weight × task" — whatever data you use to probe the weight, that is what the weight's importance reflects. At that moment, calibration data quietly became the protagonist, yet the community was still measuring progress through "pruning method" benchmarks and paying no serious attention to calibration data.
  - In 2025 ("Beware"), calibration data was formally recognized as the protagonist — but the paper was still asking "what calibration is best for ordinary LLMs?"
  - Late 2025 (SSGR/RAC), reasoning models forced the community to acknowledge a deeper layer: importance depends not just on data but on **model behavior** — a model that reasons must have its weight importance measured according to "what it looks like while reasoning fully," not "what it looks like processing a single input passage."

In other words, pruning increasingly resembles a biopsy on a living organism. If you want to know which parts are essential, you must first put the organism into its actual working state, not its resting state. A reasoning model sampled only while being fed web text yields "important weights" that correspond to its half-asleep face.

**The two most likely directions forward:**
  1. Merge SSGR and RAC — select high-quality self-generated reasoning chains and also inject those chains' decode activations into the reconstruction objective. In theory, gains should stack.
  2. Escape the "pruning-via-calibration" paradigm entirely. Since the core problem is "activation distribution must match the true working state," RL post-training (letting the pruned model continue learning on real tasks for a while) may be more direct than any calibration scheme. The cost is high, but the ceiling looks highest.

* Implications

Three takeaways for how to do research:

1. **A neglected hidden variable is a goldmine.** SparseGPT and Wanda used calibration data as background for two years. "Beware" elevated it to a research object and immediately produced an ICLR paper. SSGR added two more filters on top and produced another paper. **For any input that "everyone uses but nobody examines," ask "what if we changed it?" — that question alone often yields a publication.**

2. **When the application landscape shifts, old evaluation metrics lie.** Wanda nearly matched SparseGPT on LLaMA, and everyone declared the pruning problem "basically solved." Then DeepSeek-R1 appeared and the old methods collapsed by 25 points. **Never trust a "solved" field — as long as the application shifts, the old method's failure mode is already waiting there.** Reasoning models have now given rise to SSGR and RAC; when the next generation of multimodal reasoning models arrives, both will be superseded.

3. **Two papers attacking the same problem from different directions at the same time are the market's strongest signal that the problem genuinely exists.** SSGR and RAC appeared almost simultaneously, striking from the data side and the objective side respectively. If you are choosing a research direction and you see this kind of "parallel collision," it is a signal to dive in — not a signal that the problem is already done.

The practical takeaway: **the next time you encounter a widely used "standard practice," trace back to the hidden assumptions that were made when it was established; then ask "does that assumption still hold today?" Half the time it has quietly become invalid, but no one has bothered to recheck. That is your paper.**
