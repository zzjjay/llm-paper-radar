* The Problem River

KV cache bloat is the life-or-death problem of large-model inference. A 70B model running on a 32K context can consume tens of gigabytes of memory for the KV cache alone — more than the model weights themselves. The question is: what, exactly, can we afford to throw away?

Every generation working on this river asks the same thing: "What do we actually not need to store?" But each generation cuts at a finer granularity: first by *head*, then by *token*, then by *page*, and finally by going *inside* the token — a single token need not be kept or discarded as a whole; you can keep half of it, a quarter of it.

Why is this river so hard to navigate? Three forces are always tangled together:

- *Correctness*: throw away the wrong thing and the model immediately gets dumber
- *Efficiency*: deciding "what to throw away" must itself be cheap, otherwise you lose more than you gain
- *Generalization*: at the moment you decide what to discard, you often don't yet know what the user is going to ask

Generation after generation shuttles between these three forces, grinding the compression granularity finer and finer, one step at a time.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Vaswani et al. - Attention Is All You Need: Throw Out Recurrence, Trust Attention Entirely

*** What problem it saw

Before this paper, RNN/LSTM was the standard for sequence modeling. But RNNs had a fatal bottleneck: token 100 had to wait for all 99 preceding tokens to finish before it could begin. Hardware like GPUs — ten thousand cores ready to work in parallel — was almost entirely wasted.

*** Its solution

Simply eliminate the temporal dependency. Every token computes its "relevance score" against every other token directly, all at once. That is the attention mechanism.

*** Why it worked (and what new trap it set)

Parallelism improved dramatically, and so did quality. But it buried a landmine: every token must store its own Key and Value so that future tokens can query them. This is the *KV cache*. As context lengths grow to 32K or 128K, the cache dwarfs the model itself.

All subsequent compression work is about defusing that landmine.

** 2019 | MQA - Multi-Query Attention: Start Cutting at the Coarsest Level — the Head

*** What problem it saw

A Transformer layer has many heads (say, 32). Each head stores its own K and V. Shazeer asked: do these 32 sets of K/V really differ that much? Could they share, trading a little quality for a lot of inference speed?

*** Its solution

Blunt and direct: all 32 heads share one K and one V. Only the Query is computed per-head. KV cache shrinks by a factor of 32.

*** Why it worked (and what new trap it set)

KV read/write bandwidth is the true bottleneck of inference, especially during the decode phase. Sharing KV is equivalent to widening that bottleneck. The cost is a drop in model quality — the 32 heads used to each look at their own material; now they are forced to share a single copy.

The trap it left: can we be less extreme?

** 2023 | GQA - Grouped-Query Attention: Heads Don't All Have to Share — Just Group Them

*** What problem it saw

MQA was too blunt. Having all 32 heads share 1 K/V caused too much quality loss. But storing separate K/V for all 32 heads was wasteful. There was no middle option.

*** Its solution

Split the 32 heads into 4 groups; each group of 8 heads shares one K/V. Quality stays close to the original; KV cache shrinks by 8×.

*** Why it worked (and what new trap it set)

It added a dial to the sharing knob. Llama 2, Mistral, and other large models use GQA by default.

But the head axis is now fully squeezed dry. To compress further you must change axes — not cutting by head, but by *token*: can we simply stop storing "useless" tokens?

** 2024 | StreamingLLM - Attention Sinks: Most Tokens Can Actually Be Dropped

*** What problem it saw

It made a simple observation: in long contexts, the final-layer attention of a model concentrates almost entirely on the first few tokens plus the most recent few dozen tokens. The thousands of tokens in between receive virtually zero attention.

So why store them?

*** Its solution

Keep only the tokens in a *recent window* plus a handful of *sink tokens* at the start (the opening tokens that get attended to repeatedly, serving as anchors). Everything in between is discarded.

*** Why it worked (and what new trap it set)

This was the first big win for the *query-independent* approach — no need to know what the user will ask; positional heuristics alone can eliminate 90%+ of the KV cache. Streaming inference became practical.

But the method was naive — some middle tokens are genuinely important (a key name, a crucial fact). Once discarded indiscriminately, they are gone forever.

** 2023 | H2O - Heavy-Hitter Oracle: Select Tokens by Contribution, Not Position

*** What problem it saw

StreamingLLM's window strategy works reasonably for narrative-style long documents, but fails on tasks that require recalling specific facts — the important token might not be in the window.

*** Its solution

Instead of position, score tokens by their cumulative historical attention. Tokens that have been repeatedly attended to are "heavy hitters" and are kept; those consistently ignored are dropped.

*** Why it worked (and what new trap it set)

This was a step toward precision — upgrading from a positional heuristic to a *data-driven* one. It holds up considerably better than StreamingLLM on QA tasks.

But it is still *query-independent*: historical average scores decide who gets kept, ignoring "what the user is asking *this time*." The KV cache used to answer question A and the one used to answer question B are identical — but they clearly shouldn't be.

** 2024 | Quest - Query-Aware Sparse Retrieval: Let the Query Decide What to Load

*** What problem it saw

The "heavy hitter" tokens H2O selects may be irrelevant to the current query. If I ask "what is the protagonist's name today," that intense battle scene — however "important" historically — is just noise.

*** Its solution

Partition the KV cache into *pages*; for each page, store a statistical summary of its K values (max/min). When a query arrives, use this summary to quickly estimate which pages likely contain relevant content, and load only those pages for attention computation.

This is the beginning of the *query-aware* approach.

*** Why it worked (and what new trap it set)

The savings are not just memory — computation too: only relevant pages participate in attention. Long-context inference suddenly became feasible.

But two traps remain:
1. *Granularity is still coarse*: a page is typically 16 or 64 tokens; the page is kept or discarded as a whole.
2. *Purely reactive*: selection can only happen after the query arrives; it cannot support scenarios where you want to compress KV first and serve many unknown future queries from it.

** 2025 | Expected Attention: Don't Wait for the Query — Estimate How Future Queries Will Attend

*** What problem it saw

Quest selects pages only after a query arrives. But in many scenarios we want to *proactively* compress and store KV (e.g., KV cache sharing, cross-request reuse) — at that point we have no idea what future queries will look like.

*** Its solution

Rather than storing everything, estimate a *probability distribution over future queries* and use it to compute the *expected attention score* for each token. Tokens with high expected scores are kept; the rest are dropped.

This transforms "query-aware" from a reactive to a proactive stance — you can make query-aware decisions without seeing a real query.

*** Why it worked (and what new trap it set)

This is the strongest token-level method on the river. This paper also uses it as the hardest baseline in its experiments.

But it still carries a fixed assumption: *a token is either kept in full or dropped entirely*. A token judged "moderately important" will very likely be dropped under Expected Attention's logic — but what if only *some dimensions* inside it are genuinely critical?

Where to go next? The token level is exhausted. The only move left is to change axes: go *inside* the token.

** 2026 | Sub-Token Routing (This Paper): A Token Can Be Split Internally, Too

*** What problem it saw

It made a counterintuitive observation: under a fixed KV budget, the tokens that are retained are actually *highly non-uniform internally* — many tokens have only some of their value dimensions kept, many tokens are dropped entirely, and only a few are kept entirely (paper Figure 1).

In other words, the binary choice of "keep an entire token vs. drop it entirely" never reflected reality. The model is already doing "partial retention" implicitly — it's just that no one had explicitly modeled this.

*** Its solution

Split each token's value vector into S groups (e.g., 4 groups, called value-groups). Introduce two routing paths:

1. *Query-independent path*: train a router to decide which value-groups inside each token to keep, paired with a trained "reconstructor" that fills in the dropped portions.
2. *Query-aware path*: use a predictor to distribute a global budget (e.g., the equivalent of 1.0 full token) across all (token, value-group) pairs in a query-aware manner — important tokens get many groups; unimportant ones get none.

This approach does *not replace* token-level methods — it *complements* them: a token-level method like Expected Attention first decides which tokens are globally more worth attending to, and then sub-token routing decides how to compress the interior of each retained token.

To apply the same philosophy to LoRA fine-tuning, the paper also slices LoRA's low-rank update by internal subspace, calling it *routed subspace LoRA* — distinct from MoE-LoRA and similar methods that route among multiple LoRA experts, this routes *among subspaces within a single LoRA*. The same "finer-grained routing" philosophy lands on both adaptation and compression simultaneously.

*** Why it worked (and what new traps it set)

The intuition: different dimensions of a value vector carry different semantics — some encode "is this word a verb or a noun," some encode "what specific entity it refers to," some encode "its emotional valence." For a given query, only one class may matter. Forcing a whole-block keep or drop is wasteful.

Experiments show: sub-token routing alone matches the strongest token-level method, Expected Attention; but *combined*, the two achieve almost no accuracy drop at much more aggressive compression ratios (e.g., total budget cut to 25%) — something no single token-level method can do.

New traps left behind:
- *Training cost*: requires training a router and reconstructor (query-independent mode) or a predictor (query-aware mode).
- *Hardware friendliness*: sub-token-level sparsity may not play well with current GPU kernels; dedicated kernels are needed to realize the theoretical speedup.
- *Can the axis go finer still?*: value vectors are already split into 4 groups. Would splitting further (e.g., selecting individual dimensions) yield sufficient benefit to justify the added complexity?

* Frontier Extensions

As of this paper's writing (May 2026, v2), this is the finest-grained publicly known scheme on this river. Whether further work exists — e.g., applying the same idea to the *key* path, or to *sub-vectors within each head* — has not yet appeared in any clear public publication.

A few plausible directions (speculative, *not confirmed*):

- *Symmetric work on the key path*: this paper only touches the value side, leaving keys unchanged. But keys constitute half the KV cache. Could similar sub-token routing be applied to keys? It would be harder, since keys directly determine "whether a token gets retrieved at all."
- *Hardware co-design*: sub-token sparsity is unfriendly to existing attention kernels (FlashAttention family), likely catalyzing dedicated "sub-token aware" kernels.
- *Combination with quantization*: sub-token routing decides "which groups to keep"; quantization decides "how many bits to use for what is kept." The two compression axes are orthogonal, leaving a large combinatorial design space.

* Insights

The most fundamental shift happening across this river is quiet but profound: *the definition of "what counts as an indivisible unit of information" keeps shrinking*.

- When attention was born in 2017, a token was a natural atom.
- MQA/GQA demoted the "head" from atomic unit to something splittable.
- StreamingLLM/H2O/Quest demoted the "token" from something that must be stored to something that can be kept or discarded.
- Sub-Token Routing goes to the extreme — *the interior of a single token is no longer atomic either*.

Each time an atomic boundary is broken, a new compression degree of freedom is unlocked. This is a deep conceptual shift:

We used to assume that the internal structures of a Transformer (heads, tokens, value vectors) were "semantically indivisible." But accumulating evidence suggests these boundaries are *engineering conveniences*, not *information-theoretically meaningful*. Inside the model, there is no such thing as "one token" — only a constellation of active patterns scattered across many dimensions.

If that reading is correct, the next step is inevitably *dynamic granularity based on actual information distribution* — no longer hand-setting how many pieces to cut, but letting the model learn "for this query and this context, what is the best way to slice."

A second thing worth noticing: *query-aware does not have to wait for the query*. Expected Attention uses a probability distribution over future queries to transform query-awareness from reactive to proactive. That idea itself is a paradigm shift, and the query-aware predictor in sub-token routing walks the same road.

A possible future direction: not just deciding "what information to keep," but letting the query distribution also determine "how that information should be *organized*."

* Takeaways

Lessons for researchers and engineers:

- *The axis matters more than the algorithm*: MQA → GQA is a small step along the same axis; GQA → StreamingLLM is a change of axis. When a field is truly stuck, it usually means the current axis is exhausted; opening a new axis unlocks an entire new region of design space. Notice how pushing "compression granularity" one notch finer in this paper illuminates the entire trajectory.
- *Things assumed to be atomic usually aren't*: every time someone asks "does this seemingly indivisible thing actually have to be indivisible?" they find something new. The next time you're stuck, list everything you've been implicitly treating as a unit, and ask each one: "Can this really not be split further?"
- *Complementary > replacement*: the strongest experimental result in this paper is not sub-token routing beating everything else single-handedly — it is sub-token routing *combined* with Expected Attention. A new method doesn't have to defeat the old one; finding a position *orthogonal* to the old method is often far more valuable.
- *Diagnose before prescribing*: Figure 1 in this paper uses one simple histogram (the internal-distribution histogram of retained tokens) to make the case for sub-token routing completely clear. Prove with data that the problem genuinely exists before presenting the solution — that is ten times more convincing than just proposing a new method.

Practical bottom line: if you are working on LLM inference optimization, ask yourself three things right now —
1. What is the "unit" I'm currently compressing? Can I go finer?
2. Is my decision query-independent or query-aware? Can I move from "wait for the query" to "estimate the distribution of queries"?
3. Is my method trying to replace something else, or can I find a complementary position alongside it?
