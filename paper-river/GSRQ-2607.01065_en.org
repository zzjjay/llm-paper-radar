* The Problem River

Squeezing an extremely long conversation into a GPU, a large model must remember every intermediate result it computes at each step — these results are called the KV cache, and as the conversation grows longer, they consume GPU memory at a linear rate, eventually filling the card entirely. The problem this line of work sets out to solve can be stated in one sentence: *How do we compress the KV cache to the absolute minimum while keeping the model coherent?*

Where is the difficulty? Scalar compression (quantizing each number independently) hits a wall at 2 bits; go further and quality collapses catastrophically. Vector quantization can push compression lower, but when plugged into large models, mainstream algorithms carry a subtle flaw — they cause all codebook centroids to quietly shrink toward the origin, destroying directional information. This flaw is amplified without bound below 1 bit, making quality dismal. The key to fixing it was buried in a 1985 speech-coding paper.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1985 | Makhoul: Magnitude and Direction Are Two Entirely Different Things

*** What Problem Did It See

In the 1980s, speech-coding engineers faced a concrete challenge: if you treat a frame of speech as a high-dimensional vector and quantize it directly, two perceptually very similar sounds — say, a loud version and a soft version of the same vowel — may lie far apart in space. Standard L2 clustering would lump the loud and soft vectors into the same cluster, learning a centroid that is a compromise between them and represents neither well.

*** Its Solution

Strip each vector into two layers of information: *gain* (how long the vector is, representing volume/energy) and *shape* (the direction after normalization, representing timbre/phoneme). Normalize all vectors onto the unit sphere, run K-means on the sphere to learn a "shape codebook," then learn a separate scalar codebook for the lengths. At decompression, combine the two codewords back together.

*** Why It Works (and What New Gap It Creates)

Gain varies smoothly in scalar space; shape varies smoothly on the sphere — each of these two spaces is individually far more "quantization-friendly" than the original space. Separating the two concerns lets each be handled cleanly. Forty years later, this idea would be revived intact in a completely different domain.

** 2011 | Jégou: Shatter High-Dimensional Vectors into Segments and Quantize Each Piece

*** What Problem Did It See

Image retrieval requires finding nearest neighbors among millions of high-dimensional descriptors (e.g., 128-dimensional SIFT features). Brute-force search is too slow, and direct VQ quantization demands an exponentially large codebook — 2^128 codewords for a 128-dimensional vector is completely infeasible.

*** Its Solution

Product Quantization (PQ): split a 128-dimensional vector into 8 segments of 16 dimensions each, run independent K-means on each segment, and the codebook needs only 8 × 256 = 2048 entries. During retrieval, compute the distance for each segment and sum them to get an approximation of the global distance.

*** Why It Works (and What New Gap It Creates)

Exponential complexity becomes linear addition. Memory goes from exponential to linear; distance computation goes from O(d) to table lookup. PQ opened the door to practical vector quantization. New gap: PQ splits vectors into segments and still uses scalar K-means within each segment — it does not address the magnitude-direction inconsistency of the vector as a whole.

** 2023 | H2O: The First to Make the KV Cache the Primary Battlefield

*** What Problem Did It See

During autoregressive inference, every newly generated token requires storing the Key and Value vectors for all previous tokens on the GPU. The longer the context, the larger the KV cache, and beyond a certain length it exceeds GPU memory — forcing shorter contexts or more expensive hardware.

*** Its Solution

Observation: attention scores follow a power-law distribution — a small number of tokens ("heavy hitters") capture the vast majority of attention weight. H2O retains only the most recent tokens plus the top-K historical tokens by current attention score, and evicts everything else.

*** Why It Works (and What New Gap It Creates)

More than 90% of attention concentrates on roughly 5% of tokens, so evicting the other 95% barely affects output. Speed and memory improve substantially. New gap: eviction is lossy compression — evicted tokens are gone forever, establishing an irreducible information-loss floor that prevents truly lossless compression.

** 2024 | KIVI: The Scalar Quantization Boundary Is 2 Bits

*** What Problem Did It See

Eviction strategies incur information loss. Can we instead quantize the KV cache — convert floats to low-precision integers, keeping every token but compressing its representation — and achieve no data loss?

*** Its Solution

Analysis reveals that the Key cache has structure along the channel dimension while the Value cache has structure along the token dimension. This motivates asymmetric quantization: Keys are group-quantized along channels to 2 bits; Values are group-quantized along tokens to 2 bits. The most recent few tokens are kept in FP16 (because they are still incomplete).

*** Why It Works (and What New Gap It Creates)

KIVI at 2 bits is nearly lossless, reduces memory to 38% of FP16, and boosts throughput 2–3×. New gap: *the physical limit of scalar quantization is 1 bit per element*. Each number is represented by only 0 or 1 — precision cannot go lower. Breaking through 1 bit requires switching tracks entirely.

** 2024 | VQLLM: Borrowing Residual Vector Quantization from Speech Coding

*** What Problem Did It See

KIVI collapses at 1 bit. Is there a method that can theoretically break the 1-bit limit? — Yes: vector quantization (VQ). VQ compresses multiple numbers from multiple vectors together into a single codebook index, enabling arbitrarily low bits per element.

*** Its Solution

Transplant Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ, widely used in high-fidelity audio compression such as EnCodec) into the KV cache:
1. Scale KV vectors by their standard deviation (normalize)
2. Split channels into small groups
3. Apply RVQ to each group: quantize, take the residual, quantize the residual, recurse for several stages
4. Learn codebooks online with Exponential Moving Average (EMA)

Results are competitive with scalar methods, achieving 5.5× compression over FP16, and the implementation is extremely simple — no extra linear layers needed.

*** Why It Works (and What New Gap It Creates)

Each RVQ stage quantizes the residual from the previous stage, so error decays exponentially in theory. In practice the approach works — quality degrades noticeably only below 1 bit.

The reason is hidden in the mathematics of K-means: *centroid shrinkage*.

The centroid in standard L2 K-means is the arithmetic mean of the vectors in a cluster. Averaging many unit vectors pointing in different directions yields a resultant vector whose length is less than 1 — the "shrunken" centroid no longer accurately represents the angular distribution of those vectors. When the bit budget decreases, each cluster absorbs more vectors pointing in diverse directions, and this bias is amplified.

Net effect: on LLaMA-3-8B at 1 bit, LongBench average score is just 11.34 — the model is essentially incoherent.

** 2026 | GSRQ: Plugging the 1985 Answer into the 2024 Pipeline

*** What Problem Did It See

VQLLM collapses below 1 bit because standard L2 K-means causes centroid shrinkage, undermining angular alignment. If we replace that K-means step with a clustering algorithm that preserves directional fidelity, can we fix the problem?

*** Its Solution

GSRQ attacks from two directions:

*Gain-Shape K-Means (GSKM)*: Before running K-means, normalize every vector onto the unit sphere — precisely the gain-shape idea from Makhoul 1985. After normalization, clustering is performed on the sphere, and centroid angular positions are no longer dragged off by magnitude differences. After convergence, gain and shape are encoded separately.

*Weighted GSKM extension*: At each stage of residual quantization, assign higher weights to vectors closer to their centroid, further stabilizing the centroid's directional estimate.

The full pipeline is called GSRQ (Gain-Shape Residual Quantization) and is a drop-in replacement for the standard K-means step inside VQLLM.

*** Why It Works (and What New Gap It Creates)

On LLaMA-3-8B at 1 bit, LongBench average score jumps from VQLLM's 11.34 to 33.54 — a gain of 22 points. The intuitive explanation:

Imagine all KV vectors as points on and around a globe. Gain is each point's distance from the center; shape is its latitude/longitude on the surface. Standard K-means finds the arithmetic mean in 3D space — averaging a point near the equator with one near the poles may yield a centroid inside the sphere, correct for neither. GSKM first projects everything onto the surface, then averages — centroids always stay on the sphere, and latitude/longitude coordinates are correct.

New gap: GSRQ requires learning codebooks (calibration overhead). Training-free methods (TurboQuant, VQKV, etc.) carry practical deployment advantages, making this an open trade-off.

* Frontier Extensions

GSRQ was submitted in July 2026. Multiple parallel branches emerged in the same period, and the competitive landscape looks like this:

*CommVQ (ICML 2025)*: A completely different angle — make the codebook commute with Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE), so that decompression reduces to simple matrix multiplication folded into the attention computation. At 2 bits it nearly matches FP16 precision (47.98 vs. 48.05); at 1 bit LongBench reaches 44.94. CommVQ targets computational efficiency (fast decompression); GSRQ targets quantization accuracy (precise quantization). The two cut in at different angles.

*AnTKV (2025)*: Observes that "anchor tokens" (attention heavy hitters) in the KV cache affect quantization accuracy far more than ordinary tokens, and selectively preserves higher precision for anchor tokens while applying ultra-low-bit quantization to the rest.

*TurboQuant (ICLR 2026, Google Research)*: Mathematically harder — apply a random rotation to make coordinate distributions uniform, then quantize the rotated coordinates. Zero precision loss at 3 bits, training-free, and only ~2.7× away from the information-theoretic lower bound. Introduces a formal lower bound proving the limits of data-free methods at the per-vector level.

*FibQuant (2026)*: Projects vectors onto the unit sphere (resonating with GSRQ's "shape" idea) and constructs a universal fixed-length codebook using Fibonacci sphere uniform-coverage, requiring no calibration data. Competitive below 3 bits.

*HQMQ (2026)*: Uses Hurwitz quaternion multiplicative quantization, encoding geometric structure directly into codewords while remaining training-free. Shares intuition with GSRQ's "gain-shape" idea (both protect the geometric structure of vectors) but takes a completely different implementation path.

The research appearing in this period makes clear: *sub-1-bit KV cache quantization is the recognized frontier battleground right now — everyone is storming the same hill by different routes.*

* Insight

What is really happening along this line of work is not "technology progressing" — it is *the reactivation of a borrowed relationship*.

Gain-shape decomposition solved a speech-coding problem in 1985, then lay dormant in signal processing for 40 years. What GSRQ does is not invent new mathematics — it *recognizes* that the difficulty of quantizing LLM KV vectors is mathematically isomorphic to the difficulty speech coding faced in 1985. Both involve quantizing high-dimensional vectors that simultaneously carry magnitude differences and directional differences; both are poisoned by L2 K-means centroid shrinkage. Recognizing the isomorphism is equivalent to receiving the answer for free.

This implies: *many "new problems" in the deep-learning era already have formalized answers in the older literature of signal processing, coding theory, and information theory* — the answers simply did not yet have a host problem to inhabit. Finding the mathematical isomorphism between structures is faster than inventing new tools.

The most likely next step: breaking the per-vector information-theoretic lower bound. TurboQuant has already shown that 3 bits is the theoretical floor for quantizing isolated KV vectors. But the KV cache is not a collection of isolated vectors — adjacent tokens' KV vectors are semantically highly correlated. Exploiting sequence structure for joint compression (e.g., PLT, 2026) may break through this per-vector floor and drive the effective bit count down by another order of magnitude.

* Takeaways

*Recognizing borrowed relationships is the highest-leverage research move*: not "find a new problem to solve," but "recognize that your new problem is mathematically the same thing as some old problem." This requires researchers to accumulate knowledge across domains and deliberately search for structural similarities in unrelated literature.

Concretely, this line of work tells us:
- Whenever your quantization/compression task involves vectors that simultaneously carry both magnitude and directional information, gain-shape decomposition is almost universally beneficial — not just for KV caches, but for any embedding quantization setting.
- "Standard L2 K-means performs poorly on spherical data" is a bug with a clear mathematical root cause, not mysterious hyperparameter behavior. Anywhere K-means is used in a cosine-similarity setting is worth auditing.
- This means you can *treat this paper as a validated method template*: in any residual vector quantization pipeline, replacing standard K-means with GSKM will very likely yield improvement, and the reason is already theoretically explained.
