* The River of the Problem

You have spent millions of dollars training a large dense model. Now you want it to run faster — ideally cutting the compute in half, without sacrificing too much accuracy.

There is a tempting approach: make the model *sparse*. The feed-forward layers (FFN) of a large model contain tens of thousands of neurons, but experiments have long shown that, for any specific input, only a small fraction are actually activated. Since most neurons are idle most of the time, could you split them into groups of "experts" and awaken only the most relevant groups for each input? This is the allure of Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) — same parameter count, but only a fraction of the computation per forward pass.

The trouble is that training MoE from scratch is expensive and unstable. So: can you take an *already-trained* dense model and "slice" its FFN into experts?

The core question of this river is one sentence: *How do you assign a pool of neurons to a set of experts — as well as possible?*

The problem is hard in a deceptively lethal way. Tens of thousands of neurons, dozens of experts, and the number of possible assignments is combinatorially astronomical — exhaustive search is hopeless. Worse, "how good is this assignment?" is nearly impossible to evaluate on the spot — you cut, then you have to run the entire model to see how much accuracy you lost. For a decade, generation after generation resorted to "proxy metrics" to guess which assignment was better, rather than directly optimizing "how does the model actually perform after the cut." This river is the evolutionary history of going from gut-feel slicing to learned slicing.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Sparsely-Gated MoE: Giving Neural Networks an "Activate on Demand" Switch

*** What Problem It Saw

The consensus of the era was: bigger models are smarter, but bigger also means slower. Parameters and compute were inextricably bound — more "brain capacity" meant more "thinking time." Shazeer et al. targeted that binding relationship itself: could a model have a vast number of parameters but use only a fraction of them at a time?

*** Its Solution

Imagine a company with a thousand specialists, but each project only assigns two or three of the most relevant ones. Shazeer built a "gating network": for each input it scores and selects the top-k experts, activates only those, and skips all the rest entirely. To prevent all the work from piling onto a few star experts, they added noise and two auxiliary "load-balancing" losses to force even distribution. With this trick they built a 137-billion-parameter model without exploding the compute budget.

*** Why It Worked (and What It Left Behind)

It decoupled "parameter count" from "compute" for the first time — this is the foundation every subsequent MoE rests on. But these experts were trained *from a blank slate*. Later, people discovered that training MoE from scratch is notoriously tricky: it demands enormous data and is prone to instability (expert degeneration, routing collapse). A natural idea then surfaced — we already have trained dense models; why not *repurpose* them instead of starting over?

** 2021 | MoEfication: The Experts Were Already Hidden Inside the Trained FFN

*** What Problem It Saw

MoEfication noticed a striking fact: in an ordinary, densely trained Transformer, *the vast majority of FFN neurons are silent* when processing any specific input — only 10%–30% light up. This bears a remarkable resemblance to functional specialization in the brain. Since existing FFNs already exhibit this natural sparse structure, Shazeer's approach of training experts from scratch seemed wasteful.

*** Its Solution

No training — just "slice." Partition the neurons of the large FFN weight matrix into groups, treat each group as one expert, then train a small router to decide which groups to activate for each input. How to group? It looks at neurons' *input-side weights* — neurons whose weight vectors are similar will likely respond to similar input patterns, so cluster them together.

*** Why It Worked (and What It Left Behind)

It turned "building a MoE" from a training problem into a partitioning problem, eliminating the prohibitively expensive from-scratch training. The payoff was a 2× speedup by replacing 25% of FFN parameters with expert routing, while retaining 95% of accuracy — and it incidentally gave researchers a microscope to study the internal mechanics of FFNs.

But the gap is here: it only looks at *input-side* weight similarity. Two neurons whose inputs look alike need not contribute alike to the *final output*. It optimizes "do neurons resemble each other?" — a proxy — rather than "does the model output correctly after the slice?" — the true goal. This "proxy vs. true objective" crack would trouble everyone who followed.

** 2024 | LLaMA-MoE: Modern Decoders Are Too Hard to Slice Cleanly — So Slice Randomly and Brute-Force Recovery

*** What Problem It Saw

MoEfication was designed for BERT-style (encoder, ReLU activation) models. By the LLaMA era, models were decoders using SwiGLU activations with a different FFN structure. Zhu et al. systematically tried four slicing strategies — weight clustering, activation clustering, random slicing, and others — and the result was counterintuitive: *non-overlapping random slicing performed best.*

*** Its Solution

Since sophisticated slicing offered no advantage, they conceded that the "slice" step was not that important and bet everything on the post-slice recovery training. Slice neurons into experts at random, then throw 200B tokens of continued pre-training at the result and let the model heal itself.

*** Why It Worked (and What It Left Behind)

It proved a point: given enough compute for continued pre-training, *how you initially slice barely matters* — the model self-repairs. LLaMA-MoE-3.5B indeed surpassed dense models with the same number of activated parameters.

But this exposed the biggest gap: random slicing *abandons the idea of slicing well* and transfers all the burden onto massive compute. It is expensive, and offers no principled explanation for why experts are grouped the way they are. If you sliced more intelligently, could you burn far less compute to recover? That counter-question directly catalyzed the next paper.

** 2024 | LLaMA-MoE-v2: Ditch Random — Slice by "Importance"; Attention Heads Can Be Experts Too

*** What Problem It Saw

It saw through LLaMA-MoE's waste: random slicing "ignores the fact that different dimensions contribute vastly differently to model performance." Why treat a critical neuron and a negligible one as equals and throw them into experts at random?

*** Its Solution

Smoothly deform the approach: keep the "slice then post-train to recover" framework, but replace "random assignment" with "importance-guided assignment" — use activations and gradients to estimate each neuron's importance and group accordingly. It also goes one step further: not only slice the FFN, but also partition *attention heads* into expert groups (different heads already attend to different patterns). Additionally, it targets instruction-tuned models and uses "post-training" rather than "continued pre-training" for recovery — cheaper.

*** Why It Worked (and What It Left Behind)

Importance-guided assignment is more purposeful than blind slicing, and the amount of training needed to recover is more manageable. But it does not escape two fundamental limitations. First, "importance" (activations, gradients) is still a *proxy metric*, side-stepping "is the output actually correct?" — MoEfication's crack remains open. Second, it is still a *two-step pipeline*: freeze the neuron assignment first, then train the router. Assignment and routing work independently and can never accommodate each other.

** 2025 | CMoE: Convert in Five Minutes — No Router Training Required

*** What Problem It Saw

CMoE zeroed in on **speed** and **cost**: prior methods required continued pre-training or post-training, often tens to hundreds of billions of tokens. Could a dense model be converted to MoE in minutes on a single GPU?

*** Its Solution

The core insight is *co-activation* — neurons that light up together belong in the same expert. High-frequency, always-on neurons are extracted first as "shared experts" (always activated); the remaining low-frequency neurons are distributed into routed experts using a "balanced linear assignment" (Jonker-Volgenant algorithm) that guarantees equal expert size. The cleverest part is the router: rather than training it, the weights are *analytically computed directly from activation statistics* — ready out of the box, with optional LoRA fine-tuning for extra precision.

*** Why It Worked (and What It Left Behind)

It compressed the conversion cost to five minutes on a single GPU. The zero-training router provides a solid starting point; one hour of fine-tuning recovers more than 70% of downstream accuracy, surpassing the LLaMA-MoE family.

Yet MoEfication's old crack is still unfilled: "co-activation" remains a proxy of *intermediate-layer activation patterns*, not "is the output correct?" And more subtly — it is still a variant of the two-step pipeline: freeze the neuron assignments via linear assignment first, then construct the router around that fixed partition. Once the assignment is frozen, the router can only adapt to it; joint optimization toward a shared optimum is impossible. This leaves exactly enough room for the final paper.

** 2026 | DOT-MoE: Turn "How to Slice" Into a Differentiable Problem and Learn Assignment and Routing Together

*** What Problem It Saw

DOT-MoE diagnoses the shared disease of all predecessors in one sentence: everyone is "optimizing proxies for intermediate representations while ignoring the true output." MoEfication looks at input weights, LLaMA-MoE-v2 looks at activation importance, CMoE looks at co-activation — all proxies, none targeting "how faithfully does the model's output reconstruct after slicing?" The paper demonstrates empirically: proxy-based methods incur 2× to 41× higher per-layer reconstruction error (MSE) than DOT-MoE.

The second shared disease: from MoEfication to CMoE, "assign neurons" and "train router" are always sequential separate steps. Freeze the assignment first, train the router second — they never consult each other.

*** Its Solution

The key conceptual shift: stop treating "assignment" as a discrete problem to solve once and freeze; instead, make it *differentiable and optimizable via gradients*, and learn it *simultaneously* with the router.

Three pieces fit together:

1. *The Optimal Transport (OT) view.* Frame neuron assignment as a shipping problem: each neuron is one unit of cargo to be shipped to some expert warehouse; each warehouse has a fixed capacity (must receive exactly s neurons). A learnable "affinity matrix A" encodes how strongly each neuron prefers each expert. The goal is to maximize total affinity subject to the capacity constraint. This capacity constraint naturally enforces balanced expert sizes — no additional hard-balancing algorithm needed.

2. *Sinkhorn-Knopp iterations.* Unfortunately, the exact discrete solution to the shipping problem is neither differentiable nor tractable (combinatorial explosion). Adding an entropy regularizer "softens" it — the hard "this neuron goes 100% to expert 3" becomes a soft assignment "70% to expert 3, 20% to expert 1, …". This soft solution has a closed form, obtained by alternately normalizing rows and columns (Sinkhorn-Knopp), and the whole process is differentiable. A temperature τ controls the hardness; as τ → 0 the soft solution converges to the original discrete optimum.

3. *Straight-Through Estimator (STE).* At deployment, you ultimately need hard assignments (each neuron lives in exactly one expert), and top-k routing is also discrete and non-differentiable. The STE trick: the *forward pass* uses the hard discrete decision (ensuring deployment consistency), while the *backward pass* pretends the soft, differentiable path was taken — allowing gradients to flow back and update both the affinity matrix A *and* the router weights simultaneously.

These three pieces together, for the first time, allow "how neurons are assigned" and "how the router gates" to be *jointly* optimized end-to-end, and what is being optimized is directly "how accurately is the output reconstructed" (KL distillation + cross-entropy, plus z-loss and load-balancing on the router). During training the dense weights are frozen; only A and the router are learned. After convergence, A is extracted and assembled into a standard MoE.

*** Why It Worked (and What It Left Behind)

It simultaneously closes both cracks: OT + Sinkhorn + STE transform the discrete, two-step, proxy-driven assignment into a continuous, joint, output-driven optimization. Results: zero-shot retention of 90% of dense accuracy while halving activated parameters; on LLaMA-2-7B, zero-shot 61.5% vs. CMoE's 44.5%; after fine-tuning, 66.6% vs. CMoE's 51.7% and LLaMA-MoE-v2's 48.1%; on Qwen2.5-32B, a 34.3-point margin over CMoE. Ablations confirm the gains come from "output-awareness + joint optimization" itself, not a stronger training recipe or more experts.

New open questions: it still requires an "alignment phase" to train A and the router (though the backbone is frozen and cost is far below continued pre-training), unlike CMoE's near-zero-training goal. The stability and overhead of differentiable OT iterations and STE gradient approximations at very large model scales also remain to be refined.

* Frontier Extensions

As of June 2026 (DOT-MoE was submitted 2026-06-01), no subsequent papers explicitly critiquing or improving DOT-MoE have been found — it is the current state of the art on this line.

Anticipated directions (speculative, not confirmed citations):
- Eliminating the alignment phase entirely, achieving near-zero-training differentiable assignment comparable to CMoE's speed;
- Extending the same "output-aware + jointly differentiable" paradigm from FFN experts to attention experts (picking up LLaMA-MoE-v2's attention-expert thread);
- Verifying the stability and cost of Sinkhorn/STE at 100B+ model scales.

* Insights

Reading this river from source to mouth, the surface story is increasingly sophisticated slicing algorithms: clustering, random, importance, co-activation, optimal transport. But beneath the surface, two deeper cognitive shifts are actually happening.

*First shift: from "optimizing proxies" back to "optimizing the true objective."* For a decade, everyone was finding a *convenient proxy* — do weights look alike? are activations high? do neurons co-activate? — essentially because the "true objective" (is the output correct after slicing?) was too expensive, non-differentiable, and not directly optimizable. Each generation invented a cleverer proxy, but a proxy is still a proxy. DOT-MoE's real breakthrough is not the mathematical tool of optimal transport itself — it is that it *makes the true objective optimizable*: by softening with Sinkhorn and patching with STE, it forces the originally non-differentiable discrete assignment problem into the gradient descent pipeline, so that "output reconstruction error" can finally be optimized directly. This is the transition from the "proxy era" to the "objective era."

*Second shift: from "pipeline" to "joint optimization."* From MoEfication to CMoE, "assign neurons" and "train router" were always sequential stages — the output of one frozen into the input of the next. The cost of this pipeline mindset is that each stage reaches its own local optimum, and the combination is not guaranteed to be globally optimal. DOT-MoE welds the two stages into one differentiable framework and lets them converge together to a shared optimum. This is a recurring theme in deep learning: *whenever two things that used to be tuned separately can be learned end-to-end together, performance tends to leap* (just as feature engineering once yielded to end-to-end representation learning).

Where will this go next? Two signals: first, "zero-training" and "output-aware" remain at odds for now (CMoE optimizes speed, DOT-MoE optimizes quality) — making differentiable assignment nearly training-free would be very attractive. Second, the "differentiable OT + STE" joint optimization paradigm is fundamentally a general-purpose tool for "turning discrete structural choices into learnable ones"; it will not stop at MoE conversion. Any two-step pipeline of "first discretize into groups, then optimize each group separately" is a candidate to be welded into one step.

* Takeaways

The hardest research lesson from this river is: *when everyone is competing to invent a cleverer proxy metric, the real breakthrough is usually to eliminate the proxy — find a way to make the true objective directly optimizable.*

Concretely actionable:
- When scoping a problem, first ask: "Is what everyone optimizes the true objective or a proxy?" If it is a proxy, then "making the true objective differentiable/optimizable" is itself a worthwhile research contribution — often worth an order of magnitude more than inventing yet another better proxy.
- When you see a "two-step / pipeline" method (A is fixed first, B is then optimized on top of A), be alert: there is almost certainly a "joint end-to-end optimization" opportunity hiding there. Welding two steps into one is a repeatedly proven performance boost in deep learning.
- Mathematical tools (here: optimal transport + Sinkhorn + STE) are not showmanship. Their sole purpose is to *connect something originally non-differentiable/non-optimizable into the gradient pipeline.* Next time you are stuck with "this discrete decision can't be learned," remember this three-piece kit: entropy regularization to soften, Sinkhorn to solve the soft version, STE to pass gradients through hard decisions via soft surrogates.
