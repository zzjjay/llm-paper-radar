* The Problem River

A reasoning model solving a hard problem might generate 32,000 tokens of thought. That long stream of ideas is flat, unsegmented, and unhierarchical — the model itself has no idea which part is critical, which is a dead end, and which can already be discarded. Every old token must stay in the attention window forever, consuming memory and compute until the very end.

Why is this hard? Because an LLM is trained to write one token after another. It was never taught to "think halfway through, compress what it just said into a single sentence, and then reason forward from only that sentence." This is something the human brain does naturally — midway through a math problem you stop and say "OK, so far I've established that b+7 divides 56; now let me find the factors of 56," and you flip the draft face-down and never look at it again. But the LLM has no "flip it face-down" action.

Successive generations of work have patched this gap: some added hacks in the prompt; some introduced special tokens as compression slots; some sliced reasoning into chunks and used RL to learn inter-chunk handoffs. MEMENTO arrives at this point and asks — can we let the model generate its own "memento," and physically erase the old chunk from the KV cache, while still preserving the information implicitly encoded in that chunk?

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022 | CoT: Let LLMs Write Out Their Reasoning

*** What problem did it see?

The pre-GPT-3 paradigm was prompt → answer in one shot. The result: slightly harder arithmetic problems, or questions requiring multi-step reasoning, were frequently answered incorrectly — because the model had no "computational space" inside itself to store intermediate results.

*** Its solution

Explicitly write out the reasoning process before the final answer. "Let's think step by step." The model externalizes its mental scratch work into the token stream. In one stroke, the model gains an external working memory — the string of tokens it just wrote.

*** Why this works (and what new trap it sets)

The Transformer's attention mechanism lets later tokens attend to all earlier tokens. So once intermediate reasoning is written out, downstream tokens can reference it and reason further from it. This is equivalent to moving "working memory" from the model's hidden states into the token sequence — hidden states are finite; the token sequence can be arbitrarily long.

New trap: =the token sequence can be arbitrarily long= turns out to be the next decade's problem.

** 2023 | Gist Tokens: Compress Information into Special Token KVs

*** What problem did it see?

Prompts were getting longer and longer. Every inference required re-encoding the full prompt from scratch, KV caches grew ever larger, and redundant compute kept accumulating.

*** Its solution

Train a small number of special "gist" tokens, and teach the model to compress the prompt's semantics into the KV representations of those tokens. From then on, only the KV cache of the gist tokens needs to be stored; the prompt itself can be discarded.

*** Why this works (and what new trap it sets)

It moves "information" from "explicit tokens" into "KV tensors." Essentially, the KV cache is used as a compressed storage medium.

New trap: what gets packed in is no longer human-readable. If the model makes an error, you cannot inspect the gist tokens to see what they encoded — interpretability is entirely sacrificed.

** 2024–2025 | o1 / DeepSeek-R1: Let Models Think for a Long Time

*** What problem did it see?

Writing one or two paragraphs of CoT isn't enough. Problems like AIME and IMO often require 20,000–30,000 tokens of repeated attempts, backtracking, and self-correction before a solution can be found.

*** Its solution

Use RL (PPO/GRPO/…) to train models, rewarding "getting the answer right" rather than "using fewer steps." The model learns on its own that it needs to "think for a long time." After DeepSeek-R1 and similar models were open-sourced, tens-of-thousands-of-token reasoning chains became standard.

*** Why this works (and what new trap it sets)

Test-time compute can be traded for accuracy — give the model more token budget and it can solve harder problems.

New trap: the KV cache explodes. A 32K-token reasoning chain must reside entirely in the attention window — every old token competes equally with new tokens for attention, yet the vast majority of old tokens are spent (a wrong intermediate assumption, a failed algebraic manipulation). The model has no ability to "erase that section."

** 2025 | InftyThink: Chunking + Text Summaries

*** What problem did it see?

Picking up from above: the reasoning chain is flat, shallow, and incompressible. But humans don't actually think this way when doing math — midway through a solution you stop and say "OK, the conclusion of this section is X; I won't look at the draft above anymore."

*** Its solution

Slice the reasoning chain into chunks. At the end of each chunk, have the model generate a summary. When starting the next chunk, the prompt is: original question + summaries of all previous chunks — not the full text of those chunks. SFT is used to train the model to follow the pattern of "finish chunk → write summary → continue from summary."

*** Why this works (and what new trap it sets)

Reasoning becomes Markovian: each new chunk depends only on the summary of the previous one, not on the full history. The KV cache no longer grows linearly with reasoning length — it is "reset" to summary length at the end of each chunk.

New traps (two):
1. Inter-chunk handoffs are learned by SFT, which is unstable. The model sometimes writes summaries from which it cannot continue, or the next chunk diverges from what the summary says.
2. =Discarding the original text= may be too aggressive — some "unstated but useful" details in the original thinking are completely erased by the summary.

** 2025 | The Markovian Thinker: Use RL to Learn Chunk-to-Chunk Handoffs

*** What problem did it see?

InftyThink's inter-chunk handoffs are learned by SFT, but SFT never receives feedback that a handoff "didn't connect." The model might omit a critical intermediate value from the summary, causing the next chunk to fail outright.

*** Its solution

Apply RL to the entire "end chunk → write summary → start next chunk" pipeline. The terminal reward is "answer the question correctly," and the model learns on its own: the summary must retain whatever the next chunk genuinely needs; unimportant content can be dropped. This is what "Markovian" means — ensuring the state transition is complete.

*** Why this works (and what new trap it sets)

RL provides feedback: which summaries led to downstream success, and which didn't. This signal is far stronger than SFT's "imitate a correct summary."

New trap: carry-over is still =text-level=. A summary is a few sentences, and a few sentences can encode only so much. The "linguistically inexpressible" information hidden in the original thinking chunk — certain geometric relationships between tokens, implicit associations in attention patterns — is entirely lost.

** 2026 Apr | MEMENTO: Dual Information Stream

*** What problem did it see?

Looking back at the InftyThink/Markovian Thinker line, both make the same mistake: =they discard the old chunk's entire KV cache and keep only the text summary=. The problem is that the old chunk's KVs contain information beyond what the summary text can express — the model's internal representations from that chunk.

MEMENTO's key experiment: they recompute the memento's KVs from scratch (without the original chunk as context) and observe a 15-percentage-point drop on AIME24. This confirms that the memento tokens' own KV tensors truly do "silently" carry information from the original chunk.

*** Its solution

Rather than physically deleting old chunks, =attention-mask them=. The procedure:

1. The model generates thinking chunk T_i (occupying the KV cache)
2. The model immediately generates memento M_i (a short compressed text, a few sentences)
3. =Key=: M_i's KVs are computed while T_i is still in the cache — so M_i's KVs have attended over the full T_i
4. T_i's KVs are then physically freed, but M_i's KVs are retained
5. Subsequent tokens, via attention masking, can only attend to all M_i's and the current chunk — no T_i is visible

This is paired with the OpenMementos dataset (228K annotated trace + summary pairs), used for two-stage SFT. A modified vLLM fork then applies RL to further close the accuracy gap with baselines.

*** Why this works (and what new trap it sets)

=Dual information stream=: each chunk's information actually reaches downstream tokens through two channels — (a) the memento text itself; (b) the memento tokens' KV tensors (which attended over the complete original chunk when they were generated). These two channels are complementary: text preserves interpretability, KV preserves implicit information.

The resource savings are real: ~2.5× peak KV reduction, ~1.75× throughput improvement. Accuracy loss is small (AIME26 on Qwen3-32B drops only 2.6 pp).

New traps (left by MEMENTO itself):
1. An SFT round is still required — the OpenMementos dataset at 228K examples is small relative to frontier training scales
2. The memento KV is "implicit information" — MEMENTO's own paper demonstrates its existence but cannot explain what it contains. Interpretability has partially regressed
3. Chunk boundaries are determined offline using GPT-5.x scoring + an algorithm; online chunking (letting the model decide when to stop) is unsolved
4. Evaluation only reaches 32B; scaling behavior at larger sizes is unknown

* Frontier Extensions

As of April 2026, MEMENTO is the latest public paper on this line. But it points to several adjacent directions worth tracking:

- *PENCIL* (Yang et al. 2025): A small model (25M parameters) that learns to "erase" intermediate reasoning using reduction rules, demonstrating the potential to solve problems beyond a fixed context length on synthetic tasks like 3-SAT. MEMENTO lists this as future work — can PENCIL's "erase" idea be carried to frontier scales and real tasks?
- *KV cache pruning* (ThinKV, R-KV, LazyEviction, Reasoning Path Compression): inference-time KV pruning based directly on attention patterns, orthogonal to MEMENTO and composable with it
- *Sliding-window attention* (Olmo-3): architecturally restricting the attention span. MEMENTO shows Olmo-3-7B can be combined with MEMENTO, suggesting a natural future stacking strategy
- *MemAgent / MEM1*: training models to manage external memory (not their own thinking), primarily for tool-use and retrieval scenarios. Same spirit of "teaching models to self-manage context," but targeting a different domain (agents vs. mathematical reasoning)

* Insights

Reading the full river reveals a deep cognitive shift:

*Insight 1: From "can think" → "can remember selectively."* CoT solved "can the model think at all," but the cost was that it remembers everything and can discard nothing. The following decade of work has been compensating for one missing ability — =selective forgetting=. The human brain does this effortlessly; LLMs have never been able to. The core breakthrough of the MEMENTO line is giving models the ability to "erase the blackboard."

*Insight 2: The medium of compression keeps migrating.* First it was tokens (CoT writes explicit steps). Then KV tensors (gist tokens compress information into representations). Then text summaries (InftyThink). Then RL-trained summaries (Markovian Thinker). MEMENTO arrives at =text + KV carried simultaneously= — which implicitly acknowledges a fact: human language is insufficient to losslessly compress an LLM's own thinking state. An implicit channel is needed to compensate.

*Insight 3: Interpretability and efficiency are in tension.* Gist tokens went to one extreme: pure KV, maximally efficient, but unreadable. InftyThink went to the other: pure text, readable, but not efficient enough and potentially lossy. MEMENTO finds a middle ground — the primary layer is human-readable, but a hidden channel is preserved for efficiency. The question is: what is actually in that hidden channel? MEMENTO itself cannot answer this. The next paper along this line will very likely explore exactly that.

*Where things go next:*
1. =Adaptive block boundaries= — let the model decide when to stop and write a memento, removing the offline chunking dependency
2. =Interpretability of memento KVs= — what is actually stored there? Can we control what gets stored?
3. =Cross-task transfer= — MEMENTO is trained on math/science/coding; can it generalize to agents, long-document understanding, multimodal settings?
4. =RL on memento policy= — MEMENTO's RL currently only closes the accuracy gap; it doesn't explicitly reward "writing a good memento." What happens if you directly reward "the memento enabled downstream success"?

* Takeaways

What this river teaches about doing research:

*Takeaway 1: Notice the residual in a paradigm shift.* When InftyThink/Markovian Thinker discarded old chunks, they discarded KV tensors — but they never realized that KV tensors are themselves a form of compression. MEMENTO's insight came from: =the thing others threw away still contained a usable signal=. This is an "archaeological" style of discovery — finding an unexploited byproduct inside an existing approach.

*Takeaway 2: Physical action ≠ information action.* InftyThink's "discard" is physical (text is replaced by summary), and the information loss is real. MEMENTO's "discard" is physical (KV is freed), but the memento has already attended over the original chunk before the mask is applied — so the information is not lost. The same "discard" action, with a tiny difference in sequencing, determines whether information survives.

*Takeaway 3: Interpretability can be layered.* You don't have to choose between "fully readable" and "fully opaque." MEMENTO's dual-stream design — explicit layer readable by humans, implicit layer serving efficiency — is a design pattern worth reusing elsewhere. For example: in medical AI diagnostic reasoning, the explicit part is shown to the physician while the implicit part serves the model's own computation.

The practical bottom line:

- If you are building a long CoT reasoning product, try MEMENTO immediately — ~2.5× KV savings and ~1.75× throughput improvement are real, concrete cost reductions
- If you are working on agents, long-document summarization, or multi-turn dialogue, the "chunk + memento + KV mask" paradigm is directly borrowable — the core is the attention mask design pattern, which is task-agnostic
- If you are building an LLM serving framework, watch closely whether vLLM will merge MEMENTO-style "in-place KV masking" — it may become a standard feature of the next generation of inference engines
- If you are doing LLM training, the OpenMementos dataset is already open-sourced (228K traces) — it is one of the rare open datasets with segment + summary annotations, and has direct value for training long-reasoning models
