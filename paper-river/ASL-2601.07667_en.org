* The Problem River

When an LLM processes long text, it must cache the key/value tensors for every
token in the context into GPU memory. With hundreds of thousands of context
tokens, that cache consumes all available memory. The question is: can we cut
that cache by ninety percent and still get answers that are just as accurate?

What makes this hard: you don't know which tokens matter. Ask "where's the
needle in the haystack" and the needle is the crux; ask "summarize the whole
document" and the entire haystack matters. A token that looks unimportant at
layer 5 may become the pivot of the model's decision at layer 25. Any
one-size-fits-all pruning strategy will inevitably collapse on some class of
tasks.

Generation after generation of researchers has been answering the same question:
*At which layer, with what signal, and by dropping which tokens can we save
memory without losing reasoning quality?* ASL is the current downstream end of
this river.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2023.09 | StreamingLLM: Keeping the Model Coherent Indefinitely

*** What problem it saw

Early on, the most intuitive fix for an oversized KV cache was a sliding
window—keep only the most recent N tokens. But once deployed, the problem was
immediate: as soon as the context exceeded the window length, model output
collapsed into gibberish. All the content was recent—so why did it break?

*** Its solution

Xiao et al. examined the attention maps and spotted something strange: the model
consistently allocated enormous attention mass to the *very first few tokens*,
even if those tokens were meaningless (e.g., a BOS symbol). They called this
"attention sinks." The cause is buried in softmax—scores must sum to 1, so when
the model has nothing particularly interesting to attend to, it dumps its mass
onto the first token.

The fix is elegantly simple: keep the sliding window, but *permanently anchor
the first 4 tokens so they are never evicted*. Memory overhead is negligible,
yet the model can generate stably for millions of tokens.

*** Why it works — and what new pit it digs

It solves "long generation without collapse," but it is fundamentally a
hack—what is preserved is not "important content" but "the softmax dumping
ground." The large middle span of tokens that actually carry semantics is still
slid away. Ask "where is the needle?" and the needle has long since left the
window.

The next natural question follows immediately: can we identify *which middle
tokens are genuinely important* and keep those?

** 2023.06 | H2O: Heavy Tokens Surface on Their Own

*** What problem it saw

StreamingLLM keeps the head and tail, discarding the middle. But critical
information is often buried in the middle—a specific number or name somewhere
deep in a long prompt. Pruning by position inevitably loses it.

*** Its solution

Zhang et al. found that attention scores follow a *power-law distribution*: a
small minority of tokens absorbs the vast majority of attention. They called
these "heavy hitters" (H2).

H2O's strategy: during each generation step, accumulate the total attention
score each token has received across all steps so far. High score = heavy
hitter, keep; low score = bystander, evict. A recent local window is also
retained. The cache thus contains two categories: heavy hitters + recency.

*** Why it works — and what new pit it digs

The power law is real—a small cluster of tokens genuinely props up the entire
attention landscape. But H2O's scoring signal is *historical accumulation*:
every token must be attended to by every generated query before its cumulative
score is known. This means:

1. Expensive—every step must update scores for all tokens
2. It measures "was important in the past," not "will be important in the
   future"—naturally unfriendly to long-prompt / short-answer scenarios such as
   most RAG pipelines

The next generation must answer: can we skip the full history and use some
local signal from the prompt itself to identify heavy tokens faster and more
accurately?

** 2024.04 | SnapKV: The Prompt Tail Votes on What Matters

*** What problem it saw

H2O must wait for generation to begin before accumulating scores. Yet in many
scenarios the prompt itself already determines which passages the answer depends
on—for example, "Answer the following question based on the passage below:
[long document] Question: X." By the time the prompt is read, the model
effectively already knows where the focus should be.

Moreover, H2O scores tokens individually, which can shred a coherent phrase into
scattered tokens and lose local context.

*** Its solution

Li et al. introduced two changes:

1. *Observation window*: retain a small segment at the end of the prompt (e.g.,
   the last 32 tokens) and use that segment's queries to score all preceding
   tokens retroactively. "Where the tail is looking is where it matters."
2. *Pooling*: apply 1D average pooling (e.g., window size 7) over the raw
   scores before selecting tokens. Instead of picking isolated high-score
   tokens, this selects *clusters* of high scores, capturing coherent semantic
   spans rather than scattered individual tokens.

*** Why it works — and what new pit it digs

The observation window plus pooling became so practical that nearly every
subsequent KV cache compression paper adopted SnapKV's scoring pipeline. But
SnapKV retains *the same number of tokens in every layer* by default. The
problem: attention behavior differs dramatically across layers—lower layers are
diffuse, upper layers are sharply focused. Allocating the same budget to each
layer leaves lower layers potentially under-budgeted and upper layers
definitively wasteful.

** 2024.06 | PyramidKV: Allocate Budget by Layer

*** What problem it saw

The PyramidKV team observed a clear pattern: as depth increases, attention
becomes progressively concentrated on fewer and fewer tokens. They called this
the "pyramid-shaped information funnel"—lower layers cast a wide net, upper
layers strike with precision.

Since upper layers already attend to just a handful of tokens, retaining 256
versus retaining 64 makes almost no difference there. SnapKV's uniform
allocation is pure waste in the upper layers.

*** Its solution

Allocate the budget as a pyramid: lower layers keep more (e.g., 1024 tokens),
upper layers keep fewer (e.g., 64), with a linear decrease in between. The total
budget is unchanged, but the distribution now matches the true "upper layers are
sparse" reality. Which specific tokens to keep within each layer still follows
SnapKV's observation-window-plus-pooling vote.

*** Why it works — and what new pit it digs

PyramidKV was the first to treat the *layer dimension* as an independent
optimization axis—prior work selected tokens; PyramidKV began selecting "how
many tokens per layer." This direction is correct, but problems remain:

1. It selects the *per-layer budget*, not *where to make the pruning
   decision*—every layer must still compute full attention to score tokens
2. The prefill stage is not accelerated at all—a long prompt still forces every
   token through every layer

Next question: can we prune at one shallow layer so that subsequent layers never
even see the discarded tokens?

** 2024.09 | GemFilter: An Early Layer Is Already a Good Enough Filter

*** What problem it saw

PyramidKV saves decode-time memory but not prefill compute. Yet prefill is the
dominant cost—routing a 128K-token context through all layers takes enormous
time and memory.

Shi et al. discovered a counterintuitive fact: *the attention pattern of an
early layer (e.g., layer 13 in LLaMA-3.1-8B) already pinpoints the critical
tokens with high accuracy.* You don't need to run to the last layer to know what
matters.

*** Its solution

Two forward passes:
1. *First pass (filter)*: run only up to layer 13, examine that layer's
   attention, and select the top-k tokens (e.g., 1024).
2. *Second pass (inference)*: truncate the original 128K-token sequence to those
   1024 tokens and run the full model from scratch.

This sounds like extra work, but: the first pass covers only ~1/3 of the model;
the second pass processes only ~1/100 of the original sequence length. Total
cost is far below one full pass over 128K tokens.

*** Why it works — and what new pit it digs

GemFilter was the first to show that prefill can be accelerated, and that
*shallow layers are sufficient* for token selection. But two forward passes still
means running one segment twice. Can we do it in a single pass—prune in place at
layer 13, then continue forward with the reduced sequence?

** 2025.02 | FastKV: One Pass, Prune in Place at the TSP Layer

*** What problem it saw

GemFilter's two passes carry two redundancies: the first pass is discarded
immediately after filtering; the second pass re-computes what was already done.
At its core, GemFilter hard-couples "filtering" to "inference"—the filter layer
is also the restart point for all computation.

*** Its solution

Jo et al. proposed *Token-Selective Propagation* (TSP):

1. A single forward pass, computing layer by layer from 0 upward.
2. Upon reaching the designated TSP layer (e.g., layer 15), perform a one-shot
   prune—select the top-k tokens and *propagate only those k tokens' hidden
   states forward*.
3. All subsequent layers process only those k tokens; the effective sequence
   length is cut by an order of magnitude right there.

It also decouples "how many tokens to propagate forward" from "how many KV pairs
to cache at decode time"—the former controls prefill speedup, the latter
controls decode memory. Two independent knobs.

*** Why it works — and what new pit it digs

FastKV is clean and elegant: one forward pass, faster prefill, controllable
decode memory. But it hard-codes the TSP layer—layer 15 for LLaMA-3.1-8B,
layer 19 for Mistral-Nemo. These are hand-tuned values that "look fine on most
tasks."

That is exactly where the trouble lies. ASL's Figure 1 lands a direct blow on
FastKV: on *KV retrieval* tasks, pruning at a fixed layer 15 causes accuracy to
fall off a cliff. The intuition is straightforward—hard tasks require the model
to reason through more layers before it knows "which tokens I actually need to
look at." Pruning at layer 15 means making that decision for the model before it
has finished thinking.

** 2026.01 | ASL: Let the Model Say "I've Figured It Out"

*** What problem it saw

From PyramidKV to FastKV, every "layer choice" was *preset*—empirical values
derived from average behavior across tasks. But the depth at which attention
converges is actually *task-dependent*:

- Simple language modeling: converges around layer 5
- Long-document summarization: converges around layer 12
- Multi-hop KV retrieval: may not converge until layer 20+

A fixed layer will inevitably fail on some task class.

*** Its solution

Taniguchi et al. reasoned: instead of guessing, *listen to the model itself.*
They focused on one observable quantity—*the variance of token rankings*:

1. Start observing from some shallow layer (e.g., layer 10).
2. At each layer, rank all tokens by their attention scores.
3. Measure how much the *rankings of the same top-k tokens* have shifted across
   the most recent 8 layers.
4. Small ranking change = the model has stabilized on which tokens matter = safe
   to prune now.
5. Rankings still shifting = the model hasn't finished deciding = wait one more
   layer.

Concretely, they compute "relative variance"—dividing the current layer's
variance by the variance at the initial observation layer. When this ratio drops
below a threshold (e.g., 0.3), pruning is triggered. The division normalizes the
signal, making the threshold transferable across different tasks.

After pruning, ASL coordinates with SnapKV: for the discarded tokens' KV caches
accumulated before the prune layer, SnapKV further compresses them during
decode; layers after the prune point process only the surviving top-k tokens.

*** Why it works — and what new pit it digs

ASL's core insight: *attention convergence is an observable signal.* No task
labels needed, no classifier to train—just look at the variance of the
model-generated rankings.

It transforms "which layer to prune at" from a static hyperparameter into a
runtime decision. Simple tasks trigger pruning at layer 12; hard tasks wait
until layer 25; the budget matches automatically. Experiments in the paper show
that ASL does recover the accuracy gap that FastKV loses on hard tasks.

Remaining pits: the threshold τ is still a hyperparameter (though the paper
reports 0.3 generalizes well); the observation window L_obs is also fixed; and
ASL only decides *where to prune*—*which tokens to prune* still uses SnapKV's
observation-window voting. If future work shows SnapKV's scoring is itself
flawed, ASL will be dragged down with it.

* Frontier Extensions

ASL is a January 2026 paper and currently represents the leading edge of this
line of work. Several foreseeable directions follow:

- *Joint adaptation*: "layer selection" is now adaptive, but "which tokens to
  select" still uses SnapKV's static rules. The next step may make token
  selection adaptive too—dynamically choosing the retention strategy based on
  query type.
- *Multi-stage pruning*: ASL performs a single one-shot prune at one layer. If
  different sub-tasks converge at different depths, progressive pruning at
  multiple layers may be needed.
- *Training-time integration*: ASL is training-free, which is both its virtue
  and its ceiling. Explicitly training the model to recognize "when it has
  already converged" could produce a much cleaner signal.

* Insight

On the surface this river is about "how to compress the KV cache." At its core
it is a recurring cognitive shift: *from static heuristics to dynamic observable
signals.*

- Generation 1 (StreamingLLM / H2O): use global statistics (position /
  cumulative scores) for static decisions.
- Generation 2 (SnapKV / PyramidKV): use local statistics (observation window /
  inter-layer decay patterns) for static decisions.
- Generation 3 (GemFilter / FastKV): move the decision point inside the
  prefill, but still at a static layer.
- Generation 4 (ASL): the decision point itself becomes dynamic—monitor a
  *runtime convergence signal* and let the model tell you when to prune.

Each generation finds the next variable frozen by the previous generation's
static assumptions and makes it dynamic. The logical next step is: *token
selection itself* also becomes runtime-adaptive (every current method still uses
the fixed observation-window-plus-pooling scoring pipeline).

At a deeper level, this line of work reveals a general methodology: *when your
system performs unevenly across different inputs, find a runtime signal that
distinguishes those inputs—rather than continuing to tune hyperparameters.*

* Takeaways

What this river suggests about how to do research:

1. *"General" isn't always general*—FastKV's fixed TSP layer looks fine across
   most tasks, but ASL tears it open with one neglected hard task class. Find a
   subset of inputs that a method quietly avoids, run your experiment there, and
   you often expose the gap in the current SOTA.

2. *Adaptive methods always live downstream of static rules*—any widely adopted
   paper that contains an "empirical value" or "fixed layer" is the attack
   surface for the next paper. Ask yourself: could this be determined dynamically
   from the input?

3. *Observation signals are cheaper than trained signals*—ASL changed no model
   weights, added no classifier, and used ranking variance as its signal. Many
   judgments that seem to "require training" can actually be replaced by a
   runtime statistic—provided you are willing to stare at the intermediate
   outputs.

Bottom line: the next time you read a SOTA paper, flip straight to the
hyperparameter table. *The values that have been fixed there are the titles of
the next paper.*
