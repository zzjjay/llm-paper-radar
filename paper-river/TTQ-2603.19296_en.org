* The River of the Problem

LLMs are too large to run cheaply — they need compression. The most direct approach is
quantization: chop 16-bit weights down to 4 or 3 bits, cut memory footprint by four to eight
times, and speed up matrix multiplications. But the harder you chop, the dumber the model
gets. Everyone working on this line since 1990 has been asking the same question: when you
shrink weights, *which ones need to be handled carefully, and which can be discarded freely*?

The criterion for "importance" has kept shifting. The earliest answer was the weight's
Hessian — theoretically perfect, but computationally explosive. Then came "look at how large
the activations are" — crude but workable. Then "let calibration data tell you which channels
matter" — faster, but calibration data and real user inputs diverge, and accuracy drops when
the model is deployed on unfamiliar tasks.

TTQ, proposed in 2026, moves the entire calibration step to inference time: *every prompt that
arrives triggers a fresh computation of which channels matter, followed by on-the-fly
quantization*. The concept of calibration data vanishes entirely, because the input itself is
the calibration data. Across 35 years of evolution, this field has really been doing one thing:
making the weight-importance criterion progressively lighter and progressively more immediate.

* Layer One: OBD — Use Second Derivatives to Find Unimportant Weights

#+begin_quote
LeCun, Denker, Solla. /Optimal Brain Damage/. NeurIPS 1989.
#+end_quote

By 1989, neural networks had grown large enough that "prune some weights to speed up
inference" had become a real engineering problem.

The pruning strategy of the day was crude: zero out weights whose absolute value was small.
LeCun's objection was precise: *a small weight is not necessarily an unimportant one*. A weight
might be numerically tiny yet lie along a steep direction of the loss surface — removing it
would cause a dramatic increase in loss. Another weight might be numerically large yet sit in
a flat region where the loss is wholly insensitive — removing it is harmless.

His fix, in one sentence: use the second derivative of the loss with respect to each weight
(the diagonal of the Hessian) as the importance score. The intuition is that *a large second
derivative means the loss is sensitive to perturbations along that direction, so the weight
matters; a small second derivative means it can safely be removed*.

This was the founding move of the "look at curvature, not magnitude" paradigm. But OBD
assumes the Hessian is diagonal — that is, it pretends all weights are mutually independent.
That assumption is badly wrong, but in 1989 computing the full Hessian was out of the
question, so the approximation had to stand.

* Layer Two: OBS — Weights Are Entangled with Each Other

#+begin_quote
Hassibi, Stork. /Second order derivatives for network pruning: Optimal Brain Surgeon/. NeurIPS 1992.
#+end_quote

OBS addressed the gap OBD left: weights in a neural network are not isolated — they are
strongly entangled. Remove one, and the optimal values of all the others shift. OBD pretended
this entanglement did not exist, which is why pruning with OBD tends to collapse once pushed
far enough.

OBS's fix is to use the *full* inverse Hessian matrix: not only compute "how much does loss
rise if this weight is removed," but also compute "how should the remaining weights
compensate after the removal." This is a more accurate second-order expansion.

The price is O(d³) for the Hessian inversion. Networks of the early 1990s were tiny —
a few thousand parameters — so the computation was barely feasible. The framework was
regarded at the time as "theoretically elegant but practically intractable" and sat dormant
for more than two decades.

Then came the LLM era — suddenly "theoretically elegant" regained its value, because a
few percentage points of accuracy could justify deploying billions of parameters.
OBS was pulled off the shelf and reexamined.

* Layer Three: GPTQ — Rebuild OBS as Quantization, Make It Tractable

#+begin_quote
Frantar, Ashkboos, Hoefler, Alistarh. /GPTQ: Accurate post-training quantization for generative pre-trained transformers/. ICLR 2023.
#+end_quote

GPTQ borrowed directly from OBS but introduced two key modifications that made it runnable on
175B-parameter LLMs.

The first modification: replace "pruning" with "quantization." OBS zeros out a weight; GPTQ
rounds a weight from fp16 to the nearest 4-bit grid point. The mathematical structure is
identical — both are a "perturbation" that demands compensation of the other weights.

The second modification: instead of computing a separate Hessian inverse for each weight,
process columns greedily and prepare all the required inverse elements in one shot via
Cholesky decomposition. Complexity drops from O(d⁴) to O(d³); with layers handled
independently, a 70B model can be quantized in a few hours.

But GPTQ left a gap that AWQ later targeted: *you still need calibration data*. To form the
Hessian, thousands of text samples must be fed through the model in a forward pass to collect
activation statistics. What data to use, how much, and how far it differs from real usage
conditions — these became arguments that occupied the LLM quantization community for years.

* Layer Four: AWQ — The Hessian Is Too Heavy; the Diagonal Approximation Is Enough

#+begin_quote
Lin, Tang, Tang, Yang, Xiao, Dang, Gan, Han. /AWQ: Activation-aware Weight Quantization for LLM Compression and Acceleration/. MLSys 2024.
#+end_quote

AWQ identified two problems with GPTQ.

First, Cholesky decomposition is still too heavy. On edge devices you do not want to run
hours of Hessian computation every time you deploy a model.

Second, a deeper insight: *in the linear layers of an LLM, most of the inter-weight entanglement
can be captured by a diagonal matrix*. In other words, most channels are essentially
independent; only a small number of "salient channels" genuinely need protection.

AWQ's solution is clean: approximate the Hessian directly as ~diag(X X^T)^α~ — a diagonal
matrix whose entries are the per-channel norms of the input activations. Before quantization,
multiply each column of the weight matrix by this scale; after quantization, divide back.
This pushes the "important channels" into the finer regions of the quantization grid, naturally
yielding higher effective precision.

Complexity drops from GPTQ's O(d³) to roughly O(d²). The calibration data requirement shrinks
dramatically. AWQ became the de facto industrial default from 2023 to 2025 — the
~awq_gemm~ CUDA kernel in vLLM was written specifically for it.

But AWQ introduced a new gap: *you still need calibration data to estimate the diagonal norms*.
Typically, a pass over C4 or WikiText is used to compute the scales, which are then baked into
the weights at deployment. If a user's actual inputs differ substantially from C4 — code, math,
medicine, multilingual text — AWQ's scales are miscalibrated, and accuracy on out-of-domain
tasks drops.

And — *once quantization is done, the original fp16 weights are discarded*, leaving no way to
recalibrate.

* Layer Five: TTQ — Move the Entire Calibration Step to Inference Time

#+begin_quote
Koike-Akino, Liu, Wang. /TTQ: Activation-Aware Test-Time Quantization to Accelerate LLM Inference On The Fly/. arXiv 2026.03.
#+end_quote

TTQ's entire motivation grows directly out of AWQ's gap: since calibration data and real inputs
do not match, why not *use the real input as the calibration data*?

The key technical observation: AWQ's diagonal norm ~D_i = (||X_i||^p + λ)^α~ is actually very
cheap to compute — it is just a p-norm over each row of the input activations, an O(dT)
operation. Compared with the original linear projection at O(d'dT), the extra overhead is only
O(1/d' + 3/T). At the scales typical of LLMs — d' in the thousands, T in the hundreds —
*this overhead is essentially zero*.

TTQ therefore restructures the entire pipeline as follows:

1. A prompt arrives; obtain the input activations X at each layer for this prompt.
2. Compute D on the spot; compute scale S and zero-point Z on the spot.
3. Quantize the fp16 weights to 4 bits on the spot (quantize W * D^(1/2), then rescale by D^(-1/2)).
4. Run the layer output using a 4-bit int_matmul kernel.
5. Repeat for the next layer.

Every prompt receives its own dedicated quantized version. *Calibration data = the current
prompt*. *Out-of-domain degradation does not exist*.

The paper also layers on a low-rank compensation: ~W_hat = W_q + B A~, where the low-rank
factors B and A are static (initialized via top-r PCA), while W_q is still recomputed for every
prompt. This combination superficially resembles QLoRA, but QLoRA freezes W_q and only
trains B and A; TTQ is the opposite — W_q moves, B and A are frozen.

Experimental results: on OPT-350M and Qwen3-1.7B at 3-bit quantization, TTQ achieves lower
perplexity than AWQ calibrated on C4, while requiring *zero offline calibration data*.

The explicit cost: *every prompt requires re-quantizing all weights*. The paper reports up to 5×
speedup on GPU because the memory-bandwidth savings from 4-bit matrix multiplication outweigh
the re-quantization overhead. Whether this arithmetic holds on edge devices is the hard
engineering battle this direction still has to win.

* Frontiers and Next Steps

TTQ is a 2026.03 paper with no publicly available follow-up work that directly critiques it.
But several natural extensions are already visible:

- *The same author group* has prior work on micro-grained MoE (Koike-Akino 2025) framed as
  "test-time pruning." TTQ is test-time quantization. The next step is most likely test-time
  low-rank adaptation combined with test-time mixed precision — completing a full "test-time
  compression" toolbox.

- *Test-time scaling* (the line of work around Muennighoff 2025's s1) has been surging —
  computing more steps or sampling more trajectories at inference to buy performance. TTQ
  pushes in the opposite direction: consuming fewer resources at inference to buy
  cost-efficiency. Both are fundamentally about "shifting training-time decisions into
  inference time." This paradigm shift will continue.

- *Hardware co-design*: AWQ became mainstream partly because the ~awq_gemm~ CUDA kernel was
  ready. For TTQ to reach production, it needs a fused "quantize + matmul" operator that makes
  the re-quantization cost disappear inside the data pipeline. That is the next engineering gate.

- *The end of calibration*: from OBD in 1990 to TTQ in 2026, three and a half decades of
  evolution amount to making the importance criterion progressively lighter and more immediate
  — from needing a full Hessian, to needing calibration data, to needing only the current
  prompt. One further step: *could we require no statistics whatsoever*? For instance, deriving
  the quantization strategy purely from the structural properties of the weights themselves —
  symmetry, low-rank spectrum — as an a priori decision? That is the contemporary echo of
  LeCun's 1989 question.

* What This River Is Really Changing

On the surface this looks like a story of "quantization algorithms getting faster and faster."
But underneath, what is really happening is that the LLM era is redefining what "deployment"
means.

In the OBD/OBS era: a trained model was fixed; pruning was a one-time surgery performed
once and then sealed.

In the GPTQ/AWQ era: quantization became a one-time "post-processing" step, but it required
"representative data" to do properly. The whole industry began accepting the idea of "ignore
compression during training; compress just before deployment."

In the TTQ era: the model itself stays high-precision (fp16 weights are retained), but
*every inference pass compresses the model fresh on the fly*. Compression is no longer a
step before deployment; it is an embedded subroutine of the inference process itself.

This shift shares its underlying logic with the rise of test-time computation: *the inference
phase is no longer passive consumption of a pre-trained model, but an active, adaptive,
per-prompt dynamic process*. The algorithm no longer serves the model; the model becomes
an artifact shaped by the algorithm at inference time.
