* The Problem River

A trillion-parameter sparse MoE model activates only a tiny handful of "experts" at inference time. The catch: all experts still have to live in GPU memory. Can we, without any retraining, simply discard half the experts and have the model continue to work normally?

The difficulty has two layers. First, within each layer — which experts can be dropped? By activation frequency? By routing weight? By activation magnitude? Second, a deeper question: if the global target is to prune half the experts, should every layer lose 50%, or should some layers lose 80% while others stay untouched? The first question has been studied for two years. The second has always been silently answered with "every layer loses the same amount," and nobody has seriously challenged that. EvoESAP is here to challenge it.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2024.02 | NAEE: First to Treat an Entire Expert as the Unit of Compression

*** What problem did it see?

Before NAEE, slimming down large models relied on essentially two tools: weight pruning (down to individual parameters) or quantization. Neither fits sparse MoE well — MoE's "bulk" is not in any individual weight but in having dozens or even hundreds of experts all packed into GPU memory. Traditional weight pruning also requires specialized hardware to exploit sparsity, making deployment difficult.

*** Its solution

Coarsen the pruning granularity: decide "keep or drop" at the level of one complete expert. The selection signal is straightforward — count how often each expert is chosen by the router on a calibration dataset. Rarely selected experts are deleted outright. The whole pipeline needs no retraining and no special hardware; it is plug-and-play.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfalls it buries)

MoE training already suffers from uneven expert utilization, and some experts genuinely carry most of the traffic. But the approach is rough in two ways: dropping purely by frequency causes noticeable accuracy loss; and it silently encodes an implicit assumption — every layer loses the same fraction of experts. That assumption would be carried forward by every subsequent paper until EvoESAP finally confronted it.

** 2024.04 | SEER-MoE: Recovering Accuracy, and Accidentally Discovering That Non-Uniform Allocation Is a Gold Mine

*** What problem did it see?

NAEE's direct removal of cold experts causes non-trivial accuracy degradation. SEER-MoE's goal is to recoup that loss while pushing the number of active experts even lower.

*** Its solution

Two stages: the first stage is still "heavy-hitters counting" — prune low-frequency experts. The second stage is the key contribution — a short regularized fine-tune that encourages the model to accomplish the same tasks with fewer experts.

But the most consequential thing SEER-MoE buries is a side experiment: when allocating the pruning budget using frequency scores, a *global allocation* (some layers lose more experts, others lose fewer) outperforms *per-layer uniform allocation* on MMLU.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfalls it buries)

That fine-tuning can recover accuracy is expected — the model gets a chance to readjust. But the global-allocation finding was received ambiguously by subsequent work: MoE-I² ran the same comparison and found that the optimal configuration for Mixtral is actually close to uniform, directly contradicting SEER-MoE. So the question of whether cross-layer non-uniformity actually helps remained open and unresolved.

** 2024–2025 | EAN: The Winner of the Within-Layer Criterion Arms Race

*** What problem did it see?

Frequency is a crude signal — a frequently chosen expert is not necessarily a large contributor to final output, while a rarely chosen expert might be decisive for key tokens. This motivated a surge of within-layer ranking criteria: activation norm, gradient magnitude, Taylor-expansion approximations of loss change, and more.

*** Its solution

Jaiswal et al. ran a large-scale evaluation of 16 within-layer criteria head to head. EAN (an Expert Activation Norm family of criteria) came out on top overall.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfalls it buries)

This generation of research collectively accepted one shared premise: every layer is pruned at the same ratio. All effort went into "within each layer, which ordering is more accurate?" That collective blind spot is precisely what EvoESAP would go on to puncture.

** 2025.10 | REAP: Routing Weight × Activation Norm, and a Definitive Stand for Pruning Over Merging

*** What problem did it see?

Running in parallel with pruning was an alternative route: *merging experts* — taking a weighted average of two similar experts into one. On discriminative, multiple-choice tasks, merging even appeared to outperform pruning.

REAP's theoretical insight: merging strips the router of independent, input-conditioned control over each expert, causing a "functional subspace collapse." Discriminative tasks (a single forward pass to compute log-likelihoods) cannot feel this damage, but autoregressive generation accumulates and amplifies it.

*** Its solution

A more precise within-layer criterion — consider both the router's gate value for a token (how important is this expert for this token?) and the expert's activation norm (how strong is its output?), multiplying the two as the importance score. This minimizes an upper bound on reconstruction error.

An empirical knockout: pruning 50% of experts on Qwen3-Coder-480B, Kimi-K2, and similar models causes almost no drop in coding and tool-calling accuracy.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfalls it buries)

REAP pushes the within-layer line to a high watermark. But it still inherits the "uniform compression per layer" default. The paper explicitly acknowledges that the effect of cross-layer allocation on generation tasks is an open question. That open question is exactly the socket EvoESAP plugs into.

** 2026.03 | EvoESAP: Step Off the Criterion Race, Harvest Cross-Layer Allocation

*** What problem did it see?

After two years, within-layer criteria have matured into something like REAP. Yet everyone still defaults to equal pruning per layer — and that assumption has never been rigorously tested. SEER-MoE says global allocation is better; MoE-I² says uniform is fine; the conclusions conflict, because in every prior study "which experts" and "how many per layer" were coupled together, making the search space explode and preventing a clean comparison.

A deeper problem: what metric lets you quickly compare thousands of different layer-wise allocations during search? MMLU accuracy? Too expensive. Perplexity? Decoupled from generation quality. Actual autoregressive generation? One evaluation takes 29.49 hours — search is infeasible.

*** Its solution

Two things:

First, decompose the problem into two halves — within-layer ranking uses whatever existing criterion you like (Frequency / EAN / SEER / REAP, fixed in place); search only over the cross-layer budget allocation. This decomposition shrinks the search space from astronomical to tractable.

Second, construct a *cheap, stable, theoretically grounded* proxy metric: ESAP. The inspiration comes from speculative decoding: a small draft model generates tokens; the large model accepts each draft token with probability α = min(1, p/q). The paper's insight is — "if a pruned candidate model can serve as a good draft for its original, then its behavior is close to the original's." Acceptance rate is therefore a natural compatibility measure.

But running actual speculative decoding for scoring is still expensive and noisy. ESAP instead derives a closed-form expression for the expected acceptance rate: under teacher-forced context, the expected acceptance rate = Σᵥ min(p(v), q(v)), which is exactly *one minus the total variation distance*. One forward pass suffices; no sampling noise; bounded in [0, 1]; 18× faster than running speculative decoding directly (1.64 h vs. 29.49 h).

Wrapped around this metric is an evolutionary search: under a fixed global budget, continually mutate the per-layer allocation, score with ESAP, and iterate to convergence.

*** Why it works (and what new pitfalls it buries)

The fundamental reason it works is that it genuinely decouples two independent problems: existing within-layer ranking methods are already high quality, but cross-layer allocation is a completely separate, neglected optimization dimension. Pulling it out and solving it alone with evolutionary search naturally captures gains that were always there but invisible.

Empirically: on 7B–30B SMoE models paired with REAP for within-layer ranking, MATH-500 improves by 19.6 points at 50% sparsity.

Remaining pitfalls: the evolutionary search itself is not free (each evaluation is fast, but many evaluations are needed); ESAP is a proxy for token-level behavioral similarity and may not perfectly reflect task-level generation quality.

* Frontier Extensions

Two distinct tracks are emerging in parallel:

- *DiEP (Bai et al. 2025.09)*: Uses differentiable search to jointly learn within-layer selection and cross-layer importance in a single end-to-end stage. The philosophy is the opposite of EvoESAP — EvoESAP advocates decoupling; DiEP advocates joint optimization. But DiEP has only been validated on MC benchmarks and has not touched generation tasks.
- *DERN (2025.09)*: Abandons the binary "keep or drop" framing. Instead, experts slated for deletion are decomposed at the neuron level, and each neuron segment is realigned into the surviving experts. A more aggressive technical route — a hybrid of merging and pruning.

The overall picture for 2026: the optimal within-layer solution has largely converged toward REAP; cross-layer allocation is the new battleground (EvoESAP vs. DiEP); neuron-level recombination (DERN) is a separate branch opening up.

* Insights

On the surface this line of work is "MoE pruning getting more accurate." Underneath it is a shift in research taste.

The first four generations of papers all did the same thing — refinement within a default search space (uniform per-layer budget, better within-layer criterion). EvoESAP did not continue refining inside that space; it stepped back and asked: are we actually solving the right optimization problem? The cross-layer allocation dimension had simply never been seriously opened up.

This is the classic evolution of an engineering problem: the first generation proves feasibility, the second improves accuracy, the third improves efficiency, the fourth uncovers *a coupled dimension that was hidden all along*. The first three generations tuned coefficients inside a known formula; the fourth discovered there was a term nobody had written down.

Also worth remembering: ESAP uses the speculative decoding acceptance rate as a proxy metric — a beautiful cross-domain transfer. Speculative decoding was originally a tool for inference acceleration; here it becomes a measure of model similarity, and taking the closed-form expectation eliminates sampling noise entirely. This kind of "borrow operator A from domain X and use it as a loss / score in domain Y" move is behind a remarkable number of step-changes in research.

The most likely next directions: truly joint optimization of cross-layer allocation and within-layer criteria (DiEP is already attempting this, though not yet mature); or extending the ESAP "behavioral similarity proxy" idea to other post-training compression scenarios — quantization, low-rank decomposition, or draft model selection for speculative decoding itself.

* Takeaways

Long immersion in an engineering problem makes it easy to get trapped inside a shared assumption. Everyone is optimizing X because everyone has silently decided Y is fixed. The biggest advances usually don't come from squeezing another 5% out of X — they come from daring to poke at Y.

Concretely:

- *When entering any research area, first ask: "What is the shared default assumption?"* The hypothesis that every paper's introduction brushes past in one sentence is usually where the next increment lives.
- *Evaluation cost determines what optimizations are even possible.* EvoESAP's real leverage is not the evolutionary search (that is just the tool) — it is that ESAP makes evaluation 18× cheaper. Whenever you want to search or optimize but the cost is prohibitive, the first question to ask is: "Can I construct a theoretically grounded cheap proxy?"
- *Cross-domain operator transfer.* Speculative decoding → model similarity metric is a textbook-quality transplant. Keep a running list in your head: which operators can be repurposed as a loss function or scoring function?

This means the next time you face a direction that looks saturated, the move is not "add another attention layer" — it is to decompose the entire pipeline, fix your gaze on the quietly accepted hard assumption, and recognize that it is usually the entrance to the next unexplored space.
