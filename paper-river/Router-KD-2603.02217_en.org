* What This River Is About

A simple question everyone overlooked for a decade:

  *If you swap out the experts, what happens to the little dispatcher that routes the work?*

At the heart of an MoE model are a group of experts and a router. The router sees a token and decides which experts to send it to. The entire research ecosystem has been obsessed with the experts — how to prune half of them, merge a batch, compress them into low-rank form — but no one ever touched the router.

The finding in this paper is almost embarrassingly simple: you change the experts, but the router is still navigating by the old map. Of course it sends tokens to the wrong door.

So reading this river in reverse, the question is not "how does MoE compression keep getting more sophisticated" — it is the more elementary question: *how badly does a dispatcher trained for the old world misfire in the new one?*

* Layer Zero: Shazeer 2017 — Fitting a Dispatcher Inside a Neural Network

Before this, neural networks ran "all hands on deck" — every parameter had to compute on every input. Scale the model up and the compute cost rose linearly. That road hit a wall.

What the "Outrageously Large Neural Networks" paper did can be said in one sentence:

  *Instead of having every expert look at every token, first send the token past a tiny gatekeeper that decides which two or three experts to wake up.*

This is *Sparse MoE*. A token arrives, passes through a small "gate" (router/gate), the gate picks the top-k experts, only those few compute, and the rest sleep. The model can hold an enormous number of parameters (large capacity), yet the compute for a single forward pass depends only on those k experts.

The seed left behind: that gate is trained jointly with the experts. It learns "when I see a token of type X, send it to expert 3" — a map deeply entangled with the current identities of the experts.

* Layer One: Switch Transformer 2021 — Cut k to 1 and Prove This Works in Production

Shazeer's design was elegant, but top-2/top-4 routing carried enormous communication overhead in distributed training. Switch Transformer spotted the problem and made one drastic simplification:

  *Each token visits exactly 1 expert.*

(top-1 routing)

That sounds self-defeating — expressivity slashed. But the authors showed that with enough experts and proper training stability, single-expert routing could scale to a trillion parameters. MoE graduated from a clever idea to an industrial-grade solution.

The seed left behind: the community accepted "many experts" as the normal regime. Once you have dozens or hundreds of experts, the router is no longer "pick from 4 options" but "pick top-8 from 128" — a much more complex map that depends far more critically on the identities of the experts.

* Layer Two: Mixtral 2024 — Open-Sourcing MoE and Making Deployment Everyone's Problem

Switch Transformer was an internal Google affair; the community had no model weights. Mixtral 8x7B turned this into a public problem:

  *A 47B-parameter open-source MoE that approaches the performance of a 70B dense model — but you have to fit 47B in memory to run it.*

Mixtral uses 8 experts with top-2 routing per token — *coarse-grained MoE*. The tension surfaced immediately:

  - Inference compute is at the 13B scale, yet VRAM must hold 47B
  - Six experts are asleep at any given moment, occupying hardware without being used

This contradiction directly spawned an entire compression research line: *since most experts are idle most of the time, can we just deal with them?*

The seed left behind: Mixtral has only 8 experts. Each one is "wide" — a 7B slab. Drop one and you lose a huge chunk of capability. This line can't really play on Mixtral; it needs finer-grained MoE to appear first.

* Layer Two (Parallel): DeepSeekMoE / Qwen3-MoE — Slice Experts Smaller and More Numerous

Around the same time, DeepSeek and Qwen took a different path:

  *Increase the expert count from 8 to 128, making each expert correspondingly smaller.*

(fine-grained MoE)

This opened the door to compression. Pruning a few dozen experts out of 128 costs only 0.78% of capacity per cut — enormous room to maneuver. Compression research finally had a real target: *in a world of 128-choose-8, which experts deserve to go?*

* Layer Three: HC-SMoE 2024 — The First Systematic "Expert Merging"

HC-SMoE (ICML 2025) identified the first clear problem:

  *In coarse-grained MoEs like Mixtral, every expert matters too much to simply drop. But perhaps experts with similar functions can be merged into one.*

Its approach:

  1. Run a calibration corpus through the model and record each expert's output
  2. Apply hierarchical clustering, grouping experts by output similarity
  3. Take a weighted average of experts within the same cluster to form one merged expert

(retraining-free expert merging via hierarchical clustering)

There is a subtle detail here: HC-SMoE explicitly uses *expert outputs* rather than *router logits* for clustering. The rationale — router outputs are dataset-dependent; change the benchmark and they drift.

  *The authors already sensed something: "the router can't fully be trusted." Their solution was to route around the router, not fix it.*

The seed left behind: after merging, the router still points at "expert 3," but expert 3 has become "the average of expert 3 and expert 7." The map is stale, but nobody thinks it matters.

* Layer Three (Parallel): REAP 2025 — Using the Router's Gate Value to Decide What to Prune

REAP (Cerebras Research) went in the opposite direction:

  *Forget merging — merging causes "functional subspace collapse." Prune directly.*

Their core argument: merging two experts is equivalent to stripping the router's ability to choose between them independently, because after merging the router can only invoke them as a unit. That loss is irreversible.

REAP's pruning criterion:

  Importance = router gate value for this expert × expert output norm

(router gate value × activation norm)

In other words, *REAP is the first method to explicitly depend on the router's output*. It trusts the router to identify "who is unimportant." After pruning, the router stays unchanged; certain expert indices are simply masked out.

The seed left behind: REAP trusts the router to say who gets pruned, but after pruning nobody asks — *are the gate probabilities the router assigns to the surviving experts still reasonable?* The probability sent to expert 3 was computed in a world where experts 1–128 were all competing. Now only 80 survive. Is that distribution still correct?

* Layer Three (Parallel): MoBE / TD-MoE — Low-Rank Decomposition of Experts

A third line "edits experts" — MoBE, TD-MoE, and similar methods apply SVD/Tucker decomposition to expert matrices, retain the principal components, and discard the small singular values. Expert count stays the same, but each expert slims down.

The logic is unchanged: *modify the experts, leave the router alone.*

* Layer Four: Router-KD 2026 — Asking the Question Everyone Ignored

By this point three lines had been running for a while: pruning, merging, editing. Each competed on "how to change experts," and each stalled at some performance ceiling.

The authors of this paper did something anyone could have done but no one had:

  *They plotted the router's outputs before and after compression.*

(Section 3.5, Figure 2 of the paper)

Experiment: take Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct, 100 ELI5 examples, and compare routing probabilities before and after compression. Findings:

  - L1 distance diverges visibly across most layers
  - Top-8 expert selection overlap collapses as layer depth increases
  - All three compression paradigms (prune, merge, edit) exhibit this problem; pruning is the worst

This converted "the router is navigating by the wrong map" from intuition into evidence.

** The Fix: Touch Only the Router, Freeze the Experts

Since the problem is that the router hasn't kept up, train the router alone. But preserve the "retraining-free" spirit — no fine-tuning the experts, or the cost comes right back.

Router-KD's design:

  1. *Freeze* all experts, all transformer layers, all embeddings — only the parameters of the router matrices are allowed to move
  2. The *teacher* is the uncompressed original model; the *student* is the compressed model
  3. The *loss* is the KL divergence of next-token distributions (with temperature τ):

#+begin_src text

  L_RKD(x) = (τ² / N_x) · Σ_t  m_{t+1} · D_KL( p_T^(t) || p_S^(t) )

  where p_T, p_S are softmax(logits / τ) soft distributions
        N_x is the number of valid tokens

#+end_src

  4. *Data* is unlabeled text from C4

This is an extremely lightweight training run — the router is a hidden_dim × num_experts matrix per layer, totaling less than 1% of model parameters. A single GPU can finish it in a few hours.

** Results and One Illuminating Counter-Example

Router-KD improves all three paradigms (prune, merge, edit) clearly on Qwen3 (128 experts, fine-grained). On Mixtral (8 experts, coarse-grained) it barely moves the needle.

The authors' explanation is elegant:

  *Mixtral is 8-choose-2, giving C(8,2) = 28 routing combinations. Qwen3 is 128-choose-8, giving roughly 1.4 × 10¹² combinations.*

When the routing space is tiny, the original router's output is nearly one-hot — there is almost no "soft" information left to distill. Fine-grained MoE routing is inherently a high-entropy decision, which is exactly why KD has leverage there.

This counter-example reinforces the central claim: *the problem really is the router, but the router has to have something to calibrate.*

* What Is Really Happening Beneath This Evolutionary Arc

On the surface this looks like an iteration of MoE compression techniques. One level deeper, what is happening is:

  *A shift from treating MoE as "a pile of experts" to treating MoE as "an integrated system with a dispatch layer."*

Early MoE research cared deeply about training the router well (load balancing loss, auxiliary loss, and so on). But at deployment time everyone instinctively treated the router as "a fixed lookup function," mentally separating it from the experts. Compression research inherited this split — hot activity on the expert side, complete neglect on the router side.

Router-KD's contribution is not that its KL loss is particularly clever (knowledge distillation is as vanilla as it gets). Its value is in stating plainly: *the router is part of the model, and it can misfire once the experts change.*

This is a genuine *reversal of perspective*. In the compression context, the dominant question for a decade was "how do we compress the experts?" — nobody asked "does the routing still match?" It is rather like debating how to downsize a workforce while forgetting that the HR system's job descriptions also need to be updated.

* Where This Line Most Likely Goes Next

1. *Joint optimization of routing and compression* — rather than compress first and calibrate after, incorporate router distillation loss directly into the pruning/merging objective. REAP's "router gate × activation" importance score will likely evolve into a "router distillation loss" importance score.

2. *More aggressive compression on fine-grained MoE* — Router-KD reveals that "the router is an undervalued degree of freedom." This means the same accuracy budget can potentially support a higher compression ratio.

3. *Coarse-grained lines like Mixtral become peripheral* — the paper's counter-example makes clear that the main arena for future MoE compression will be fine-grained models, which aligns with the direction DeepSeek/Qwen are heading.

4. *Dynamic routing at inference time* — if the router can be adapted offline via KD, perhaps it can also be adapted online based on the input distribution (per-domain router, test-time router adaptation).
