* One-liner

The biggest obstacle to compressing large models below one bit per weight is not "too few bits" but "wrong geometry" — this paper finds a way to first correct the shape, then flatten it.

* Evolutionary Narrative: From the Origin

** Layer 0: Where It All Begins (Eckart-Young, 1936)

Ninety years ago two mathematicians proved one thing: if you are allowed only the top-k largest directions to approximate a matrix, the truncated SVD is the best possible answer. This theorem has been the bedrock of "compression" ever since — whenever you want to approximate "many directions" with "fewer directions," you are implicitly invoking it.

The open question it left: it assumes the retained directions can be *arbitrary real numbers*. Hardware does not like real numbers.

** Layer 1: Turning "Fewer Bits" into Engineering (GPTQ, 2022)

Frantar et al. did something simple yet powerful in 2022: take an already-trained multi-billion-parameter model, leave the training pipeline untouched, and layer by layer compress the weights post-hoc to 4-bit, carefully compensating for the compression error along the way. The result was surprisingly good — LLaMA 70B at 4-bit lost almost no accuracy.

This set the tone for the entire field: *post-training quantization* is viable; no need to retrain from scratch.

The open question it left: 4-bit is still too large. A single 80 GB GPU cannot fit a 700B model; to run truly large models you need to halve the footprint again and again.

** Layer 2: Train a 1-Bit Model Directly (BitNet, 2023; b1.58, 2024)

The Microsoft line took a more radical stance: since post-hoc compression always loses information, let weights take only {-1, +1} from the very first training step (BitNet), or {-1, 0, +1} (b1.58), and let the model adapt to that constraint. The community was stunned — at the 3B scale, a ternary model could match an fp16 baseline.

The open question it left: training cost is enormous, and the industry already has well-trained fp16 models like Llama-3 and Qwen. Nobody wants to start over. Can we compress post-hoc?

** Layer 3: Post-Hoc Compression of Existing Models to 1-Bit (OneBit / BiLLM, 2024)

OneBit and BiLLM arrived at "post-hoc 1-bit." BiLLM observed that a small number of "salient weights" are disproportionately important, kept those in higher precision, and binarized the rest. OneBit used a "sign matrix + two floating-point vectors" for scaling. Both methods compressed already-trained models to an average of 1 bit per weight while retaining most capability.

The open question they left: 1 bit per weight is the floor of the discrete world. How do you go sub-1-bit?

** Layer 4: Breaking Through the 1-Bit Floor (ARB-LLM, STBLLM, 2024)

To achieve an *average* below 1 bit, structural tricks are needed. ARB-LLM and STBLLM used sparsification, group sharing, and structured encoding to push *total bits / total weights* below 1. But these methods are fundamentally subtracting from the "binary floor" without changing the underlying representation.

The open question they left: is there a representation that is *naturally* sub-1-bit?

** Layer 5: Low-Rank × Binary — An Elegant Composite (LittleBit v1, Lee 2025)

Last year, Lee et al. proposed a mathematically elegant idea: since Eckart-Young tells us low-rank approximation is optimal, why not decompose the weight matrix into a product of two *binary matrices*:

#+begin_src
  W (n x m, fp16)  ~=  A (n x r, +/-1)  *  B (r x m, +/-1)
#+end_src

Each weight then costs roughly (n+m)*r / (n*m) bits — as long as r is small enough, this is naturally < 1 bit. This was *LittleBit*, the first method to multiply "low-rank" by "binary."

Yet in practice it consistently trailed pure 1-bit SOTA. Why? Nobody had a clear answer.

** Layer 6: This Paper (LittleBit-2, 2026)

The authors tackle the "why does it trail" question head-on. Their diagnosis is: *Latent Geometry Misalignment*.

Specifically: the singular vectors produced by SVD are typically "spiky" — energy concentrated in a few coordinates (high coherence). But binarization can only output {-1, +1}, and it is worst at representing spiky vectors. If you take a vector with energy packed into 3 coordinates and try to fit it with ±1 entries, no matter how you try the approximation is far off.

It is like trying to build a thin needle out of LEGO bricks — it simply will not look right.

*Key insight*: then turn the needle into a rod first.

They borrow an old trick from Gong et al.'s 2012 work on binary hashing — *ITQ (Iterative Quantization)*: before binarizing, apply a rotation to the vectors so that energy spreads across all coordinates (turning "spiky" into "uniform"). A rotation is an orthogonal transformation; it does not destroy the information in the original matrix. Binarizing afterwards produces far smaller errors.

LittleBit-2 generalizes ITQ to the low-rank × binary composite decomposition, calling it *Joint-ITQ*: it jointly optimizes the rotation matrix and the two binary factors. An *Internal Latent Rotation* then absorbs the rotation into the matrix itself, adding zero inference overhead.

Result: across the 0.1–1 bpp range on Llama-2 and Llama-3, the low-rank × binary line catches up with pure 1-bit SOTA for the first time.

* A Glimpse Ahead

LittleBit-2 genuinely opens a door: *geometric priors* + *discrete representations*.

Foreseeable directions:
- Replace ITQ with stronger rotations (e.g., Hadamard transforms, already proven effective at 4-bit by QuaRot/SpinQuant)
- Encourage incoherent singular vectors *during* training rather than correcting for coherence post-hoc
- Extend to KV-cache and activations, not just weights

* Deeper Insight

On the surface this lineage is about "fewer and fewer bits." Look one layer deeper and it is about "stricter and stricter hardware constraints." But LittleBit-2 exposes an even deeper turning point —

*The paradigm of quantization research is shifting from "numerical precision" to "geometric shape."*

For the past decade everyone asked: how many bits are enough? The answer kept shrinking (32 → 16 → 8 → 4 → 1 → < 1). But the LittleBit-2 line shows that as you approach the bit-count limit, what truly determines the error is not the bit count itself but *whether the geometry of the object being quantized matches the geometry of the quantizer*.

The geometry of a binary quantizer is "vertices of a hypercube" — a uniform, symmetric discrete set.
The geometry of neural network weights is "spiky" — energy concentrated along a few directions.

When the shapes are mismatched, more bits cannot save you; when the shapes are aligned, 1 bit is enough.

So the coming keywords in this field will not be "0.5-bit" or "0.3-bit." They will be *coherence*, *rotation*, *incoherence processing* — all the geometric tools for turning spikes into uniformity.

QuaRot and SpinQuant already proved the same point at 4-bit. LittleBit-2 pushes that story into sub-1-bit territory. The next step will very likely see someone formalize this whole suite of *geometric preconditioning* into a framework that becomes the third generation of quantization infrastructure, following GPTQ and AWQ.
