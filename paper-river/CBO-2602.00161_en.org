* The River of the Problem

A question simple enough to make you scratch your head: a large model has dozens of Transformer layers — can you chop them out whole, making it smaller and faster without making it stupid?

It sounds like "throw away a few unwanted parts." The hard part is this: with 32 layers and a desire to remove 16, the number of combinations is 600 million. Running the model for every combination to check quality is completely infeasible. Worse, layers are not independent — dropping layer A alone leaves the model fine, dropping layer B alone leaves it fine, but dropping A and B together can cause collapse. This "coupling" means greedy methods (removing the least important layer one at a time) are guaranteed to miss the mark.

The entire research thread is one generation after another pressing forward, trying to force this combinatorial "which layers to drop" problem from exponential complexity down to something computable.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1989 | OBD: The First Time "Which Weight to Prune" Became a Math Problem

*** What Problem It Saw

At the end of the 1980s, the pain point of neural networks was "too large, too slow, overfitting." People knew that many weights were useless, but deciding "which ones to prune" was pure intuition — "prune whichever has a small absolute value." This magnitude pruning was blunt and often wrong: a weight with a very small magnitude might be exactly the switch on some critical pathway.

*** Its Solution

LeCun argued: pruning a weight is equivalent to pushing it from its current value to zero. The effect of this perturbation on the loss can be estimated with a Taylor expansion. The first-order term is zero at convergence, so the second-order term dominates — the Hessian. The "saliency" of each weight is =w² · H_ii= (the Hessian diagonal). Weights with low saliency are the ones that truly should be pruned.

Analogy: you don't know which bolt on a bridge you can safely remove. OBD's approach is — for each bolt, imagine "if I pulled this one, how much would the bridge shake," then only pull the ones that cause the least shaking.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

It was the first time pruning was transformed from intuition into optimization. Using second-order information was far smarter than just looking at weight magnitudes.

But OBD had a lazy assumption: that the Hessian is diagonal — i.e., weights do not affect one another. In reality this is completely false: if you pull bolt A, the stress distribution around bolt B changes. OBD could not see that layer of interaction.

** 1993 | OBS: After Pulling a Bolt, Retighten the Ones Around It

*** What Problem It Saw

OBS's authors directly exposed OBD's diagonal assumption. They showed experimentally that in many networks the off-diagonal entries of the Hessian are far from negligible — weights are coupled. Because OBD ignores this coupling, models after pruning degrade far more than theoretically predicted.

*** Its Solution

Use the full Hessian. Moreover, when pruning a single weight, simultaneously update all remaining weights according to the Hessian's coupling structure to compensate — equivalent to "after pulling a bolt, retighten every bolt around it." This way no retraining is needed after pruning; the model rebalances itself.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

It upgraded OBD's "independent scoring" to "global rebalancing." The theory is cleaner, the practical results better — for the next twenty years, essentially every paper on second-order pruning is a descendant of OBS.

But OBS still prunes one weight at a time, then rebalances, then prunes the next. That is greedy. If "the optimal set of 100 weights to prune together" differs from "the 100 weights picked one by one" — and they often do — OBS cannot find the global optimum.

** 2022 | CBS: If You're Pruning a Group, Choose the Whole Group at Once

*** What Problem It Saw

CBS's authors saw OBS's limitation clearly: greedily pruning one weight at a time essentially solves a combinatorial optimization problem as a sequential one. But what you actually want is "prune M weights such that the loss increase is minimized" — that is a one-shot combinatorial question, not a series of greedy choices.

*** Its Solution

Assign each weight a binary variable =x_i ∈ {0, 1}=: =1= means prune it, =0= means keep it. The problem then becomes:

  Minimize  =Σ Δloss(x)=
  subject to  =Σ x_i = M=  (prune exactly M weights)

This is a standard constrained binary optimization problem. The loss increment is approximated with a second-order Taylor expansion, making the objective function take the form =xᵀHx= — quadratic, constrained, with 0/1 variables. Solvers can be IP solvers, QUBO solvers, or even quantum annealers.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

For the first time, "globally optimally choosing a group of weights to prune" became a math problem you could write down. Pruning now had a formal language: constrained binary optimization (CBO) ≡ Ising model.

But CBS still operates at the level of individual weights — a large model has billions of weights, so there are billions of binary variables and the Hessian matrix cannot even be stored. The formalism is elegant but blocked by scale.

** 2024–2026 | ShortGPT / SLEB / SWM / Norm Ratio: The LLM Era Turns to Pruning Whole Layers

*** What Problem It Saw

After LLMs took off, the "prune individual weights" route began hitting walls:

- Irregular sparse weights yield almost no inference speedup (GPUs dislike sparsity)
- The bottleneck in large models is excessive depth, VRAM limits, and oversized KV caches
- Dropping an entire Transformer block is far more directly beneficial for inference than removing individual weights

So the field pivoted en masse to *block pruning*: cut 32 layers down to 16, halve the model, double inference speed.

*** Its Solution

All of these methods are straightforward: compute an "importance score" for each layer, rank them, and chop the lowest-scoring ones:

- *ShortGPT (BI)*: measure the cosine similarity between a layer's input and output — if they're nearly identical, the layer isn't doing much
- *SLEB*: try removing layers one by one and see how much perplexity rises
- *SWM*: sliding-window merging of adjacent redundant layers
- *Norm ratio*: ratio of the output norm to the input norm

Empirically: later layers are easier to prune; pruning a contiguous segment from the middle-to-back is most stable. This became the "industry consensus."

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

Simple, runnable, and genuinely fast at inference. The most practical tier in the short run.

But they all make the same mistake — *ignoring inter-layer interactions*. Scoring each layer independently is essentially OBD replayed at the block scale: once again assuming the interaction matrix is diagonal. Reality is entirely different: pruning 16 contiguous layers might cause collapse, while pruning 16 scattered layers might be fine, or vice versa. These methods cannot see the coupling, so they can only produce greedy solutions.

And the so-called empirical rule of "prune a contiguous segment from the middle-to-back" is actually a bias arising from an incompletely explored search space.

** 2026 | CBO (This Paper): Lifting CBS to the Block Scale

*** What Problem It Saw

The authors connected the two threads above:

- Heuristic methods (the ShortGPT tier) do whole-layer pruning but are locally greedy and blind to inter-layer coupling
- The second-order combinatorial optimization framework of CBS is correct, but written at the weight scale it explodes

So: can the "combinatorial optimization language" of CBS be transplanted verbatim to the block scale? There are only a few dozen blocks in total (=N ~ 10²=), the combinatorial count =C(N, M)= is fully enumerable, and the Hessian is only a few tens × a few tens — a CPU can compute it in minutes.

*** Its Solution

Attach a gating variable =α_i= to each layer:

  =h_out = ATTN(h_in) · α_i + h_in=

=α_i = 1= is the original model; =α_i = 0= prunes that layer. Then perform a second-order Taylor expansion over these =α= values and write down the same constrained binary optimization as CBS:

  =min  xᵀ H⁰ x=
  =s.t. Σ x_i = M=

This is formally equivalent to a fixed-magnetization Ising model — each x is a spin, up = keep, down = prune, total number of down spins fixed. Low-energy states correspond to high-quality pruning configurations.

The number of variables drops from "billions of weights" to "a few dozen blocks"; the Hessian drops from an astronomical size to =N×N=. All the CBS toolbox (IP solvers, QUBO solvers, quantum annealers, QAOA) instantly becomes applicable.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

Three things make this particularly interesting:

1. *Breaking the "contiguous pruning" superstition*: On Llama-3.1-8B pruning 16 layers, the ground state recommends [14, 16–24, 26–31] — contiguous segments. But the 17th excited state recommends [2, 16, 18–31] — it also prunes layer 2, very near the front, something no heuristic method would ever suggest. This "counterintuitive" configuration actually scores one point higher than the ground state on MMLU and 12 points higher than ShortGPT.
2. *Low-energy excited states = free diversity*: CBO doesn't just give one optimal solution — it naturally produces an entire spectrum of low-energy states. Different pruning configurations suit different downstream tasks; you can pick.
3. *Handles heterogeneous architectures*: On NVIDIA Nemotron-Nano-30B (a Mamba + Attention + MoE hybrid), heuristic scoring largely breaks down, but CBO does not depend on architectural homogeneity and works regardless — preserving 94% / 88% accuracy even without retraining.

Remaining gaps:

- When =N= is truly large (e.g., 128+ layers in a giant model) or =M= approaches =N/2=, the combinatorial count still explodes and approximate solvers are required
- That is exactly where quantum annealing / QAOA / modern SAT solvers come in — and that is precisely the core business of Multiverse Computing, the authors' employer

So this paper is, at its heart, finding an LLM application niche that is "just large enough, and just favorable enough," for quantum optimizers.

* Frontier Extensions

CBO has already pushed this thread to a new level of formalization, and several follow-on directions are already in motion:

- *Altearrea-Ferré et al. (2025) - Beyond Ground States*: explicitly explores low-energy excited states of the Ising model — the CBO paper already cites it, signaling that "using excited states for pruning" is being taken seriously as a future direction.
- *Automatic configuration selection*: the paper itself mentions using Pearson correlation coefficients or diversity scores to automatically pick the configuration best suited to a specific downstream task from the low-energy spectrum, rather than choosing manually.
- *Combined layer merging*: CBO is pure pruning and does not touch SWM's merging idea. Incorporating "prune some + merge some" into CBO's objective function could compress further.
- *Quantum hardware deployment*: The real test is whether, for 100B+ models with =N > 100=, D-Wave / IonQ / QAOA can find solutions faster and more accurately than classical brute force. That is the true next step this paper is preparing for.

* Insights

Reading through this thread, three things surface:

1. *The same mathematical structure re-emerges at a higher level of abstraction every so often.* The OBD vs. OBS opposition (independent vs. coupled) replayed itself twenty years later, verbatim, in LLM block pruning — ShortGPT is OBD in disguise, CBO is OBS+CBS in disguise. This implies: whenever a pruning problem appears at a new granularity (individual unit, layer, module, subgraph…), the path "independent scoring → model coupling → write as combinatorial optimization" can almost be predicted in advance.

2. *The sweet spot in scale determines a method's feasibility.* CBS didn't take off in 2022 because there were too many variables at the weight scale; the same idea transplanted to the block scale (=N ~ 10²=) immediately becomes runnable. This tells you: when a classical algorithm is revived, it usually isn't because the algorithm changed — it's because the scale of the problem changed. The Multiverse team is shrewd — they didn't go after weight-level sparsity, the hardest nut to crack; they targeted the sweet spot where "the combinatorial count is just barely enumerable, and just barely favorable for quantum optimizers."

3. *The ground state is not necessarily the best solution — the side products of a formal framework can be more valuable than the main dish.* CBO's most counterintuitive finding is that "the 17th excited state scores one MMLU point higher than the ground state." It overturns the industry rule of thumb that "pruning a contiguous mid-to-back segment is the most stable." Deeper still: casting the problem as an Ising model gives you not just "one optimal solution" but "the entire low-energy spectrum" — a free lunch that heuristic methods can never provide.

Where is the next move most likely to go? The answer is not in the algorithm, but in the hardware. The day =N= grows to 200+ layers and =M= approaches =N/2= — when classical brute force truly cannot cope — quantum annealing and QAOA will finally be forced into the open. CBO is the admission ticket prepared for that day.

* Takeaways

On "how to find problems":

- Look for two seemingly unrelated fields (here: "classical pruning theory" and "LLM whole-layer compression") and ask whether they can be bridged with the same mathematical language — many new papers don't invent something new, they move something old to a new scale.
- Look for the "just feasible" sweet spot: if an old method runs on small problems but breaks down at LLM scale, ask "is there an intermediate scale where the old method lands back in the feasible region?"

On "how to read papers":

- A paper's real contribution often isn't in its claimed "we outperform SOTA by X points," but in its implicit "we translated this problem into another language." CBO's true value is equipping the LLM compression problem with the grammar of the Ising model.
- Pay attention to a paper's "side products." CBO's most interesting finding (excited states beating the ground state) fell out of the formal framework — the authors weren't looking for it originally. Side products of this kind are often more worth pursuing than the main claim.

On "how to do engineering":

- When an engineering problem has been written down as "score → rank → pick the top few," stop and ask: are the variables coupled? If they are, the correct formulation is combinatorial optimization, not ranking.
- The fact that an LLM has only a few dozen blocks means that many decisions that look NP-hard (which layers to prune, which experts to keep, which heads to disable) actually have a combinatorial count that is fully enumerable — don't be intimidated by apparent complexity; count the variables first.
