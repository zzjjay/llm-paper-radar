* The Problem River

Compress a massive language model into a smaller one, run it faster, spend less — but
what if, after compression, the model quietly generates three times as many tokens?
Where did those savings go?

The central tension of this thread is: *quantization* (packing floating-point weights into
integers) never carries just one cost — "loss of precision." As language models evolved
from "answer questions" to "think at length before answering," problems we thought
were solved in quantization suddenly reappear in a completely new form — not wrong
answers, but a silently bloated reasoning process before the answer.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022 | OBC: Connecting "Smart Pruning" to Quantization

*** What Problem It Saw

In 1989, LeCun proposed "Optimal Brain Damage" (OBD): use the Hessian matrix to
identify which weights can be removed with the least increase in loss, then remove
them. The idea is elegant — the diagonal elements of the Hessian tell you the
curvature of the loss with respect to each weight; low curvature means the weight
is unimportant. In 1993, Hassibi upgraded this to "Optimal Brain Surgeon" (OBS),
accounting for interactions among weights and achieving higher theoretical precision.

But this framework was never seriously extended to *quantization* — it was used only
for pruning (zeroing out weights). Frantar et al. noticed that quantization and pruning
are fundamentally the same problem. Changing an FP32 weight from 0.347 to 0.375
(the nearest INT4 grid point) is, like pruning, a forced constraint imposed on a weight.
The same Hessian framework can therefore be applied.

*** Their Solution

*Optimal Brain Compression (OBC)* / *Optimal Brain Quantizer (OBQ)*:
extend the OBS framework to quantization. Core idea: quantize one weight at a time,
then use Hessian information to "redistribute" the quantization error onto the remaining
weights, letting them compensate. Think of a class exam: if one student has an off day,
the other students' scores are nudged slightly to keep the total on target.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Buried)

OBQ substantially outperforms naive quantization on medium-scale models. But there
is one fatal flaw: every weight quantized requires a Hessian inverse update, giving
O(d_col^3) complexity per layer. For a 175B-parameter GPT model, this translates to
weeks of GPU time. OBQ is theoretically sound but practically unrunnable.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Three Engineering Tricks That Made OBQ 1000× Faster

*** What Problem It Saw

OBQ's cubic complexity made it completely unusable at GPT-3 scale and above. Yet
the demand to compress large models to 4-bit was real — LLM.int8() (Dettmers et al.,
July 2022) had shown that INT8 quantization could fit a 175B model on a single
machine, making people realize low-bit quantization was the key to democratizing
large models. OBQ's approach was correct but had to be accelerated.

*** Their Solution

Frantar et al. identified three assumptions in OBQ that could be broken:

1. *Fixed order, no search needed*: OBQ greedily searches for "which weight to
   quantize next with minimum loss," but for large models, any fixed order works
   almost as well. The search is eliminated.

2. *Shared Hessian inverse*: With a fixed order, all rows of a weight matrix share
   one Hessian inverse, computed only once. Complexity drops from
   O(d^3 × n_weights) to O(d^3 per layer).

3. *Lazy Batch Updates*: Rather than updating all remaining weights immediately after
   quantizing each one, accumulate a batch (128 columns) and update together,
   dramatically improving GPU cache utilization.

Three tricks combined: quantizing a 175B model shrinks from weeks to *4 GPU hours*;
accuracy at INT3/4 precision is nearly intact (perplexity increase < 0.1).

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Buried)

GPTQ made 4-bit inference an engineering commodity — it was quickly adopted as the
default compression scheme in a large number of serving systems. But GPTQ was
designed to "minimize layer output error" using generic calibration data (WikiText).
It treats all weights equally, with no awareness of which weights matter most for the
target task (e.g., mathematical reasoning).

** 2023 | AWQ: Look at Activation Magnitude, Not Weight Magnitude

*** What Problem It Saw

GPTQ's engineering success revealed a new phenomenon: activation distributions are
extremely non-uniform. Certain weight channels correspond to activations that are tens
of times larger than others — quantization errors in these channels are amplified by
those activations, causing outsized impact on the output. GPTQ does not distinguish
these channels from ordinary ones, leading to over-quantization of critical channels.

An analogy: when compressing a photo, you need to preserve the most important pixel
details. GPTQ allocates the same number of bits to every pixel; AWQ realizes that
certain pixels (those corresponding to high-activation weight channels) are 100× more
expensive to get wrong, and deserve special protection.

*** Their Solution

*AWQ (Activation-aware Weight Quantization)*: instead of mixed precision (which
is hardware-unfriendly), apply an equivalent transformation — scale up the "important"
weight channels before quantization and scale them back down after. This effectively
increases the resolution of those channels while keeping the mathematical operation
equivalent.

Importance is judged not by the Hessian but directly from activation statistics:
channels with large activations = important channels. Protecting only *1%* of weight
channels is enough to substantially reduce quantization error.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Buried)

AWQ outperforms GPTQ on instruction-following tasks and became the de facto 4-bit
quantization standard in 2023–2024, winning MLSys 2024 Best Paper.

But both AWQ and GPTQ share a silent assumption: *the quality of quantization equals
accuracy on standard benchmarks*. During evaluation, the model answers questions,
generates a few dozen tokens, and we check whether they are correct — that seemed
sufficient. By 2025, this assumption began to crack.

** 2025-01 | DeepSeek-R1: "Think Before Speaking" Changed the Arithmetic of Inference Cost

*** What Problem It Saw

Standard LLM reasoning chains are short: "2 + 2 = 4," five tokens, done. But complex
math problems (competition problems, physics, code generation) require multi-step
reasoning, and standard LLMs are trained to jump straight to the answer, making
them prone to skipping steps and erring.

*** Their Solution

DeepSeek-AI trained models with *pure reinforcement learning*: given math problems,
with only the correctness of the final answer as the reward signal, and no reasoning
traces provided as supervision. The result was striking: models spontaneously produced
internal monologues thousands of tokens long — self-verifying, backtracking,
switching approaches, exactly like a human working through scratch paper.

DeepSeek-R1 matched OpenAI o1 on multiple competition math and code generation
benchmarks.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Buried)

Reasoning models became the new paradigm: a single request is no longer ~50 tokens
but a 2000–8000 token reasoning chain plus a final answer.

This fundamentally changed what quantization means. Before: "quantization makes the
model 3× faster." Now: "quantization makes each token 3× faster, but if the token
count doubles, how much faster is it really?" No one had asked this question.

** 2025-01 | Li et al.: Quantization Hurts Math Reasoning — The Accuracy Perspective

*** What Problem It Saw

After DeepSeek-R1, a natural engineering question arose: can we quantize it? But
before that, no one had systematically studied how quantization affects *math reasoning
accuracy*. GPTQ and AWQ benchmarked against language-modeling perplexity, not
reasoning accuracy.

*** Their Solution

Li et al. (January 2025) quantized the Llama-3 family with AWQ and GPTQ and
conducted systematic evaluation on a math benchmark (the MATH dataset). They also
proposed a multidimensional error-diagnosis framework, classifying reasoning errors into
four types: conceptual errors, method errors, execution errors, and logical errors.

*Findings*: 4-bit quantization reduced math accuracy by an average of 11.31%, and
up to 32.39% in the worst case. The fix: just 332 carefully selected samples with
QLoRA fine-tuning — 3 minutes of GPU time — recovered most of the lost accuracy.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Buried)

This paper sounded an alarm for the community: quantization damages reasoning far
more than perplexity suggests. But it studied ordinary LLMs (Llama-3) doing math,
not reasoning models (DeepSeek-R1 family). And it still focused solely on accuracy,
never asking "did quantization change the token count?"

** 2025-04 | Liu et al.: Quantization of Reasoning Models — Accuracy Is Actually Fine

*** What Problem It Saw

Li et al. studied ordinary LLMs, but the DeepSeek-R1 family is a different kind of
model — specially alignment-trained on reasoning data, and fundamentally more robust.
The conclusion that "quantization necessarily harms reasoning" needed to be reverified
on reasoning models specifically.

*** Their Solution

Liu et al. (COLM 2025) systematically tested the DeepSeek-R1-Distill family (1.5B to
70B), QwQ-32B, and Qwen3-8B under W4A16, W8A8, and lower bit-widths, covering
four benchmarks: AIME, MATH-500, GPQA, and LiveCodeBench.

*Findings*:
- W4A16 (4-bit weights + 16-bit activations): accuracy nearly intact
- W8A8 (8-bit weights + 8-bit activations): likewise nearly intact
- INT2, INT3: noticeable degradation, not recommended

This conclusion was adopted by many engineering teams: "Quantize reasoning models
with W4A16; safe to deploy."

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problem It Buried)

This conclusion is correct along the *accuracy* dimension.

But the evaluation never measured how token counts changed. A problem where the
full-precision model reaches the correct answer in 1200 tokens, and the quantized
model reaches the same correct answer in 2400 tokens — these two cases look
identical in Liu et al.'s framework: both are scored as "lossless."

In reality, they differ by 2× in inference cost.

** 2026-06 | Lian et al. [Target]: The Hidden Bill of "Lossless" Quantization — Token Inflation

*** What Problem It Saw

Lian et al. noticed the blind spot in Liu et al.: the cost of a reasoning model is not just
"right or wrong" but also "how many tokens did it take to get there."

They posed a simple question: even if W4A16 quantization does not reduce accuracy,
does the quantized model produce longer reasoning chains?

The answer is: yes, and significantly so.

*** Their Solution

They introduce *CoT Token Inflation Ratio (CoTIR)*: the number of reasoning tokens
generated by the quantized model divided by the number generated by the full-precision
model. CoTIR > 1 means token inflation.

They systematically measured CoTIR for multiple quantization methods (GPTQ, AWQ,
HQQ, ParoQuant, etc.) on reasoning models such as DeepSeek-R1, finding:
- INT4/INT3 quantization significantly increases reasoning token counts while
  maintaining accuracy
- The increase comes from two sources: more intermediate reasoning steps +
  more semantic repetition (the model starts going in circles)
- Actual serving latency: the per-token speedup from quantization is partially or
  fully cancelled by the increase in token count

Mitigation attempts:
- *Prompt intervention* and *decoding sampling adjustments*: unstable effects;
  the accuracy–length tradeoff is difficult to control
- *Quantization-Aware Training (QAT)*: most effective, but at higher cost

*New norm proposed*: quantization papers should report both accuracy *and* reasoning
token counts; reporting only one is incomplete.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Problems Remain)

Token inflation is a real engineering problem: if quantization makes each token 2×
faster but the token count grows by 1.8×, the net saving is only 11% — far below
expectations. In worse cases, inflation exceeds speedup and quantization actually
costs more.

Open questions:
1. What is the root cause of token inflation? Is it that reduced precision makes the
   model "less confident," so it uses more tokens to verify? Or does quantization
   disturb some internal "rhythm" of the model?
2. Can QAT fully eliminate token inflation, or does it pay elsewhere?
3. Is there a method to detect and truncate token inflation in real time during inference?

* Frontier Extensions

The target paper was published in June 2026 and no direct citations have appeared yet.
But two parallel research threads have independently discovered related phenomena and
may soon converge with this work:

** Token Budget Control Thread (Parallel, From Different Motivations)

- *TALE: Token-Budget-Aware LLM Reasoning* (arxiv 2412.18547, ACL Findings 2025):
  Coming from an efficiency angle, discovers that reasoning models generate large
  amounts of redundant tokens; injecting a token budget into the prompt can reduce
  token overhead by 68.9% with less than 5% accuracy loss.

- *SelfBudgeter* (arxiv 2505.11274):
  Uses RL to train models to first estimate how many tokens they will need before
  beginning to reason, then generate strictly within that budget, achieving adaptive
  token control.

- *Reasoning on a Budget Survey* (arxiv 2507.02076):
  Systematically characterizes "overthinking," finding that reasoning chains for
  correct answers are on average shorter than those for incorrect answers —
  longer does not mean more correct; sometimes it is the opposite.

The open question for this thread: can these methods repair the token inflation caused
by quantization? Or does quantization-induced token inflation have a different
mechanism that requires a different fix? This intersection remains unanswered.

* Insight

Behind this evolutionary thread lies a deeper pattern: *evaluation metrics always lag
behind changes in model behavior*.

GPTQ and AWQ measured quantization quality with perplexity, because at the time
models were just "predicting the next word" and perplexity was sufficient.

When reasoning models arrived, researchers switched metrics — using accuracy —
and discovered that quantization harms mathematical reasoning, then found solutions
(QAT, QLoRA).

But reasoning models have a new dimension that neither perplexity nor accuracy can
see: *how much did it cost to arrive at the correct answer?*

Lian et al.'s contribution is not merely "discovering token inflation" — it is
*asking the right question*: efficiency evaluation of quantization must include token
counts, or it is measuring an incomplete projection.

*Where things are most likely to go next*:

1. *Causal analysis*: Why does quantization cause token inflation? The most likely
   hypothesis is "quantization lowers the model's confidence in intermediate steps,
   causing it to use more tokens to verify." Testing this hypothesis requires
   interpretability tools.

2. *Quantization-aware reasoning alignment*: Rather than patching after quantization,
   teach the model during training "how many tokens the quantized version of itself
   should use." This sits at the intersection of QAT and RL alignment — a space
   almost entirely unexplored.

3. *Emergence of new benchmarks*: Eventually there will be a standard quantization
   evaluation suite that jointly reports accuracy, CoTIR, actual latency, and
   throughput. Whoever proposes the version the community adopts first will define the
   discourse of this direction.

* Takeaways

This thread illustrates a pattern for finding research problems: *find the dimension
that is invisible to the projection everyone else is measuring*.

Li et al. found the accuracy projection; Liu et al. aimed that projection at the right
target (reasoning models); Lian et al. added token count as a second dimension, and
suddenly the "lossless quantization" conclusion collapsed.

Each step did not require a more complex experiment — it required asking a more
correct question.

This suggests a systematic method for finding problems:
- Find the evaluation metric that a field universally relies on
- Ask: what is this metric a projection of?
- Ask: is there a dimension that is invisible to this metric but matters enormously
  in real deployment?
- There lies the next paper.
