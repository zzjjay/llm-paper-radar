* The River of the Problem

The most natural way to pour a large model's "knowledge" into a small one is to have the small model imitate the probability distribution the large model assigns to each token. But the word "imitate" conceals a fork in the road — should the student *cover* all of the teacher's possible answers (forward KL), or should it *lock onto* the few answers the teacher favors most (reverse KL)?

For the past three years, the LLM distillation community has almost unanimously believed: reverse KL is better. The reasoning sounds solid — a small model has limited capacity, so rather than spreading effort thinly across the teacher's full range of possibilities (ending up unable to learn any of them well, and scattering probability mass where it doesn't belong), it should concentrate fire on a few high-quality modes.

What this paper punctures is a variable lurking beneath that "consensus" that everyone ignored: *temperature*. Its central claim is that reverse KL only "wins" because every comparison was made with temperature set to 1. Yet temperature is the very soul of Hinton's distillation from ten years ago: it "warms" the teacher's distribution, coaxing the "dark knowledge" hidden in the corners to the surface. Once temperature is raised, the outcome flips — forward KL reliably surpasses reverse KL.

The truly hard part of this river is: *a hyperparameter dismissed as an "irrelevant implementation detail" has been quietly deciding the fundamental question of "who wins" all along.* The entire field drew a potentially untenable conclusion from a competition that was never fair.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Hinton KD: The Founding Insight — Dark Knowledge Hides in Warmed Distributions

*** What Problem It Saw

The pain point of that era was simple: well-trained models (often ensembles) worked well but were too heavy to deploy. Others (Caruana et al.) had already shown compression was feasible; Hinton wanted a compression method more tightly coupled to the softmax.

*** Its Solution

Have the small model learn not the ground-truth "right answer" (hard labels) but the teacher's *full probability distribution* — "soft targets." A photo of a BMW: the teacher knows it's a BMW, but it also dimly senses "the probability of garbage truck is much higher than the probability of carrot." Hinton called this relative relationship among wrong answers *dark knowledge* — it encodes how the model actually generalizes.

The trouble is that at T=1 this dark knowledge is nearly invisible — non-target class probabilities are pinned near zero and offer almost no gradient signal. Hinton therefore introduced *temperature T*: divide logits by T before softmax; the larger T, the flatter the distribution, and those near-zero probabilities are "amplified" into learnable signals. That is the original purpose of temperature.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Pitfall)

It worked because it compressed "everything the teacher knows about the world" into a set of soft labels — far more informative than hard labels for the student. Fundamentally, matching soft targets is equivalent to minimizing *forward KL* (teacher in front).

But it buried two pitfalls. First, the whole process is *static and offline* — the teacher labels a fixed dataset and the student learns from it; nobody ever asked "what if the student's own generated sentences diverge from this dataset?" Second, Hinton himself left an open question: at low temperature, distillation ignores logits that are "very negative" — is that beneficial or does it discard information? He called it "an empirical matter." Ten years later, this paper settles that account.

** 2023 | MiniLLM: Founding the "Reverse KL Is Better" School

*** What Problem It Saw

Transplanting Hinton's approach (forward KL) directly to generative large models broke things. The teacher distribution for a generative model is highly *multi-modal* — any given sentence has countless reasonable continuations. Forward KL is "mode-covering": whenever the teacher assigns nonzero probability somewhere, the student is severely penalized for being near zero there. A capacity-constrained student is thus forced to cover *every* teacher mode, and ends up smearing probability into the "gaps" between modes. During free-generation sampling, these incorrectly elevated gaps become hallucinations and incoherence.

*** Its Solution

Flip the KL: minimize *reverse KL* (student in front). Reverse KL is "mode-seeking" — the student is penalized only where it has probability but the teacher does not, so it intelligently retreats into the teacher's main modes and voluntarily abandons the long tail. In a word: *better to firmly learn a few things well than to half-learn everything.* Because the student distribution appears inside the expectation in reverse KL, direct differentiation is intractable; MiniLLM uses policy gradients (REINFORCE-style) to optimize it.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Pitfall)

It works for small models because capacity is a hard constraint: better to concentrate fire on high-quality behavior at the cost of diversity than to be confidently wrong in the gaps. This single move established the dominant belief for the next three years: "reverse KL > forward KL."

But the pitfalls are deep: policy gradients inherently suffer from *high variance and unstable training*, requiring a battery of patches (teacher-mixed sampling, length normalization, reward baselines) to run at all; and the method must start from a student that has already been fine-tuned, otherwise the garbage sentences it generates produce nothing but noisy feedback. Most critically — *like everyone who followed, it declared reverse KL the winner at T=1.* The water in that "victory" would not be wrung out until this paper.

** 2023/24 | GKD: Making "Which Divergence" a Tunable Dial

*** What Problem It Saw

GKD targeted a crack Hinton never considered: *exposure bias*. During training, the student is fed sentences generated by the teacher (or from a fixed dataset); at inference, it must autoregressively condition on *its own* previously generated tokens. The training distribution and the inference distribution are mismatched; since each token depends on earlier ones, a small early error snowballs.

*** Its Solution

Two moves. First, *on-policy*: have the student learn directly on sentences *it generated itself*, with the teacher scoring token by token — training and inference distributions finally align. Second, and more important for this line: GKD explicitly lifts "forward KL vs. reverse KL vs. JSD" out into a *tunable hyperparameter* and states plainly that "the optimal divergence depends on the task." It even offers an empirical recommendation: for instruction-following, reverse KL is clearly better (mode-seeking "locks onto the main intent").

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Pitfall)

It works because it is the first to acknowledge "the choice of divergence is not self-evident — it is a design decision." This step was already at the doorstep of this paper. If divergence is tunable, what determines which is better under what conditions? GKD attributed the answer to "the task." But it missed an equally tunable and more hidden variable: *temperature*. GKD's online sampling is fixed at T=1. So "divergence is tunable" and "temperature is nailed at 1" coexist, and the new pitfall is — *is a comparison of tunable divergences at a fixed temperature a reliable one?*

** 2024 | DistiLLM: Patching the Instability of Reverse KL

*** What Problem It Saw

DistiLLM hammered on MiniLLM's weak point: reverse KL + policy gradients is *slow and brittle*. Every step requires both models to generate samples, variance is high, and the student must be initialized from a fine-tuned checkpoint. The problem is traced to the gradient of the standard KL — when student probability q approaches zero, the ratio term explodes into enormous noisy updates.

*** Its Solution

*Skew KL*: mix a small amount of the other party's distribution into the KL denominator. For example, skewed forward KL is D_KL( p ‖ α·p + (1−α)·q ) — the denominator can never reach exactly zero, gradients are clamped and no longer explode. Combined with an off-policy replay buffer (reusing previously generated samples rather than regenerating every step), overall training is about 4× faster. Empirically α=0.1 works best, outperforming plain KL, plain reverse KL, and JSD.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Pitfall)

It works because skewing simultaneously softens forward KL's "mode averaging" failure and reverse KL's "mode collapse" failure while preserving each one's strengths. But note: DistiLLM resolves the *engineering stability* of the reverse KL route; its default worldview remains "reverse KL (and variants) is the protagonist." It still *does not touch temperature*. From MiniLLM through GKD to DistiLLM, the entire mainstream line was built on the same untested premise — *T=1*. That premise is the table this paper flips.

** 2026 | This Paper: Bring Temperature Back, and Forward KL Overtakes

*** What Problem It Saw

This paper asks a question everyone skipped: *"At what temperature was the conclusion 'reverse KL is better' reached?"* The answer: almost always T=1. And T=1 is precisely the most unfavorable setting for forward KL — at temperature 1 the teacher distribution is too "peaked," with the target token claiming nearly all the probability, and *non-target token probabilities are squashed near zero*. But forward KL depends on exactly those non-target probabilities (Hinton's dark knowledge) to transmit information. The conclusion is sharp: forward KL's poor performance *is not a fundamental deficiency — it has always been evaluated under a teacher distribution that was never warm enough.* The whole field judged it on a rigged arena and declared it the loser.

*** Its Solution

Bring temperature back into the design space, then redo the comparison. The core finding is that temperature acts *asymmetrically* on the two divergences, captured in a single sentence: *"Temperature changes what forward KL learns, but primarily changes how reverse KL learns."*

- Forward KL's loss is directly *weighted by the teacher's probabilities*. Warming the teacher directly replaces the supervisory target — non-target signals are amplified, and what forward KL learns undergoes a *qualitative change*.
- Reverse KL is weighted by the *student's distribution*, operating on the teacher–student logit difference weighted by the student. Raising temperature simply rescales gradients by 1/T² — what it learns is unchanged; only *how* it learns is adjusted.

Viewing the mechanism through its limiting behavior clarifies things: in the high-temperature limit (Proposition 2.1), both divergences degenerate to "teacher–student logit matching," converging to the same point. Their real difference therefore exists only in the low-temperature regime. As temperature rises, forward KL is fed progressively more useful non-target information, while reverse KL stays put. Result: *at T=1 reverse KL wins; as T rises, forward KL consistently surpasses reverse KL across a range of instruction-following benchmarks.*

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Leaves Open Questions)

It works because it reduces an "empirical law" to a "local phenomenon under a specific temperature." Going further, temperature is effective across a broad class of KL-based objectives — once temperature is added, *a plain KL method can compete with sophisticated state-of-the-art distillation methods.* This raises a pointed question in return: might some of the gains claimed by recent "new methods" actually just reflect the fact that nobody tuned the temperature? That suspicion is left for future work.

* Frontier Extension

** 2025 | DistiLLM-2: No Allegiance — Apply the Right Tool

If this paper proves "forward and reverse KL each have their moment," DistiLLM-2 from the same author group takes the insight to its logical conclusion: *stop using a single global loss.* Its contrastive approach (CALD) is — use skewed forward KL on *teacher-generated outputs* ("pull up" in the directions the teacher favors) and skewed reverse KL on *student-generated outputs* ("push down" where the teacher assigns near-zero probability), doing both simultaneously — much like transplanting the DPO intuition into distillation. It also replaces the manually tuned α in DistiLLM with a curriculum-style α that adapts automatically to difficulty.

In direction, DistiLLM-2 echoes the same deep theme as this paper — *divergence should not be one-size-fits-all* — but takes the path of "assign divergences by data type," while this paper takes the path of "re-evaluate divergences by temperature." Both say: treating "which KL to use" as a binary allegiance is itself a mistake. As of now, on the specific question of "how temperature reshapes FKL vs. RKL," this paper is the latest word on this line.

* Insights

On the surface this line is a technical tug-of-war between forward KL and reverse KL; underneath, what is really happening is a cognitive reversal about *what counts as a fair comparison*.

- *A suppressed hyperparameter can undermine an entire field's conclusions.* Temperature was the soul of Hinton's method, but by the MiniLLM generation it was treated as an "irrelevant detail" and defaulted to 1 — and from that point the entire mainstream (GKD, DistiLLM) tacitly left it untouched. The result: something treated as an implementation detail quietly determined the "common knowledge" written into the introductions of countless papers. The lesson: when a field converges on an iron consensus, the thing most worth revisiting is often the variable that *everyone agreed to omit*.
- *"A is better than B" is almost never unconditional.* From GKD's "depends on the task," to this paper's "depends on temperature," to DistiLLM-2's "depends on data type" — this line progressively dismantles an absolute claim into a conditional one. Real progress is not finding "the better divergence"; it is understanding "which is better under what conditions."
- *Asymmetry is the key.* The most elegant contribution of this paper is that it does not stop at "experimentally, forward KL overtook reverse KL" — it provides the mechanism: temperature changes what forward KL *learns* and how reverse KL *learns*. The same dial acts completely differently on two different objects. Grasping this asymmetry explains *why* the reversal happens, not merely *that* it happens.

Where does the line go next? Most likely toward elevating "temperature" from a global scalar to a learnable *policy* that varies per token, per position, or per difficulty — since temperature can determine which divergence wins, it should itself be treated as part of the distillation objective to be optimized, not a constant set to 1 on a whim. DistiLLM-2's adaptive α is already half a step in this direction.

* Takeaways

For researchers, the most practical lesson from this line is: *before reproducing a "received result," list the hyperparameters that were held fixed in the original comparison, then ask one by one "what happens if I release this?"* Many "settled conclusions" are not wrong — they simply hold only under a particular default setting that was never written into the ablation study because it seemed too obvious.

Concretely, you can:

1. *Challenge a field's consensus* — find the experimental conditions under which it was originally established, then deliberately release one "supposedly irrelevant" knob (temperature, learning rate, initialization, sampling temperature, …) and check whether the conclusion holds. This entire paper is that single move taken to its limit.
2. *Before declaring your method wins, prove the arena is fair.* If you claim "method A beats baseline B," first confirm that B's key hyperparameters are also tuned to their optimum — otherwise you may be winning only because your opponent never tuned, not because your method is superior.
3. *Whenever you read "X generally outperforms Y," develop the reflex: under what conditions?* Habitually translating absolute claims into conditional ones almost always reveals a research gap that nobody has claimed yet.
