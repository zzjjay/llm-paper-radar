* The River of the Problem

Generating an image with a diffusion model requires running hundreds of denoising steps. To squeeze such models onto a phone or edge device, the most direct approach is quantization—compressing 32-bit floating-point weights down to low-precision 4-bit or 8-bit representations. The catch: quantization needs *calibration*, which means feeding a batch of samples through the model so it can estimate the optimal rounding directions.

How should calibration samples be chosen? How should they be used? This river has wound around that single question for five years.

In the beginning, practitioners treated diffusion models like ordinary convolutional networks and reused CNN calibration recipes—all samples weighted equally. Then came the gradual realization that diffusion models have a timestep dimension, and the data distribution at step 1 is nothing like that at step 999. Later still: samples from different timesteps don't merely differ in distribution—they pull the model in different directions, with gradients fighting each other. Every layer deeper you dig, the concept of "calibration samples" becomes more refined.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2020 | AdaRound: Making Rounding Direction a Learnable Variable

*** What Problem It Saw

The most naive PTQ approach is to round every weight to the nearest quantization grid point. That sounds obvious, but the Nagel team discovered a counterintuitive fact: for many weights, *rounding toward the farther grid point actually works better*. The reason is that a single weight's rounding error propagates forward through layers, and the rounding choices of neighboring weights can cancel or amplify each other. Blindly snapping every weight to the nearest value ignores these interactions.

*** Its Solution

Turn each weight's "round up vs. round down" decision into a learnable binary variable. Use a small calibration set to minimize the discrepancy between the quantized model's output and the full-precision model's output. After optimization, each weight selects its own best rounding direction.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

Calibration becomes a small optimization problem—no original training data needed, no retraining, just a few hundred images passed through for a forward sweep. This established the foundational paradigm that "PTQ is a lightweight optimization problem."

But AdaRound optimizes layer by layer, each layer independently. For deep networks, errors accumulate across layers; for architecturally unusual networks like diffusion models, the approach is even more ill-suited.

** 2021 | BRECQ: Finding a Middle Granularity Between Layers and the Full Network

*** What Problem It Saw

Layer-wise optimization (AdaRound) is myopic—optimal per layer does not mean optimal for the whole network. But optimizing the entire network at once overfits the calibration set: with only a few hundred images and very high degrees of freedom, the model simply memorizes them. Neither extreme works.

*** Its Solution

Partition the network into *blocks* (e.g., one residual block of ResNet, one Transformer layer). Within each block, jointly optimize rounding and activation quantization parameters; blocks are processed sequentially. Granularity is locked at the middle ground.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

Block-wise reconstruction became the standard foundation for subsequent PTQ work. But BRECQ's design assumes "the network is static and calibration data is i.i.d."—an image is just an image, feed it in and be done. That assumption falls apart immediately with diffusion models.

** 2023 | Q-Diffusion: Porting BRECQ to Diffusion Models, the Simplest Way Possible

*** What Problem It Saw

A diffusion model is not a static network—the same weights are reused across hundreds of different denoising timesteps. When feeding calibration data, you have to decide which timesteps to sample from. Before Q-Diffusion, no one had handled this systematically.

*** Its Solution

The most straightforward approach: sample *uniformly* across timesteps. For 1000 total steps, sample every 10th step, collecting 100 timesteps' worth of calibration data. Then apply BRECQ's block-wise reconstruction verbatim. A few additional tweaks were made for the shortcut connections specific to diffusion models.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

This was the first work to fully run PTQ on DDPM and LDM, proving that "diffusion models can be 4-bit quantized without collapsing." But "uniform sampling" is a naked shortcut—it assumes every timestep matters equally to the model. That assumption is the target of the next paper.

** 2023 | PTQ4DM: Timesteps Are Not Equally Weighted

*** What Problem It Saw

Q-Diffusion samples uniformly, but the diffusion process is far from uniform—early timesteps (near pure noise) and late timesteps (near real images) have completely different feature and activation distributions. More critically, late denoising steps tend to have a disproportionately large impact on final generation quality. Uniform sampling dilutes the influence of high-value timesteps.

*** Its Solution

Change the timestep sampling distribution to a *Gaussian*—biased toward certain "important" regions (which region depends on the model). One additional adjustment: use samples generated during the denoising process for calibration rather than samples from the forward noising process, since the former better matches the inputs the model sees at actual inference time.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

This was the first work to acknowledge that "timesteps have structure and are not equal." But PTQ4DM's Gaussian is a heuristic—why Gaussian? Why these particular mean and variance values? No principled answer is given. And even if the *selection* of timesteps is improved, the selected samples are still treated with equal weight.

** 2024 | TFMQ-DM: Timestep Embeddings Cannot Be Naively Quantized

*** What Problem It Saw

Diffusion models contain a special module—the timestep embedding—which encodes "which step are we on" and injects that information into the backbone network to modulate features. Q-Diffusion and PTQ4DM quantized this module like any other layer, and the result was striking: quantization error in the timestep embedding strips the model of its sensitivity to "which step is this," causing temporal coherence to collapse.

*** Its Solution

Isolate the time-related modules and apply a more careful quantization strategy to them (retaining higher precision or calibrating them separately). The rest of the backbone still follows the Q-Diffusion approach.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

This was the first recognition that "diffusion models are not homogeneous—certain modules are particularly sensitive to quantization." But the fix operates at the *model-architecture level*; on the calibration-data side, samples are still treated with equal weight.

** 2024 | APQ-DM: A Theoretical Framework for Timestep Selection

*** What Problem It Saw

By this point, "timesteps need differentiated treatment" was consensus, but the specifics of how to select them were still ad hoc—Gaussian here, uniform there, a sprinkle of heuristic weights somewhere else. A principled criterion was missing.

*** Its Solution

Formulate timestep selection as a *structural risk minimization* problem: choose a set of timesteps such that the calibrated model's expected generalization loss across all timesteps is minimized. This yields a computationally tractable objective derived from optimization principles.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

This turned "selecting timesteps" from an engineering judgment call into an optimization problem. But APQ-DM's perspective is still fixed on "which timesteps to pick"—it optimizes the *set* of timesteps while treating samples within that set as equals. What GAC sees is a deeper problem lurking one level below.

** 2026 | GAC: The Real Bottleneck Is Not Which Timesteps to Pick, But That Samples Are Pulling Against Each Other

*** What Problem It Saw

GAC ran a simple experiment: partition the calibration set into subsets corresponding to different timesteps, compute each subset's gradient with respect to the model parameters, then measure pairwise cosine angles between them. The finding: gradients from different timesteps are nearly orthogonal or even anti-aligned for many pairs. This means the "average gradient" computed during calibration is in reality the resultant of several opposing forces—some samples want to push weights left, others push right, and the net force is small and directionless.

This gradient conflict phenomenon also exists in full-precision multi-task training, where the continuous parameter space allows for gradual reconciliation. But quantized weights can only take discrete values (e.g., 0 or 1)—there is no room for a "compromise." Gradient conflict manifests directly as performance collapse on certain timesteps.

No matter how sophisticated the timestep selection strategy (APQ-DM's SRM approach included), if the selected samples' gradients fight each other, some timesteps will always be sacrificed.

*** Its Solution

Assign each *calibration sample* a learnable weight $\omega_i$. Then use *meta-learning* (bi-level optimization) to learn these weights:

- Inner loop: use the weighted samples to update the quantized model (standard MSE reconstruction loss).
- Outer loop: compute the pairwise angles between gradients from different timesteps on a validation set; the objective is to *align* these gradient directions.

In short: let the algorithm learn to down-weight samples whose gradients go against the grain, and up-weight those whose gradients align with the majority direction.

Directly optimizing this objective involves third-order derivatives, which are intractable. GAC derives a surrogate loss and proves that optimizing it implicitly optimizes the original objective (Theorem 4.1), enabling ordinary first-order gradient methods to run the whole procedure.

*** Why It Worked (and What New Pit It Dug)

This elevates calibration samples from "equal-weight tokens" to "weighted, schedulable resources." It is the first work on this river to *directly address gradient conflict*—all prior work adjusted indirectly (swapping timesteps, protecting modules, changing sampling distributions). GAC directly adjusts per-sample weights on the fly.

New pit: bi-level optimization is not cheap; a surrogate objective plus block-wise scheduling is needed to keep costs manageable. And GAC assumes "aligning gradient directions is good"—but could fully aligned gradients discard optimization signals specific to certain timesteps? That is a seam worth pulling on in future work.

* Frontier Extensions

As of 2026-02 (GAC's submission date), no publicly available follow-up work has appeared on the main thread of "calibration-data optimization for diffusion-model PTQ." From first principles, plausible directions include:

- *From gradient alignment to gradient routing*: rather than forcing all timesteps' gradients to agree, explicitly model "which sub-direction each timestep should follow"—akin to a Mixture-of-Experts framing.
- *Joint PTQ and QAT*: GAC is purely PTQ; with a small fine-tuning budget, sample-weight learning and parameter updates could be unified.
- *Extension to video diffusion / 3D diffusion*: the dual dimensionality of timestep + spatial step may make gradient conflict even more severe there.
- *Theoretical characterization of gradient conflict*: GAC is empirical observation plus heuristic remedy; a mathematical account of why discrete parameter spaces amplify gradient conflict remains absent.

* Insights

On the surface this river is about iterating quantization techniques; underneath it is about something more universal—*the descent of epistemology*.

The first phase (AdaRound / BRECQ) views things through "weights and data": calibration means using data to tune weights correctly.
The second phase (Q-Diffusion through APQ-DM) adds "weights and data + timestep structure": data is no longer i.i.d.; it carries a time label, and the time label encodes different stages of the task.
The third phase (GAC) adds "weights, data, timesteps + optimization dynamics": it recognizes that samples from different timesteps are *competing with each other* in parameter space, and that calibration is fundamentally about coordinating this competition.

Each descent makes a previously invisible interaction visible and optimizable—rounding direction → reconstruction granularity → timestep distribution → sample weights. The next descent can be predicted: *making "which timesteps should share one set of weights vs. follow their own path" itself learnable*—moving from "a single quantized model with weighted calibration" toward "a conditioned quantized model."

A second observation: PTQ for diffusion models is so hard precisely because it forces a *multi-task problem* (each timestep is a subtask) onto a single set of *discrete parameters*. Continuous models can compromise; discrete models can only pick a side. GAC's "soft weighting + gradient alignment" is the most elegant compromise currently conceivable, but the fundamental tension—multiple tasks versus a single set of discrete parameters—will keep this river flowing.

* Takeaways

Three lessons for doing research:

1. *Don't just watch the loss curve—look at the gradient structure behind the loss*. The reason GAC exists is not that its authors had a cleverer algorithm; it's that they ran a simple experiment (Figure 1a: compute the pairwise gradient-angle matrix across timesteps) and found a phenomenon nobody had plotted before. Experiments that "look at an old problem from a new angle" often have more leverage than new algorithms.

2. *"Equal weighting" is almost always suboptimal—but you must first demonstrate exactly where it fails*. Every step on this river is dismantling some form of equal weighting: AdaRound dismantles equal-weight rounding; BRECQ dismantles equal-weight blocks; Q-Diffusion through APQ-DM dismantle equal-weight timesteps; GAC dismantles equal-weight samples. Each dismantling is a paper. But before each dismantling, you must quantify what concrete loss equal weighting is causing.

3. *Bi-level optimization is an old tool that opens new territory when it meets a new setting*. Meta-learning and bi-level optimization had their moment in 2017–2018 and then cooled off, but when they encounter "diffusion-model quantization"—a setting whose essence is *choosing the training data for a model once more*—they find a new stage immediately. The revival of a technical tool usually isn't because the tool changed; it's because a new setting's *shape* happens to fit the tool's capability curve.

The practical bottom line: the next time you work on model compression, knowledge distillation, or any task involving "calibrating a large model with a small batch of data," don't start by asking "which algorithm should I use?" Start by asking "are these calibration samples fighting each other—do their gradients with respect to the parameters point in compatible directions?" That single question can locate at least 30% of the performance gap.
