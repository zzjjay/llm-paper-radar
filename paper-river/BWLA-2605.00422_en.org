* The Problem River

Compressing every weight in a large language model down to 1 bit — meaning only +1 or −1 — would theoretically cut storage and memory bandwidth by 16×, making end-to-end inference fly. But there has been an awkward reality: for the past six years this path has only ever quantized weights, leaving activations at FP16.

Why? Because weights and activations have completely opposite "temperaments." Weights are roughly bell-shaped, clustered near zero, well-behaved; activations contain a small cluster of extreme outliers — within a single channel one or two values can be tens of times larger than the rest. Quantizing activations to low bit-width clips or overflows those outliers into the quantization bins, and the model collapses on the spot.

So binarization methods compress weights to 1 bit while keeping activations at FP16, and during inference the weights must still be de-quantized. The advertised "1-bit speedup" is never realized — memory is halved, but throughput barely moves.

The real problem along this research line lives in that contradiction: *how can 1-bit weights and low-bit activations coexist?* BWLA's answer is — stop treating weights and activations as two separate problems, and use a single orthogonal rotation to clean up both distributions at once.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2021 | BinaryBERT: Can Binarization Transfer to Transformers?

BinaryBERT is the origin of this line. Before it, binary neural networks (BNNs) were mostly applied to CNNs; binarization on transformers was essentially a blank slate. BinaryBERT offered an engineering compromise: first train a ternary BERT (weights in {−1, 0, +1}), then use "weight splitting" to decompose each ternary weight into the sum of two binary weights.

This told the community two facts: first, transformer weights are in principle compressible; second, you need something to *twist the single bell into a double bell* — ternary training plus splitting is essentially doing exactly that, just clumsily and only via QAT.

Its obvious limitation: QAT is impractical at LLM scale — hundreds of billions of parameters cannot be trained; and it targets encoders only.

** 2023 | PB-LLM: Not All Weights Are Equally Important

Shortly after LLaMA was released, PB-LLM gave the first serious answer to "how do we binarize LLMs?" Its insight is straightforward: use the Hessian to measure each weight's sensitivity, keep the most salient ~10% at FP16, and compress the other 90% to 1 bit.

Relative to BinaryBERT, this is a two-step retreat: abandon QAT in favor of PTQ; abandon full binarization and acknowledge that "partial high precision" is necessary. The payoff is that LLaMA-7B can produce usable accuracy.

But the cost is steep: every layer, every channel, carries FP16 weights, so the "1-bit model" is far from 1-bit in practice. And the entire paper touches only weights — activations are permanently FP16, and nobody questioned why.

** 2024 | BiLLM: Bell-Shaped Weights Need Segment-Wise Treatment

BiLLM picks up PB-LLM's logic and pushes further: that ~10% salient weights is too many — can we compress further? Simultaneously it addresses the other half: the remaining 90% non-salient weights still carry large error when hard-pushed to ±1, because they are single-bell-shaped, concentrated near zero, and binarization forces a cluster of near-zero values to ±δ.

BiLLM's solution is two-tier processing:
- Salient weights (now a smaller fraction): refined via a sparse residual matrix
- Non-salient weights: the bell is sliced into a "central segment" and "two tail segments," each independently binarized with its own scale

This is the first time anyone *directly acknowledged* that "bell-shaped distributions mismatch binary codebooks." BiLLM's answer is "then break the bell apart" — but the break is mechanical and never actually makes the weights *become* double-bell-shaped.

Activations? Still FP16.

** 2024 | ARB-LLM: Optimization Must Alternate; Scales Must Correct Column Bias

ARB-LLM is a direct iteration on BiLLM, identifying two new issues:
1. BiLLM treats scale factors and binary codes as two independent quantities, but they influence each other — alternating optimization is needed
2. Column-by-column processing causes errors to accumulate along columns, producing "column bias"

The fix is alternating refined binarization (cyclic updates of scales and codewords) plus column-group bitmap (a shared correction bitmap per group of columns). Accuracy advances another step.

But by this point the ceiling of the line is palpable: all effort goes into "how to pack bell-shaped weights into a ±1 codebook," and the root problem is that *bell-shaped weights should not be packed into a ±1 codebook at all*. Activations remain untouched — and any attempt to touch them would render all prior fine-tuning useless.

** 2025 | DBellQuant: Double-Bell Is a Target, but the Mechanism Is Missing

DBellQuant is the first to shift attention toward *changing the weight distribution itself*: rather than forcing a single bell into ±1, first "twist" the weights into a double-bell shape, with two peaks naturally aligned with +1 and −1.

It is also the first in this line to seriously attempt low-bit activations — targeting W1A8 directly.

Unfortunately, DBellQuant's double-bell is only a *target distribution*; the operational tool is learnable per-channel scaling: learn a γ per channel and pull weights toward two peaks. This has two problems:
- Scaling is an element-wise operation; it cannot systematically *restructure* the weight layout — the double-bell effect is unstable
- Activation outlier suppression also relies on learned scaling, but single-variable scaling cannot tame truly extreme outliers

The result: W1A8 holds, but entering W1A6, activation outliers cannot be suppressed and the double-bell is not reliable, so performance collapses (Wikitext2 PPL 38).

DBellQuant named "double-bell" but did not provide the true mechanism for generating it.

** 2026 | BWLA: One Orthogonal Rotation Reorganizes Both Sides

What BWLA does in this line is essentially *find the true mechanism for the "target" that DBellQuant identified* — and that mechanism incidentally solves the activation problem too.

Breaking it into four steps:

Step 1 — Why rotation can create a double bell. If you take a Gaussian random vector and rotate it with an orthogonal matrix, the distribution is unchanged (rotation preserves Gaussianity). But if you rotate an entire weight *matrix* W′ = W R, the marginal distribution of each row (each output channel) *does* change. BWLA's Theorem 1 argues that there exists an orthogonal R such that after rotation, the marginal distribution of each row best approximates a symmetric two-component Gaussian mixture (GMM) — i.e., a double bell.

Step 2 — How to find R. Frame the objective via EM: treat each row's weights as samples from a two-component GMM (means +c_i and −c_i), run E-step to compute posteriors, and use an MM upper bound in the M-step to reduce the update for R to a Procrustes problem with a closed-form solution. To keep R tractable for large matrices, BWLA restricts R to Kronecker form R = R₁ ⊗ R₂, dropping complexity from O(d³) to O(d^{1.5}).

Step 3 — This R also fixes the activations. At inference, X W = (X R)(R^T W) = X′ W′, so X is rotated by the same R. The Hadamard/QuaRot line has already shown that orthogonal rotation spreads activation outliers across all channels, turning heavy tails mild. The R learned by BWLA is not a fixed rotation like Hadamard but one *specifically learned to create a double bell* — yet it still retains the side effect of "flattening activations."

Step 4 (refinement) — PSP. After OKT there remains residual error. BWLA adds a low-rank correction: take the residual "original weight − binarized weight" and apply truncated SVD, retaining the top-r singular values. This contributes very few parameters but further tightens the double-bell fit.

Result: W1A6 PTQ works for the first time — Qwen3-32B perplexity 11.92 (previous SOTA: 38), 70%+ average improvement across five zero-shot tasks, 3.26× end-to-end speedup. This time the "1-bit model" is genuinely 1-bit.

* The Core: What This Line Is Really About

On the surface this is engineering progress in binarization accuracy, from collapse to usability. Underneath are *three perspective flips*:

1. *From "selecting weights" to "constructing weights"* — PB-LLM / BiLLM / ARB-LLM all asked "which weights should stay at FP," while after DBellQuant the question became "can the shape of the weight distribution be changed?" This is a shift from "diagnosing the status quo" to "reengineering the status quo."

2. *From "weights in isolation" to "weights and activations in tandem"* — the first four papers all assumed "compress the weights first; deal with activations when the technology matures." BWLA resolves both with the same rotation matrix R: R is learned for the weights, but activations benefit from exactly the same orthogonal transformation. This is the victory of coupling.

3. *From "target-driven" to "mechanism-driven"* — DBellQuant knew the answer should be "double-bell" but used learnable scaling as the tool. BWLA provides a mathematical path — GMM + EM + Procrustes — explaining *why* rotation produces a double bell. Same target, a root-level mechanism found, and the outcome changes qualitatively.

* Where This Line May Go Next

- *W1A4 or even W1A3*: BWLA demonstrated W1A6; the next push is inevitably toward lower activation bit-widths. The challenge is that OKT's rotation capacity may be insufficient at 4-bit activations, requiring more expressive rotation families (beyond Kronecker structure) or mixed-precision fallbacks.
- *Joint training of R and the model*: BWLA is pure PTQ. If a small-scale QAT phase is permitted, R can be co-optimized with weights to further narrow the double-bell's standard deviation.
- *Special handling for MoE and attention*: BWLA is validated primarily on dense linear layers; whether the same OKT recipe applies to expert routing in MoE architectures and KV-cache in attention remains an open question.
- *Hardware adaptation*: The 3.26× end-to-end speedup is measured on a general-purpose GPU. A dedicated 1-bit weight × 6-bit activation accelerator (NPU/ASIC) could push that figure above 10×.
