* The River of the Problem

When an LLM generates a long piece of text, the thing that ultimately devours GPU memory is usually not the model itself but the "notepad" that the attention mechanism must keep — the KV cache.

What is actually written in that notepad? Dozens of layers, each with dozens of attention heads, each head recording a copy for every token. The question is: are all those heads really doing different things, or are most of them just copying each other's homework?

This river has been flowing for six years — from "we don't actually need this many heads" (2019), to "then prune the surplus heads" (2019), to "just let a group of heads share one record from the very start" (2023), to "cluster similar heads and merge them" (2024), to "use the previous layer to predict the current one" (2025), and finally to this paper — "a handful of heads within the same layer can linearly reconstruct all the others."

Each generation more precisely acknowledges the same fact: the redundancy among attention heads is far greater than we thought.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2019 | Michel et al.: Are Sixteen Heads Really Better Than One?

*** What Problem It Saw

The Transformer burst onto the scene in 2017, powered by multi-head attention — pack a dozen or so heads side by side in the same layer, each attending to different patterns. But Michel et al. ran an experiment so simple it almost doesn't look like research: take a trained model, remove heads one by one, and measure how much performance drops.

The result was embarrassing: pruning most heads had almost no effect on translation quality. Some layers were fine with just a single head. This meant that among the heads a Transformer grows during training, *a large fraction are redundant*.

*** Its Solution

At test time, assign each head an "importance score" (sensitivity of the head's output to the gradient), then prune heads from lowest to highest score and see how far pruning can go.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Problem)

It worked because Transformers tend to be over-parameterized during training — many heads are expressing the same thing in slightly different ways. Remove one, and the rest compensate.

But this left a new problem: *head pruning is blunt*. Among the heads being pruned, perhaps 30% carry unique information and 70% carry redundant information; pruning everything loses that 30%. Could we be more surgical?

** 2019 | Voita et al.: Heads Are Not Equal — Some Are Specialists, Some Are Extras

*** What Problem It Saw

Michel treated heads as binary — keep or prune. Voita disagreed: heads within the same layer show clear functional differentiation. Some heads focus on syntactic positions ("find the subject"), some attend to rare words, some barely do anything — you cannot lump them all together and call them "redundant."

*** Its Solution

Apply L0 regularization during training: give each head a "gate" and let the model learn which gates to close. The result: more than half the gates close, and almost all of them belong to the "extra" heads.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Problem)

It worked because it turned "redundancy" from a global judgment into a head-level judgment. Specialist heads stay; diffuse heads go.

New problem: *all this work is spinning at the abstraction level of "pruning heads."* Once pruned, a head is gone — can we instead find ways to let multiple heads share storage without removing any?

** 2023 | Ainslie et al.: GQA — Start Saving at the Architecture Level

*** What Problem It Saw

All prior work was "train first, prune later," but redundant heads had already consumed compute during training. Even worse at inference time — KV cache grows linearly with the number of heads; more heads means a larger cache, and long sequences simply blow up memory.

Post-hoc head pruning helps a little, but it treats the symptom rather than the cause: the model was already trained that way, each head's function already differentiated, and pruning means performance loss.

*** Its Solution

Constrain the architecture from pre-training onward: let a group of (say, 8) Query heads share a single Key head and Value head. This is Grouped-Query Attention. The extreme version, MQA (Multi-Query Attention), has all Query heads share one K/V pair.

Result: KV cache shrinks by 8×, with almost no performance drop — provided the model is pre-trained this way or up-trained on a small amount of data.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Problem)

It worked because it took the Michel/Voita finding — heads are genuinely redundant — and encoded it into the architecture. The model is forced during pre-training to organize information in a way that supports shared K/V, so it does.

New problem: *GQA uses static grouping* — which heads share is fixed at architecture time and locked after training. But the true situation may be: for some tokens, head A and head B are similar; for other tokens, head A and head C are similar. Static grouping cannot capture this dynamic similarity.

** 2024 | Agarwal et al.: CHAI — Dynamic Clustering at Inference Time

*** What Problem It Saw

GQA hard-codes "which heads are similar" into the architecture. But the CHAI team observed that similarity within a head group is actually *token-dependent*.

Concretely: for token X, the attention maps of heads 1, 3, and 5 are nearly identical; for token Y, it is heads 1, 2, and 7 that match. If you can identify this momentary similarity at inference time, you can save more than GQA.

*** Its Solution

At inference time, cluster the attention maps in each layer, group similar heads together, compute attention only once per group, and reuse the result for the other heads in the group. This saves on attention computation and some output cost — not on KV cache storage (the cache is still there).

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Problem)

It worked because it turned "head similarity" from an architectural constant into a runtime variable.

But it has two deeper limitations. First, *it only looks at similarity at the level of attention maps* — the output pattern that a head produces. This is result-level similarity, not similarity in the head's internal representations. Second, *it requires the trained model to happen to have this clustering structure* — if the model wasn't trained to have it, the gains evaporate.

And there is a more fundamental question: all these methods (Michel, Voita, GQA, CHAI) operate at the granularity of whole heads. Can we go one level finer — is there structure among the *internal vectors* of heads (the K, V, Q vectors that each head computes)?

** 2025 | Shutova et al.: AQUA-KV — Targeting the Vectors Themselves

*** What Problem It Saw

Six years of prior work had been circling at the "head" granularity. But the AQUA-KV team asked a new question: *can the Key vector that a given token produces at layer 5 be predicted from the Key vector it produces at layer 4?*

If so, storing layer 5 requires only the "prediction residual" — the better the prediction, the smaller the residual, and the harder you can quantize it.

*** Its Solution

Learn a compact linear predictor for each layer: use the reconstructed K from the previous layer to predict the current layer's K, and use the previous layer's reconstructed V plus the current layer's K to predict the current layer's V. Then store only the quantized residuals, not the full-precision KV.

This was the first time anyone had captured linear structure at the level of the vectors themselves.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left a New Problem)

It worked because the intermediate representations of deep networks evolve slowly from layer to layer — the next layer is not far from the current one, so linear prediction is effective.

New problems (which are also this paper's starting point):
1. AQUA-KV examines *cross-layer* relationships, not relationships *among heads within the same layer*
2. AQUA-KV *still stores a residual for every head* — it does not truly eliminate storage for any head
3. AQUA-KV uses *all heads from the current and previous layers* as input, so its parameter count is large

If heads within the same layer also have strong linear relationships — strong enough that just 2-5 heads can predict all the others — could we simply *skip storing the other heads' KV entirely*?

** 2026 | Shaikh et al.: Within the Same Layer, Heads Are Linearly Predictable (This Paper)

*** What Problem It Saw

Building on AQUA-KV's findings, this paper asks a more aggressive question: *within the same layer*, can the K vector of head A be predicted as a linear combination of the K vectors of heads B, C, and D?

If it can, the question is no longer merely about "compressing residuals" — you can *skip storing head A's K entirely* and reconstruct it from B, C, and D on demand.

*** Its Solution

For each target head, fit a simple linear regression: select 2–5 reference heads from the same layer, learn a linear map W such that W @ [K_ref1, K_ref2, ...] ≈ K_target.

Experiments across four models (Llama-3.1 8B, Falcon3-10B, OLMo-2-7B, Qwen3-32B) yield:

- On the C4 dataset, 5 reference heads predicting Key gives mean R² ≈ 0.76
- On GSM8K, R² frequently exceeds 0.85
- 75% of head pairs have R² > 0.5

In other words, the K vectors of most heads genuinely live in a low-dimensional subspace and can linearly express one another.

Even more interesting is the *evolutionary analysis*: using OLMo-2 intermediate checkpoints (trained from 1B tokens to ~4T tokens), the authors track how R² evolves. Key findings:
- At random initialization, R² < 0.05 (essentially no linear relationship)
- The longer training proceeds, the higher the within-layer R²
- *Key subspace alignment happens fastest, Query next, and Value stays nearly orthogonal throughout*

This is a learned property, not an architectural built-in. The paper also provides a theoretical lower bound (Theorem 3.1) proving that, at random initialization, MSE is large with high probability.

For practical application: store only the reference heads' KV; reconstruct all other heads on-the-fly; achieve 2× KV cache compression. On Qwen3-32B, the average across five benchmarks drops 5.5 percentage points; on Falcon3-10B, 4.5 percentage points; on Llama-3.1 8B the drop is larger, at 9.9 percentage points.

*Reconstructing Key is far safer than reconstructing Value* — consistent with the training-time observation of subspace alignment speed: the Key subspace becomes highly aligned, so the linear map generalizes across different data; the Value subspace stays orthogonal, preserving more task-specific structure that a single linear map cannot capture.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Left New Problems)

It worked because it pushed the concept of "redundancy" down one more level: not head-level redundancy (Michel/Voita), not head-group redundancy (GQA/CHAI), not cross-layer redundancy (AQUA-KV), but *the internal K vectors of heads within the same layer literally living in a shared low-dimensional subspace*.

New problems:
1. *Let it happen during pre-training*: since this is a learned property, can we explicitly encourage it in the pre-training objective so that R² is higher from the start?
2. *What about Value?*: Value staying orthogonal means it genuinely carries independent information — naive linear prediction is unfriendly to Value and will need a different compression strategy (perhaps sparse coding or higher-order relationships)
3. *Composing with orthogonal methods like MLA*: DeepSeek's MLA compresses KV from a low-rank projection angle; this paper compresses from a cross-head redundancy angle. The two are orthogonal and may compound well

* Frontier Extensions

As of March 2026 (the paper's release), the most active downstream developments on this same thread are fermenting in three directions:

1. *MLA (Multi-head Latent Attention)*: the DeepSeek-V2/V3 line. Projects K/V into a shared low-rank latent from which each head reads. Mathematically isomorphic to the "linear prediction" framing here — both say KV lives in a low-dimensional space — but MLA bakes this in during pre-training, whereas this paper discovers it post-hoc.

2. *Dynamic KV selection / eviction* (H2O, StreamingLLM, SnapKV): instead of compressing along the head axis, compress along the token axis — keep only the KV of important tokens. Orthogonal to this paper and can be stacked: first compress by head (this paper), then compress by token.

3. *Cross-layer sharing* (LCKV, KVSharer): let multiple layers share the same KV cache. This pushes AQUA-KV's cross-layer linear relationship to the extreme — treating it as exact equality rather than a prediction.

Predicted next step: the finding in this paper — "within-layer heads are linearly predictable" — is most likely to be absorbed into the *pre-training phase*. Concretely, adding a regularization term to the loss that "encourages K subspace alignment," so that the model grows into an easily compressible shape from the start. This is the canonical path from "structure discovered post-hoc" to "structure designed in from the beginning" — GQA followed exactly this path, emerging from Michel and Voita's post-hoc pruning findings.

* Insights

After following this river to its end, I see two things:

*First: each generation refines the concept of "redundancy" by one more level.* From "heads are redundant" (prune heads), to "head groups are redundant" (share K/V), to "within-head vectors are redundant" (linear prediction). Each level finer reveals more savings, because the structure you are seeing is more precise. This is a classic cognitive refinement process — at first you can only say "these things are duplicated"; gradually you can articulate "at what level they duplicate, and in what manner."

*Second: every "structure discovered post-hoc" gets absorbed into "architecture designed in advance."* Michel discovers head redundancy → GQA writes it into the architecture. Shutova discovers cross-layer linearity → people are already trying to encode it in pre-training regularization. The within-layer linear predictability discovered in this paper can almost certainly be predicted to be absorbed into some new architecture within one to two years.

Going deeper: this river is essentially solving the inverse of a *compression theorem*. Shannon tells us information has an entropy lower bound, yet real systems always far exceed it. Each generation is estimating "what the true information entropy of Transformer attention actually is." From Michel ("50% of heads are noise") to this paper ("75% of heads can be linearly expressed by 5 heads"), the estimated lower bound keeps falling. The endpoint may be the discovery that a 32B model's KV cache needs to store only 1/10 of its current information — the rest is entirely determined by functional relationships.

* Takeaways

On how to do research:

1. *Target the granularity the prior work missed*: every breakthrough in this river came not from finding a better algorithm at the existing granularity but from *switching granularity*. Michel looks at heads, Voita looks at functional types of heads, GQA looks at head-group architecture, AQUA-KV looks at within-head vectors across layers, this paper looks at within-head vectors within the same layer. Switching granularity is worth ten times more than switching algorithms.

2. *Track evolution, not just the final state*: the most elegant section of this paper traces how R² changes with training step using OLMo-2 intermediate checkpoints. This transforms a static finding ("trained models have linear relationships") into a dynamic mechanism ("K subspaces gradually align during training"). Any static conclusion can be interrogated with "how did it grow?"

3. *Package "post-hoc discoveries" as "pre-hoc design"*: if you discover that a trained model has a particular structure, you have an opportunity to invent a training objective that encourages that structure — GQA, MoE, and sparse attention all emerged this way. This paper's finding will almost inevitably become some KV-friendly pre-training loss in the next step.

On the practical side:

- If you are optimizing long-context inference, *Key is safer to compress than Value* is a conclusion you can apply directly — given the same budget, prioritize saving Key
- Different models tolerate head compression very differently (Qwen3 is highly tolerant, Llama-3.1 is less so) — this is a new dimension to consider when choosing a model
- If you are building KV cache systems (vLLM, SGLang, etc.), this opens a new compression axis — orthogonal to quantization and token eviction, and can be stacked on top of both
