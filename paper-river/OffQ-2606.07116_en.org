* The River of the Problem

A naive question: large language models routinely carry tens of billions of parameters, each stored as a 16-bit float. Could we compress them down to 4-bit integers? That would cut memory by four times, speed up computation substantially, and finally let phones and edge devices run big models.

It sounds like merely "chopping numbers shorter," yet this road has been walked for four years without a clean solution. The sticking point is one word: *outliers*.

During inference, the model produces a large volume of intermediate "activation values." The vast majority sit quietly in a modest range, say −2 to 2. But certain channels spike to tens or even hundreds. When you compress to 4 bits you have only 16 tick marks. To fit the value that shot up to 100, your ruler must be stretched very coarse—and as a result, the 99 % of normal values are squeezed into the first two or three ticks, all flattened, all distorted.

That is the contradiction: *to accommodate a tiny minority of monsters, the vast majority of ordinary values are sacrificed*. Generation after generation of researchers has wrestled with these monsters—single them out for special treatment? Somehow flatten them? Change the coordinate system so they vanish entirely? This river is the evolutionary history of "how to deal with a handful of monsters."

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2022 | LLM.int8(): The First to Name the Monster

*** What problem it saw

Before it, people assumed quantization was merely an engineering detail: shorten numbers a bit, lose a bit of precision, no big deal. But once models grew beyond 6.7 billion parameters, Dettmers discovered something strange — direct quantization caused models to *completely collapse*, not just drop a few points but produce incoherent gibberish.

Tracing the culprit, he found that in certain fixed channels, activation values would suddenly spike to tens or hundreds of times the normal magnitude. These "outliers," though numerically few, dictated the full range of the quantization ruler.

*** Its solution

Since the monsters are a minority, *lock them up separately*. LLM.int8() identifies the channels containing outliers and computes them in high-precision FP16; the remaining ordinary channels use low-precision INT8. The results are merged afterward. This is called mixed-precision decomposition.

*** Why it works (and where a new pit is dug)

It works because it treats the symptom at its source: precision loss comes mainly from outliers, so spend the high-precision budget only there. Precision is preserved.

But it digs a large pit: *it is not fast*. GPUs love uniform, homogeneous operations. Now you ask them to alternate between INT8 and FP16, constantly selecting channels, moving data, and converting formats — all that fragmented overhead erases the time saved. In practice, LLM.int8() often runs slower than plain FP16. Memory is saved; time is not. The next person's job is to fix the "slow."

** 2022 | SmoothQuant: Don't Lock Up the Monster — Redistribute Its Strength to Its Neighbors

*** What problem it saw

SmoothQuant zeroed in on LLM.int8()'s pain point: mixed precision prevents hardware from running fast. It asked a smarter question — can we skip mixed precision entirely, use uniform INT8 throughout, and still not be ruined by outliers?

*** Its solution

Key insight: *activations are hard to quantize, but weights are easy*. Weight distributions are naturally flat and well-behaved. So why not transfer the "difficulty" from activations to weights?

The math is elegant. In the matrix product Y = X·W, divide activations X by a scaling factor s and multiply weights W by s. Division and multiplication cancel out; the result is identical. But the spiking channel in activations is "pressed down," at the cost of making the corresponding weights slightly steeper — and weights can tolerate that. This is an *equivalent transform*: model output is unchanged, but the distribution becomes friendlier.

*** Why it works (and where a new pit is dug)

It works because it moves the burden to the side that can bear it, and the scaling factor can be precomputed offline and folded into weights, adding zero overhead at inference time. Pure INT8 throughout — hardware can finally run fast, with a measured 1.5× speedup.

But the pit is this: scaling only *shrinks* the monster; it does not make it *disappear*. At W8A8 (8-bit) that is sufficient, but the moment you push to W4A4 (4-bit), with only 16 grid points, the shrunk monster is still a monster, still stretching the range. Scaling hits its ceiling. For 4 bits, a more aggressive weapon is needed.

** 2024 | SliceGPT: An Accidental Theoretical Stepping Stone

*** What problem it saw

SliceGPT was not working on quantization — it was doing pruning. But it proved a theorem that the entire downstream river would rely on: *Computation Invariance*.

*** Its solution

The theorem in brief: in architectures that use RMSNorm, you can multiply activations by *any orthogonal matrix* (rotation or reflection) and, as long as you multiply the adjacent weights by its inverse, the final model output is *completely unchanged*. In other words, you can freely change the coordinate system of activations at zero cost.

*** Why it works (and where a new pit is dug)

This handed later researchers a master key: since changing the coordinate system is free, find one that makes outliers disappear. SliceGPT itself did not fully exploit this for quantization, but its author (Ashkboos) immediately used that key to unlock quantization — and that is QuaRot.

** 2024 | QuaRot: Use Rotation to Make the Monster Vanish Entirely

*** What problem it saw

QuaRot saw through SmoothQuant's limitation: scaling can only compress outliers along the original coordinate axes — treating symptoms, not causes. What it wanted was for outliers to *cease to exist*.

*** Its solution

Imagine a 2-D vector (1, 100) where the second dimension is an outlier. Now rotate the coordinate system by 45°; the new coordinates of the same point become approximately (71, 70) — the two components are roughly equal, and the outlier *has been smeared away by rotation*!

QuaRot brings this to high dimensions: a random Hadamard matrix (a special orthogonal matrix composed of +1/−1 entries) rotates the activations. Leveraging SliceGPT's computation invariance, this rotation matrix is folded into the weights with near-zero inference overhead. The rotation spreads each outlier's "energy" evenly across all dimensions; the activation distribution suddenly becomes as smooth as a Gaussian. Thus *all* matrix multiplications can proceed at 4 bits, with no channel needing a high-precision exemption.

*** Why it works (and where a new pit is dug)

It works because it changed the paradigm: instead of "dealing with" monsters, change the coordinate system so they are no longer monsters. This is a conceptual leap from "special handling" to "geometric transformation," and W4A4 becomes viable for the first time.

The pit is the word "random." The Hadamard rotation is fixed in advance, chosen randomly. It was later found that *switching the random seed can shift results by 13 points*. That is, better rotations exist; QuaRot just happens to stumble onto a decent one. Leaving one's fate to chance is inherently unreliable.

** 2024 | SpinQuant: If Rotations Vary in Quality, Learn the Best One

*** What problem it saw

SpinQuant grabbed QuaRot's weak point directly: random rotation has too much variance, and quality is pure luck. Since different rotations differ so much, an "optimal rotation" must exist — so why not *find it*?

*** Its solution

Transform the rotation matrix from "a pre-fixed constant" into "a learnable parameter." But rotation matrices must remain orthogonal (otherwise computation invariance breaks), so ordinary gradient descent is off the table; optimization must proceed on the special space of orthogonal matrices (the Stiefel manifold) using Cayley SGD. The objective is quantization error — the rotation is learned directly toward "minimum post-quantization loss."

*** Why it works (and where a new pit is dug)

It works because it replaces luck with targeted optimization. The learned rotation not only achieves better average performance but also dramatically reduces variance, giving stable results. On LLaMA-2 7B, the W4A4KV4 gap relative to full precision narrows to just 2.9 points.

But the pit SpinQuant leaves is precisely OffQ's starting point: *no matter how well the rotation is optimized, it only "spreads" the activation distribution; the overall activation variance remains elevated*. In other words, rotation can scatter monsters but cannot *remove their energy from the arena* — the energy is still in the activations, just redistributed. W4A4KV4 therefore remains unreliable. And learning the rotation requires backpropagation, which is neither cheap in compute nor in memory.

** 2026 | OffQ: Rather Than Spreading the Monster, Move Its Energy Out of the Arena

*** What problem it saw

OffQ delivers a pointed verdict on the entire rotation lineage: from QuaRot to SpinQuant, everyone competed over whose rotation was better, yet *no matter how good the rotation, residual activation variance remains too high and W4A4KV4 remains unreliable*. The flaw is in the paradigm itself — rotation redistributes energy within the arena; the energy never leaves the activations.

It also noticed a structure that predecessors had overlooked: according to recent work on "singular defects," these outliers are not random noise but carry strong *low-dimensional structure* — high-norm tokens, across different layers, positions, and token IDs, almost always point in *the same direction*. In other words, the monsters are lined up, all facing one way.

*** Its solution

Since the monsters all face one direction, use a tailored coordinate system to pack them all into *a single channel*, then move that channel out of the arena. Three steps:

1. *Top-1 PCA finds the direction.* Ordinary PCA is swamped by the multitude of normal tokens and misses the outlier direction. OffQ's trick: for each sequence, use only the *one* token with the largest L∞ norm to run PCA. With normal tokens excluded, the principal eigenvector aligns precisely with the outlier direction.

2. *Rotate to concentrate monsters into one channel.* After rotating along this principal direction, all outlier energy is funneled into the first channel of the rotated space; the remaining channels immediately become clean and easy to quantize.

3. *Offset: convert the monster into an additive constant and absorb it.* This is the most elegant step. A specially constructed Hadamard matrix (first row all ones) is applied to the outlier channel; it *converts the large value uniformly into a constant offset* added to every channel. Asymmetric quantization already has a "zero-point" parameter — this constant offset is *entirely absorbed by the zero-point*, effectively for free. The monster's energy is gone; the remaining activation variance shrinks dramatically; 4-bit quantization proceeds with ease.

OffQ names this fifth class of equivalent transformation *outlier2offset*, placing it alongside prior art: rotation, scaling, and mixed precision.

*** Why it works (and where new pits remain)

It works because it achieves a conceptual upgrade: predecessors all *redistributed* outlier energy (locked it up, compressed it, scattered it, spread it); OffQ *evicts* the energy from the activations and *stashes it in the zero-point* — which exists for free anyway, so the monster is cleared at no cost. And the entire pipeline requires no mixed precision, no non-uniform grid, no backpropagation to learn rotations — pure uniform grid, uniform precision, maximally deployment-friendly.

In terms of results: for Llama 3-8B at W4A4KV4, perplexity drops from SpinQuant's 7.4 to 6.98 (full precision is 6.1), and 0-shot accuracy improves from 63.76 to 65.49. On small-scale Qwen models where QuaRot fails outright, OffQ holds up.

New pits (acknowledged by the authors as future work): the current method uses only the simplest Hadamard for the offset step, without learning a better constrained rotation to further flatten the non-outlier channels; the offset mechanism needs to be extended for new hardware formats like MXFP4/NVFP4; and applicability to vision Transformers remains unverified.

* Frontier Extensions

After OffQ (and in parallel with it), the river branches into several streams:

- *Mixed-precision resurgence*: Methods such as ResQ combine rotation with "keeping a few channels at higher precision." OffQ lists ResQ as a primary baseline and surpasses it on most models. OffQ's selling point is precisely that it needs *no* mixed precision to match or beat these methods.

- *Fancier rotations and transforms*: DartQuant (rotation optimized via Whip loss), DFRot (rotation learned with weights), KurTail (rotation guided by kurtosis), OSTQuant (rotation + scaling), and others continue pushing the SpinQuant line of "learn an ever-better rotation." OffQ characterizes these as "paying the price of extra parameters, compute, and custom kernels," and notes that outlier2offset can be *stacked on top of* them.

- *A larger controversy is approaching* (speculative for the field, not a claim in the OffQ paper): recent work suggests that almost all rotation-based methods clash badly with new formats like MXFP4, which use power-of-two block scaling — global rotation spreads outlier energy in a way that directly conflicts with the within-block scaling assumption. This could undermine the hardware premise of the entire "rotation-first" line from QuaRot to OffQ. OffQ explicitly lists MXFP4 adaptation as future work, which puts it squarely in front of this oncoming wave.

As of now, OffQ represents the latest conceptual leap in the line of "curing outliers through equivalent transforms": from redistributing energy to evicting it from the system entirely.

* Insights

Reading this river from source to sea, one finds that the technical vocabulary (mixed precision, scaling, rotation, PCA, offset) is merely surface; what truly changes underneath is *the conceptual stance toward the outlier as an object*:

1. *From "handle it" to "dissolve it" to "evict it."* LLM.int8() treated outliers as troublemakers needing *special care* (lock them up); SmoothQuant treated them as a burden that could be *transferred* (handed to the weights); QuaRot/SpinQuant treated them as illusions that could be *geometrically erased* (scattered by rotation); OffQ treats them as energy that can be *expelled from the system* (converted to an offset, stuffed into the zero-point). Each generation asked "what exactly is an outlier?" and arrived at an answer that took it less and less seriously.

2. *The free lunch keeps getting eaten more cleverly.* Computation invariance provides a degree of freedom — "swap coordinate systems without changing output" — that costs essentially nothing. All innovation in the latter half of this river is, in essence, finding better transforms within that free space. OffQ goes further still: the zero-point parameter in asymmetric quantization has existed for years, idly sitting there; OffQ is the first to use it as a rubbish bin for outliers, effectively freeloading on another degree of freedom. *The progress of experts often lies in discovering something free in the system that nobody was using.*

3. *Structure is something to exploit, not to fight.* Early methods brute-forced outliers as random noise; OffQ does the opposite — it first acknowledges that "outliers have low-dimensional structure and all point the same way," then strikes precisely along that structure (top-1 PCA). The shift from "fighting disorder" to "riding order" is the deepest turning point in this entire lineage.

Where next? Two directions are practically written on the wall: first, *stack* outlier2offset on top of learned rotations to compound the gains of both generations; second, the hardware confrontation with MXFP4/NVFP4 — whoever can resolve the conflict between "global rotation" and "within-block scaling" holds the ticket to the next chapter.

* Takeaways

The lessons this river offers for research practice are concrete:

1. *Find the next problem by staring at what the previous solution left unresolved.* Every breakthrough here came not from inventing a new idea from scratch, but from fixating on the one thing the prior generation *did not fully fix* — LLM.int8() left "slow"; SmoothQuant left "only works at 8-bit"; the rotation school left "variance still too high." To find a research problem, read the limitations section of the strongest baseline: the title of the next paper is written there.

2. *Use continuous deformation to understand differences.* Every method in this river can be continuously morphed from the previous one: scaling is a degenerate rotation restricted to the axes; rotation generalizes scaling to arbitrary directions; offset is a rotation followed by a transport step. *When you can knead method A into method B step by step, you truly understand the difference* — that is ten times more useful than memorizing "what are the differences between A and B."

3. *Systems routinely harbor unused degrees of freedom.* The zero-point in asymmetric quantization has existed for years; OffQ is the first to think of using it as a receptacle for outliers. The lesson: when you inherit a mature system, don't only look for new modules to add — first audit the *existing but idle resources*. Free degrees of freedom are often exactly where the breakthrough hides.
