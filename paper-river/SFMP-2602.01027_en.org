* Target Paper

*SFMP: Fine-Grained, Hardware-Friendly and Search-Free Mixed-Precision
Quantization for Large Language Models* (Nie et al., 2026)

In one sentence: turn "how many bits per layer" from a discrete search into a
continuously tunable knob, then use intra-block reordering + a unified GEMM
kernel to capture both flexibility and hardware efficiency at once.

* First Anchor: Discovering "Weight Inequality"

The story traces back to LeCun's 1992 *Optimal Brain Surgeon* (OBS): when
pruning, which weights can be discarded and which must be kept can be computed
using the Hessian matrix. This is the origin of the idea that "weights differ
in significance." The seed it planted is: *not all numbers in a neural network
are equally important*.

Thirty years later, in 2022, that seed bloomed in large language models.
Dettmers' *LLM.int8()* found, during 8-bit quantization, that large-model
activations contain a small number of massive outliers — and these outliers are
precisely what sustains the model's capability. Quantize them along with
everything else and accuracy collapses. His solution was straightforward: pull
the outliers out and keep them in FP16, let the rest run in int8.

This told everyone: *to compress a large model to low bit-widths, you must
confront the reality of "a critical minority of values."* But LLM.int8() only
targets 8 bits, and its handling of outliers is a workaround rather than a
systematic treatment.

* Second Step: GPTQ Pushes PTQ to 4 Bits

Right after, Frantar 2022 proposed *GPTQ*. The challenge: to achieve near
lossless compression at 4 bits or below, each weight matrix must be optimized
individually — a second-order problem that naive methods cannot handle for
175B-parameter models.

GPTQ revived the mathematics of OBS: using a Hessian approximation, it
*quantizes weights column by column, and after each column is quantized it
compensates the error into the remaining unquantized columns*. This
"quantize-then-compensate" loop compresses a 175B OPT model to 3–4 bits in a
few hours with only a tiny accuracy drop.

GPTQ was the first "practical" paper on this line. But it carries an inherent
flaw: *all weights are treated equally and given the same bit-width*. That
amounts to assuming every weight matters equally — a direct contradiction of
what OBS and LLM.int8() established.

* Third Step: AWQ Gives High Bits to Salient Weights

Lin 2023's *AWQ* targeted exactly that contradiction: if roughly 1% of weights
are "especially important" (identifiable via activation magnitudes), why
allocate bits uniformly?

But AWQ does not actually "mix precisions" — keeping high-precision outliers
is cumbersome and hardware-unfriendly. Instead it uses a clever compromise:
*scale the important weights up before uniform quantization*. The scaling is
equivalent to giving them effectively higher precision without breaking the
clean integer format.

AWQ works well at 4 bits, but it exposes the next problem: when the total
budget is pushed to extremes like 2 bits or 2.5 bits, the scaling trick can no
longer save accuracy. The paper's own numbers show LLaMA3.1-8B perplexity
exploding to ~1.86e6 at BPW=2.25. *The ceiling of uniform bit-width has been
reached.*

* Fourth Step: SliM-LLM Introduces Genuine Mixed Precision

Huang 2024's *SliM-LLM* drops the pretense: if weight importance is uneven,
then *actually assign different bit-widths to different parts*. Its key
observation: salient weights are not randomly scattered — they *cluster along
the channel dimension*.

So SliM-LLM computes salience per group (a small segment within a row),
assigns 4 bits to groups that are collectively important and 2 bits to the
rest, averaging to around 3 bits overall. It calls this "Salience-Determined
Bit Allocation."

This step genuinely opens the door to mixed precision. But it exposes two new
problems:
- *Throughput collapse*: unequal bit-widths within groups force the GPU to
  handle each width separately during GEMM; SFMP reports that SliM-LLM's
  throughput is 30–50% lower than GPTQ's at the same average bit-width.
- *Per-matrix average bit-width is fixed*: you cannot let an overall-important
  layer receive a higher average; the entire model must conform to one global
  average.

SliM-LLM made mixed precision usable, but *it turned "how to allocate
precision" into a combinatorial optimization problem*.

* Fifth Step: AMQ Uses AutoML to Tame the Combinatorial Explosion

Lee 2025's *AMQ* externalizes the problem: since choosing how many bits each
layer gets (2, 3, or 4) is a search problem, just search — but the search space
is on the order of 3^560 for LLaMA3.1-70B, so brute force is out.

AMQ's four tricks:
1. *Prune the space*: use a small calibration set to measure sensitivity; lock
   clearly sensitive layers at 4 bits immediately.
2. *Use a proxy*: during search, use HQQ (an activation-free quantization
   method) as a cheap stand-in, then convert to GPTQ/AWQ at the end.
3. *Quality predictor*: avoid running full evaluation on every candidate.
4. *NSGA-II genetic algorithm*: multi-objective search.

The results are solid — LLaMA-2-7B finishes in 5 hours, and 70B models are
feasible. But SFMP's counterexample is: *AMQ still requires 44 hours to search
LLaMA3.1-70B*. Moreover, this is layer-granularity mixed precision, coarser
than SliM-LLM's group granularity, leaving less room for accuracy gain.

The deeper problem: *bit-widths can only be integers*. If you want an average
of 3.7 bits, you must make some layers 4-bit and the rest 3-bit — but you
cannot say "this layer is 3.7-bit." This hard-locks the search space in the
discrete domain and keeps it NP-hard forever.

* Sixth Step: SFMP Steps Outside the Discrete Domain

By this point the accumulated pain along the entire line is clear:

- *Salient weights must be treated differently* (shared consensus across
  LLM.int8 / AWQ / SliM-LLM)
- *Group granularity is more accurate than layer granularity but is
  hardware-unfriendly* (lesson from SliM-LLM)
- *Search is NP-hard; no matter how clever, it is slow* (lesson from AMQ)
- *Uniform bit-width collapses at 2 bits* (lesson from AWQ)

Nie 2026's *SFMP* answers by reframing the problem:

** Replace Integer Bit-Widths with "Fractional Bits"

This is the core move. A block is no longer "all 3-bit" or "all 4-bit" but
"3.7-bit" — *implemented by having some weights in the block at 3 bits and
others at 4 bits, with the ratio continuously adjustable*.

Effect: *discrete search becomes continuous optimization*. The per-layer
average bit-width can now be set by gradient descent or simple allocation —
no NSGA-II needed. AMQ's 44-hour search simply disappears.

** Intra-Block Reordering to Cluster Salient Weights

SliM-LLM assumes salient weights cluster along rows/columns, but SFMP shows
that within a row or column they are actually *scattered*. Cutting naively into
blocks mixes salient and non-salient weights together, making fractional-bit
allocation within the block imprecise.

SFMP performs a row-column reordering that consolidates weights by importance
within each block. This is done only on the weight side; activations need only
one lightweight reordering, so throughput loss is minimal.

** Unified GEMM Kernel

The final barrier: mixed-precision implementations have historically been slow
because different block bit-widths require the GPU kernel to branch across
paths. SFMP implements a "unified GEMM kernel for arbitrary average bit-width"
that fetches 3-bit and 4-bit weights within the same block in a single memory
access and computes them jointly.

** Closing the Loop

SFMP's essence is not inventing new "quantization ideas" — salience, mixed
precision, and automatic allocation all appeared earlier. What it does is
*change the problem domain*: from "searching in a discrete bit-width space" to
"adjusting in a continuous ratio space." Once the domain changes, "search" as
an operation becomes unnecessary (hence "search-free" in the paper's title),
and hardware-friendly integer arithmetic is preserved.

* Postscript: ScaleBITS

Shortly after SFMP was posted, *ScaleBITS* (arXiv 2602.17698) appeared,
focusing on "scalable bit-width search." It stays within the search paradigm,
effectively continuing AMQ's line and acknowledging that search still has room
to improve. This suggests that SFMP's "continuization," however elegant, has
not been universally adopted — fractional bits require new kernel support,
while the search route is plug-and-play. The two paths are evolving in
parallel.

* Insights

** What Is Really Changing Along This Line

On the surface it is "bit-widths being carved ever more finely" — layer, group,
block, and finally fractional bits. But the deeper shift is in *the mathematical
character of the quantization problem*:

- Early era (OBS–GPTQ): quantization as a *reconstruction problem* — given a
  bit-width, minimize output error.
- Middle era (AWQ–SliM-LLM): quantization as an *allocation problem* — given a
  budget, assign bits to the weights that need them most.
- Present era (AMQ–SFMP): quantization as an *optimization problem* — the
  allocation itself must be automated.

And SFMP takes the furthest step: *turning combinatorial optimization into
continuous optimization*. This is a leap from "engineering heuristics" to an
"analytically tractable problem."

This echoes an old pattern in deep learning history: whenever a problem is
stuck in the discrete domain, the eventual breakthrough almost always comes from
someone finding the right relaxation to move it into the continuous domain (the
neural network itself is, after all, a continuous relaxation of a discrete
decision machine).

** Where the Next Steps Might Go

- *End-to-end differentiable bit-widths*: SFMP's fractional bits are still
  post-training (PTQ). Injecting fractional bits into the training loop so that
  bit-widths and weights are adjusted together by gradients could be the next
  step for QAT.
- *Fractional bits for activations too*: everything so far targets weights only.
  Mixed precision for activations is harder (dynamic range changes in real time)
  but the potential gain is larger.
- *Better characterizations of salience*: from Hessians to activation
  magnitudes to continuous importance under fractional bits, the next step may
  be task-conditional salience — a weight that matters for reasoning tasks but
  not for generation tasks.
