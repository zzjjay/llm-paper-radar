* The Problem River

One sentence: *How do you fit a massive language model into exactly the GPU you have on hand — not wasting a single byte?*

This sounds like a packing problem. In practice it has been the hard bone that a community of researchers has been cracking their heads against for the past five years.

A large model is, at its core, a huge collection of numbers (weights). In their raw form each number occupies 16 bits. A 70 B model is 140 billion numbers — roughly 140 GB. Your GPU has 24 GB. What do you do? You compress each number from 16 bits down to fewer bits. That is "quantization."

Where is the difficulty? Not in whether compression is possible, but in the fact that "how many bits" is itself a locked-in choice.

Virtually every quantization method in the field produces only *integer bit-widths*: 2 bits, 3 bits, 4 bits. Yet the reality is that Llama-3-70B at 3 bits takes 26 GB — too much; at 2 bits it takes only 17 GB and fits, but the model's reasoning ability collapses catastrophically — it becomes incoherent.

The sweet spot (say, 2.4 bits) would fill a 24 GB GPU perfectly while preserving capability, but *no method can quantize to 2.4 bits*. Integer bit-widths are like a clothing rack with only S, M, and L — you happen to be between M and L, and neither fits.

This is what LiftQuant calls the *deployment gap*: there is always a sliver of mismatch between a model's footprint and the hardware's capacity, and you have no tool to close that gap precisely.

Why is this problem so stubborn? Because "bit-width must be an integer" is not an arbitrary rule — it *grows naturally from the bottom of the quantization mechanism itself*. To store a number you use either 2 bits or 3 bits; you cannot cut a bit in half. To achieve "2.4 bits" you have to rethink, from the ground up, what it means to store a number at all. This river is the record of successive generations doing exactly that.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

We walk through this chain from the oldest paper forward. For each work the focus is not "what it is" but "what flaw it spotted in its predecessor and what it did about it."

** 2022 | GPTQ: Round one weight at a time — but round it cleverly

*** What problem did it see?

Before GPTQ, the most naive way to compress weights was plain rounding: each number is independently snapped to its nearest grid point. The problem is that some weights have an enormous influence on the model's output while others are nearly irrelevant. Rounding everything with the same blunt instrument inflicts fatal rounding errors on the critical weights, and model quality falls sharply.

*** Its solution

GPTQ's core intuition: *rounding is not an isolated event — it can be compensated.*

Imagine filling in a row of numbers one by one. Each time you round a number you incur a small error. GPTQ's move is to immediately "redistribute" that error across all the weights not yet processed, nudging them to absorb the damage. It uses a Hessian matrix (which measures how important each weight is) to decide the most efficient way to spread the compensation.

By continually diffusing and cancelling individual errors as it goes, overall quality is preserved.

*** Why it works — and what new pit it digs

It works because it acknowledges that weights influence one another and exploits that with a compensation mechanism.

But it digs two pits. First, it is fundamentally *scalar quantization* — storing numbers one at a time, never capturing the structure of a group of weights as a whole. Second, below 2 bits there are so few grid points that no compensation strategy can save the model — it collapses outright. The next paper attacks this by doing something before quantization even begins.

** 2023 | QuIP: Smooth the weights before you quantize

*** What problem did it see?

QuIP spots GPTQ's weak spot: the weight distribution is "messy" — a small number of extreme outliers stand far above the rest. These outliers force the quantization ruler to be stretched very wide, leaving the vast majority of ordinary weights squeezed into a tiny segment in the middle, wasting precision. The collapse below 2 bits is largely the outliers' fault.

*** Its solution

QuIP's key technique is *incoherence processing*.

Analogy: the raw weight distribution looks like a jagged mountain range with a few sharp, anomalous peaks (outliers). Before quantizing, QuIP applies a special rotation matrix (a Hadamard transform) to "shake everything uniform" — after rotation the energy spreads evenly across all directions, the peaks are flattened, and the whole distribution resembles a well-behaved Gaussian bell curve. A well-behaved distribution is far more efficient to quantize with a fixed ruler. After quantization the rotation is undone.

*** Why it works — and what new pit it digs

It works because it converts a hard-to-quantize irregular distribution into an easy-to-quantize regular one. This preprocessing step is so effective that every subsequent method inherits it.

New pit: after smoothing, QuIP still stores numbers *one scalar at a time*. But now that the distribution is regular, storing a whole group of numbers at once and exploiting the geometric relationships among them could squeeze out far more information. This insight directly spawns the next line — vector quantization.

** 2024 | QuIP# / AQLM / VPTQ: Store a whole group at once using a codebook

*** What problem did they see?

All three papers (contemporaneous, closely related in spirit) see the same thing: *scalar quantization is leaving information on the table.*

The intuition: if you need to store two numbers (x, y), scalar quantization snaps x to a grid point on one axis and y to a grid point on another — producing a rectangular grid. But in two-dimensional space the most storage-efficient arrangement of points is not a square grid but a hexagonal lattice (like a honeycomb). Treating (x, y) as a single 2-D point and snapping it to the nearest lattice point achieves better precision with the same number of bits. The higher the dimension, the greater this "group storage" advantage.

*** Their solution

This is *vector quantization* (VQ): instead of storing numbers individually, weights are cut into small groups (e.g., 8 at a time) and a *codebook* is prepared in advance — a list of representative vectors. Storage requires only the index of whichever codebook entry is closest to the group. Decoding is a simple lookup. AQLM uses additive combinations of multiple codebooks; VPTQ targets extremely low bit-widths; QuIP# marries QuIP's incoherence processing with the codebook approach.

*** Why it works — and what new pit it digs

It works because group storage is geometrically more efficient — higher dimension means greater savings. This is mathematics, not engineering.

But the new pit is severe: *codebook size grows exponentially with group dimension*. Storing groups of 8 already demands a large codebook; groups of 16 or 32 produce codebooks too large to fit in GPU memory and too slow to query. So this entire line is hard-capped at low dimensions (≤ 8). "Want higher dimensions but strangled by the codebook" — that is the problem QTIP is born to solve.

** 2024 | QTIP: Replace the codebook with trellis coding; dimension is now free

*** What problem did it see?

QTIP is direct: the ceiling of VQ is not the idea itself but *the codebook as dead weight*. As long as you insist on an explicit lookup table, dimension will always be shackled to the table's size. To free dimension you need a way to decode without storing a table at all.

*** Its solution

QTIP borrows a classic weapon from communications — *trellis coded quantization* (TCQ).

Instead of a lookup table, it uses a *stateful decoder*. Analogy: a codebook is a thick phone directory that must be memorized in full to use; a trellis is a small state machine — feed it a stream of bits and it computes the next number from "current state + input bits," then advances its state. The machine itself is tiny, but the sequences it can generate (the effective dimension) can be arbitrarily long. QTIP adds several key engineering refinements: a bitshift trellis for parallel decoding, pseudo-random Gaussian codewords generated on the fly to avoid storing a codebook, and inherited incoherence processing to shape weights into a Gaussian.

*** Why it works — and what new pit it digs

It works because it fully decouples codebook size from dimension — dimension can be as large as desired, quality approaches the theoretical optimum for Gaussian distributions, and inference runs 3× faster than unquantized. This is the current state-of-the-art along the VQ line.

But the pit from the very beginning is now glaringly exposed: *bit-width is still integer*. QTIP gives you 2 bits, 3 bits, or 4 bits. Freedom in dimension produces quality gains but does not produce freedom in bit-width. The next paper attacks exactly this.

** 2025 | Q-Palette: Want fractional bits? Assemble a whole palette of quantizers

*** What problem did it see?

Q-Palette does a foundational theoretical calculation: for Gaussian-shaped weights, *the information-theoretically optimal bit-width assignment is, in general, fractional*. Different layers, different sensitivities — the optimal solution might be 2.3 bits here, 2.7 bits there, 3.1 bits somewhere else. Integer bit-widths always waste something: round up to 3 bits (waste capacity) or round down to 2 bits (waste quality). The theory is clear: fractional bit-widths are necessary.

*** Its solution

Q-Palette's approach is to *assemble a "palette" of quantizers*: collect all available quantizers — fast scalar ones, intermediate vector quantizers, the most precise trellis quantizer — into a single toolbox, with each variant tuned for a specific bit-width. To hit a target fractional bit-width, select or blend the closest entry from the palette. A companion framework automatically decides which palette entry to use for each layer and how to fuse it with that layer.

*** Why it works — and what new pit it digs

It works because it is the first system to turn "fractional bit-widths" from theory into a runnable system, and decoding is 36% faster than NF4.

But the new pit is *engineering fragmentation*: the palette is a jumble of heterogeneous quantizers. Every distinct quantizer variant and every distinct bit-width requires its own hand-written, hand-tuned CUDA kernel. Covering a continuous spectrum of bit-widths means maintaining a sprawling kernel library — heavy, hard to extend, hard to maintain. Fractional bits are real, but architectural unity is shattered. That is precisely LiftQuant's opening.

** 2026 | LiftQuant: One unified architecture; bit-width is a freely tunable ratio

*** What problem did it see?

LiftQuant distills every predecessor's dilemma into one sentence: either you have architectural unity but integer-only bit-widths (QTIP), or you have fractional bit-widths but a shattered architecture of specialized kernels (Q-Palette). *No one could have both continuous bit-widths and a single unified architecture at the same time.*

It also names a deeper tension: scalar quantization cannot capture cross-dimension correlations; vector quantization can, but codebooks and lookup tables are hardware-unfriendly with large decoding overhead. Each approach has its own fatal flaw.

*** Its solution

LiftQuant's core technique is *lift-then-project*.

Reframe the idea of "bit-width." Instead of agonizing over "how many bits per number," ask: "how many low-precision grid points do I use to represent a group of numbers?"

Concretely: take the original d-dimensional weight vector and *lift* it to a higher dimension D (in this higher-dimensional space it has more degrees of freedom and can be represented more neatly at low precision), quantize there, then *project* back to the original d dimensions. The key insight: *bit-width = D / d*. Both D and d are integers, but their ratio can be any real number — D = 12, d = 5 gives 2.4 bits. Continuous bit-widths emerge naturally, with no need to split a bit; you simply adjust a dimension ratio.

Moreover the entire process is the same mathematical operation throughout (a lifting matrix followed by a projection matrix). Whether you target 2.1 bits or 2.9 bits, *the architecture is identical* — only the shapes of the matrices change. This discards Q-Palette's heterogeneous kernel zoo. Theoretically the approach rests on a classical result (Diaconis–Freedman 1984): high-dimensional lattice projections into low dimensions converge to Gaussian, which guarantees that the lift-then-project distribution is regular and easy to quantize.

*** Why it works — and what new pit it digs

It works because it replaces "number of bits" with a *ratio* as the bit-width dial, instantly turning a discrete problem into a continuous one, while retaining vector-quantization-level expressiveness (capturing cross-dimension correlations) without requiring a large codebook. The paper's signature demonstration: compress the 70 B model to 2.4 bits, fitting exactly into a 24 GB GPU — the deployment gap from the opening is closed precisely.

The remaining pit (honestly acknowledged): at strictly identical bit-widths it cannot yet definitively surpass QTIP's trellis high-dimensional coding — only "a small additional fraction of a bit" is needed to overtake it. In other words, the last narrow gap between "unified architecture" and "quality approaching the theoretical ceiling" has not been fully welded shut.

* Frontier Extensions

LiftQuant is an ICML 2026 Spotlight published in June 2026. *As of this writing it is the most advanced development along this line.*

Likely next steps (speculative, not confirmed papers):

- *Continuous bit-width ratio + automatic per-layer allocation* combined. Q-Palette already proved "each layer has a different optimal bit-width"; LiftQuant provides the unified mechanism for "any bit-width" — merging the two, letting a framework automatically assign each layer its own continuous optimal ratio, is the natural next paper.
- *Welding shut the last gap*: making the unified architecture match or surpass QTIP's trellis quality at the same bit-width.
- *Hardware co-design*: lift-then-project is matrix multiplication, which is natively GPU/accelerator-friendly and may catalyze "inference kernels or hardware tailored to continuous bit-widths."

* Insights

Reading the full river, it looks on the surface like "quantization quality keeps improving," but the deeper cognitive shift underneath is *the continuous elevation of the storage unit and the deconstruction of "bit-width" as a concept.*

1. *The storage unit keeps rising*: from "one number" (scalar) → "a group of numbers" (vector) → "an infinitely long sequence" (trellis state machine) → "a geometrically lifted object" (lift). Each elevation occurs because the previous unit "cannot hold enough structural information." The hidden through-line of this river is people continually expanding how many weights they are willing to regard as a single thing.

2. *"Bit-width must be an integer" is a hidden assumption that went unquestioned for twenty years.* None of the five preceding papers challenged it — they iterated endlessly on precision, dimension, and codebooks while silently accepting 2/3/4 as God-given. Q-Palette was the first to show theoretically that "the optimal bit-width is fractional," but its solution was a brute-force patchwork. LiftQuant is the first to *mechanically* let fractional bit-widths arise naturally. The deepest breakthroughs are often not solutions to problems but *the act of spotting an assumption everyone treated as air — and removing it.*

3. *Unity vs. peak performance is an eternal tension.* QTIP is extreme but integer-only; Q-Palette is flexible but fragmented; LiftQuant re-unifies with "a single ratio," at the cost of not yet fully matching QTIP at equal bit-widths. Every "unification" along this river clears clean ground for the next "peak."

Most likely direction forward: *continuous bit-width + automatic per-layer allocation + hardware co-design*, all three together. Now that bit-width is continuous, "finding the optimal real-valued bit-width for each layer" transforms from an impossibility into an optimization problem — that is the obviously next mountain.

* Takeaways

The sharpest single lesson this river offers about how to do research and how to find problems is:

*Go looking for the assumption everyone shares but no one has written down.*

The most valuable step in the entire chain was not making 2-bit quantization more accurate. It was someone stopping to ask: "Why does bit-width have to be an integer?" Once asked, the answer is almost self-evident (because you cannot cut a bit in half), but *posing the question* required stepping outside the existing frame. Everyone was optimizing inside the cage of "integer bit-widths," and the real breakthrough came from seeing the cage.

The practical takeaway: the next time you read the SOTA in a field, instead of asking "how do I push this metric up another 1%," first list every *unstated premise* that the whole field shares — data format, evaluation protocol, input assumptions, some "obvious" discretization — and ask of each: "What if this were not true?" LiftQuant's entire contribution is, in essence, saying "no" to the premise that bit-width must be an integer. You can say "no" to the equivalent air-assumption in your own field.
