* The River of the Problem

*How do you compress a hundred-gigabyte large language model down to something a phone can run, while losing almost none of its intelligence?*

The hard part is not "cutting precision" itself — dropping 16-bit weights to 4-bit, 3-bit, or even 2-bit is straightforward arithmetic. The hard part is: after the cut, the model still has to make sense.

Every bit shaved off every weight adds a tiny sliver of noise. That noise compounds across a hundred-plus transformer layers and the output collapses. So the question is never "can we cut?" but "where do we cut a little more, and where do we cut a little less, so the total noise is minimized?"

That question has been answered — and re-answered — from 2020 to 2026, by one generation of researchers after another: *How do I know which weights matter?*

And every generation discovers the same thing: the tool the previous generation used to "judge weight importance" was itself biased. Fix the bias, discover a new one, fix again — that is this river.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2020 | AdaRound: Quantization Should Not Simply Round to Nearest

*** What problem did it see?

Before AdaRound, compressing a 32-bit weight to 4-bit was embarrassingly simple: each weight was independently rounded to its nearest discrete value. 0.37 became 0.375, 0.62 became 0.625.

It seems perfectly natural. But Nagel et al. showed it is wrong.

Why? Because the thousands of weights in a single layer *compensate for each other*. Two weights that shift in opposite directions can leave the layer's output unchanged. If you look at each weight in isolation, rounding to nearest looks optimal; but if you look at the entire layer, you find that some weights should be *deliberately rounded in the wrong direction* so they cancel out a neighbor's error — making the overall output error smaller.

*** Its solution

It introduced a second-order quantity to measure "does it hurt less to round this weight up or down with respect to the final loss?" — that quantity is the Hessian H_(w) = E[∇²_w L]. The Hessian tells you the shape of the loss surface at the current weight location: which directions fall away steeply.

AdaRound learns a mask matrix that decides, for each weight, whether to round up or down. This was the first time the Hessian was written into the PTQ story.

*** Why it worked (and what new hole it opened)

It worked because it stopped treating weights as isolated points and instead treated them as a crowd of mutually interacting forces on the loss surface.

Where is the hole? *It needs gradients, it needs training, it needs time.* AdaRound is wonderful on ResNet, but when LLMs arrived — ten billion parameters — just computing one Hessian blows up memory, let alone learning a mask.

The next generation had to solve this.

** 2021 | BRECQ: Block-wise Reconstruction, but Still Slow

*** What problem did it see?

AdaRound operates layer by layer. Each layer requires solving an optimization problem. LLMs have over a hundred layers, each with thousands of columns of weights — a full pass takes days.

BRECQ's insight: don't go layer by layer; instead, reconstruct by *block* (a few layers bundled together). The optimization target then better approximates the true output.

*** Its solution

Slice the transformer into blocks, and within each block use gradient-based methods to minimize the gap between "quantized output" and "full-precision output." Coarser than AdaRound, but much faster.

*** Why it worked (and what new hole it opened)

It worked because it found a sweet spot between accuracy and speed.

Where is the hole? *Even with this speedup, gradients are still required.* Gradients require backpropagation, which requires fitting the model, activations, and gradients into memory simultaneously. For 70B-scale models, that constraint alone is a dead end.

Researchers realized: gradients had to be *completely* abandoned.

** 2023 | GPTQ: Quantize an Entire Model Overnight

*** What problem did it see?

BRECQ's gradient-based approach was still impractical for LLMs. Frantar et al. decided: *no gradients at all.*

But without gradients, how do you know which weights matter? The answer was still the Hessian — but a Hessian that can be computed without backpropagation.

*** Its solution

This is the most pivotal mathematical move in the entire river; it deserves a close look.

For a linear layer z = W x, the full weight Hessian H_(w) can be factored into a Kronecker product of two terms:

  H_(w) = (X X^T) ⊗ ∇²_z L  =  H_in ⊗ H_out

- H_in = X X^T is *purely a second-order statistic of the activations* — no loss needed, no backpropagation. Run a calibration batch through a single forward pass and you have it.
- H_out = ∇²_z L is the second derivative of the loss with respect to the pre-activations. *This is the hard part to compute.*

GPTQ made one bold simplification: *assume H_out = I (the identity matrix).*

Once H_out is the identity, the full Hessian collapses to H_in. Then, using a Cholesky decomposition and greedy column-wise updates — quantize one column at a time, and after each column, propagate the error onto the remaining columns.

Overnight, an entire 175B model is quantized. No gradients. No training.

*** Why it worked (and what new hole it opened)

It worked because it *reduced a training problem to a linear algebra problem.*

Where are the holes? Two of them:

1. *H_out = I is false.* The loss is obviously not isotropic; some pre-activation dimensions are far more sensitive than others.
2. *H_in is computed from sequences of a fixed length (2048 tokens).* Nobody questioned this, because everyone did it.

The first hole was caught by the next generation. The second hole went unseen for five years — until this paper.

** 2024–2025 | BoA & GPTAQ: Fixing H_out

*** What problem did they see?

GPTQ's treatment of H_out as identity causes quantization errors to accumulate across layers — each layer thinks it is "locally optimal," but its output is already shifted, and the next layer receives that shifted input, quantizes again, and shifts further.

BoA (attention-aware) and GPTAQ (asymmetric calibration) both attack this problem.

*** Their solutions

- *BoA*: incorporates the reconstruction error of the entire attention block, effectively replacing the identity with a more accurate H_out.
- *GPTAQ*: forces each layer's quantized output to *match the full-precision model's output at the same position* — "asymmetric calibration." This is equivalent to using the full-precision model as a teacher that corrects the accumulated drift.

Both approaches say the same thing: *H_out should not be I; it should reflect how sensitive the downstream layers are to the current layer's output.*

*** Why it worked (and what new hole it opened)

It worked because cross-layer accumulated drift was genuinely suppressed, yielding consistent benchmark gains.

Where is the hole? *Everyone was fixing H_out; nobody looked back at H_in.*

H_in = X X^T had been treated for five years as "determined by the data, objective, not worth tuning." But what is "the data"? It is the 256 sequences of 2048 tokens you happened to feed in. Does that truly represent all the inputs the model will see in deployment?

** 2026 | MaCa: The Term Nobody Watched Was the Most Biased

*** What problem did it see?

The authors ran a simple — almost embarrassingly simple — experiment: on the down-proj layer of Qwen3-4B, they computed the diagonal of H_in separately using "short sequences" and "long sequences," then plotted both curves.

What they saw: *two completely different curves.* Short sequences carry high energy in certain channels; long sequences carry high energy in entirely different channels — nearly complementary patterns.

What does this mean? It means that if you calibrate with sequences of only one fixed length (2048 tokens), your conclusion about "which input channels matter" is *only true for 2048-token inputs.* When the deployed model receives a 200-word question from a user, or a 32k-token long document, the channels you prioritized during PTQ may not be the bottleneck at all.

An analogy: *you tailored a suit using measurements taken exclusively from people who are 1.75 m tall, then used those measurements to dress everyone.*

*** Its solution

Matryoshka Calibration — "nesting doll" calibration. Two steps:

1. *Multi-scale length aggregation*: calibration data is no longer uniformly 2048 tokens, but drawn uniformly from {256, 512, 1024, 2048, 4096}. This lets H_in see sequences of every length.
2. *Per-sample rather than per-token normalization*: GPTQ's running-average weight is N_old / (N_old + L), which is intrinsically token-count-weighted — long sequences contribute more tokens and therefore dominate, pulling H_in back toward the "long-sequence" shape. MaCa instead divides each sample's contribution by its own L_m first, so short and long samples contribute *equally* to the final H_in.

Only after both steps does the Hessian become truly length-aware.

*** Why it worked (and what new hole it opened)

It worked because it untangled a coupling that had been hidden for five years: *the statistical shape of calibration data ≠ the statistical shape of deployment data.*

The deeper insight: H_in is not "objective." It is *determined by which calibration data you choose.* In other words, PTQ had been making precise measurements with a biased instrument all along.

Experimental numbers: +8.7 points on Qwen3-8B at 2-bit. A gain of this magnitude is rare in the PTQ literature of 2026.

Where are the remaining holes?

- The length set {256, 512, 1024, 2048, 4096} is still manually specified — why these five? The true deployment length distribution may look completely different.
- Only *length* diversity is addressed, not *content* diversity. The X X^T shapes for code, dialogue, and long documents also differ.

These two holes are the starting point for the next generation.

* Frontier Extensions

As of February 2026, this is the most recent progress along this line. MaCa was just posted; its citation count is zero.

But following the direction it opens, the next developments are foreseeable:

- *Distribution-aware calibration*: not just sequence length, but folding in the actual input distribution of the target deployment scenario (code vs. dialogue vs. long documents) when constructing H_in.
- *Online adaptive calibration*: after deployment, continuously refine the H_in estimate from real user inputs rather than fixing it once at PTQ time.
- *Applying the MaCa idea to activation quantization*: this paper only touches the Hessian for weight quantization; the scale estimation for activation quantization has the same length-bias problem.

* Insights

Two things in this river keep coming back to me.

*First: the most dangerous assumptions are the ones that go unquestioned.* From GPTQ in 2023 to MaCa in 2026 — three full years — the entire field was busy fixing the H_out term. Nobody touched H_in, because everyone assumed it was "determined by the data, objective, nothing to optimize." But "the data" doesn't fall from the sky — you chose the calibration set. You chose 256 sequences of 2048 tokens, so H_in is simply the statistics of that distribution.

The entire PTQ community needed three years to notice this blind spot. Such blind spots don't disappear because researchers are smart; they only become visible when *someone is willing to ask what looks like a dumb question* — "Why do we always use 2048 tokens?" — and actually push on it.

*Second: once a decomposition is made, it shapes the next three years of research.* After GPTQ factored the Hessian into H_in ⊗ H_out, the community's attention was automatically partitioned by that decomposition. Fix one side (H_out), then fix the other (H_in). If GPTQ had arrived at a different factorization, the subsequent three years of papers would look completely different.

*The form of the decomposition = the boundary of subsequent research.* This holds in every field.

Where does the work go next? I expect two directions:

1. *The Hessian estimate itself gets re-examined* — the assumption that "the Hessian is a fixed statistic" may also be wrong. It should be a function of the input distribution.
2. *Calibration-to-deployment alignment is explicitly modeled* — PTQ is no longer "grab some data and compute," but "whatever distribution I'll deploy under, that's what I calibrate under."

* Takeaways

For researchers: *look back at the steps nobody questions.* GPTQ used 2048 tokens for calibration from 2023 to 2026 and nobody changed it. Those "default parameters" are gold mines — they sit there for five years because nobody thought them worth questioning.

For engineers: if you are quantizing models with GPTQ, AWQ, or GPTAQ, *you can already mix sequences of different lengths into your calibration set right now*, without waiting for any new code. The two changes in MaCa add up to fewer than 20 lines of Python. That means you can — this very afternoon — pick up several benchmark points in your quantization pipeline.

For thinking in general: when a field seems to have exhausted every angle of competition, it is usually because everyone is competing along the same dimension. Step back and ask: "We have been tweaking A for years — what about B? Has B been quietly assumed to be a constant?" The MaCa story is a perfect illustration of exactly that move.
