* The River of the Problem

Large models are too large to run. Can we cut out half the useless components and still have them speak coherently? That is the same question neural-network pruning has been asking for the past thirty years.

The hard part is not the cutting — it is the judgment: which parameters are redundant and which are critical? Early work relied on mathematics (second-order derivatives); later work relied on engineering (activation magnitudes); and now a new problem has emerged: when a model not only "speaks" but also "reasons" and "sees images," every traditional pruning criterion breaks down. In a long reasoning chain, only a handful of pivot tokens may actually determine the conclusion; in an image, the activation distribution of visual features and text are not even in the same ballpark — yet every pruning method treats them all with a uniform average. MuCRASP is the latest step along this line: it argues that pruning must "understand" reasoning structure and can no longer treat all tokens as equal.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1989 | OBD: Use Second-Order Derivatives to Decide What to Cut

*** What Problem Did It See?

Neural networks caught fire in the 1980s, but trained networks were often riddled with redundant parameters. The practice at the time was to prune by intuition or trial and error — remove small weights, remove weights that barely moved. The problem: a small weight is not the same as an unimportant one. A numerically tiny but structurally pivotal weight, once removed, can collapse the entire network.

*** Its Solution

LeCun went straight to mathematics: removing a weight is equivalent to setting it to zero. How much does that move the loss function? A Taylor expansion to second order, assuming convergence (so the first-order term vanishes), leaves only the second-order term. For simplicity, only the diagonal of the Hessian is used — the "self-importance" of each weight. Importance = (1/2) * w^2 * H_ii.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

It transformed "what to prune" from an intuitive question into a computable mathematical one — a paradigm shift. But it buried a trap: it ignored coupling between weights. Two weights might each appear unimportant in isolation while together they carry some function; OBD cannot see this.

** 1993 | OBS: Adjust All Other Weights When One Is Removed

*** What Problem Did It See?

OBD only looks at the Hessian diagonal, effectively assuming all weights are mutually independent — obviously wrong. The parameters in a neural network are strongly coupled; removing A shifts the optimal value of B.

*** Its Solution

Hassibi used the full Hessian — not just to decide who to prune, but also to compute how all remaining weights should shift to recover the lost loss. A closed-form solution: delta_w = -(w_q / [H^{-1}]_qq) * H^{-1} * e_q.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

Theoretically superior, but computing the inverse Hessian is completely impractical for large networks — with billions of parameters, the Hessian is a billion-by-billion matrix. This path hit a ceiling in the 1990s and went quiet for nearly thirty years.

** 2023 | SparseGPT: Decompose the Global Hessian into Layer-Wise Solutions

*** What Problem Did It See?

The LLM era arrived, with 175B-parameter GPT sitting on the table — directly applying OBS was out of the question. But leaving the pruning potential of these oversized models untapped was a waste, since many weights genuinely serve no purpose.

*** Its Solution

Frantar found a compromise: instead of computing the Hessian for the whole network, decompose the problem layer by layer. Each layer independently solves a sparse regression — given input activations and weights, find the sparsest weight combination whose output stays as close as possible to the original. This is essentially a layer-wise approximation of OBS, yet it can run through a 175B model in a few hours, reaching 60% sparsity with almost no quality drop.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

Replacing "global optimization" with "within-layer optimality" made the engineering feasible. But two traps remained: (1) within-layer optimality is not global optimality; (2) the Hessian still has to be computed (only approximated), so compute and memory costs are still substantial.

** 2023 | Wanda: Throw Away the Hessian Entirely

*** What Problem Did It See?

SparseGPT was fast enough, but it still had to solve a sparse regression and approximate the Hessian. Sun et al. observed a simple fact: in a well-trained large model, the importance of a weight can be approximated by a brutally simple proxy — the weight multiplied by the magnitude of the corresponding input activation.

*** Its Solution

importance_ij = |W_ij| * ||X_j||_2. One line. One forward pass over calibration data, record the activations, pruning done.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

Simple to the point of disbelief — yet it matches SparseGPT empirically. This tells us: in the overparameterized regime of large models, the bottleneck in pruning is not "how precisely you compute" but "how fast you compute." But Wanda and SparseGPT are both unstructured — the result is a sparse matrix, and a standard GPU does not get a 2× speedup from 50% zeros; specialized sparse kernels are required.

** 2023 | LLM-Pruner: Structured Pruning — Remove Entire Channels/Heads

*** What Problem Did It See?

Unstructured sparsity is hardware-unfriendly. To actually shrink model size and accelerate inference, you must remove entire neurons, entire attention heads — leaving a dense sub-network. But structured pruning is far harder than unstructured: you cannot judge each weight independently; you must judge "what downstream units are affected if I remove this entire unit."

*** Its Solution

Ma used a dependency graph to trace consequences: removing one weight affects which upstream and downstream weights — bundle them into a "coupled group" and remove them together. A Taylor expansion (first- and second-order terms) scores each group's importance.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

The dependency graph turned structured pruning from hand-crafted rules into an automatic algorithm. But it estimates importance from a single-shot activation — if a channel is important for some samples and unimportant for others, a single estimate will be highly inaccurate.

** 2024 | FLAP: Use Activation Fluctuation Rather Than Activation Magnitude

*** What Problem Did It See?

LLM-Pruner draws conclusions from one look at activations. But activation values fluctuate wildly across different inputs — the "average activation" of a channel can be misleading.

*** Its Solution

An et al. used activation fluctuation across samples as the importance metric. A channel that produces stable activations across all samples is doing something generic; a channel with high fluctuation is doing something functional. Structured pruning is applied on this basis.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

Incorporating statistical stability into the pruning criterion is a clean engineering improvement. But all these methods — SparseGPT, Wanda, LLM-Pruner, FLAP — share one hidden assumption: all tokens contribute equally, and activations are averaged uniformly across the token dimension. This assumption barely holds for ordinary LLMs, but it collapses completely on CoT reasoning.

** 2026 | MuCRASP: CoT-Aware + Cross-Modal Alignment + Global Knapsack

*** What Problem Did It See?

Dutta and Aditya ran FLAP, LLM-Pruner, and Wanda on Qwen2.5-VL — perplexity looked fine, but the generated reasoning chains fell apart. By 25–30% pruning the model was already hallucinating.

Looking closely revealed two fundamental issues:

(1) *CoT reasoning is not uniform.* In a multi-hundred-word reasoning chain, the "pivot tokens" that actually determine the conclusion — the few tokens around "therefore," "but," "consequently" — number just a handful. Everything else is scaffolding. Traditional pruning averages loss across all tokens, diluting the signal from these critical tokens to invisibility.

(2) *Visual and text activations differ by orders of magnitude.* In a VLM, the activation distributions of visual tokens and text tokens live on completely different scales. When layer-wise pruning applies a uniform threshold to judge importance, it either wipes out visual information (visual activations are small but important) or retains too much text information.

*** Its Solution

Three-part system:

1. *Trajectory Pivot Attribution* — Identify pivot tokens in the CoT chain (via pivot-marker heuristics) and up-weight them during attribution. Parameters that the pivots strongly depend on are protected.

2. *Cross-Modal Dependency Score (CMDS)* — Explicitly model which parameters are sensitive to both visual and text activations simultaneously; these are critical for cross-modal alignment and must not be pruned.

3. *Global Knapsack with Layer Sensitivity* — The structural units across VLM layers differ enormously in cost (one GQA group ≈ 5.2M parameters; one MLP neuron ≈ 21K); it is wrong to distribute cuts evenly across layers. A global knapsack problem is solved to allocate how much each layer should be pruned under a total parameter budget.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Left Behind)

Experiments show that at 30% pruning, MuCRASP achieves an LLM-Judge score of 8.87 while the best baseline reaches only 7.32; at 50% pruning it still maintains reasoning coherence, whereas traditional methods collapse at 25–30%.

It remains training-free pruning with no recovery step. The paper itself notes that knowledge distillation [13, 14] can serve as a post-processing recovery stage complementing this approach — that is the next direction.

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026, MuCRASP is the latest known work along this line (arXiv 2605.25842, submitted 2026-05-25).

The next step — which the paper itself points to — is combining pruning with distillation: use MuCRASP's reasoning-critical parameter preservation first, then apply CoT-aware distillation to recover lost capability. Another direction is replacing the pivot-token identification from heuristics (punctuation / connectives) to something learnable: let the model itself tell you which tokens are its reasoning turning points.

* Insights

On the surface this line of work is "pruning algorithms getting better," but what is really changing underneath is that *the word "importance" keeps being redefined*.

In the OBD/OBS era, importance was a mathematical concept — the second-order term of the loss. SparseGPT/Wanda engineered it into "activation magnitude." LLM-Pruner structured it into "the aggregate contribution of a coupled group." FLAP statisticized it into "fluctuation stability." By MuCRASP, importance has become *"semantic role"* — what part does this token play in the reasoning flow, and how many modalities does this parameter simultaneously serve?

The core contradiction at every step is the same: *the previous generation's notion of "importance" fails in the new setting.* CoT reasoning breaks the "tokens are equal" assumption — 1% of the tokens in a sentence determine 99% of its semantic direction. Multimodality breaks the "channels are equal" assumption — visual and text activations are simply not on the same scale.

Where is the next step most likely to go? Two directions: (1) importance moves from "heuristic annotation" to "the model saying so itself" — a small network learns which tokens are pivots and which parameters are cross-modal keystones; (2) pruning moves from "one-shot decision" to "dynamic gating" — decide which parameters participate in computation given the current input, akin to MoE sparse activation.

* Takeaways

For research: when a field has been doing the same thing for thirty years (pruning), new progress often comes not from the algorithm itself but from *changing the data or changing the model* and having old assumptions exposed. MuCRASP's contribution is not a new importance metric — it is the discovery that "all existing methods share the token-equality assumption." That kind of shared blind spot is a gold mine for research.

For engineering: you can apply this mindset directly to your own pruning or compression tasks — first ask "what are the hidden assumptions of the existing methods," then ask "do those assumptions hold for my data?" The VLM + CoT combination naturally shatters those assumptions. Any scenario where "existing methods drop quality on my data" is worth probing with exactly this framing.
