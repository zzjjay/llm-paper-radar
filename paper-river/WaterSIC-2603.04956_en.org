* One sentence

  For the past decade everyone working on LLM weight compression to low bit-widths has been tuning heuristics; this paper is the first to frame the problem as a well-posed information-theoretic question and proves its solution is within 0.255 bits of optimal.

* What this river looks like

  The story runs from pruning theory in 1993 all the way to WaterSIC in 2026. The through-line is a gradual process of "figuring out what we are actually solving." Each paper sees something the previous one missed.

* Starting from the source

** Paper 0: The legacy of the pruning era (OBS / OBD, 1989–1993)

   The story begins with the oldest form of neural-network compression: pruning.

   LeCun proposed Optimal Brain Damage in 1989; Hassibi upgraded it to Optimal Brain Surgeon in 1993. The question is straightforward: given a trained network with too many parameters, can we identify a single weight to remove while minimizing the resulting loss?

   Their answer is a Taylor expansion. Near the optimum the loss function is approximately a quadratic form, whose shape is described by the Hessian matrix. Both "which weight hurts least to delete" and "how should the remaining weights be adjusted after deletion" therefore have closed-form second-order solutions.

   *Here, for the first time, appears an idea that would go unchanged for the next thirty years:* pruning and quantization are the same class of problem — both ask "I need to change a weight; how do I minimize the resulting loss?" The only difference is that pruning sets the weight to zero, while quantization sets it to the nearest lattice point.

** Paper 1: OBQ — transplanting pruning directly into quantization (2022)

   *What did it see that its predecessors missed?* It realized that OBS theory never required the weight to be changed to zero. Any target value is valid, including "the nearest discrete lattice point."

   Frantar & Alistarh therefore launched OBQ (Optimal Brain Quantizer) in 2022: take the OBS second-order machinery and apply it unchanged to quantization. The procedure is greedy — pick the weight with the lowest quantization cost, commit it, then use the inverse Hessian to compensate all remaining unquantized weights so that the layer's output error is minimized.

   Elegant in theory. The problem: every time one weight is quantized the inverse Hessian must be updated, a cubic-complexity operation. Fine for networks under 100 M parameters; for GPT-175B the authors themselves estimated it would take *half a year* to finish.

   *What it left open*: theoretically beautiful, practically a dead end.

** Paper 2: GPTQ — replacing greedy selection with a fixed order (2023)

   *What did it see that its predecessors missed?* It discovered that OBQ's greedy strategy of "always quantize the cheapest weight next" is essentially irrelevant at large scale — *quantizing in any fixed order yields nearly identical results*.

   This is a deeply counterintuitive observation. The reason OBQ is slow is precisely that it must re-rank and re-update at every step. If the order does not matter, just process columns 1, 2, …, n in sequence — the inverse Hessian needs to be computed only once (via Cholesky decomposition), and the rest is constant-time subtraction.

   Result: a 175 B model can be quantized on a single GPU in a few hours. This was the first time 4-bit LLM quantization was genuinely practical.

   A fact only later recognized: when GPTQ runs in back-to-front column order, it *is exactly Babai's 1986 nearest plane algorithm* — the most classical approximate nearest-vector algorithm in lattice cryptography. GPTQ had unknowingly reinvented it.

   *What it left open*: no clean theoretical guarantees. GPTQ is heuristic; nobody could prove how far it was from "optimal quantization."

** Paper 3: QuIP — weights must be made uniform first (2023)

   *What did it see that its predecessors missed?* GPTQ performs poorly on certain weight matrices because of "outliers" — a small number of abnormally large weights that stretch the quantization grid, sacrificing precision for all the smaller weights.

   Chee et al proposed: *before quantizing, rotate both the weights and the Hessian* so that energy is spread evenly across all dimensions. Concretely, multiply on both sides by random orthogonal matrices U, V. Because orthogonal transformations preserve the quadratic form ‖(W − Ŵ)X‖², the problem is unchanged, yet the transformed matrix is "incoherent" — no direction concentrates disproportionate energy.

   QuIP made 2-bit quantization viable for the first time, and provided the first end-to-end theoretical analysis of a quantization algorithm.

   *What it left open*: the random rotation matrices U, V cannot be computed efficiently at inference time.

** Paper 4: QuIP# / NestQuant — getting both the rotation and the quantizer right (2024–2025)

   QuIP# replaces the random orthogonal matrix with the Hadamard transform — fast and theoretically grounded — and upgrades the quantizer from "nearest integer per coordinate" to "nearest vector in the E8 lattice," the densest known sphere packing in 8-dimensional Euclidean space.

   NestQuant (Savkin et al 2025, *co-authored by one of the present paper's authors*) goes further: nested lattices handle quantization directly, eliminating the need for a lookup table at encode/decode time.

   At this point everyone has been doing the same thing: find a better quantizer, find a better preprocessing transform. But *nobody had asked a more fundamental question*:

   *Given the activation covariance Σ_X, what is the information-theoretic limit for quantizing this layer's weights? How far are we from that limit?*

** Paper 5: WaterSIC — finally framing the problem as channel coding (2026)

   That is this paper.

   Its operation looks almost identical to GPTQ at first glance: quantize columns in order, subtract the "interference" of each committed column from the remaining ones. Written out, the algorithmic core is a tiny variation on GPTQ:

#+begin_src text
   GPTQ:     all columns use the same quantization step size α
   WaterSIC: column i uses step size  α_i = c / |ℓ_ii|
                                            ^^^^^^^^^^
                       reciprocal of the Cholesky diagonal element,
                       varying from column to column
#+end_src

   This change looks like a bug fix. But the authors prove:

   - This is *exactly equivalent to the classical information-theoretic water-filling theorem*. The solution from 1948 that allocates power across parallel channels turns out to be the optimal bit allocation for quantizing each column of weights.
   - This is *exactly equivalent to SIC* (successive interference cancellation), the classical decoding order in multi-user communications. Cholesky decomposition is precisely the operation of committing one signal and subtracting it from the rest, carried to completion.
   - GPTQ is a special case of WaterSIC, corresponding to water-filling *with only one allowed power level*. As a result, GPTQ's distortion scales with the *arithmetic mean* of the Cholesky diagonal elements, while WaterSIC's scales with the *geometric mean*. The AM–GM inequality guarantees GPTQ ≥ WaterSIC always, with a gap that can be arbitrarily large when the diagonal elements are highly uneven.
   - WaterSIC is within *0.255 bits* of the information-theoretic lower bound, and this gap is independent of Σ_X. The constant 0.255 = ½log(2πe/12) is the "space-filling loss" of a scalar lattice relative to an optimal vector quantizer — a purely geometric constant.

   *What did it see that its predecessors missed?* It saw that quantization is not a new problem at all. It is the "Gaussian channel capacity" problem that Shannon solved in 1948, wearing a different costume. Every heuristic of the past decade — smarter rotations, finer lattices, better calibration — has been reinventing tools that information theory already possessed.

   Once you adopt this viewpoint, the seemingly intractable engineering question "how far is GPTQ from optimal?" immediately yields a one-line formula answer.

  What is really changing along this line is not "algorithms getting more complex" — it is *people's understanding of what problem they are actually solving*:

  - OBS era: I am solving a quadratic optimization problem.
  - GPTQ era: I am doing a structured greedy search.
  - QuIP era: I am solving a quantization problem after preprocessing.
  - WaterSIC era: I am solving a hybrid of lossy source coding and multi-user channel coding — and that was solved in the 1960s.

* What comes next

  WaterSIC attributes the 0.255-bit gap entirely to the *geometric loss of the scalar quantizer itself*. The natural next question is therefore: *if the per-column scalar quantizer is replaced by a low-dimensional vector quantizer (e.g., the E8 lattice), can the 0.255-bit gap be further closed?*

  QuIP# is already attempting this, but without an information-theoretic lens. The WaterSIC framework naturally supports replacing "one column at a time" with "one group at a time." The prediction for the next paper: a formal merger of WaterSIC's bit-allocation principle with the lattice quantization ideas of QuIP#/NestQuant — with an explicit, provable, Σ_X-independent gap number.

* Takeaway

  *Many problems that look "fully squeezed out by engineering" simply have no one who has thought to open an information-theory textbook.*

  Quantization research produced dozens of papers over the past three years — endless streams of rotations, smoothing schemes, calibration tricks. WaterSIC's contribution is not a new trick; its algorithm is nearly identical to GPTQ. The contribution is pointing out that *GPTQ already is SIC, and you should be fixing it with water-filling*.

  This is a particularly valuable kind of work: when everyone is competing on engineering cleverness, find a neglected, decades-old mathematical framework and reorganize the entire field's confusion into a single formula. Next time you see a field where everyone is piling on tricks, ask yourself — is this just an old problem wearing new clothes?
