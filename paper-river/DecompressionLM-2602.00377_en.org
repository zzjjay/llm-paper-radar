* One sentence
What exactly gets lost when you compress a model? Not just that slight perplexity drop — it's entire swaths of the concepts the model could think of collapsing all at once, and your old benchmarks never saw it coming.

* Evolutionary narrative

** Starting point: LAMA (2019) — you can only ask questions you already know the answers to

Petroni's paper, "Language Models as Knowledge Bases?", is the origin of this line of work. It set out to do something bold: treat a language model as a queryable knowledge base. The method was fill-in-the-blank — "Dante was born in __X__" — and the model was expected to fill in "Florence." Get it right, and it counts as knowing the fact.

This paradigm rests on a premise that looks small but is actually fatal: *you have to know the relation before you can write the template*. "Birthplace," "nationality," "author of" — every relation type requires a hand-crafted fill-in-the-blank sentence. Researchers can only ask what they thought to ask in advance.

Does the model know anything about "Mozart and the starling he kept as a pet"? Nobody asked, so nobody knows. What Petroni measured was a model's ability to answer questions humans already thought to pose — not the model's actual breadth of knowledge.

The field term for this is *closed-world probing* — the evaluation is sealed shut by the questions you wrote yourself.

** AutoPrompt (2020) — templates are automated, but the door is still locked

Shin et al. wanted to spare researchers the chore of writing templates by hand — use gradient search to automatically find the best prompt. It sounds more sophisticated, but structurally it is the same approach.

Subtract AutoPrompt from LAMA and what remains is: the template is no longer hand-written, it is machine-searched. But *you still have to specify which relation you are querying*. "I want to find birthplaces" — then let the machine search for the best way to ask about birthplaces.

The closed-world wall was not broken; it was just repainted. You can still only retrieve what you decided to look for in the first place.

** Arithmetic Sampling (2022–2023) — a completely separate track, but it supplies the key tool

The narrative branches here. What Vilnis et al. (Google) solved has nothing to do with probing knowledge — they addressed a different problem: *how do you sample many distinct sequences from a language model in parallel without repetition?*

Beam search guarantees diversity but is inherently sequential — each step must inspect other beams. Temperature sampling parallelizes easily, but sample 100 times and roughly 80 of them may collide (identical sequences).

Their solution is elegant: reframe "sampling" as "picking a point in [0, 1)." Each point maps to one generated sequence. Points far apart from each other tend to produce distinct sequences. This is the arithmetic codebook.

Swap ordinary sampling for Vilnis's scheme and what you gain is: you can now lay out 1,000 workers on a GPU, hand each worker a pre-assigned code point, let each run a full independent decode, and *the results are guaranteed to be spread out and to cover the space uniformly*.

This tool was built for machine translation. It took DecompressionLM to pick it up and use it to pry open the door that LAMA had kept shut for seven years.

** The state of quantization evaluation (2023–2025) — benchmarks stare at text surfaces

A parallel track: model quantization. GPTQ uses second-order information to allocate bits; AWQ finds that "protecting just 1% of the most salient weights" is enough to keep error small. Both compress 16-bit weights to 4-bit.

How does the field decide which quantization method is better? Perplexity, and downstream scores like MMLU. The two methods look similar on these metrics — both drop a little, but perplexity remains acceptable.

All of these evaluations share the same blind spot: they measure *whether the model can answer predefined questions correctly*. They do not measure *which concepts the model can think of, how many, and where its long tail has collapsed*.

At this point the problem is sitting in plain sight: you compress a model, perplexity barely moves, downstream benchmarks hold up — and you have no idea how large a hole has been torn in the model's internal semantic map.

** DecompressionLM (2026) — pick up the hammer, pry open the door

Hong and You stitch the three tracks above together:

1. *From the LAMA line, they take the parent problem of "knowledge probing"* — but flip it from closed-world to open-world. Instead of asking "Where was Mozart born?", the question becomes: *emit every concept the model knows and assemble it into a graph*.
2. *From the Vilnis line, they take arithmetic sampling* — but repurpose it. Vilnis used it to generate diverse translations; they use it as a gradient-controllable, parallel, replayable concept-extraction engine.
3. *They aim it squarely at the blind spot in quantization evaluation* — using the extracted concept graph as a new diagnostic metric to see exactly where GPTQ and AWQ diverge.

Concretely, they drive the sampler with the Van der Corput sequence, a low-discrepancy quasi-Monte Carlo sequence. Low-discrepancy means the points are more evenly spread over [0, 1) than random draws — *the same budget covers more ground*. Paired with arithmetic decoding, each point decodes independently into one sequence: no interference, fully parallel, perfectly reproducible.

Subtract DecompressionLM from everything that came before, and the differences are:

- Minus LAMA: *no templates needed* — you do not have to think up the questions
- Minus AutoPrompt: *no relation types pre-specified*
- Minus nucleus / beam: *no rich-get-richer* — high-frequency prefixes cannot crowd out the long tail. The paper's own words: "competitive decoding dynamics … create a rich-get-richer effect"
- Minus ordinary i.i.d. arithmetic sampling: adds *order-independent, seed-free* coverage guarantees

** Then they found something

They ran this machine over five quantization variants (including AWQ-4bit and GPTQ-Int4) and the results surprised even them:

- *AWQ-4bit caused concept coverage to increase by 30–170%* — the quantized model could actually think of more concepts than the baseline
- *GPTQ-Int4 caused coverage to collapse by 71–86%* — the majority of concepts vanished
- *Perplexity revealed none of this* — the two methods looked essentially the same on perplexity

On top of that, on the MMLU-Pro Law legal benchmark, the gap in hallucination rate between the highest-coverage and lowest-coverage models was 19.6 percentage points.

In other words: if you follow the old rules and pick a quantization method by perplexity, you may end up deploying a model that has had *half its knowledge hollowed out* in a legal-advice setting — and you would never know.

* What is really changing (the core)

On the surface this evolutionary line is "probing methods got better." Underneath it is *a generational shift in evaluation philosophy*.

Old paradigm: the model is an *examinee*. Researchers write questions; the model answers them. Correct answers score points; wrong answers lose them.

New paradigm: the model is a *specimen under dissection*. Researchers write no questions — they mount a parallel extractor on the model and let the model pour out its own contents according to its internal probability density. What pours out assembles into a graph, and the graph itself is the diagnostic report.

This shift could not happen earlier because it required two tools to mature simultaneously:
1. Quantization and compression proliferating everywhere, making "the model got silently damaged" a genuine, pressing problem
2. Arithmetic sampling — a *parallel, reproducible, coverage-controllable* decoding method — being invented

Only when both arrived together could DecompressionLM appear.

* Where does this go next

Two most likely directions:

1. *Concept coverage becomes a standard metric* — just like perplexity. Future quantized-model releases will report coverage scores. AWQ-style "protect salient weights" methods will attract deeper study: why are they so friendly to long-tail concepts? Is there a more refined way to protect even more of them?

2. *From seeing the damage to repairing the damage* — now that we can pinpoint which concepts GPTQ destroyed, the natural next step is: can we explicitly list those concepts in a protection set at quantization time? Knowledge-aware quantization may become the next buzzword.

Further out, the "query-free full-graph extraction" idea may spill into model distillation, training data selection, and even alignment evaluation — any scenario where you want to know "what exactly is this model carrying inside?"
