* The Problem River

Take a large language model with tens of billions of parameters and store every weight in only 2–3 bits — while keeping the model just as capable as before. That is what low-bit quantization sets out to do.

Where is the difficulty? Fewer bits means coarser representable values. A weight that was a 16-bit float could distinguish 65,536 different values; compressed to 3 bits, an entire group of weights has only 8 values to choose from. You must cram a large set of numbers into those 8 slots and, after cramming, keep the model's outputs as unchanged as possible.

Two tensions pull in opposite directions during that cramming:
- *Viewed within one group*: how do you pack this group's values as closely to the originals as possible? This is local optimality.
- *Viewed across the whole layer*: if one group is packed imperfectly, should another group shift a little to compensate? This is global optimality.

GPTQ solved the second problem in 2022 — it made that "compensation" step computationally feasible at scale. But it cut a corner on the first problem: when choosing the "grid spacing" (scale) for each group, it assumed the data was uniformly distributed and that groups were independent of one another. That corner-cutting left a gap of several accuracy percentage points. The story along this research line is: how to close that gap.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1990 | OBD: Second-Order Derivatives Tell You Which Weights Can Be Dropped

*** What Problem It Saw

In LeCun's era, neural networks had just been proved useful, but scaling up parameter counts made them unmanageable. "Downsizing" was needed — pruning weights to make networks leaner. The most naive approach ranked weights by absolute value and discarded the smallest. But this worked poorly, because a small-valued weight is not necessarily unimportant.

*** Its Solution

Borrow the Taylor expansion. If you change a weight from w to 0, how much does the loss rise? The first-order term is approximately zero at convergence; the second-order term (1/2) H w² dominates. So computing the diagonal of the Hessian H_{ii} reveals the cost of removing each weight. Remove those with low cost.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

For the first time, "importance" received a mathematical definition. But OBD made a large simplifying assumption: it treated the Hessian as diagonal, meaning "removing w_i does not affect any other weight." In real networks, weights are coupled; this assumption throws away significant information.

** 1993 | OBS: When You Remove One, Nudge the Others to Compensate

*** What Problem It Saw

OBD's diagonal-Hessian assumption does not hold in real networks. Hassibi calculated: when you remove a weight w_q, the remaining weights w_{-q} can collectively shift to cancel most of the loss incurred by that removal. OBD left this compensation opportunity on the table.

*** Its Solution

Retain the full Hessian H. The optimal compensation when removing w_q is:

#+begin_example
delta w = - (w_q / [H^-1]_{qq}) * H^-1 * e_q
#+end_example

That is, when one weight is removed, every other weight slides along a particular column of H^-1. The resulting loss increase becomes w_q² / (2 [H^-1]_{qq}) — more accurate than OBD's version.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

It packages "removal" and "compensation" into a single operation. The cost is that H^-1 must be computed explicitly. For networks with more than a few million parameters, H^-1 simply cannot be stored — which is why this approach went unused on large models for 30 years.

** 2022 | OBQ: Adapting the "Prune + Compensate" Loop for Quantization

*** What Problem It Saw

Once large models emerged in the 2020s, quantization — not pruning — became the primary battleground. You do not want to set weights to zero; you want to compress them from 16-bit floats to 3-bit integers. OBS was designed for pruning, and nobody had proved it could be adapted for quantization.

*** Its Solution

Frantar noticed that setting w_q to 0 and setting w_q to quant(w_q) are mathematically the same kind of operation — both "pin w_q to a fixed value, then let the others compensate." So OBS applies directly: at each step, pick the weight with the smallest quantization cost, quantize it, update all remaining weights. Repeat until every weight is quantized.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

For the first time, the OBS framework was applied to quantization. But it inherited OBS's fundamental bottleneck: every time a weight is quantized, H^-1 must be updated. A 1B-parameter model takes days; a 175B-parameter model is inconceivable.

** 2022 | GPTQ: Drop the Optimal Ordering, Quantize Column by Column

*** What Problem It Saw

OBQ's slowness stemmed from one step: at each iteration it had to "select the weight with the minimum cost." That selection required recomputing H^-1, causing complexity to explode.

*** Its Solution

Frantar et al. made a counterintuitive simplification: abandon the "optimal ordering" and quantize weights strictly in column order, left to right. Theoretically suboptimal, but in practice the accuracy loss is negligible — because large models have so many weights that statistical averaging washes out the difference.

Without the dynamic ordering, H^-1 no longer needs to be maintained incrementally; a single Cholesky decomposition can be computed once and reused throughout. Result: a 175B-parameter model quantizes in 4 hours. GPTQ also introduced *group-wise quantization* — every 128 weights form a group sharing one scale — simultaneously saving memory and preserving accuracy.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried a New Problem)

It turned OBQ from a "lab toy" into an "industry standard"; nearly every open-source LLM quantization tool is built on top of it.

*But* — and this is exactly where TSGO enters — when GPTQ selects the scale for each group, it applies a simplification: it assumes the Hessian for that group is the identity matrix I. In other words, it pretends "input data is uniformly distributed" and "this group has no correlation with any other group." This assumption makes scale computation trivial (just use the group's min/max), but it directly contradicts GPTQ's own objective — minimizing the whole-layer reconstruction error. That error inherently depends on the input statistics X and on inter-group coupling, so discarding both when choosing scales is self-defeating.

This contradiction sat unaddressed for three years, until 2026.

** 2026 | TSGO: Add One Pass Before and One After GPTQ to Recover What Was Lost

*** What Problem It Saw

GPTQ's two simplifications in scale selection are both wrong:
1. *Ignoring input statistics*: given the same weight distribution, if the corresponding input activation distribution differs, the optimal scale should differ too. GPTQ is blind to this.
2. *Ignoring inter-group correlations*: could the quantization error of group i be partially compensated by adjusting the scale of group j? GPTQ treats groups as independent and wastes that opportunity.

The consequence: at 2-bit and 3-bit low-precision, GPTQ leaves a clearly closeable gap between the quantized model and the original.

*** Its Solution

Rather than redoing GPTQ's integer weight selection, TSGO *only re-selects scales*, wrapping GPTQ in a two-stage procedure:

*Stage 1 (before GPTQ)*: compute an individual scale for each group with the objective of minimizing *that group's reconstruction error*. This uses the Hessian diagonal block H_{i,i}, which naturally brings the input statistics X_i back in. This step gives GPTQ a better initial scale than min/max.

*Stage 2 (after GPTQ)*: once GPTQ has fixed the integer weights, freeze the integers and *adjust only the scales*. Now the full Hessian (including off-diagonal blocks H_{i,j}) is used, so inter-group correlations enter the picture. Coordinate descent is applied — adjust one group's scale at a time while holding all others fixed. The key insight: the authors derive a *closed-form solution* for each such adjustment, requiring no inner iteration, so this stage adds almost no computational overhead.

*** Why It Worked (and Where It Buried New Problems)

It hits the blind spot GPTQ left untouched for three years: decoupling "scale selection" from "integer selection" and optimizing each for its proper objective. The two optimizations no longer interfere with each other.

Results:
- 2-bit Llama3-8B: 6 percentage-point improvement over GPTQ
- Llama3.2-1B-Instruct perplexity on C4: 429.4 → 149.5
- 3-bit: nearly matches the original FP model, dropping only ~2 percentage points

Remaining open problems: the method still operates within the "per-layer reconstruction error" framework. How quantization error propagates and accumulates across layers is not directly addressed by TSGO. That is precisely what the QEP line is working on.

* Frontier Extensions

** 2025 | QEP: Rewrite the Optimization Objective Itself, Rather Than Re-selecting Scales

QEP (Quantization Error Propagation) takes a different path. Instead of adjusting scales, it modifies GPTQ's optimization objective: quantization error produced by earlier layers is made explicit and propagated to later layers, which are then asked to compensate for it.

Comparing the two lines:
- TSGO: selects scales more accurately within each individual layer
- QEP: handles cross-layer error propagation more faithfully

The two are theoretically orthogonal and can be combined. The TSGO paper itself notes that the Stage 2 derivation can be extended to incorporate quantization error from preceding layers — which is the direction QEP also pursues.

* Insights

What is really happening along this line is not simply "quantization algorithms getting better" — it is *the repeated repayment of debts incurred by one-time approximations*:

1. *The cost of simplification is deferred, not avoided.* OBD assumes a diagonal Hessian — wrong. OBS corrects it, but the correction is too expensive for anyone to use. GPTQ simplifies along a different dimension (fixed ordering) to make it runnable, then introduces yet another simplification on scales (Hessian = I). Every generation of algorithms oscillates between "approximations made for speed" and "approximations removed for accuracy."

2. *The most valuable work is not inventing new frameworks but removing the compromises predecessors made for speed.* TSGO introduces no new mathematical concepts. What it does is replace the step in GPTQ that computes scales via min/max with a step that uses the actual objective function. This sounds obvious in retrospect — yet for three years nobody did it, because GPTQ was already "good enough."

3. *2-bit quantization is the true battleground for this research line.* At 4-bit or 8-bit, the gap between methods is small. Drop to 2 bits, and every neglected detail gets amplified. This signals where the next wave of work will come from: at 1.58-bit, ternary, and binary precision, more TSGO-style contributions will surface, each patching a previously ignored detail.

The most likely next step: merging *scale optimization* with *cross-layer error propagation* (the QEP line) into a single unified objective. The two lines are currently addressing separate blind spots, but the underlying mathematics — Hessian + coordinate descent — is the same. Their convergence is a matter of time.

* Takeaways

- *The gold mine in research is often the approximations predecessors made just to get the method to run.* When reading a widely-used paper, ask yourself: where does it invoke "assume X equals the identity matrix" or a similar device? That is almost certainly where the next improvement enters.
- *Improvements do not require disruptive new ideas.* All of TSGO is essentially removing one simplification from GPTQ. The two-stage framework had existed for a decade; coordinate descent dates to the 1950s. What is new is *placing them at the overlooked position*.
- *When studying a research line, do not read only the latest paper.* If you do not know why the OBQ → GPTQ transition required that simplification, you cannot understand why TSGO needs to undo it. Every paper is a repayment of some debt the previous one incurred.
