* The River of the Problem

A MoE large model packs dozens or even hundreds of "experts" into every layer, yet at inference time each token activates only a handful of them. Computation is fast, but storage requires loading all experts into GPU memory at once. If you prune away half the experts, is the model still usable?

That sounds like the old pruning problem. So where is the real difficulty? *How do you know which experts can be safely removed?* An expert is not a simple neuron; it is an entire feed-forward network encoding the model's preferences for certain kinds of input. Prune the wrong one and the model may suddenly fall apart on mathematics, or lose the ability to write code.

For the past several years the community has been doing the same thing: feed the model a batch of "representative sentences," observe which experts are rarely activated or contribute little when they are, and cut them. That sounds reasonable—until you ask what "representative sentences" actually means. Change the batch and the pruned outcome changes too. The field has gone deeper and deeper into increasingly elaborate methods, yet none of them escape a dependence on a *calibration set*.

The question AIMER sets out to answer is one sentence: *Can we identify the experts that should be pruned by looking only at the model weights themselves, with no data at all?*

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Sparsely-Gated MoE (Shazeer et al.): Decoupling "Scale" from "Cost"

*** What Problem It Saw

At the time everyone wanted larger models, but doubling parameters meant doubling compute on every forward pass. GPUs couldn't afford it.

*** Its Solution

Replace the feed-forward layer in a standard Transformer with a group of N FFNs called "experts." A small "router" decides which experts each token visits—typically top-2. The result: parameter count scales by N, but compute only doubles.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Set)

Intuitively sound—different tokens care about different things, so there is no need to run every token through all computation. But it buried a long-term trap: *at inference time you still have to load all N experts into GPU memory*, because you cannot know in advance which expert the next token will be routed to. Compute was saved; memory was not. That trap became, a decade later, the entire motivation for the expert-pruning research line.

** 2022 | Chen et al.: Pruning Experts Unused by a Specific Task

*** What Problem It Saw

When deploying a MoE model the memory pressure exceeds the compute pressure. A 7B-parameter MoE with 64 experts cannot even fit its weights on a consumer GPU. Someone had to fill the hole Shazeer left.

*** Its Solution

Target a downstream task (say, machine translation for a particular language pair), run the model on that task, count how often each expert is activated, and prune the low-frequency ones.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Set)

If you only care about one task, many experts genuinely sit idle—and pruning them works. But the new trap is large: *the pruned model can only handle that one task.* The same MoE model needs a separate pruned copy for translation, a separate one for dialogue, a separate one for code. The whole selling point of MoE is generality; this approach prunes the generality away.

** 2024 | NAEE (Lu et al.): The First Genuinely Task-Agnostic Pruning

*** What Problem It Saw

The Chen 2022 path is a dead end—in the large-model era, general-purpose capability is paramount and cannot be sacrificed for one task. Can we prune without binding to any task?

*** Its Solution

Change the scoring objective: for each expert, *measure how much the whole layer's output changes when that expert is removed.* Small change → prune. The difference is measured with Frobenius norm between the original output and the post-pruning output.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Set)

No longer tied to a specific task—more general in principle. But it still needs *some data*: you must feed a batch of general-purpose text to compute the output. That batch is the calibration set. The new trap follows immediately: *change the calibration batch and the pruning result changes.* The paper uses C4, but would Wikipedia work? What about GitHub code? Nobody can guarantee consistency.

** 2024–25 | Seer-MoE / EAN / REAP: Ever-More-Refined Scoring

*** What Problem It Saw

NAEE uses only one signal—output difference—yet MoE models carry much richer information: the router's weight for each expert, activation frequency, the norm of expert outputs. None of it was exploited.

*** Its Solution

- *Seer-MoE* (Muzio 2024): count router "hits"—how many times an expert is activated (hard count) or the cumulative router softmax probability (soft count).
- *EAN* (Jaiswal 2025): score by the expert activation norm. The authors compare 16 scoring methods and find EAN the strongest.
- *REAP* (Lasby 2025): combine router weights with activations—"router-weighted activation"—for a finer importance estimate.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Set)

Each paper adds one more signal on top of the previous one, and each gains a few points on benchmarks. But *they all share the same root problem: every one of them needs a calibration set.* AIMER presents the key evidence: even when you sample exclusively from C4, *just using different sub-samples*, the pruned models can differ by several percentage points on certain tasks. The entire research line is built on an unstable foundation.

** 2026 | AIMER (This Paper): No Data—Only Weights

*** What Problem It Saw

The preceding generation grew increasingly complex—routing signals, activation statistics, weighted combinations—yet as long as they needed a calibration set, their outputs remained unstable. Preprocessing cost also climbed: running REAP to completion takes hours.

A deeper question: *After a MoE model finishes training, is the "importance" of each expert already encoded in the weights?* If so, there is no need to run any data through the model.

*** Its Solution

Look only at the expert's own weights. Specifically, flatten the gate, up, and down projection matrices of an expert into a single long vector w and compute:

#+begin_example
        |w|_1
AIMER = -----------
        sqrt(N) * |w|_2
#+end_example

That is, *L1 norm divided by L2 norm* (with a normalization constant). This L1/L2 ratio is known in sparse signal processing as the *Hoyer measure*—it quantifies how "spread out" versus "concentrated" a vector is. Intuitively:

- An expert whose weights are spread across all dimensions has a high AIMER score.
- An expert whose weights are concentrated in a few dimensions (extreme case: a single non-zero entry) has a low AIMER score.

High AIMER → *weights diffuse and wide-ranging, so another expert is more likely to cover its role; safe to prune.*
Low AIMER → *weights concentrated with a clear specialization; irreplaceable; keep it.*

This judgment requires no data whatsoever. One scan of the weights is enough, reducing scoring time from hours to 0.22–1.27 seconds.

*** Why It Worked (and What Trap It Set)

The approach enters from a counter-intuitive angle: not "keep whoever is used most often" but rather *"keep whoever cannot be replaced by anyone else."* The former looks at usage frequency; the latter looks at the uniqueness of the weight fingerprint. The latter requires no data, because uniqueness is frozen into the weights the moment training ends.

The remaining gaps are also clear:

- Only validated on MoE; not extended to other sparse structures.
- Performs well at 25 % / 50 % pruning ratios—would more aggressive pruning (e.g., 75 %) cause collapse?
- The intuition "high AIMER = replaceable" lacks rigorous theoretical proof; it is empirical observation.
- The question of whether the retained experts are genuinely complementary to each other is left unanswered.

* Frontier Extensions

AIMER was posted in March 2026. As of this writing (2026-05) no explicit critique or successor has appeared. Two extension directions seem most likely:

1. *Combining pruning with expert merging*: The field currently runs two parallel lines—"prune" vs. "merge." MEO, MC-SMoE, and HC-SMoE are iterating quickly on the merging side. Could AIMER's score guide merging decisions—leave low-AIMER experts untouched, merge several high-AIMER experts into one?

2. *From static scoring to dynamic adaptation*: AIMER scores once and fixes the result. But different users in deployment have very different request distributions. Could AIMER serve as a "skeleton pruning" step, with certain experts dynamically activated or dormant based on live traffic?

* Insights

Reading through this line of work reveals a deeper shift.

*On the surface*, this is a history of methodological iteration in MoE expert pruning: from frequency scoring to output difference, to multi-signal fusion, to weight-intrinsic metrics—each generation a little more refined than the last.

*But underneath lies a cognitive reversal.* The first three generations all assumed: "We must infer importance from the model's runtime behavior"—hence the need to feed data, observe activations, and track routing. That assumption sounds self-evident. AIMER overturns it directly: *importance is written into the weights during training; runtime behavior merely expresses it. If the original text is already written, why read a translation?*

This has the flavor of *"the information is already in the system; we were just looking at it the wrong way"*—the same class of insight as when the Hoyer measure revealed in signal processing that sparsity can be expressed as an l1/l2 ratio. An old tool, transplanted into a new context, solves a new problem across a gap of twenty years.

The most likely next steps are not in "refining the scoring formula by another 1 %" but in two directions:

- *Theory*: Why does the l1/l2 ratio predict replaceability? Is there a mathematical grounding? It may generalize to pruning Transformer attention heads, LoRA modules, and similar componentized structures.
- *Dynamization*: Replace "prune once, then deploy" with "prune a skeleton first, then adapt dynamically to traffic," making zero-data scoring like AIMER a component of a larger live system.

* Takeaways

This research line offers two especially clear lessons about how to find problems:

1. *When everyone in a field is optimizing the same component, examine the assumption they all share.* Seer / EAN / REAP were competing over "whose scoring formula is more refined." AIMER did not join that race; instead it asked: "Is the shared premise—that you need a calibration set at all—actually sound?" When a common assumption falls, an entire competitive track reopens.

2. *An old tool from a neighboring field is often the best answer to a new problem.* The Hoyer measure appeared in signal processing in 2004 and spent twenty years creating no waves in deep learning. AIMER's core innovation was not inventing new mathematics; it was recognizing that "expert pruning" and "sparse signal measurement" are instances of the same problem. Next time you are stuck, ask whether anyone in a neighboring field has already solved something with the same shape.

Practical takeaway: *When compressing any "componentized" model (MoE, LoRA, Adapter pools), consider using a weight-intrinsic metric like AIMER for a zero-data first pass, and reserve the expensive calibration-set pipeline for the genuinely ambiguous boundary cases.*
