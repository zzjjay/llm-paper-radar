* The Problem River

Once a large model is trained, it needs to fit into all kinds of hardware: a server might have 80 GB of VRAM to spare, a phone is left with only 4 GB, and edge devices have even less. Each hardware target tolerates a different "precision" — more memory means more bits, less memory means heavier compression.

The most direct approach: compress a separate model for each precision level. One copy at 2-bit, one at 3-bit, one at 4-bit — three copies in total. The problem is that compressing a 70B model can take weeks on a single GPU, and three copies require three times the disk space.

So the real question surfaces: *Can we train and store just one model, and let the user dial in whatever bitwidth they need at deployment time?*

Why is this hard? Compression is inherently a "one-shot deal" — a codebook optimized for 2-bit will collapse if you try to use it at 4-bit, or conversely chop it down to 1-bit, because the optimization targeted only a single precision with no regard for whether "half of it" remains usable. To make one model perform well across multiple precisions simultaneously, the compression itself must be structured in layers — more bits yields higher accuracy, fewer bits doesn't cause a crash. This is precisely the hard bone this river has been gnawing at all along.

* Evolutionary Narrative

This river has two tributaries flowing independently before converging in this paper. One is "how to compress weights to the extreme" (GPTQ → QuIP# → AQLM); the other is "how to make compression layered and scalable" (successive refinement theory + Matryoshka). We follow the main current first, then bring in the tributaries.

** 1991 | Equitz & Cover: Sketch First, Add Detail Later — Does It Cost Extra?

*** What Problem Did It See?

This is an old information-theory problem, but it connects directly to today's work. Suppose you want to transmit data to a receiver: first you send a coarse version (say, "this number is roughly positive or negative"), and later you send refinements to make it more precise. A natural worry: does sending in two rounds cost more bits than transmitting the precise version all at once? In other words, does "coarse first, fine later" incur an installment-plan surcharge?

*** Its Solution

Equitz and Cover gave a clean criterion: if and only if the optimal solutions at each precision level can be chained into a Markov chain, staged transmission costs nothing extra — they called this "successively refinable." They also proved a key special case: *Gaussian-distributed data under mean-squared error is successively refinable.*

An analogy: drawing a sketch — first the outline, then shading, then fine detail. If every step produces the best possible drawing at that level of detail, you have wasted not a single stroke. Gaussian sources have exactly this nice property.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left Behind)

This gives "one model, multiple precisions" a theoretical safety net: *as long as LLM weights approximately follow a Gaussian distribution, "compress a little" and "compress hard" can be seamlessly linked — adding bits is never wasted.* This is the foundation this paper cites repeatedly.

But in 1991 nobody was compressing neural networks. This is an existence theorem — it says "it can be done" without saying "how to do it on a real 16-bit codebook." The pit was left for people thirty years later: the theoretical Gaussian source is beautiful, but what about those outlier values in real weights?

** 2022 | GPTQ: Compress a 175B Behemoth in a Few Hours

*** What Problem Did It See?

Jump to the era of large language models. A prior method called OBQ had a classic approach (derived from the even older Optimal Brain Surgeon pruning): quantize weight by weight, and after each quantization use second-order information (the Hessian) to fine-tune the remaining weights and compensate for error. It worked well on small models. But the GPTQ authors did the math: compressing GPT-175B with OBQ would take roughly half a year. Completely infeasible.

*** Its Solution

GPTQ applied three engineering "unlocks." First, OBQ insists on greedily choosing the next weight to quantize by error magnitude; GPTQ found that *for large models the order simply doesn't matter* — quantizing all rows in one fixed sequence works just as well, eliminating a mountain of redundant computation. Second, lazy batch updates: accumulate a batch of columns and process them together to saturate the GPU. Third, Cholesky decomposition to handle numerical instability at scale.

Put these three together: take OBQ's "carefully pick, individually compensate" process and replace it with "fixed order, batch compensation, numerically stable," and you get GPTQ — capable of compressing a 175B model in a few hours.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left Behind)

For the first time, a 175B model could fit on a single GPU for inference at 4-bit with nearly no quality loss. But it is *scalar quantization* — each number compressed individually. That is also its ceiling: at 2-bit, scalar quantization lacks the "shape freedom" to fit the true weight distribution, and the model simply collapses. Pushing further down demands a different approach that quantizes a group of numbers at once. That pressure produced the next paper.

** 2024 | QuIP#: Smooth Weights Into a Sphere, Then Fit a Lattice Around It

*** What Problem Did It See?

QuIP# zeroed in on the root cause of GPTQ-style scalar methods collapsing at 2-bit: weights contain outliers, their distribution is skewed and irregular, and scalar quantization cannot handle that.

*** Its Solution

Two steps. Step one, "incoherence processing": multiply the weight matrix by a random Hadamard transform, which miraculously *smooths the skewed, outlier-ridden distribution into an approximately Gaussian, spherical distribution* — note that this step turns the weights into exactly the kind of Gaussian source that the 1991 theory loves. Step two: now that the distribution is spherical, use *vector quantization* — no longer compressing one number at a time, but instead quantizing groups of 8 numbers jointly onto a carefully designed E8 lattice codebook. E8 is the optimal sphere-packing lattice in 8 dimensions and can be fit into an L1 cache on hardware.

Viewed as a transformation from GPTQ: replace "quantize each number individually" with "quantize groups of 8 to a lattice," and prepend a "Hadamard smoothing" step — that gives you QuIP#. It made 2-bit models usable for the first time, even producing the curious result that a 3-bit model can be more cost-effective than theoretically lossless 4-bit.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left Behind)

Vector quantization codebooks can capture the joint distribution of a group of values, using information far more efficiently than scalar methods. But QuIP# uses a *fixed E8 lattice* — mathematically predetermined, not following the specific weight data at hand. A natural follow-up question emerged: if the codebook is not a fixed lattice but instead learned from the data, could it be more accurate?

** 2024 | AQLM: Don't Fix the Codebook — Let It Grow from the Data

*** What Problem Did It See?

AQLM looked at QuIP#'s fixed E8 lattice and saw a missed opportunity for data adaptation. It borrowed an old technique from information retrieval — Additive Quantization.

*** Its Solution

Instead of one fixed lattice, use *several learned codebooks*, each contributing one codeword; *sum* those codewords to approximate the original weight. For example: weight ≈ codeword from codebook 1 + codeword from codebook 2 + codeword from codebook 3. All codebooks are jointly trained under a data-aware objective: minimize the layer's output error.

Viewed as a transformation from QuIP#: replace the fixed E8 lattice with "a sum of codewords from several learnable codebooks," and train them using output error on real data — that gives you AQLM. It set the state of the art at 2-bit at the time.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left Behind)

Learned codebooks can precisely fit the weight distribution of each layer, more efficiently than a universal lattice. But AQLM has a critical "static" flaw — precisely the point this paper attacks: *its codebook count is fixed and all codebooks are jointly optimized together, with no inherent hierarchy or ordering.* A model trained with 4 codebooks (roughly ~4-bit) will suffer severe quality collapse if at inference time you want to save memory by dropping 2 codebooks to reach ~2-bit — because those codebooks were never trained to be useful on their own as a prefix. One model, one precision. To change precision, go back and retrain.

This brings us back to the opening problem, and draws in the second tributary.

** 2022 | Matryoshka (Parallel Tributary): One Representation, Truncate at Will

*** What Problem Did It See?

This tributary comes from representation learning and has no direct connection to quantization, but its insight is the key. Kusupati et al. faced this problem: when training embeddings, the downstream task's required dimensionality is unknown in advance — a fixed dimension is either too large (wasteful) or too small (insufficient quality).

*** Its Solution

Like Russian nesting dolls. Train a d-dimensional embedding, but simultaneously supervise its first d/2, d/4, d/8, … prefixes in the loss function, requiring each prefix to be a good representation on its own. The result: dimensions are sorted into "importance order" — the first dimension matters most, the first two together next, and so on. At inference you take however long a prefix you need, no retraining required.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left Behind)

The key insight is: *scalability is not free — it must be explicitly supervised at every granularity level during training.* You have to tell the model "half of it must still be good" in the loss, and only then will it actually sort information by importance. Matryoshka did this for embedding dimensions — but nobody had done it for quantization codebooks.

** 2026 | Drop-by-Drop: Teach Codebooks to Nest, Drop Them One by One at Inference

*** What Problem Did It See?

This paper simultaneously targeted the gap on both sides: AQLM's additive codebooks are powerful but "static" (drop one and quality crashes), while Matryoshka's "scalability" idea had never been applied to quantization codebooks. It identifies in one sentence the shared pathology of AQLM and residual methods like QuIP#/VPTQ: they "jointly optimize all components end-to-end without ever supervising the base level alone," so dropping later levels at inference causes severe degradation. One model still serves only one precision.

*** Its Solution

Twist three things into one:

1. *AQLM's additive codebooks* as the skeleton — weights are still represented as sums of codewords.
2. *Matryoshka-style supervision* transplanted into the quantization loss — instead of only making "the sum of all codebooks" accurate, require that "the first 1 codebook," "the first 2 codebooks," "the first 3 codebooks," … each *partial sum* is itself a good approximation. The loss aggregates the distortion across all levels and optimizes them jointly.
3. *Successive refinement theory* as theoretical grounding — since Hadamard smoothing makes weights approximately Gaussian, and Gaussian sources are successively refinable, adding codebooks one level at a time is theoretically lossless (the paper proves that a Gaussian source is successively refinable under a weighted MSE objective).

Viewed as a transformation from AQLM: keep the additive codebooks, change the training objective from "only make the full sum optimal" to "make every prefix sum optimal" (the Matryoshka move), and use successive refinement theory to guarantee this is not wasteful — and you get Drop-by-Drop. At inference, a user tight on memory can *drop codebooks one by one (drop-by-drop)* from the end, sliding smoothly from high precision to low precision, all from *the same single trained model.*

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Left Behind)

It transforms "multiple precisions" from "store multiple models" into "one model + drop codebooks on the fly." The core difference from AQLM in one sentence: *AQLM's codebooks are an unordered bundle; Drop-by-Drop's codebooks are sorted by importance, so it is safe to trim from the tail.* On Qwen, LLaMA, Gemma, and Mistral, a single checkpoint maintains competitive perplexity and accuracy across multiple bitwidths.

What pits remain? The entire line is built on *vector / additive quantization*. And right next door, there are already people questioning the ceiling of vector quantization as a foundation.

* Frontier Extension

** 2024 | QTIP: Maybe Vector Quantization Itself Has a Hard Ceiling

QTIP shares the same roots as Drop-by-Drop (both follow the QuIP lineage, both use Hadamard smoothing into Gaussian), yet poses a more fundamental challenge: *vector quantization (including AQLM's additive codebooks) is inherently dimensionality-limited* — codebook size grows exponentially with dimension, so all existing VQ methods are stuck at ≤8 dimensions and quantization quality is locked by that constraint.

QTIP instead uses *trellis coded quantization*: a stateful decoder decouples codebook size, bit rate, and effective dimension, so cost grows *linearly* with dimension, enabling extremely high effective dimensions. The result: QTIP surpasses QuIP# and AQLM at every bit rate; "untuned QTIP matches fine-tuned AQLM."

This creates an interesting tension: *Drop-by-Drop pushes additive quantization to its limit in the direction of "one model, multiple precisions," while QTIP questions the additive quantization foundation itself.* One optimizes for scalability, the other for single-point quality. The most natural next question: can Drop-by-Drop's "successive refinement + Matryoshka supervision" be grafted directly onto trellis quantization, achieving both scalability and QTIP's high-dimensional quality? As of now, no one has publicly taken that step.

* Insights

On the surface this line looks like a technical iteration of "compress to ever-lower bitrates." What is actually happening underneath is a *shift in the quantization objective from "optimal at a single point" to "optimal across an entire curve."*

- Three generations — GPTQ, QuIP#, AQLM — all answered the same question: "At *this one* given bit rate, how do we compress most accurately?" Their codebooks, lattices, and additive structures all serve a single precision.
- Drop-by-Drop changed the question: "How do we compress to produce *an entire* precision-quality curve, letting users pick any point on it?"

This shift is isomorphic to what Matryoshka did for embedding dimensions. At its core it is the same cognitive upgrade: *faced with an unknown deployment environment, rather than betting on one precision, train a model with an inherently ordered, on-the-fly-truncatable structure.* To achieve this, training must explicitly pay the supervisory cost for "what it will look like after being trimmed" — scalability is never a free byproduct; it must be written into the loss and explicitly demanded.

At a deeper level, information theory (successive refinement), representation learning (nesting), and systems compression (additive codebooks) — three fields that had nothing to do with each other — were forced to converge by the practical requirement of "one model, multiple precisions." This paper's contribution is less the invention of a new operator and more *the recognition that these three fields are saying the same thing*, and then welding them together.

Where does this go next? Two directions are pulling in opposite directions: one is grafting this "scalability" approach onto a stronger foundation (such as QTIP's trellis), and the other is moving from the discrete steps of "dropping codebooks" toward more continuous precision adjustment. Whoever first reunites "scalability" and "single-point optimality" — currently two diverging paths — will claim the next bend in this river.

* Takeaways

For "how to do research, how to find problems," this line offers three concrete hints:

1. *Changing the question beats changing the method.* Three generations competed within the same "compress a single point more accurately" frame. This paper did not invent a more accurate compression operator — it simply changed the question from "one point" to "one curve," and immediately opened a new space. When a direction reaches diminishing returns, step back and ask: "Am I answering a question that is assumed by default but could actually be replaced?"

2. *Cross-domain isomorphisms are gold.* Successive refinement (information theory, 1991), Matryoshka (representation learning, 2022), and additive codebooks (quantization systems) were three separate bodies of work. The paper's real insight was seeing that they are all saying the same thing. The practical takeaway: *when facing a hard problem, look in neighboring fields for whether someone has already solved a structurally identical problem — the answer often already exists, just under a different name.*

3. *If you want a property, ask for it explicitly in the training objective.* Both Matryoshka and this paper reveal that scalability does not emerge on its own — you must add a dedicated supervision term to the loss for "what it looks like after being trimmed." This principle can be applied in reverse as a design tool: *first write down the deployment form you ultimately want (truncatable, composable, early-stoppable, …), then work backward to figure out which term to add to the loss function.*
