* The Problem River

Push neural-network multiplications down to 4 bits and hardware flies — but how do you keep accuracy from collapsing?

This tension has been struggling in a gap for forty years: floating-point offers wide dynamic range but is slow; integers are fast but too narrow; a single outlier in the activations can destroy an entire block. Each generation searches for a new middle form — one that satisfies hardware's appetite for regular structure while meeting mathematics' need for dynamic range. By 2026, hardware has grown FP4 Tensor Cores capable of multiplying sixteen 4-bit numbers in a single instruction. The question has finally shifted from "which format?" to "now that the format is settled, are we actually squeezing the most out of it?"

ScaleSearch takes a step back and notices: we are not. The entire industry's approach to NVFP4 is equivalent to buying a manual-transmission sports car and driving it exclusively in first gear.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2016-2018 | Fixed-point: integers are fastest, but too narrow

*** What problem it saw

Models trained in FP32 require exponent alignment at every multiplication during deployment — transistors are spent entirely on this bookkeeping. Mobile and edge chips simply cannot afford it. Jacob et al. (CVPR 2018) made a clean call: no floating-point at inference; go full INT8, feed it back through quantization-aware training (QAT), and let the weights adapt to the narrow range in advance.

*** Its solution

Multiply the entire weight tensor by a single global scale and bucket the result into INT8 [-128, 127]. Multiplications become pure integer multiply-accumulate (MAC) operations; hardware density soars.

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it left)

CNN weight distributions are relatively concentrated, so INT8 suffices. But the moment a single "outlier" large value appears, it drags the scale of the entire tensor upward, squashing most small values into just a few buckets — effectively rounding them to zero. Transformers exposed this pathology immediately: a few attention-sink channels in the activations can be 100× larger than their neighbors, and INT8 collapses entirely.

The necessary next step: don't let one outlier penalize a whole tensor. Slice the tensor into small blocks and give each block its own scale.

** 2018 | HBFP (Drumond et al., NeurIPS): shared block exponent as the rescue

*** What problem it saw

Pure fixed-point cannot train (the dynamic range of DNN gradients spans ten orders of magnitude); pure floating-point is too expensive. The previous generation's fixed-point scheme was tensor-level scale — one outlier polluted the entire tensor.

*** Its solution

Borrowing an old DSP trick from the 1980s: Block Floating Point. Cut a vector into blocks of a few dozen elements; elements within a block share one exponent while each retains its own mantissa. Intra-block multiplications can then use pure integer MAC (the exponent has been hoisted to block level), and blocks do not affect each other — an outlier in one block only contaminates that block.

A cleverer move: use BFP for matrix multiplications but keep FP32 for other operators. In dot products the large values dominate anyway and small ones are washed out; but pointwise operators like activation functions and softmax cannot tolerate precision loss.

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it left)

It decoupled "dynamic range" from "intra-block precision," two things that had been entangled. FPGA prototypes showed arithmetic density approaching pure fixed-point and accuracy approaching FP32 — having it both ways.

Remaining gap: this was an academic demo on small models. Block size selection, scale computation — none of it was standardized. If every vendor did its own thing, the software stack would splinter.

** 2020 | MSFP (Darvish Rouhani et al., NeurIPS): pulling BFP into cloud production

*** What problem it saw

HBFP was an academic prototype; no cloud vendor dared to deploy it in production — because production models come in hundreds of varieties (CNN/RNN/Transformer all need to work), and they must hold precision at Azure-scale throughput.

*** Its solution

Microsoft Floating Point. The basic structure is BFP, but three things were added: (1) precision audits across a large set of real models — MSFP12 matches INT8 and MSFP16 matches BF16, at one-third to one-quarter the cost; (2) co-design with the in-house FPGA accelerator (Brainwave), which natively consumes MSFP; (3) integration into the production inference pipeline without changing model topology.

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it left)

It proved BFP is viable at production scale, silencing the "BFP is just an academic toy" skepticism.

But MSFP was a Microsoft dialect; AMD, Intel, and NVIDIA would not adopt it verbatim. The necessary next step: a cross-vendor standard, otherwise the PyTorch/CUDA layer remains permanently fragmented.

** 2023 | OCP MX Standard / MXFP4 (Rouhani et al.): cross-vendor unification

*** What problem it saw

By 2023, MSFP, AMD's BF8, Intel's FP8, and various other variants were everywhere. Export a model and switch to a different GPU — it breaks. Training frameworks had to write a separate kernel for each format.

*** Its solution

AMD, Arm, Intel, Meta, Microsoft, NVIDIA, and Qualcomm sat down under the Open Compute Project (OCP) framework and hammered out the MX (Microscaling) standard. Specification: elements use E2M1 (FP4), E3M2 (FP6), E4M3 (FP8), etc.; every 32 elements form a block sharing an E8M0 scale. E8M0 = pure exponent, all 8 bits are exponent (power of 2).

This is MXFP4: 32 FP4 elements + one power-of-2 shared exponent.

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it left)

The standard unified the ecosystem; the software stack could finally interoperate across vendors. MXFP4 achieves near-INT8 accuracy on LLM inference at half the bit-width.

Remaining gap: the block is large (32 elements), and scale granularity is coarse (only powers of 2 — jumps of 1/2 → 1 → 2). For intermediate tensors with large dynamic range such as attention scores, those jumps are a direct source of MSE.

** 2025 | NVFP4 (NVIDIA Blackwell, Alvarez et al.): upgrading scale from "coarse sieve" to "fine sieve"

*** What problem it saw

MXFP4's scale is exponent-only — too coarse. Given a block whose maximum value is 5.7: a scale of 1 is too small (elements overflow), a scale of 2 is too large (wastes dynamic range), and there is no way to choose something like 1.5 or 1.7. This roughness costs several PPL points on LLMs.

*** Its solution

NVFP4 modifies MXFP4 in two places:
1. Block size shrinks from 32 to 16 (finer granularity; outliers contaminate a smaller region).
2. Scale upgrades from E8M0 (pure exponent) to E4M3 (4-bit exponent + 3-bit mantissa) — still 8 bits but far more expressive.

E4M3 can represent 1.0, 1.125, 1.25, 1.375, 1.5, and so on — no longer restricted to powers of 2. On the hardware side: Blackwell B200/B300 adds dedicated NVFP4 Tensor Cores with FP4 throughput 2–3× that of FP8.

Average cost per element: 4.5 bits (16 × 4-bit elements + 8-bit scale, amortized over 16 elements).

*** Why it worked (and what new pit it left)

Accuracy approaches FP8; throughput is 2× FP8. Direct NVFP4 weight + activation quantization of LLMs barely loses accuracy, and it is training-free.

But — the default rounding algorithm shipped by NVIDIA (copied verbatim by vLLM and TensorRT) is:

#+begin_example
scale = round_E4M3( max(|x|) / 6 )
# Round max absolute value divided by 6 (the max FP4-representable number)
# to the nearest E4M3 value
#+end_example

This means: scale always lands on *one* discrete E4M3 point near the max-scale. Yet E4M3 actually offers roughly 8 more legal scale values in that neighborhood — all of them ignored.

This gap is exactly where ScaleSearch begins.

** 2026.05 | ScaleSearch (Gupta et al., this paper): treat the scale as a hyperparameter and search it

*** What problem it saw

Restating the previous point: NVFP4 gives you an E4M3 scale format with a mantissa, which effectively opens up a dense discrete neighborhood of legal scales around the max-scale. But the industry's rounding code takes only the nearest neighbor — computes the default scale and stops.

The authors ran a direct experiment: quantize a standard Gaussian tensor with the max-scale; MSE = 0.0990. Exhaustively search the best scale among all 256 neighboring E4M3 values; MSE = 0.0066. A 93% reduction.

What does this mean? It is exactly the image of a manual-transmission sports car driven exclusively in first gear.

*** Its solution

The ScaleSearch algorithm reduces to one sentence:

#+begin_example
Take the max-scale s as the starting point; add an integer offset f
to its 8-bit integer representation (f ranges from -2 to +6, giving 9 values);
compute MSE for each offset; keep the one with minimum MSE.
#+end_example

Why -2 to +6, exactly 9 values? The authors plot an "offset histogram":
on the key tensors of real LLMs, the optimal offset follows a bimodal distribution —
one peak near 0 (the block maximum maps naturally to FP4's ±6),
another peak at 4–5 (the block maximum is better represented by FP4's ±4,
so enlarging the scale by ~1.5× is preferable).

There is no third peak, because ±3 is never better than ±6 (halving the scale).
Searching 9 values thus captures 99% of the available gain.

Side product — *ScaleSearchAttention*: ScaleSearch applied to the Q, K, V, and P matrices of FlashAttention, combined with two additional patches:
- *Incoherence Processing* (from QuIP/QuIP#): Hadamard transform to scatter outliers across all dimensions;
- *Attention sink-aware mixed precision* (from StreamingLLM): the first few tokens and the most recent few tokens are kept in FP16; the bulk of the sequence is quantized to NVFP4.

Result: end-to-end NVFP4 attention + KV cache on Llama 3.1 70B achieves Wikitext-2 PPL only 0.04 higher than FP16.

*** Why it works (and what new pit it leaves)

It works because this is a *training-free, format-preserving, hardware-agnostic* pure algorithmic patch. Add a 9-iteration loop inside the quantization kernel; quantization time increases by 1.74×; inference time is unchanged (the searched scales are computed once and cached). Every existing NVFP4 deployment can adopt this directly.

Remaining gaps:
- The 9 offsets are empirically chosen; there is no theoretical guarantee of optimality.
- For MXFP4's E8M0 scale (no mantissa), ScaleSearch yields almost no benefit — the authors acknowledge this honestly.
- Can the search be extended to training? Would the search operator disrupt backpropagation? The paper does not address this.

* Frontier Extensions

This paper is too new (May 2026) to have direct follow-on work. But several parallel and adjacent lines in the same time window are worth noting:

- *SageAttention3* (Zhang et al., 2025): the first work to apply FP4 to attention, using default max-scale. ScaleSearchAttention takes it as the baseline and beats it by 14 points (Mochi VQA-t), demonstrating that SageAttention3's accuracy loss comes primarily from a suboptimal scale choice rather than the format itself.
- *MX+* (Lee et al., MICRO 2025): a different improvement line — since an outlier already dominates the shared scale, the element-level exponent bits become redundant and can be repurposed as extra mantissa bits. Orthogonal to ScaleSearch and theoretically stackable.
- *NVFP4 training* (Wang 2025, Tseng 2025, Chmiel 2025): pushing FP4 to the training side — a different extension from ScaleSearch, which deepens inference-time quantization while training-side work advances the front line into training.
- *AMXFP4* (ACL 2025): asymmetric FP4 for handling activation outliers — yet another orthogonal direction.

A reasonable prediction: over the next 6–12 months, someone will integrate ScaleSearch + MX+ + asymmetry + training-side work, while also pushing the 9-offset search from an empirical heuristic toward a theoretically optimal result — most likely borrowing geometric tools from vector quantization.

* Insight

Reading this entire line, what I see is not "formats growing ever more complex" — it is a cycle of trial and error over *where to place the degrees of freedom in numerical precision*.

- Float places freedom at the *per-element* level (each element carries its own exponent); hardware cannot afford it.
- INT8 strips all freedom away (one scale for the whole tensor); outliers destroy everything.
- BFP/MX find the middle ground: freedom at the *block* level.
- ScaleSearch goes further: even at the block level, degrees of freedom need not be "computed" — they can be "searched."

At a deeper level: throughout the entire history of BFP, the scale has always been treated as a *byproduct of the data* — whatever the max is, the scale follows. ScaleSearch is the first work to treat the scale as an *independently optimizable variable*. This mirrors the cognitive shift from hand-tuning hyperparameters to grid search to NAS: taking something that seemed to be "naturally derived" and reframing it as a search space.

Where is this line most likely to go next? Three directions:

1. *Amortizing scale search into training*: treat the choice among 9 offsets as a learnable discrete variable, backpropagate through it during training using STE (straight-through estimator), and simply read from cache at deployment.
2. *Joint optimization across blocks*: ScaleSearch currently treats each block independently, but blocks along the same row or channel may be correlated; a joint search could yield better results.
3. *Discrete variables beyond the scale*: block size, scale format (E4M3 vs. E5M2), rounding mode (RNE vs. RTZ) — all currently hardcoded, but equally amenable to inclusion in a search space.

* Takeaways

The biggest takeaway: *do not trust that default values have been rigorously justified*.

NVIDIA's default rounding algorithm was written by an engineer on intuition, copied wholesale into vLLM and TensorRT, and adopted industry-wide — yet it discards more than half the benefit of the E4M3 mantissa. An eight-character `for f in -2..+6` loop reduces quantization error by 26%.

This means you can:
- Take any "industry-standard implementation," stare at its default values first, and ask: were these constants chosen by intuition, or did someone actually sweep the parameter space?
- Any closed-form formula of the shape "derived parameter = f(data)" is worth rewriting as "search for the optimal solution in the neighborhood of f(data)." Compute is cheap; the cost of searching is far lower than the accuracy gain.
- The most valuable part of a paper or product is often not a newly invented operator, but the discovery of *how much existing freedom is being wasted*.

Second takeaway: *read hardware format specifications backward*. NVFP4's E4M3 scale was not designed for ScaleSearch — it was added by NVIDIA as a side effect of wanting a bit more dynamic range. But that side effect conceals an entirely unmined search space. The next time you encounter any new hardware format (FP6, INT4 with shared scale, the next generation after Hopper/Blackwell), the first thing to look at should not be throughput — it should be *what degrees of freedom the default values silently discard*.
