* TL;DR — What Problem This Thread Is Solving

Distilling a capable reasoning teacher into a smaller student sounds straightforward — collect the teacher's step-by-step solutions, fine-tune the student on them, done.

But practitioners quickly discovered a paradox: the stronger the teacher, the worse the student turns out. A 671B DeepSeek-R1 fails to teach a 3B Qwen, while a 32B QwQ teaches it just fine.

This river is about one question: *what kinds of reasoning trajectories actually help a specific student learn to reason?*

RSR (Rank-Surprisal Ratio) gives a one-line formula answer: select trajectories where each token has a low probability but a high rank from the student's perspective.

* Evolutionary Narrative — How the Problem Grew, Step by Step

** Stop 1: CoT — Reasoning Can "Emerge", but Teaching Is Another Matter

In 2022, Wei et al. showed that prompting a large model to write out its reasoning before giving an answer dramatically improves accuracy. That is chain-of-thought.

At that point, the community focused on: *how do we get models to think longer and deeper on their own?* OpenAI's o1 and DeepSeek-R1 pushed this to the extreme — using RL to train models to spontaneously produce thousands of tokens of extended reasoning chains, self-verifying and self-correcting throughout.

Large models could now reason. The next question surfaced immediately: *these models are expensive to serve — can we distill their reasoning ability into a smaller model?*

The most direct approach: let DeepSeek-R1 solve a set of problems, then use those long CoT outputs as SFT data for a small student. This is reasoning distillation.

** Stop 2: The First Surprise — Stronger Teachers Fail to Teach

Common sense says a stronger teacher produces a stronger student. But starting in 2025, several papers found the opposite: *a 671B R1 teaching a 3B student leaves that student weaker than if a mid-sized 32B teacher had been used instead*.

Table 1 of this paper quantifies the phenomenon starkly. Same student, Qwen-2.5-3B:
- Taught by DeepSeek-R1 (671B) → 29.6
- Taught by QwQ-32B → 33.0
- Taught by Qwen-3-8B → 34.2

The 671B giant loses to an 8B mid-size model. This is not noise; it is a systematic effect.

Why? The community gradually understood: *the teacher's solutions are too sophisticated for the student to absorb*. R1's reasoning chains are full of turns the student cannot predict — from the student's perspective, those tokens have near-zero probability. Training on such data is like asking an elementary-school pupil to memorize a doctoral dissertation: the pupil might reproduce the words but never internalizes the reasoning process.

The realization: *the issue is not how strong the teacher is, but whether the data fits this particular student*. Data–student suitability became the new central concept.

** Stop 3: First-Generation Fix — Select by "How Much the Student Likes It"

If data must fit the student, the most intuitive approach is: *let the student score the data itself*. Compute the student model's likelihood (or equivalently, surprisal = −log p) on each trajectory; high-likelihood trajectories are ones the student is already familiar with; select those.

Representative works:
- Zhang et al. 2024: select trajectories by average log-probability
- Just et al. 2025: use log-probability in local context as local surprisal

This approach immediately hits a counter-intuitive wall, exposed clearly in Table 2 of this paper.

Sorting by Avg-Surprisal, the trajectories that best "match" the student are those from Nemotron-Super and Magistral-Small (lowest surprisal: 0.60 and 0.55). Yet training the student on those yields only 48.3 and 47.6 accuracy.

Meanwhile, Qwen-3-8B trajectories with middling surprisal (0.65) produce the best student — 52.0.

The reason is simple: *re-teaching what the student already knows teaches nothing*. Selecting high-likelihood data means selecting trajectories full of tokens the student can predict with eyes closed — there is no new information. This is the absence of *informativeness*.

But the opposite extreme also fails: training on the most opaque trajectories (GPT-OSS-20B, surprisal 1.36) drops accuracy to 42.7. The student is completely lost.

The tension is now sharp: *we need data the student can't predict (informative) yet can still follow (aligned) — and those two requirements look like antonyms*.

** Stop 4: Second-Generation Fix — More Elaborate Student-Specific Metrics

This tension pushed researchers toward more sophisticated tools. Two main lines appeared:
- G-Norm, GRACE (Park 2025): compute the gradient norm each trajectory induces on the student's parameters — how much would training on this data move the model?
- Influence Score (Liu 2025): estimate each trajectory's effect on validation-set loss.

Both approaches have the student model actively evaluate each candidate. The problem: *computing gradients is expensive* — every candidate trajectory requires a backward pass. And correlation with actual downstream quality reaches only about 0.5 (Table 4 of this paper: G-Norm 0.55, GRACE 0.59).

The gap was now clear: *we need a metric that is cheap (one forward pass), accurate (correlation well above 0.5), and simultaneously captures both informativeness and alignment*.

** Stop 5: This Paper — Translating the Tension into "Absolutely Unfamiliar + Relatively Sensible"

The key insight of RSR is not the formula — it is a conceptual move:

informativeness and alignment only look like contradictions because we measure them with one blunt instrument (probability). Switch the instrument and the contradiction dissolves.

Split "how familiar is this token to the student" into two independent dimensions:

- *Absolute unfamiliarity* = surprisal = −log p(token). The token's absolute probability under the student model.
- *Relative familiarity* = rank. Where this token sits in the vocabulary when tokens are sorted by probability.

These are not the same thing. A token can have an absolutely low probability (say 0.01) yet still rank second or third in the vocabulary — simply because the student model is globally uncertain at that position and all candidates have low probability.

RSR's insight: *good training data should show the student a token that is absolutely surprising yet relatively plausible*.

- Absolutely surprising → high surprisal → there is new information to learn
- Relatively plausible → low rank → the student can still follow along

This is *informative alignment*. One-line formula:

#+begin_src
RSR = sum(rank) / sum(surprisal)
#+end_src

Large denominator (high information content) and small numerator (student can recognize the token) → small RSR. Lower RSR = better fit for this student.

Only one forward pass required; no backprop; no validation set. Result: across 55 teacher–student pairs (5 students × 11 teachers), mean Spearman correlation is *0.86*. All prior methods — G-Norm, GRACE, Influence Score — top out at 0.5–0.6.

RSR is also used as a trajectory *selector*, not just a scorer: the subset it picks from all available teachers lets the student reach accuracy that *exceeds any single teacher's full dataset* (Table 6 vs Table 1). RSR genuinely identifies the solutions scattered across many teachers that are the best match for this particular student.

** Stop 6: Likely Next Steps

RSR introduces the informative-alignment framework, but §7 of the paper leaves several doors open:

- Can the rank/surprisal approach transfer to non-CoT data? (e.g., instruction tuning, dialogue data)
- How does it perform for subset selection within a single large dataset?
- Rank is currently computed using the student model itself — could a cheaper proxy model substitute?

The natural next direction for this thread is probably: *generalizing the informative-alignment principle to broader training-data selection*.

* What Is Really Happening Under the Surface

On the surface this looks like an iterative sequence of "data-selection methods." But underneath, a deeper conceptual shift is taking place.

*From the teacher's perspective to the student's perspective.* Early work asked "which teacher is stronger?" The papers just before this one began asking "does the student find this data comfortable?" That is a shift of attention from *what is being taught* to *how it can be learned*.

*From single-dimensional assessment to decomposed dimensions.* Avg-Surprisal uses one number (probability) to describe "fit," and inevitably runs into contradictions. RSR's key move is not a more complicated formula — it is splitting "fit" into two independent dimensions, absolute unfamiliarity and relative familiarity, letting each one do its own job. This is reminiscent of resolving a force vector into components in physics: the components are actually clearer than the combined magnitude.

*From finding an absolute quantity to finding a ratio.* Earlier methods hunted for an absolute measure (how large is this trajectory's likelihood / gradient?). RSR finds a ratio. Ratios self-normalize — absolute unfamiliarity calibrated by relative familiarity acquires a new semantic meaning: "informative alignment."

At a more abstract level, this thread is rediscovering something fundamental about teaching: *the best instruction is not the highest-quality content in the abstract, but the content whose information density is highest relative to what this particular learner can just barely follow*. This is almost exactly Vygotsky (1978)'s zone of proximal development, and the paper's introduction explicitly cites that concept.

Where does the thread go next? Two likely directions:
1. *Apply RSR to reward shaping in RL training* — trajectory selection during RL is structurally the same problem.
2. *Generalize the absolute-vs.-relative decomposition to other metrics* — many apparently contradictory training objectives may simply be unmeasured along the right axes.
