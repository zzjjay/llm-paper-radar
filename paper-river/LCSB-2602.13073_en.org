* The River of the Problem

Could you, on the iPhone in your pocket, *fine-tune* a multi-billion-parameter large model so it learns your own way of speaking?

It sounds absurd — training large models typically demands dozens of A100s. How could it possibly run on a phone? The entire thrust of this research line is to answer exactly that question. Two things make it hard: first, backpropagation itself is memory-hungry — every layer's intermediate activations must be retained, and the deeper the model the worse the explosion; second, every compromise made to save memory bites back — either training becomes too slow to be usable, or it fails to converge at all.

Over the span of a decade, a succession of researchers has pushed this from "absolutely impossible" to "a 4B model can be fine-tuned on an iPhone 15 Pro Max." LCSB is the latest step along that path.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2016 | Gradient Checkpointing: Trading Compute for Memory

*** What Problem It Saw

Backpropagation has an unavoidable deadlock: computing gradients requires the intermediate results (activations) from the forward pass. A 100-layer network means keeping all 100 layers' activations in memory simultaneously, to be consumed in reverse order. The deeper the model, the larger this bill — and eventually it blows the memory budget entirely.

*** Its Solution

Store only a handful of "checkpoints," say one every √n layers. During the backward pass, whenever it reaches a layer whose activations weren't stored, it restarts from the nearest checkpoint, re-runs the forward pass through that segment, produces the activations on the fly, uses them, and discards them immediately.

Analogy: instead of memorizing all 100 pages of a book, you memorize only the opening sentence of every 10th page. When you need page 47, you re-read from page 40 to 47. The cost is one extra reading; the savings is not having to hold 100 pages in your head.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Dug)

The math is clean: memory drops from O(n) to O(√n) at the cost of one additional forward pass. For scenarios where memory is the hard constraint and compute has slack, this is a free lunch.

But it only solves the "activations take memory" problem. Model parameters, gradients, and optimizer states — the big-ticket items — are untouched. A 175B model's parameters plus Adam's optimizer states are enough to blow any single card, checkpointing or not. The next barrier lies there.

** 2021 | LoRA: Don't Update Everything — Just a Pair of Small Matrices

*** What Problem It Saw

Fine-tuning GPT-3's 175B parameters updates all 175B weights, with gradients and optimizer states consuming another 2–3× memory on top. Even with checkpointing this scale is unmanageable — the root issue is the very idea of "full-parameter fine-tuning."

And storing a separate 175B copy for each fine-tuned task means a hundred fine-tuned variants cost 35 TB.

*** Its Solution

Leave the backbone alone. Freeze all original weights and attach a pair of small matrices A and B alongside each weight matrix, letting W + BA replace the original W. Both A and B are low-rank (e.g., rank 8), and together they may represent only one-thousandth of the original matrix's parameters.

Analogy: instead of renovating an entire building, you hang wallpaper on each wall. The wallpaper is far thinner, but it can completely change the look. A hundred different room styles = one building + a hundred sheets of wallpaper, not a hundred buildings.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Dug)

Empirically, the weight changes induced by fine-tuning are themselves low-rank — you don't need full-dimensional freedom to learn a task. GPU memory requirements drop by 3×, trainable parameters by 10,000×, with almost no quality loss.

But note: LoRA reduces the count of trainable parameters and optimizer states. The forward pass still traverses every layer, and the backward pass still traces every link in the chain. Mobile fine-tuning is still out of reach — just loading the 4B backbone is a struggle, let alone storing all intermediate activations during backprop.

** 2023 | MeZO: Just Drop Backpropagation

*** What Problem It Saw

Even with LoRA + checkpointing, the act of backpropagation itself consumes memory — activations, gradients, the backward graph. A phone's RAM is a few GB total; subtract the OS and other apps and what remains for training may be under 1 GB. The backpropagation route looked genuinely impassable.

*** Its Solution

Revisit a 1992 paper, SPSA: you can estimate gradients without backpropagation. Add a random perturbation z to the parameters, observe the loss change L(θ+εz) − L(θ−εz); this is the gradient's projection along z. Two forward passes, zero backward passes — memory footprint equals inference.

The technical highlight is reusing z via a random seed so z never needs to be stored explicitly.

Analogy: you can guess what's behind a wall without an X-ray machine — throw stones at the wall from different angles and listen to how the echo changes. Far slower, but all you need is one hand and some stones.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Dug)

In theory ZO should be d times slower (d is parameter count) — catastrophic for a 66B model. But Malladi's team found that pre-training plus prompting endows the loss landscape with "intrinsically low-dimensional structure," so ZO is in practice only 10–100× slower rather than billions of times slower.

But 10–100× is still a real penalty. Fine-tuning a single task on a phone could take days. The next paper therefore turns back: can we keep backpropagation but make it genuinely small?

** 2025 | MeBP (Apple): Backpropagation Fits in an iPhone

*** What Problem It Saw

MeZO's memory frugality came at an intolerable speed cost. Apple's team asked: can backpropagation really not fit in 1 GB?

They diagnosed three concrete bottlenecks: (1) activations — checkpointing already handles these; (2) parameters — LoRA has compressed the trainable portion to almost nothing, but the *backbone parameters* themselves occupy memory throughout; (3) intermediate activations persist in RAM between the forward and backward passes.

*** Its Solution

Combine three existing techniques and add two engineering surgeries:

1. *Gradient checkpointing*: each transformer block is one checkpoint unit
2. *LoRA*: train only low-rank adapters
3. *Lazy weight loading*: backbone weights are stored compressed on flash; each layer is decompressed on demand and released immediately after use
4. *Memory-mapped activations*: intermediate activations are not resident in RAM; they are written to an mmap file and read back on demand during the backward pass

Result: on an iPhone 15 Pro Max, models from 0.5B to 4B can be fine-tuned with first-order backpropagation using less than 1 GB of memory. Far faster than MeZO, and quality much closer to full precision.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Dug)

This is an engineering victory — none of the individual techniques are new, but combining them clears the "1 GB red line."

The new pit is visible in the performance profile: every backward step decompresses every layer's weights, and that single operation consumes 32–42% of total backward time. As models grow deeper and flash bandwidth fails to keep pace, this cost grows linearly. LCSB targets exactly this.

** 2026 | LCSB (This Paper): Only Back-Propagate Through a Subset of Layers per Step

*** What Problem It Saw

MeBP tamed memory but took it for granted that every layer must be back-propagated through on every step. Park's team found that each backward step decompresses every transformer layer's weights just to compute gradients — and on a phone, *decompression itself* is more expensive than the gradient computation: 32–42% of backward time is spent there.

The sharper question: what if each step only back-propagated through a subset of layers? The conventional wisdom says no — breaking the gradient chain means unselected layers never learn anything. But is that actually true?

*** Its Solution

Propose *Layer-Cyclic Selective Backpropagation*: each step back-propagates through only one subset of layers; the next step rotates to a different subset, cycling through all layers. Selected layers receive a normal backward pass; unselected layers participate in the forward pass but are "detached" during the backward pass — gradients continue propagating downward via the transformer's residual connection (the identity path) rather than through the layer's internal computation.

Theoretical framing: this is interpreted as *Block Coordinate Descent (BCD)* in the LoRA parameter space. BCD is well-established in convex optimization — optimize one block of variables at a time, freeze the rest, and the whole problem still converges.

But LCSB has a subtler point: the unselected layers' LoRA parameters are also being updated — through *AdamW's momentum*. Momentum retains gradient directions from past steps, providing a kind of "inertial update" to layers not selected in the current step.

Analogy: you manage 30 employees and can't hold a meeting with everyone every day. Instead you build a system where people who weren't in today's meeting continue at the pace set by last week's meeting. Over a week everyone gets their turn, and the overall pace is actually faster.

*** Why It Works (and What New Pit It Dug)

Three things interlock:
1. Residual connections keep the gradient chain intact (skip a layer, and gradients route around it via the residual path)
2. LoRA makes "update only some layers per step" semantically clean (each layer has just a handful of matrices)
3. AdamW momentum serves as an implicit catch-up mechanism for unselected layers

Measured results: 1.40× speedup with less than 2% quality degradation. There is also an unexpected finding: in 4-bit quantization training, a 3B model that diverges under full backpropagation converges stably under LCSB — selective backpropagation acts as implicit regularization.

Remaining gaps: the scheduling policy (which layers are selected on which step) is currently heuristic; the authors themselves note that a meta-controller should learn this. The convergence rate of BCD in the LoRA parameter space is also not yet fully characterized theoretically.

* Frontier Extensions

** 2026 | MeSP (Sibling Work, Same Month)

Nearly simultaneously, MeSP (Memory-efficient Structured Backpropagation, arXiv 2602.13069) took a different path:

- *LCSB reduces the number of layers* that are back-propagated through
- *MeSP reduces per-layer storage*: by hand-deriving the LoRA backward formula, it discovers that the intermediate projection h = xA — because rank r ≪ d — can be recomputed during the backward pass rather than stored

MeSP reports an average 49% memory reduction across the Qwen2.5 series (0.5B–3B), with gradients that are mathematically equivalent to MeBP.

The two approaches are *orthogonal* and can be combined. LCSB's concluding discussion explicitly lists MeSP as a composable direction. The next paper to appear in this space will very likely combine both.

* Insights

This line of work contains three escalating conceptual turns.

*First turn (2016–2021)*: Memory is not fate — it is an engineering choice. Checkpointing trades compute for memory; LoRA trades structure for parameter count. Once you are willing to challenge the default assumptions about what training should look like, memory can be compressed.

*Second turn (2023)*: MeZO is a "radical negation" — just eliminate backpropagation entirely. It fails productively: it proves the ZO route is not the answer, but it also forces the whole field to think carefully about *why* backpropagation is expensive.

*Third turn (2025–2026)*: Once MeBP rescued BP, the battlefield shifted. The old bottleneck was *memory* — "can it fit?" The new bottleneck is *time* — "is it fast enough?" Neither LCSB nor MeSP optimizes memory anymore; both optimize the latency composition of each step. This is a subtle but important change of metrics.

At a deeper level: the early techniques (checkpointing, ZO) are *architecture-agnostic* general-purpose compressions. LCSB is different — its core argument ("residual connections keep the gradient chain intact") holds only for transformer-style residual architectures. One hallmark of a maturing research area is precisely this move from generic recipes toward *architecture-specific* ones. When a field's papers start saying "exploiting property Y of architecture X," it means researchers have internalized the foundation architecture thoroughly.

Where does the field go next? Three high-probability directions:
1. *Intelligent scheduling*: use a small model to predict which layers to select next — analogous to MoE routing
2. *Horizontal stacking*: LCSB × MeSP × quantization × sparsity — exploring compounding gains
3. *From phone to edge*: replicating the same approach on automotive chips, IoT devices, and embedded GPUs

* Takeaways

For research: when a field's central barrier is broken ("can it?"), don't linger optimizing the old metric — immediately search for the next bottleneck ("fast enough?", "stable enough?", "power-efficient enough?"). MeBP had barely landed before it forked into LCSB and MeSP, because whoever first recognized that "the memory gate has been passed" claimed the next six months of publication slots.

For engineering: LCSB's success hinges on a not-so-obvious key — a seemingly "destructive" change (skipping some layers' backward passes) is rescued by *side effects of existing mechanisms* (residual connections + momentum). When evaluating whether a system can be modified, the critical question is whether its existing mechanisms leave you a "free escape route." The transformer's residual connection was designed for training stability; LCSB repurposes it as a gradient bypass at no extra cost.

One final point: every advance in this line built on *algorithms that were not newly invented* — SPSA dates to 1992, BCD is textbook optimization, and the ideas behind gradient checkpointing trace back to automatic differentiation in the 1980s. The genuinely new thing each time is: taking old tools, assembling them under a new hardware constraint (< 1 GB phone RAM), and discovering which combination just barely threads the needle. This means your next paper may not require a new algorithm at all — it may require finding a new tight constraint, then seeing which pieces of existing machinery can be assembled into the key that fits.
