* The River of the Problem

How does a large model that can reason teach a smaller model to reason — *without burning a fortune*?

That sounds like a trivial question — isn't it just fine-tuning? The trouble is that "reasoning" is not a short answer; it is a long, rambling scratchpad. For a high-school math problem, the teacher model might write five thousand tokens of internal monologue — false starts, backtracking, "wait, let me verify that once more." Feeding those scratchpads to a student model for imitation means every single training sample is ten times longer than a normal conversation. Training time, GPU memory, and FLOPs all explode together.

What makes it worse is a nagging suspicion: most of those five thousand tokens are "overthought." The student probably does not need every one of the teacher's zigzags — yet nobody has systematically explained which parts are genuinely useful and which are filler. Will cutting half of them cause collapse? Where exactly is the cutoff?

That is the river carved open by this question. From "teaching a small model to reason" to "being able to afford teaching a small model to reason," the gap has been crossed by generation after generation of researchers asking: what, inside a chain of thought, actually counts as the essence?

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2020 | GPT-3: Give a Large Model a Few Examples and It Just Works

*** What problem it saw

The prevailing paradigm was "pre-train then fine-tune on every task" — translation data for translation, QA data for QA, a separate set of weights per task. Heavy.

*** Its solution

Scale the model to 175B parameters, and something clicks: no fine-tuning required. Drop a handful of examples into the prompt (few-shot), and the model generalises to new tasks on the spot.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

At sufficient scale the model internalises task patterns, so a few examples are enough to activate them. But one class of tasks resisted entirely: *multi-step reasoning*. "Bob has 5 apples, gives 2 to Alice, then buys 3 more — how many does he have?" GPT-3 guesses at the answer rather than computing it. It learned to retrieve, not to calculate.

That hole is exactly what the next paper had to fill.

** 2022 | CoT Prompting: Let It Write a Scratchpad Before Answering

*** What problem it saw

A large model like GPT-3 is not incapable of arithmetic — it is forced by prompt design into "one-shot output." If every example in the few-shot prompt is (question, answer) with nothing in between, the model learns to skip straight from reading the question to producing an answer. The intermediate accounting is compressed away.

*** Its solution

Include the reasoning steps explicitly in the few-shot examples:

#+begin_example
Q: Bob has 5 apples, gives away 2, then buys 3 more. How many does he have?
A: Bob starts with 5. After giving away 2 he has 3. Buying 3 more gives 6. The answer is 6.
#+end_example

Once the model sees "scratchpad-included" examples, it writes its own scratchpad at inference time. Accuracy on math benchmarks jumped from single digits to tens of percentage points.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

Writing out intermediate steps makes the "compute a few more passes over tokens" process explicit. A transformer's per-forward compute is fixed, but generating 200 reasoning tokens is equivalent to running 200 more forward steps — more compute, purchased cheaply.

But this is *prompt engineering* — relying on a handful of examples to make the model improvise correctly. Reliability depends on luck: change the prompt template and it may collapse; one wrong step in a long chain propagates the error. The next generation needed to weld this capability into the model's weights permanently.

** 2024 | OpenAI o1: Train Reasoning into the Weights

*** What problem it saw

CoT prompting is a temporary scaffold. The model was never *trained* to be a reasoner; it was occasionally tricked into acting like one. Hard problems, long chains, and adversarial inputs exposed the seams.

*** Its solution

Skip the prompt tricks. During training itself, have the model generate extensive internal reasoning chains and use reinforcement learning to reward getting the final answer right. The model learns to produce thousands of tokens of internal monologue: try one approach, hit a dead end, backtrack, try another, then deliver an answer.

Test-time compute also changes: difficulty determines how long the model is allowed to think. A hard problem warrants thousands of extra tokens.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

Converting improvisation into muscle memory stabilised performance dramatically. But o1 is closed-source — weights, training data, and reasoning traces are all withheld.

The open community immediately faces a new question: how do you grow your own o1? And once you have an open "thinks-for-a-long-time" model, how do you transfer that capability into a small model that runs on consumer hardware?

** 2025-01 | DeepSeek-R1: Open-Source o1, and Distil It Into Small Models While You're at It

*** What problem it saw

o1 is neither inspectable nor reproducible. The community has no training data and no path to replication. There is also a practical downstream problem: even if a capable open reasoning model existed, it would be too large to deploy.

*** Its solution

Two steps. First: use RL to let an open large model grow its own reasoning capability — the result is R1. Second: have R1 write down its reasoning traces and use them as supervised fine-tuning data for a family of small models (Qwen-7B, Llama-8B, etc.). The small models read R1's scratchpads and, remarkably, learn the `<think>...</think>` long-chain-of-thought format for themselves.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

The essence of distillation: write down how long the teacher is willing to think, and let the student imitate. The student needs no RL of its own — supervised fine-tuning suffices, and that is far cheaper.

The new hole is concrete: *the teacher's reasoning traces are very long*. A single sample can be several thousand tokens; training costs explode. And DeepSeek did not release the distillation dataset, so the community had to reconstruct one from scratch.

** 2025 | OpenThoughts / Sky-T1 / Bespoke-Stratos: Open-Sourcing the Distillation Datasets

*** What problem it saw

R1 validated the "long CoT trace + SFT" approach, but the data stayed private. To run experiments and train custom models, the community needed standardised (Prompt, CoT, Answer) data.

*** Its solution

Run R1 (or similar open reasoning models) to generate large volumes of long-CoT data, wrap it in a unified format (OpenThoughts uses `<begin_of_thought>...<end_of_thought>` tags; DeepSeek uses `<think>...</think>`), and release it publicly.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

Reproducible, comparable experiments became possible overnight. But every one of these datasets made the same silent assumption: *train on the entire (P, CoT, A) sequence*. Whether a reasoning trace was ten thousand tokens or five thousand, nobody questioned the full-length training paradigm.

The hole is buried in that assumption — nobody had systematically asked: how much does the CoT segment actually contribute? Is the second half wasted? Can it be dropped at training time?

** 2025 | The Overthinking-Critique Branch (Think 2/3, Stop Overthinking, etc.)

*** What problem it saw

Practitioners using R1-style models quickly noticed: the model frequently *keeps self-verifying* even after it has already arrived at the correct answer. A problem might be solved correctly at token 800, yet the model keeps thinking for another 3,000 tokens before stopping.

These bloated traces end up verbatim in datasets like OpenThoughts, and the student model dutifully learns not just reasoning but verbosity.

*** Its solution

This branch focused on *inference-time* interventions: make the model stop earlier at test time, or elicit the same answer with a shorter CoT. Work like "Think 2/3" showed that using only two-thirds of the reasoning budget costs almost nothing in accuracy.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

The evidence is solid: truncating the second half at inference time barely affects final correctness. But this branch *never touched the training data* — models were still trained on full-length sequences; the truncation was applied only at inference time.

The missing step: if the second half is useless at inference time, why train on it at all?

** 2025-12 | Distilling the Essence: Truncate the Second Half at Training Time

*** What problem it saw

Scanning back along the entire river —

- Upstream: OpenThoughts / Sky-T1 assume full-length training by default; nobody had decomposed the contribution of each of (P, CoT, A)
- Side channel: the overthinking-critique branch proved CoT's second half is redundant, but acted only on inference, not training

Between the two lines there is an obvious gap: *what happens if you truncate at training time?* Which of the three segments is worth supervising?

Nobody had systematically filled that gap.

*** Its solution

Two surgical interventions, both deliberately conservative:

*Surgery 1: Decompose the three segments — find out which is worth supervising.* Split each training sample into Prompt, CoT, and Answer segments, then compute the KD loss on one or more segments at a time. Conclusion: *supervising the CoT segment alone is sufficient.* Adding P and A contributes almost nothing — because a long CoT already implicitly re-states the prompt and works its way to the answer; it subsumes both.

*Surgery 2: Truncate every sample to its first 50 %.* Sweep over truncation ratios — 100 %, 75 %, 50 %, 25 %. The sweet spot is 50 %: keeping only the first half of tokens retains roughly 91 % of math benchmark performance while halving training time, memory, and FLOPs.

Combine Surgery 1 (CoT-only supervision) with Surgery 2 (first 50 % only) and you get an "essence-only" training recipe.

*** Why it worked — and what new hole it dug

Two observations underpin the approach:

1. CoT is self-redundant — during derivation it naturally re-states problem conditions and naturally leads to the answer. Separately supervising P or A is therefore duplicated effort.
2. The "first correct answer" in a reasoning trace typically appears around the midpoint of the sequence; the second half consists largely of verification, retrospection, and "let me think again" — consistent with the overthinking-critique findings.

Remaining holes: 50 % is a blunt, fixed ratio. Different problem difficulties and different model scales should each have their own optimal truncation point. The natural next step is *adaptive truncation* — deciding per sample how much to cut. Further out: fine-grained token-level selection (which tokens are "essence," which are filler), and more radical latent-reasoning approaches that remove visible tokens from the reasoning process entirely.

* Frontier Extensions

As of May 2026, Distilling the Essence is the most direct work on the "truncate reasoning traces at training time" line. Published only six months ago, formal follow-up papers have not yet solidified, but several trajectories are visible:

- *Adaptive truncation*: instead of a fixed 50 %, detect the position of the first correct answer in each sample and cut there. This requires a lightweight "when to stop" detector.
- *Token-level selection*: cut by importance rather than position. Naturally converges with token pruning and speculative decoding research.
- *Latent reasoning*: more radical — stop letting the reasoning process appear as visible tokens at all, and let it execute in the hidden-state space. Work like Coconut is already exploring this. If it succeeds, "truncation" becomes a problem that is circumvented rather than solved.

* Insight

Looking back along the whole river, the *surface* story is "how models learn to reason → how reasoning gets cheaper." But the deeper process is more precise:

*The community has repeatedly redefined what reasoning fundamentally is.*

- In the CoT prompting era: "reasoning = writing out intermediate steps." The essence was *the act of writing*.
- In the o1 era: "reasoning = the model's willingness to invest enough test-time compute for a hard problem." The essence was *compute expenditure*.
- In the R1 + OpenThoughts era: "reasoning = a complete long CoT trace, including trial-and-error and self-verification." The essence was *trace completeness*.
- The overthinking-critique branch began shaking that consensus: perhaps only part of a "complete trace" is the true essence.
- Distilling the Essence turns that suspicion into a measurable claim — the essence lives in the CoT segment, within the first 50 % of tokens.

Each redefinition carves the essence smaller. From "write it out" to "the part that actually does the work." The river is, at bottom, a process of *progressively converging on the minimum sufficient set for reasoning*.

Where does it go next? Deeper into that minimum. Short term: adaptive truncation, token-level selection. Medium term: discard some visible tokens entirely and let reasoning run in latent space (latent reasoning). Long term: perhaps the concept of a "reasoning segment" dissolves altogether, replaced by a model that autonomously allocates depth of processing per forward pass — returning to the spirit of o1 by a completely different mechanism.

* Implications

Two concrete, actionable takeaways for researchers:

*First: find the intersection of two parallel lines.* This paper did not appear from thin air — it combined "truncate reasoning data" with "shift the supervision focus," two independent observations that each had a piece missing. Both had been noticed; neither had been joined. Anyone who spots an "A is proven, B is proven, but A+B has never been tried" gap has a paper waiting to be written.

*Second: attack default assumptions.* The crucial move that made this paper possible was questioning OpenThoughts / Sky-T1's silent default that the full (P, CoT, A) sequence must be trained on. That assumption was never stated explicitly in any paper precisely because it felt obvious. But whatever everyone takes for granted is usually whatever nobody has verified. When reading papers, pay special attention to the steps the authors *don't discuss* — those are typically where the unexcavated gold lies.

Final point: the "train on half the data + supervise only one segment" principle is not confined to reasoning distillation. It transfers directly to other long-sequence supervision tasks — agentic trajectory distillation, long-context fine-tuning, multi-turn dialogue SFT — and may be reusable with minimal adaptation.
