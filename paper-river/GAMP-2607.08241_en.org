* The River of the Problem

What goes wrong when you try to compress a "look twice before you paint" diffusion model to save power?

State-of-the-art high-quality image diffusion models run the network twice at every inference step: once with a condition ("draw a cat"), and once with no condition at all (blank input). The two outputs are then combined in a weighted sum to produce a final image that is both sharp and obedient. This is classifier-free guidance (CFG). That "run twice, then combine" structure is central to model quality — yet it is also the detail most easily ignored during compression, because virtually every compression method is designed for a single-input, single-output network and never treats the two branches as something worth caring about separately.

Where exactly is the bottleneck: if, after compression, the "blank-condition" branch quietly drifts off course in a way that does not affect your standard quality metrics — or even makes the scores look better — you will never notice the model is broken until you see the generated images fall apart. This river is about two things: how to detect that a hidden branch is drifting, and how to prevent it from drifting during compression. The path to both answers was forced, step by step.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2021 | CFG: Fitting a "Compliance Knob" to Diffusion Models

*** What problem it saw

Early conditional diffusion generation was either completely uncontrollable, or required training a separate classifier to "steer" generation — cumbersome and error-prone.

*** Its solution

No external classifier needed: train the same network to operate in two modes — one with a condition, one with nothing. At inference time, amplify the difference between the two outputs and add it back in, artificially turning up how faithfully the model follows the condition.

*** Why it works (and where it buries the next problem)

It works because the difference naturally encodes "what the condition actually changed"; amplifying that difference amplifies adherence. But this also means: from this moment on, diffusion-model inference is no longer "one forward pass" — it is "two forward passes plus one linear combination." This dual-branch structure becomes a background fact of life — and it would take years before anyone noticed that almost every downstream compression and distillation method was pretending it did not exist.

** Common Practice | Distilling and Compressing Dual-Branch Models as If They Were Single-Branch

*** What problem it saw

There was no explicit "it" — this was the community default: when compressing a model, care only about whether the student's final output resembles the teacher's, using a standard output-layer distillation loss.

*** Its solution (which is really no solution)

Apply off-the-shelf distillation and quantization pipelines directly, making no distinction between the conditional and unconditional pathways inside the model.

*** Why it falls short (the new gap)

Because the loss only watches the final output, the unconditional branch is free to become whatever it likes. In the extreme, the student can drive both branches to nearly identical outputs — the guidance difference approaches zero, the model appears to be "working normally," but it has already lost the ability to follow conditions. This "two-branch collapse" phenomenon would not be formally named until DASH.

** 2026 | DASH: Explicitly Supervising the "Unsupervised" Branch

*** What problem it saw

Single-branch distillation deprives the unconditional branch of any supervision; in the worst case both branches degenerate to the same function and guidance becomes meaningless — even when the standard distillation loss looks perfectly fine numerically.

*** Its solution

Design a dedicated loss for the unconditional branch (not just "incidentally distilled"), add an anchoring term that pulls the conditional branch's output toward the true noise target, and transfer the teacher's converged per-timestep importance curve directly to the student (TIRT Transfer) to save the student from having to rediscover a training curriculum from scratch. Ablations show that "explicitly supervising the unconditional branch" alone accounts for more than sixty percent of the total improvement.

*** Why it works (and where it buries the next problem)

It works because it turns "prevent two-branch collapse" into an explicit training-time optimization objective, rather than hoping the distillation loss will incidentally keep things in check. But the approach rests on a premise: DASH proves the null space can be closed in a training setting — by relying on hundreds of thousands of gradient updates applied to unfrozen weights. The new gap appears immediately: switch to post-training quantization (PTQ) — weights fully frozen, only a few hundred threshold-calibration iterations, no gradients whatsoever — and DASH's "train the null space away with gradients" strategy simply does not apply.

** Background Thread | Diffusion-Model PTQ Methods: Also Not Treating the Dual Branch Seriously

*** What they saw

PTQ4DM first validated the feasibility of post-training quantization for diffusion models; Q-Diffusion introduced skip-connection partitioning and block-wise reconstruction; PTQD modeled quantization noise as a perturbation within the diffusion SDE; TFMQ-DM, APQ-DM, and TCAQ-DM pushed further with temporal-feature maintenance and per-channel calibration. On the mixed-precision assignment side, BRECQ and HAWQ used reconstruction error or Hessian curvature to decide how many bits each layer deserves.

*** Their solutions (and shared blind spot)

These methods made steady progress on "how to quantize more precisely," but their calibration objectives were either block-wise reconstruction error or fidelity of the guidance difference Δ — not a single one treated "the guided output itself must be correct" as the calibration target, and not one considered the CFG dual-branch structure as such.

*** Why this left a gap

Because the entire line of work stayed inside the "single-branch assumption." In a pure PTQ setting (no gradients, frozen weights), DASH-style solutions are unavailable, while these methods themselves never realized they needed a "dual-branch-aware" calibration objective. That is precisely the gap GAMP is designed to fill.

** 2026 | GAMP (Target Paper): Proving the PTQ Null Space Cannot Be Trained Away — Unless You Calibrate the Guided Output Directly

*** What problem it saw

The authors prove a proposition: if the PTQ calibration objective is merely to minimize the reconstruction error of the guidance difference Δ, then the unconditional branch admits an exact null space — you can add an arbitrary offset δ to the entire unconditional branch and the reconstruction error of Δ stays zero, while calibration diagnostic metrics (ρ, cos(Δ)) still report "near-perfect." The authors call this the branch-drift trap, and confirm it experimentally: the quantization configuration with the best diagnostic scores produces the worst generation quality of all configurations tested (FID 334 — worse than a configuration with even lower precision, W4A4). Crucially, this time there are no hundreds-of-thousands of gradient steps available to train the offset away — PTQ calibration allows only a few hundred threshold adjustments, with weights frozen, and no mechanism capable of suppressing δ.

*** Its solution

Stop calibrating the "guidance difference Δ"; calibrate directly against the guided prediction (the final guided output). Use each layer's contribution to guided-output error as a sensitivity measure, then apply a greedy knapsack algorithm to assign activation precision (4-bit or 8-bit) to the most sensitive layers, while keeping weights at a uniformly low bit-width. With this calibration target in place, δ is pinned to zero from the start; the null space is blocked at the root — not compressed away by training.

*** Why it works (and where gaps may remain)

The reason is straightforward: since the root cause is "the calibration objective contains no constraint on the unconditional branch," pull the unconditional branch back into the calibration objective (by requiring the full guided output, not just the difference, to be accurate). This mathematically closes the null space described in Proposition 1. The paper itself acknowledges several limitations: experiments were conducted only on 32×32 CIFAR-10 pixel-space diffusion with a 6.1 M-parameter model; large-scale latent-space CFG models such as Stable Diffusion have not been validated. Guidance scale was also tested only near the default setting, yet the authors' own analysis predicts that "the larger the guidance scale (7–15, as commonly used in text-to-image), the more severe the branch-drift problem" — exactly the regime most in need of verification and still untested.

* Frontier Extensions

As of now (paper submitted 2026-07-09), no subsequent work clearly citing or extending GAMP has been found — the paper is too new to have attracted downstream activity. The two direct follow-on directions the authors themselves identify in their conclusion are: (1) injecting a DASH-style "unconditional anchoring term" directly into the PTQ calibration loss as an alternative way to close the null space; and (2) combining GAMP's per-layer sensitivity-based precision assignment with PTQD-style error-accumulation assignment along the denoising trajectory. These are better described as signposts the authors have left for future readers than as research that has already happened.

* Insight

What is actually changing along this line of work is not "quantization techniques becoming ever more refined" — it is "what the calibration objective is actually optimizing" being excavated ever more deeply: from "does the output look right," to "is the guidance difference accurate," to "has each individual branch drifted." The lesson at every level follows the same pattern — whenever a calibration or loss function leaves some direction unconstrained, the model will drift freely in that direction, and conventional diagnostic metrics will be completely blind to it. DASH and GAMP are in fact the same insight applied twice under two different constraint regimes (with gradients vs. without): which also explains why GAMP heavily reuses DASH's mathematical framework (the ρ and cos(Δ) diagnostics) yet concludes that "these diagnostics are insufficient in the PTQ setting."

A caveat worth noting: the segment of this "evolutionary line" from DASH to GAMP is two consecutive papers by the same author group (self-citation), not independent cross-team validation; the paper itself has not yet undergone peer review or attracted downstream citations; and experiments are limited to a small CIFAR-10 model. It is more accurate to read this as "a problem evolution that one research group has worked out for itself" than as "a consensus trajectory recognized by the field at large."

* Takeaway

"Diagnostics look perfect, yet the model may already be broken" — this pattern is not unique to diffusion-model quantization. Whenever a system has a "visible formal objective" (here: Δ fidelity) and an "actual behavior we care about" (here: quality of the guided output), and the two are not strictly equivalent, a null space can hide in the gap between them. The practical takeaway: the next time you compress or calibrate a model, first ask — "Is there a degree of freedom between my calibration/loss objective and the final behavior I actually care about — one that can vary freely without affecting the loss?" If there is, go block that degree of freedom directly, rather than continuing to add terms on top of the existing diagnostics.
