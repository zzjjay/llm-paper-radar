* The River of the Problem

During LLM inference, every newly generated token requires the model to look back over all previous tokens' "notes" — this pile of notes is the KV cache. As the context grows longer, the notes stack into a mountain and GPU memory gives out first.

This gives rise to a deceptively simple question: *Who stays, who goes?*

The difficulty lies in not knowing what the model will ask next. Dropping a token that looks "unimportant" might turn out to be the critical intermediate step for the math problem that follows. Over a decade, generation after generation of researchers have proposed countless approaches — inspect attention scores, inspect vector norms, inspect geometric diversity, predict future attention — each effective in its own way, yet each falling just short. Nobody could articulate what "important" actually means.

CapKV's answer is: all these approaches are in fact different approximations of the same thing — *maximizing retained information capacity*. The only difference is that before, people arrived there by intuition; now the same idea can be written as a closed-form expression.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2000 | Information Bottleneck: Finding a Mathematical Definition of "What to Remember"

*** What Problem It Saw

More than two decades ago, machine learning harbored a fundamental confusion: when you are forced to compress information — say, squeezing an image into a feature vector or a passage into an embedding — *what should you discard?*

The intuitive answer is "discard what is unimportant." But what does "important" mean? Nobody could give a definition that did not depend on the specific task.

*** Its Solution

Tishby et al. borrowed tools from communications theory and wrote a simple equation: find an intermediate representation T that retains from input X only the information useful for predicting target Y — specifically, minimize I(X;T) (compress aggressively) while maximizing I(T;Y) (preserve task relevance). The two objectives are balanced by a weight β.

This is the *Information Bottleneck*. In one sentence: *the limit of compression is not "discard less" but "discard the irrelevant."*

*** Why It Works (and Where the New Trap Lies)

It translates the fuzzy word "important" into mutual information — something computable. Any "select a subset" problem can in principle be cast within this framework.

The trap: mutual information in high-dimensional real spaces has no closed-form solution; in practice one must resort to variational approximations or neural-network fits, which are complex and expensive. So for over twenty years the framework lived mainly in theory, while engineering took a different path.

KV cache eviction was one such independent path — until this paper reconnected the two.

** 2023 | H2O: The First Practical KV Eviction Tool

*** What Problem It Saw

After GPT-4 arrived, everyone started chasing long contexts. The problem appeared immediately: context lengths grew from 2K to 32K, and KV cache memory footprint grew 16×. An A100 80 GB running a 13B model would OOM as soon as the prompt got long.

At the time, no systematic eviction method existed. Options were: keep everything (memory blows up), FIFO-drop the oldest tokens (naive), or use a sliding window (lose global information).

*** Its Solution

Zhang et al. stared at attention maps and noticed a counterintuitive pattern: *most tokens are attended to almost never; only a small number of "heavy hitters" are attended to repeatedly.*

The fix followed naturally: accumulate each token's total attention score over history, keep the top-B, discard the rest. This is H2O (Heavy-Hitter Oracle).

*** Why It Works (and Where the New Trap Lies)

It works because attention is inherently sparse — a small number of tokens carry the bulk of the semantic load, while the rest are mostly filler. Dropping the filler causes almost no degradation.

The trap: *cumulative scores react sluggishly to recent events.* Suppose the end of the prompt contains a critical instruction — "answer in French" — but that token's cumulative score has not had time to climb, so H2O treats it as unimportant and drops it. The model then answers in English.

That passes the ball to the next paper.

** 2024 | SnapKV: Don't Look at a Lifetime — Look at "Just Now"

*** What Problem It Saw

The SnapKV team noticed a finer-grained pattern: at the *moment the model finishes processing the prompt*, it already "knows" which tokens it will attend to next — that information is fully written into the attention pattern of the prompt's final short segment.

H2O, by using cumulative scores, averages this most critical "end-window signal" together with ancient historical noise.

*** Its Solution

Look only at the attention distribution from a fixed final window of the prompt (e.g., the last 32 tokens), and score each historical token by how often it is attended to within that window. Keep the high-frequency ones.

In one sentence: *replace "has it been important historically?" with "is it important for the question about to be asked?"*

*** Why It Works (and Where the New Trap Lies)

Empirical gains are substantial — at the same budget, long-context QA accuracy noticeably beats H2O. This confirms that "recent signal" is a better proxy for true query relevance than "cumulative signal."

The trap: it still *uses only attention scores* as its signal source. Attention scores are noisy (softmax is easily skewed by tail outliers) and depend on the query — yet future queries are invisible. If the window is poorly chosen, the method still makes errors.

A more subtle problem: the selected top-B tokens may be *highly redundant* — synonymous, co-located, expressing the same meaning. Low diversity means wasted budget.

** 2024–25 | Knorm / KeyDiff / Expected Attention: Ignore Attention, Look at Geometry

*** What Problem It Saw

Attention scores are expensive (requiring a softmax pass), noisy, and query-dependent. A cluster of researchers turned to ask: can we *look only at the geometry of the key vectors themselves*, without computing attention at all?

A deeper question: earlier methods score and rank each token independently. But the value of a cache is *the value of the set* — the B selected tokens should *cover a wide area* rather than cluster in the same semantic corner.

*** Its Solution

Three diverging paths were explored simultaneously:

- *Knorm* (Devoto et al., 2024): use the ℓ₂ norm of the key vector directly as its importance score. Rationale: keys with larger norms are more likely to win out in softmax. One division, one sort — extremely cheap.
- *KeyDiff* (Park et al., 2025): greedily select a set of keys that maximize pairwise distances. The goal is "semantic diversity," avoiding redundancy.
- *Expected Attention* (Devoto et al., 2025): model the *distribution of future queries* and estimate each key's expected attention — sidestepping the "future queries are invisible" dead end.

*** Why It Works (and Where the New Trap Lies)

This family proves one thing: *in KV eviction, "diversity," "geometry," and "attention scores" are signals of equal standing.* In certain scenarios, geometric signals are even more stable.

The trap — and the opening CapKV exploits — is this:

Each method is partly right, but *nobody can explain why*, let alone identify which is optimal. Knorm looks at scale, KeyDiff looks at pairwise distance, Expected Attention looks at query expectation, SnapKV looks at attention scores. *Are they all chasing the same thing?* Or each chasing something different?

If the same thing, what is it? If not, why does each win on some benchmarks?

That is what CapKV set out to answer.

** 2026 | CapKV: Writing a "Parent Formula" for the Entire Family

*** What Problem It Saw

After reading through five years of papers, the authors reached a verdict: *the entire KV eviction field is in a "pre-Newtonian" state* — all sorts of local regularities have been observed, but a unified law of motion is missing.

Every time an engineer designs a new method, they have to guess a new heuristic by intuition, run experiments, tune hyperparameters, and benchmark-chase. No principles, only tricks.

*** Its Solution

Model the KV cache as *a set of linear communication channels*: each key-value pair is a channel that "transmits" the information carried by future queries to the attention output. Under a *linear-Gaussian approximation* (attention is not linear-Gaussian, but this is a tractable surrogate), one can write a closed-form mutual information:

"The mutual information between the retained subset Z_C and future query q" = a log-determinant expression.

This is the *capacity objective*.

Then the paper demonstrates something striking:

- Take a *first-order Taylor expansion* of the capacity objective, projected onto key norms — you rediscover *Knorm*.
- Include the *second-order term*, accounting for correlations among keys — you rediscover the diversity of *KeyDiff*.
- Treat the query not as a random variable but as an observed value — you rediscover the query-aware flavor of *SnapKV/Expected Attention*.

The entire family stands revealed under the parent formula: they are *different approximations of the same mutual-information objective*.

Finally, CapKV forgoes approximation altogether and uses a standard tool — *statistical leverage scores* — to greedily maximize the log-determinant directly. A single scan determines which tokens to keep.

*** Why It Works (and Where the New Trap Lies)

It works because it *finally knows what it is optimizing.* All prior methods were implicitly optimizing capacity, but because the objective was unknown, the optimization was always incomplete — using only first-order terms (Knorm), ignoring the query dimension (KeyDiff), or ignoring diversity (SnapKV). CapKV unifies these dimensions.

Empirically, it consistently outperforms prior methods on LongBench and reasoning benchmarks. More importantly, the authors show that *the capacity score itself* is strongly correlated with downstream performance — capacity is a predictive ruler for performance, not merely an internal optimization target.

The remaining traps:

- The linear-Gaussian assumption is a *modeling convenience*, not ground truth. Real attention is nonlinear; the distribution has heavy tails. The unifying framework holds under this approximation — what could a sharper approximation unlock?
- Leverage scores are still computed in a *one-shot*, static manner. As decoding proceeds, the "optimal subset" of the cache changes dynamically, yet this paper still uses a single up-front budget allocation.
- The channel-independence assumption is too strong — real cache keys are strongly correlated, which is precisely why H2O under-selects and SnapKV over-selects certain tokens.

* Frontier Extensions

CapKV was released in April 2026; at the time of writing no papers explicitly building on it have appeared. But several directions are foreseeable:

- *Dynamic capacity*: replace one-shot leverage scores with rolling re-selection that advances with each decoding step, compatible with attention sinks or streaming modes.
- *Nonlinear capacity*: abandon linear-Gaussian and estimate capacity from the true softmax distribution (possibly via kernel methods or low-rank attention decomposition).
- *Cross-head / cross-layer joint selection*: the current framework is derived for a single head within a single layer. Joint capacity across multiple heads and layers is the obvious next step, but combinatorial explosion looms.
- *Joint capacity-precision compression*: co-optimize with quantization (KIVI, KVQuant) — the same IB framework naturally accommodates a bit-width dimension.

Broader reach: the way CapKV pulls KV eviction into the IB framework may be reproducible for *token selection in speculative decoding*, *expert routing in MoE*, and *chunk selection in long-context retrieval*. All three are fundamentally "choose who to keep under a budget" problems.

* Insights

Reading this line of work, the surface story is "KV cache compression keeps getting better." Underneath, three deeper shifts are at play.

*First layer: from "score individually" to "optimize the set."* In the H2O/SnapKV era, each token received an independent score and the top-B were kept. Not until KeyDiff did anyone realize that "two high-scoring but semantically redundant tokens are worth less than one slightly lower-scoring token that fills a coverage gap." CapKV formalizes this observation. It is a paradigm shift *from ranking to set selection* — almost identical in spirit to recommendation systems moving from pointwise to listwise ranking.

*Second layer: from "empirically works" to "knowing why it works."* The past five years of KV eviction was trick accumulation: observe a phenomenon → propose a heuristic → benchmark → observe new phenomenon → propose new heuristic. CapKV changes the rules of the game — instead of designing another trick, it *finds the common denominator of all prior tricks.* This kind of "unification paper" recurs whenever a field reaches maturity: think Adam → AdamW → SOAP for optimizers, or the ELBO framework for generative models.

*Third layer: information theory brought back to the engineering front line.* After its proposal in 2000, Information Bottleneck was largely confined to theory — because it was intractable. What CapKV uses is not true IB but a *linear-Gaussian simplification* plus *leverage-score approximation.* This is a noteworthy engineering strategy: *use a known-imprecise theoretical model to obtain a closed-form, computable objective, then let experiments validate whether the approximation is good enough.* This is more pragmatic than demanding theoretical exactness or rejecting theory altogether.

Where next? Two bets: (1) This IB perspective will rapidly spread to *every LLM sub-problem with a "select K under a fixed budget" structure* — MoE routing, speculative decoding, retrieval chunking, tool selection. (2) The true next breakthrough will not be computing leverage scores more accurately, but *abandoning the one-shot assumption* — capacity itself shifts dynamically as decoding proceeds. Whoever can build "streaming capacity" will truly merge KV eviction with streaming LLM inference.

* Takeaways

For people doing research, this thread offers three concrete lessons.

*One: unifiers are rarer than innovators, and more valuable.* Once a field has accumulated twenty-plus heuristics, proposing the twenty-first has near-zero marginal value. But writing a paper that says "these twenty are all the same thing" has enormous marginal value — it lifts the entire field from empirical science to theoretical science. CapKV is exactly this kind of work. Have the courage to look for a unifying framework in a field that already "seems mature," rather than grinding for the next 0.5% SOTA.

*Two: use a "known-imprecise" model to obtain a "computable objective."* The linear-Gaussian assumption *certainly does not hold* for attention — softmax is nonlinear and the distribution has heavy tails. But the authors accepted that approximation in exchange for a closed-form mutual-information expression. Experiments then confirmed: *the approximation is coarse, but adequate.* This is the core strategy of engineering-oriented research — *don't pursue theoretical perfection; pursue theoretical "good-enough-and-computable."*

*Three: read papers in reverse.* If you read only CapKV, you come away thinking "oh, another KV compression method." But reading backward through H2O, SnapKV, and KeyDiff, you see five years of people repeatedly trial-erroring toward the same truth. Reverse reading reveals not "a new method" but *a group of people feeling around an elephant in a dark room* — for five years, until someone finally sketched the elephant's outline. Next time you enter a new field, *read backward three layers first*, then read the latest work. The efficiency gain is an order of magnitude.

A practical landing point:

- Whenever you face an engineering problem of "select K under a budget" (feature selection, retrieval, prompt compression, in-batch sample selection), first write it as an IB / channel-capacity objective, then consider approximations. This is more principled than immediately reaching for a heuristic.
- When you see five or more heuristics fighting each other in a field, resist the urge to add a sixth. Instead ask: *what is their parent formula?* Write it down — that may be your next paper.
- In your next literature survey, try the reverse-reading method: follow the related-work citations backward three layers from the most recent paper, then narrate it forward. You will understand the field faster and more deeply than reading in chronological order.
