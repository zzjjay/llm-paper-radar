* The River of Problems

Compress an 8-billion-parameter language model to 2-bit, and you might expect something like
"a rougher 4-bit" — instead the model dies outright, accuracy drops to zero, and all it can
say is "the" "." ".". Squeeze to 4-bit and it still resembles itself; squeeze one notch
further and it falls off a cliff.

That cliff has been a black box for thirty years. Every method has been doing the same thing:
*staring at numerical error and trying to make it smaller*. Round weights to the nearest grid
point, minimize reconstruction error, smooth out activation distributions — every paper is
racing to minimize MSE. Yet even when MSE is driven down to near-zero, the 2-bit model still
dies.

Every milestone along this river is, at its core, asking the same question: *which "part" of
the weights cannot be compressed without breaking the model?* The answer has been upgrading
over thirty years — from "look at second derivatives" to "look at the inverse Hessian" to
"look at activation outliers," and finally to this paper: *stop looking at numbers; look at
the computational mechanisms inside the model.*

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 1989 | OBD: Which Connections Should a Neural Network Cut?

*** What Problem It Saw

By the late 1980s, neural networks already knew they needed pruning — too many parameters
led to overfitting, and deployment was slow. The standard approach at the time was
straightforward: look for weights with the smallest absolute value and cut them. "A weak
signal on this connection means it probably doesn't matter."

But LeCun spotted the flaw immediately: *a small weight does not mean an unimportant one*.
A weight can be numerically tiny yet have the loss function be exquisitely sensitive to it —
nudge it and the whole network collapses. Conversely, a large weight might sit in a saturated
region where any change barely affects the output. Pruning by magnitude is therefore
indiscriminate killing.

*** Its Solution

Switch the criterion: *look at the second derivative of the loss L with respect to each
weight w*.

The intuition: if the second derivative is large, L has steep "curvature" near that weight —
any small change causes L to spike — so this weight must not be touched. If the curvature is
flat, the weight can be cut. Formalized, this is the "saliency":

#+begin_example
  s_i = (1/2) * h_ii * w_i^2
#+end_example

where h_ii is the diagonal element of the Hessian matrix. Prune the weights with the smallest
s_i, and the loss rises least.

*** What Pit It Left Behind

It only looks at the *diagonal* of the Hessian — it assumes the second-order effects of each
weight can be computed independently. This is a lazy approximation: in reality weights are
tightly coupled, and pruning one often affects a whole neighborhood through the off-diagonal
terms. That pit would wait four years for OBS to fill.

** 1993 | OBS: Compensate While You Cut

*** What Problem It Saw in the Previous Paper

Hassibi and Stork attacked OBD's approximation directly. They ran experiments on small
networks comparing "prune a weight + leave everything else unchanged" against "prune a weight
+ apply H^{-1}-based compensation updates"; the latter's accuracy loss was an order of
magnitude smaller.

More critically — after OBD pruning, you still had to retrain to recover accuracy. If there
were a way to prune and *skip retraining* entirely, that would be truly useful.

*** Its Solution

Throw away the diagonal approximation and use the *full inverse Hessian* H^{-1}. Each time
a weight w_q is pruned, all remaining weights are simultaneously updated according to:

#+begin_example
  delta_w = -(w_q / [H^{-1}]_qq) * H^{-1}_:,q
#+end_example

The meaning: the "task" previously carried by the pruned weight is redistributed to the
other weights according to Hessian correlations. This step is called a *closed-form update*
and requires no backpropagation whatsoever.

*** What Pit It Left Behind

The inverse Hessian is a d×d matrix, where d is the number of weights. Computing H^{-1}
once costs O(d^3) memory and O(d^3) computation. Networks in the 1990s had only a few
thousand parameters — manageable — but as networks grew, this became untenable. So OBS was
essentially shelved after deep learning took off, until someone remembered it thirty years
later.

** 2022 | OBQ: Transplanting OBS to Quantization

*** What Problem It Saw

Fast-forward to 2022. Deep learning models had ballooned from thousands of parameters to
hundreds of billions. Pruning still happened, but a bigger demand had arrived: *quantization*.
Compress FP32 weights to INT8, INT4, INT2 — cutting memory and bandwidth by 4–16×.

But quantization methods at the time were quite crude: either round-to-nearest (each weight
independently rounded to the nearest grid point, coupling entirely ignored), or QAT
(quantization-aware training, requiring a full model retrain, cost explosion).

Frantar and Alistarh asked: could the *closed-form compensation* idea from pruning be
ported over? "Round one weight, then update all others"?

*** Its Solution

Redefine "pruning = set weight to zero" as "quantization = round weight to a grid point."
All of OBS's mathematics carries over:

#+begin_example
  OBS: after pruning w_q (→ 0), update other weights via H^{-1}
  OBQ: after rounding w_q, update other weights via H^{-1}
#+end_example

Each quantized weight gets one optimal compensation solve. This is *Optimal Brain
Quantization*.

In practice, OBQ significantly outperforms round-to-nearest on small models (BERT, ResNet)
without any retraining. Theoretically it is also very elegant — a direct port of a
1990s-era tool into a new setting.

*** What Pit It Left Behind

Still the old OBS problem: *too slow to run*. A full OBQ pass is O(d^4). On BERT (~110M
parameters) it was still feasible; on GPT-3 (175B) it was completely impossible. Frantar
himself acknowledged this limitation at the time — which is precisely why GPTQ appeared a
year later.

** 2023 | GPTQ: Engineering to Run on Hundred-Billion-Parameter Models

*** What Problem It Saw

OBQ had the right idea, but was too slow. Specifically:
1. It used a "greedy" ordering — each step selects the column with the smallest current
   error. This requires re-scanning all remaining columns after every quantized column,
   causing complexity to explode;
2. It maintained a separate inverse Hessian for each row. GPT-3 has tens of thousands of
   columns; updating every row each time is completely unrealistic.

*** Its Solution

The Frantar team made three engineering compromises to reduce O(d^4) to O(d^3):

1. *Fixed order*: abandon greedy column selection; quantize in a fixed order (left to right).
   Sacrifice a little theoretical optimality for an enormous speedup;
2. *Lazy batch updates*: synchronize H^{-1} only once every 128 columns. GPU-friendly;
3. *Cholesky decomposition*: reparameterize H^{-1} via Cholesky, ensuring numerical
   stability and reuse of the upper-triangular structure.

Result: *OPT-175B 4-bit quantization in just 4 GPU-hours*, with near-zero loss. 3-bit is
also usable. GPTQ became overnight the de facto standard for LLM quantization; all subsequent
work uses it as a baseline.

*** What Pit It Left Behind

GPTQ pushed weight-only PTQ to the edge of "compress any further and it collapses" — 4-bit
is fine, 3-bit is marginal, 2-bit is completely broken. But GPTQ cannot tell you *why* 2-bit
breaks. Its entire mathematical framework is "minimize reconstruction error"; it can guarantee
"after rounding, the output distribution is as close to FP16 as possible," but it cannot see
"which internal component has been knocked out."

That blind spot would not be torn open until this paper, in 2026.

** 2023-2024 | AWQ/SmoothQuant/QuIP#/SpinQuant: A Parallel Branch Driving the Nail Deeper

*** What Problem They Saw

After GPTQ, it became clear that the real challenge was not weights but *outliers in
activations*. Certain channels in LLM activations can spike to 100× the normal value; at
quantization time, these outliers are either clipped (losing information) or they blow up the
entire scale (destroying the precision of all normal values).

*** Their Solutions (One Line Each)

- *SmoothQuant* (Xiao 2023): "migrate" some of the activation outlier magnitude onto the
  weights, making both easier to quantize;
- *AWQ* (Lin 2024): apply per-channel scaling by activation magnitude to protect the weight
  channels that correspond to large activations;
- *QuIP#* (Tseng 2024) / *SpinQuant* (Liu 2025): rotate weights and activations with a
  *random orthogonal matrix*, spreading outliers into a uniform distribution in the new
  coordinate system.

These methods progressively pushed 4-bit to near-lossless and made 3-bit usable.

*** What They Did Not Solve

*They never answered "why does collapse happen."* Their entire objective is "make output as
close to FP16 as possible." So they can reduce MSE and perplexity, but 2-bit still dies —
because the problem is not numerical precision; it is that *the model's internal computational
mechanisms have broken down*. That is what forced this paper into existence.

** 2025 | RMSNorm Reversal: The First Person to Look Inside

*** What Problem It Saw

Chang et al. (2025) were the first to stop fixating on "reducing error" and instead ask:
*what is actually happening inside a quantized model?* Using mechanistic interpretability
tools to inspect a 2-bit model, they discovered a concrete failure phenomenon — on certain
tokens, the direction after RMSNorm is reversed (sign reversal), causing all downstream
computation to be completely misaligned.

*** Its Solution

Identify RMSNorm as the vulnerable point and protect it specifically (e.g., keep it at high
precision).

*** What Pit It Left Behind

The perspective was *too local*: it only saw one phenomenon (RMSNorm reversal) and did not
answer "is this the only cause of failure, or just the tip of the iceberg?" Contemporary
mechanistic works of the same period — component sensitivity analyses, studies of collapsed
refusal mechanisms, truthfulness drift analyses — all suffered from the same limitation:
*each blind man feeling a different part of the elephant*. The entire motivation of this paper
is: we need a *systematic diagnostic framework*, not a pile of isolated phenomena.

** 2026 | Two Failure Modes: One Cut Revealing Two Distinct Deaths

*** What Problem It Saw

Looking back along the entire river, the authors realized something: *4-bit failure and 2-bit
failure may not be the same thing at all*. Everyone had previously assumed they were
continuous — "4-bit loses 1%, 3-bit loses 5%, 2-bit loses 99%" — differing only in degree.
But the 99% number itself is screaming: "This is not a matter of degree; it is a phase
transition."

*** Its Solution (Three Core Steps)

*Step 1: Layer-wise knowledge probing.* Use logit lens to project each layer's hidden state
into vocabulary space and observe how the correct answer's probability evolves across layers.
Results:
- FP16 model: correct-answer probability rises gradually in the middle-to-late layers;
- 4-bit model: the curve has almost the same shape, just a slightly lower endpoint —
  *the signal is still there, merely attenuated*;
- 2-bit model: starting from layers two or three, the correct answer is completely buried
  under high-frequency tokens like "the / . / ." and never recovers —
  *the signal is destroyed in the early layers*.

*Step 2: Causal analysis.* Transplant a layer's activations from the good model into the
corresponding position of the bad model:
- Transplant a FP16 layer into the 4-bit model → performance recovers substantially
  (conclusion: *components still work; they were just receiving polluted input*);
- Transplant a FP16 layer into the 2-bit model → no effect
  (conclusion: *the components themselves are broken; even clean input cannot fix them*).

This gives rise to the *Two Failure Modes Hypothesis*:

| Mode | Manifestation | Nature | Analogy |
|------+---------------+--------+---------|
| Signal Degradation | 4-bit type | Components functionally intact; input polluted by noise | An old photo fading |
| Computation Collapse | 2-bit type | Structural failure of components themselves | Camera lens shattered |

*Step 3: Mechanistic validation + targeted intervention.*

For Signal Degradation: locate the "First Domino" (the earliest layer that begins amplifying
error) and apply a lightweight *two-stage repair strategy* (training-free); performance can
be recovered.

For Computation Collapse: apply the same repair — failure. Apply low-rank compensation —
failure. Conclusion: *compensation cannot save a collapsed model; structural reconstruction
is required* (e.g., QAT or retraining).

*** This Is a Watershed

It shifts LLM quantization research from "minimize numerical error" toward "diagnose internal
mechanisms." Once this framework is accepted, the question future PTQ papers must answer is
no longer only "what is the PPL?" but also "are you fixing Degradation or Collapse?"

* Core Insight

Reading back over this entire river, what is truly changing is *the ontology with which
researchers view "compression loss"*:

| Era | What loss is treated as |
|-----+-------------------------|
| 1989–1993 | A *scalar* (increment of the loss function) |
| 2022–2024 | A *distributional distance* (gap between output distribution and FP16) |
| 2025–2026 | A *mechanical failure* (which component failed, and is it noise or structural damage?) |

This is a paradigm shift from "numerical optimization problem" to "mechanism diagnosis
problem." In the OBD/OBS era we asked "which connection can we most afford to cut?"; in the
GPTQ era we asked "how do we minimize error?"; this paper's question has become "which
capability of the model has been broken, and can it be repaired?"

At a deeper level: *as models grow ever larger and ever more organism-like, quantization
research is quietly shifting from signal processing to neurology*. We are no longer talking
about "signal-to-noise ratio" but starting to talk about "which brain region is still
functional and which has died" — logit lens and causal tracing were originally developed for
analyzing "why does the model know this?"; now they are being turned around to analyze "why
does it no longer know?"

* Where to Go Next

The paper openly acknowledges two limitations at the end: it only examined weight-only PTQ,
and only used factual recall tasks. Three directions for follow-up work are foreseeable:

1. *Extend the diagnostic dimensions*: Do activation quantization and KV-cache quantization
   also exhibit two distinct failure modes? Where is the Computation Collapse boundary on
   complex reasoning tasks (GSM8K, math, code)?
2. *Structural methods targeting Collapse*: Since pure PTQ cannot repair Collapse, should
   *mixed-precision* (keeping critical early layers at high precision) be treated as a
   first-class design choice? Or can lightweight QLoRA-style fine-tuning perform local
   reconstruction?
3. *Predictability of the Collapse threshold*: Can we predict, *before* quantizing, whether
   a given model + bit-width configuration will collapse? If so, the "catastrophe" could be
   surfaced at model-selection time rather than deployment time.

A more radical direction: take this mechanistic diagnostic framework and *reverse its
direction* — use it not to diagnose quantization failure but to diagnose fine-tuning failure,
prompt injection attacks, or catastrophic forgetting. If the concept of "Two Failure Modes"
can generalize across settings, it would not merely be a quantization-analysis paper; it
would provide the first vocabulary for "model failure diagnosis" across all of deep learning.
