* The River of the Problem

You ask a large model to process a hundred-thousand-word document. Every time it generates a new token, it must look back at the keys and values of every previous token. That heap of keys and values is the *KV cache*. It grows linearly with sequence length, and each access drags the whole thing out of GPU memory.

In the era of agents and test-time compute, sequences routinely stretch to hundreds of thousands of tokens, and the KV cache devours both GPU memory and memory bandwidth simultaneously.

For nine years everyone has been grinding on the same question: can we *store* less, or *read* less of the KV, without making the model dumber? Every step forward is another bite of the same bone: which tokens matter, which can be discarded, when to judge, and how.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Transformer: The Origin of Everything — and the Source of All Debt

*** What problem it saw

RNNs process one token at a time serially; long-range dependencies fade layer by layer. CNNs are bounded by their kernel width. Neither can capture "the relationship between word 1 and word 1,000."

*** Its solution

Every token attends directly to every other token. Three matrices Q, K, V; one softmax; all relationships laid on the table at once.

*** The debt it buried

Inference is *autoregressive*: generating each new token requires recomputing the K and V of every past token. To avoid redundant computation, they are cached — this is the *KV cache*.

It grows *linearly* with sequence length. For a 70B model with a 100K context, the KV cache can consume tens of gigabytes. Everything everyone has done in the nine years since is, at its core, paying back this debt.

** 2020 | Longformer: If You Can't Attend to Everything, Decide in Advance What to Attend To

*** What problem it saw in the predecessor

Full attention is intractable for long documents — both O(N^2) compute and O(N) memory are prohibitive. But you cannot simply abandon long-range information.

*** Its solution

Each token attends only to a local sliding window (e.g., 512 tokens), plus a small number of "global tokens" that every token can communicate with. The pattern is *fixed* and *hand-designed*.

If you let the global positions be arbitrary and turn "is this token global?" into a learned scalar, you begin moving toward H2O.

*** The debt it buried

The pattern is written by a human. But *which token actually matters* is determined by the data at hand, not by its position in the sequence. In a source file the critical tokens are function names; in a contract they are monetary figures — scanning with the same fixed pattern wastes half your bandwidth on unimportant positions.

** 2023.06 | H2O: Let the Data Tell You Who Gets Cut

*** What problem it saw in its predecessor

Longformer treats fixed positions as "important," but importance is *data-dependent*. The same position can be critical in one input and irrelevant in another.

*** Its solution

Run a forward pass and record the cumulative attention score each token *receives* from others. Tokens with high cumulative scores are "heavy hitters."

At inference, keep only heavy hitters plus a recent sliding window; evict the rest from the KV cache. No retraining required — a purely *post-hoc* pruning strategy.

*** The debt it buried

Two fissures form here that the next three papers will excavate:

1. *Train-test mismatch*: the model *trains* on a complete KV cache, always. It has never experienced "some of your past tokens have been hidden from you." Handing it a pruned cache at inference time is handing it an environment its internal representations were never prepared for.
2. *The sink-token phenomenon*: in practice, the first few tokens (BOS, punctuation, etc.) must always be kept or the attention distribution explodes. H2O has no explanation; it just keeps them empirically.

** 2023.10 | StreamingLLM: Giving Sink Tokens an Official Name

*** What problem it saw in its predecessor

H2O must always keep the first few tokens to remain stable — a fact H2O itself never addressed head-on.

*** Its solution

Acknowledge this as a distinct phenomenon — *attention sinks*. Softmax forces attention weights to sum to one, but sometimes the model has nothing it really "wants" to look at, so it dumps the excess attention into the opening tokens as a garbage bin.

The policy simplifies to: always keep the first 4 tokens + a recent sliding window of size N. Everything else is discarded. One rule, even simpler than H2O's statistics.

*** The debt it buried

It strips away H2O's statistics in exchange for dramatically more stable extreme compression (enabling streaming inputs of millions of tokens). But it shares H2O's fundamental disease: *post-hoc*. The model's weights have no idea its cache is being pruned; it has no preparation for what it will look like after pruning.

The higher the compression ratio, the deeper this wound.

** 2025 | DMS: Making the Model "Know" It Will Be Pruned

*** What problem it saw in its predecessors

H2O and StreamingLLM both take a dense pretrained model and apply sparse inference directly. The model has *never* been trained under the condition "your KV will be pruned."

*** Its solution

*Continue training* on a pretrained checkpoint for a short period. The goal is to teach the model an eviction policy. To keep the model from collapsing during this brief training, a *teacher* is introduced: distill from the original dense model's output logits, overlaid with a sparsification loss.

The model therefore trains while being pruned, learning what environment it will inhabit at inference time.

*** The debt it buried

The direction is right, but the path is roundabout:

- Requires an external teacher (the dense version of itself) to stabilize training
- Requires three objectives stitched together: distillation loss + sparsification loss + LM loss
- The pipeline is far more complex than any post-hoc method

If the model could learn to tolerate pruning *with only* next-token loss — the teacher and distillation would be unnecessary scaffolding.

** 2026.04 | KVZap: Learning a Scorer Smarter Than Statistics

*** What problem it saw in its predecessors

DMS modifies the model weights — complicated. H2O's attention-mean scoring is passive statistics — not accurate enough. Can we *leave the model weights frozen* and just learn a lightweight scorer that evicts better than H2O?

*** Its solution

Freeze the pretrained LLM. Train a separate *KV utility predictor* — a lightweight neural network that takes a token's hidden state and outputs a "how useful will this be in the future?" score.

Use that score to drive eviction at inference time. Smarter than H2O's statistics; simpler pipeline than DMS.

*** The debt it buried

It solves "is the scorer smart enough?" while sidestepping the deeper question of "does the model itself know?" The LLM weights have still never seen a pruned cache.

That train-test gap remains ferocious at high compression ratios: in SP-KV's Table 2, KVZap's NLL rises +1.23% at ~20% density and +1.77% at 15% density — the strongest result in the post-hoc camp, yet still an order of magnitude worse than SP-KV.

** 2026.05 | SP-KV: Tearing Down All the Scaffolding

*** What problem it saw in its predecessors

DMS identified the right direction (the model must *know* it will be pruned) but leaned on the scaffolding of teacher + distillation. KVZap identified the right simplicity (lightweight scorer) but kept the LLM frozen and never truly resolved the train-test gap.

Each was half right.

*** Its solution

Do both things at once:

1. A lightweight utility predictor — same intuition as KVZap — that scores each KV pair.
2. But *don't freeze the LLM*. Train LLM weights and the predictor *together*, end-to-end, with *only* next-token loss as the objective.

During training, replace the hard threshold with differentiable gating (the paper uses a continuous gate with temperature annealing) so gradients can flow through. At inference, switch back to a hard threshold: if the score clears the bar, write to cache; if not, discard. The most recent 128 tokens are always retained (absorbing StreamingLLM's lesson).

If you take KVZap's frozen LLM and unfreeze it, then take DMS and remove the teacher, the distillation loss, and every sparsification auxiliary objective, leaving only next-token loss — you get SP-KV.

*** Several unexpected benefits

- *No fixed compression ratio*: the model decides for itself whether to write each position. The result is that *longer inputs compress more* (more redundancy in long sequences), averaging 3–10× compression.
- *NLL barely moves*: at 25% density, NLL rises only +0.08% — roughly one-tenth to one-twentieth of KVZap's degradation.
- *Byproduct: architecture guidance*: examining the average utility learned by each head reveals that sparsity distributes in a structured pattern across layers and heads. Designating high-utility heads as global and low-utility heads as local yields a hybrid architecture that outperforms standard interleaved designs.

*** The new questions it leaves open

- Joint training requires continued pretraining; the compute cost is higher than KVZap's lightweight post-hoc approach (though cheaper than training DMS from scratch)
- The threshold is set by hand; the optimal threshold across different tasks and sequence lengths is still being explored
- Whether this mechanism transfers to more aggressive architectures (MLA, Gated DeltaNet, etc.) has not been verified

The entire arc is doing one thing: evolving the decision of "whether to retain a given KV" from *hand-coded positional rules*, to *passive statistics over history*, to *actively learned scoring*; and simultaneously evolving the model itself from *completely unaware*, to *aware but taught by distillation*, to *aware and self-taught*.

* The Core Insight

The true theme of this nine-year evolutionary arc is not "how to compress the KV cache."

It is *train-inference consistency* — an older, more fundamental topic — playing out again in a new setting.

- H2O / StreamingLLM / KVZap: attempt to patch the consistency crack from the *inference side* with progressively smarter strategies
- DMS: the first to acknowledge that the crack must be sealed from the *training side*, but reaches for the traditional crutch of teacher-student distillation
- SP-KV: discovers the crutch can be thrown away — if the sparsification mechanism is *present from the very first training step*, next-token loss will teach the model everything it needs to know

This is actually the same lesson deep learning has re-learned repeatedly in recent years:
- Dropout was initially applied post-hoc; it was later found that it must be present during training
- Quantization-aware training defeated post-training quantization
- Mixed-precision training defeated reduce-precision-after-training

Every time, the same story: *don't patch things up after the fact — let the model live, from the very beginning, in the environment where it will ultimately have to work*.

* Where Things Might Go Next

1. *The joint-training + minimal-loss paradigm will be reused* — a similar recipe can be applied to quantization-aware, tool-use-aware, and retrieval-aware training. The essence is "let the model learn under its true inference environment."
2. *Architecture and scoring unified* — SP-KV's byproduct (using learned head utility to guide local/global design) hints at a more aggressive direction: KV cache compression, head-type selection, and layer-type selection all folded into a single joint optimization objective, growing the optimal architecture out of training rather than hand-ablating it.
3. *The dynamic-vs-fixed compression ratio pendulum* — production deployments prefer *predictable* memory footprints; SP-KV's "longer inputs compress more" property actually makes memory budgeting harder to plan. Someone will build a "guaranteed-at-most-X%-density SP-KV variant."
4. *Convergence with sparse-read methods* — SP-KV decides "whether to write"; DeepSeek Sparse Attention decides "whether to read." The next paper will likely put both into the same end-to-end objective and optimize them simultaneously.
