* The River of Problems

One language model scores 85% on a benchmark, another scores 83% — you assume the first is stronger. But what you don't know is: rephrase the question, and the first drops to 10% while the second stands firm. Or worse, that 85% isn't the model's achievement at all — it's your *scoring script* being too rigid, marking the other model's correct answers as wrong.

This is the hole everyone has been slowly climbing out of for the past six years: *a single prompt plus a single number simply cannot tell you how reliable a model is*. Each generation sees the blind spots left by the previous one, and each generation's "clearer view" leaves new blind spots of its own.

The protagonist of this river is not any particular model — it is the question of *"are we actually measuring what we think we are measuring?"* — and how that question grew from an unnoticed footnote into the central issue in SLM evaluation today.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2020 | GPT-3: Making "One Prompt, One Score" the New Normal

Before GPT-3, evaluating a model was straightforward — fine-tune on the training set, score on the test set. After GPT-3, things changed: no fine-tuning; write a prompt ("Translate the following sentence:") and the model answers directly. This in-context learning made evaluation *look extremely simple*: write a prompt, run once, record accuracy.

But that "simplicity" planted a time bomb — *from this point on, a model's performance was tightly coupled to how you wrote the prompt*. The GPT-3 paper itself didn't worry much about this, since its headline was "look, a model this large can few-shot." But the next generation would soon face an awkward truth: change the prompt, and the score changes completely.

** 2021 | Calibrate Before Use: The First Time Someone Shouted "Wait — the Score Is Wobbling"

Zhao et al. did one thing: they ran GPT-3 on SST-2 sentiment classification and deliberately varied only the order, wording, and positive/negative ratio of the few-shot examples — *accuracy fell from 90% to 50%*.

What does that mean? It means you take the same model and the same test set, shuffle the four examples in the prompt, and the conclusion flips.

Their diagnosis: few-shot prompts were quietly adding *bias* to model outputs — for instance, the label of the last example was more likely to be echoed. The fix was to estimate this bias using "content-free inputs" (e.g., N/A) and then correct for it.

But this only addressed *one variety* of prompt sensitivity (in-context examples), and after correction everyone still reported a single number. The bedrock of the evaluation paradigm remained untouched — *one prompt, one number*.

The next person would immediately ask: what about everything outside in-context examples? What about the instruction itself?

** 2022 | Webson & Pavlick: Models May Not Be "Reading" Instructions at All

This paper is a challenge, not a fix.

They ran a simple experiment: write 30 semantically reasonable instructions ("Determine whether the following sentence is sarcastic"), then write 30 *complete nonsense instructions* ("If the moon is made of cheese, output yes"), and have the model perform NLI.

Result: accuracy across the two groups was *about the same*.

This finding cuts deep. It says: when you write a prompt asking the model to classify something, the model most likely is *not understanding* your instruction — it is using certain *surface features* of the prompt to trigger a pattern-matching response.

This punched a hole through the semantic foundation of prompt-based evaluation. When you test a model and say "it achieves 85% accuracy on this task," you think you're measuring "it understood the task" — but what you're actually measuring is "its reaction to this specific prompt template."

Webson & Pavlick offered no alternative; they just placed the problem on the table. The next generation would begin asking: which *specific* surface features are to blame?

** 2023 | FormatSpread: Even Swapping a Colon for a Space Can Shift Scores from 0.04 to 0.85

Sclar et al. pushed "prompt sensitivity" to an extreme: they defined a "semantics-preserving format transformation space" — the same question could be written as ~Q: ... A:~, ~Question: ... Answer:~, ~q) ... a)~, ~Q | ... | A |~, and so on. These transformations *changed no semantics*, only punctuation, spacing, and capitalisation.

They then enumerated this space and ran the same model on the same task. For LLaMA-2-7B on certain tasks, *the worst format scored 0.04 and the best scored 0.85* — same model, same dataset, same semantic content, *only the prompt layout differed*.

This result made Webson & Pavlick's challenge concrete: models really are using surface features. And those "surface" details are fine enough that you would never expect them to matter.

Sclar's methodology was also a breakthrough — *report a spread* (min–max accuracy across formats) rather than a single number. But accuracy was still the only thing she watched; calibration, verbal confidence, and scoring scripts were untouched.

Their approach was also expensive: enumerating hundreds of formats. The next generation would want: *methods that capture spread with far fewer prompts*.

** 2024 | Mizrahi et al.: Stop Publishing Papers with a Single Prompt

Mizrahi et al. is a manifesto. Their argument: FormatSpread had made the problem clear, but *top-venue 2024 papers were still reporting single-prompt scores*. Such rankings are essentially prompt-luck — swap in a set of semantically equivalent paraphrases and model A and model B may switch places.

Their fix: use a set of semantics-preserving paraphrases (lighter-weight than Sclar's format enumeration), run multiple prompts, report mean ± std of the distribution.

The call to action was useful — HELM, BIG-bench, and similar frameworks subsequently added multi-prompt options. But the work was *still watching accuracy alone*, and it *still assumed the scoring script was bug-free* — for instance, it assumed a first-character scorer would always correctly read the model's answer.

That assumption of "bug-free scoring script" is the next hole MVRA punctures.

** 2026 | MVRA: The Scoring Script Itself Is Manufacturing Invisible Bugs

Karmakar & Chatterjee stand on Mizrahi's shoulders and push one step further. They say: fine, we accept multi-prompt as a requirement. So let's do it rigorously — 15 open-source SLMs in the 1–8B range, 5 benchmarks, 5 prompt variants per benchmark, 5 quantities per cell: accuracy, token-ECE, verbal-ECE, verbal parse rate (VPR), and prompt-perturbation spread.

Running this grid reveals *four independent blind spots*, each lying outside Mizrahi's methodology:

*Blind spot 1: Scorer–prompt incompatibility can manufacture false collapses.* On ARC-Challenge, prompting models to chain-of-thought reason (an extremely common prompt variant) caused five major models to plummet from ~80% accuracy to 9.8%–19.8% — below the 0.25 random baseline for a 4-choice question. It looks as though CoT broke the models. But two independent "fixes" tell a different story: reparse outputs with a regex matching "Final answer: X" and you recover 93.8% of the gap; use constrained decoding to force the model to emit one letter and you recover 102.7%. Conclusion: *the models didn't break — the first-character scorer failed on CoT output*, because the first token is "The…" or "Looking…" and gets marked wrong.

What makes this frightening? *Nobody thinks to question the scoring script.* Everyone would conclude that CoT genuinely hurts ARC-Challenge performance.

*Blind spot 2: ECE, a "standard" metric, is secretly two different algorithms.* What is uniformly called "ECE_token" can mean either the raw probability ~P(predicted letter)~ or the normalised probability ~P(predicted letter) / sum(P(all valid letters))~. Both are common, but they share a name while implementing different formulas. Averaged across 125 cells, the absolute difference is 0.149. 54% of cells diverge by more than 0.05, 24% by more than 0.20; the largest single-cell gap is 0.825 (0.891 vs. 0.066).

If one paper uses raw and another uses normalised, comparing ECE values means comparing *algorithms*, not models.

*Blind spot 3: "Verbal confidence works" is actually two separate claims bundled together.*
- (a) Is the probability the model states accurate? On MMLU-Pro, every model's verbal confidence exceeds its accuracy by 60–78 percentage points; ECE_verbal − ECE_token is always positive. They are *systematically overconfident*.
- (b) Can what the model says even be parsed into a probability? Mistral-7B on one prompt variant has VPR = 0.002 (only 1 in 500 answers yields a parseable number). But *switch to another prompt and VPR recovers to 1.0* — this is a *prompt-specific* failure, not a model property.

If you average VPR across prompts as previous work does, you get 0.6–0.7, which looks "acceptable" — but you have *quietly discarded 20–25% of verbal outputs*, and the discarding is uneven.

*Blind spot 4: Parameter count does not predict prompt robustness, at least in the 1–8B range.* Across 10 instruct models, the Spearman correlation between parameter count and spread ranges from −0.244 to +0.474, all with p > 0.10. The cleanest contrast: Phi-4-mini (3.8B) has a spread of 0.084 on SST-2; Mistral-7B (7.2B) has a spread of 0.500 — nearly twice the parameters but *six times worse robustness*.

MVRA's ultimate claim is not "we propose a new metric" but rather: *a benchmark score should be a deployment-readiness profile, not a single number*. Report all five quantities for every cell, so readers can see how prompts and scorers interact, see under which prompts verbal confidence becomes unparseable, and see how widely accuracy scatters across prompt variants.

** Looking Ahead | Not Yet Formed

This paper appeared on arXiv on 3 May 2026; no explicit follow-up work is visible yet. But predictable directions include:

- *Automated evaluator–prompt compatibility linting* — the "CoT × first-char scorer" bug MVRA found by hand becomes a tool that automatically scans all prompt–scorer combinations before a new benchmark goes live.
- *Verbal parseability as a training objective* — since models frequently produce output from which no probability can be extracted, training this capability explicitly (or as an RL reward) is a natural next step.
- *Spread metrics entering HELM / Open LLM Leaderboard* — single-point accuracy will yield to reporting median + 25th/75th percentiles; the leaderboard format will change.

A deeper conjecture: as agent and multi-model collaborative systems proliferate, *calibration and parseability will matter more than accuracy* — because an agent must use the model's "confidence in its own answer" to decide whether to invoke a stronger model or tool. A model with 87% accuracy but verbal confidence locked at 0.99 is useless to an agent system. The evaluation paradigm MVRA opens up is precisely the one that future will require.

* Insights

** From "Testing the Model" to "Testing the Entire Pipeline"

On the surface this lineage is about "ever finer-grained prompt sensitivity," but underneath a deeper cognitive shift is happening:

- 2020: the object of evaluation is the *model*. Given a model, run it, get a score.
- 2026: the object of evaluation is the *(model × prompt × scorer × calibration definition × parser) 5-tuple*. Change any one component and the conclusion may reverse entirely.

In other words, over six years the field has slowly come to recognise: *"model capability" is not a standalone property — it only has meaning inside a specific evaluation pipeline*. When you say "Phi-4-mini scores 80% on ARC-Challenge," you are really saying "Phi-4-mini + this prompt template + this scorer + this calibration formula scores 80% on ARC-Challenge" — remove or swap any component and that 80% ceases to exist.

This feels something like the uncertainty principle in physics. It's not that the model is changing — it's that *the act of measurement itself* intervenes in what is being measured.

** Where the Next Step Is Most Likely to Land

Three directions seem most promising:

1. *Evaluator–Prompt Compatibility Linter.* The "CoT + first-char scorer" bug MVRA found was discovered by hand — the natural next step is turning this into an automated tool that scans all prompt–scorer combinations before a new benchmark goes live.
2. *Verbal Parseability as a Training Objective.* Unparseable verbal confidence is a frightening *silent failure* — the model is speaking, but your downstream system cannot extract a number. Making "produce parseable probabilities" an explicit training objective (or RL reward) is a direct path forward.
3. *Pushing Spread into HELM / Open LLM Leaderboard.* Single-point accuracy will inevitably give way to reporting median + 25th/75th percentile. The leaderboard format will evolve.

A deeper conjecture: as agent and multi-model collaborative systems become widespread, *calibration and parseability will outrank accuracy in importance* — because an agent must consult the model's expressed confidence to decide whether to escalate to a stronger model or tool. A model with 87% accuracy but verbal confidence permanently at 0.99 is useless in an agent system. The evaluation paradigm this paper opens is exactly the one that future will demand.
