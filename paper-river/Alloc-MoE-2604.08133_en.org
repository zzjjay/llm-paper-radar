* The Problem River

During MoE inference, every token must light up several experts to compute; the more experts activated, the more latency explodes. Can we activate fewer experts without losing accuracy?

The difficulty lies in a simple fact: experts do not contribute equally. In some layers, for some tokens, removing one expert causes a collapse; in others it hardly matters at all. Yet if you apply a blanket rule (Top-K=2 everywhere), you are cutting "can't-be-skipped" positions and "easily-skipped" positions with the same blade — and you suffer on both ends: the positions that could have been skipped are not skipped enough, and the ones that cannot afford to be skipped are cut too deeply.

Every generation of researchers on this river has answered the same question: *Given a fixed compute budget, how do you spend the "expert activation" coin where it counts most?* From Shazeer's "sparsely-gated" a decade ago to Alloc-MoE in 2026 writing "budget" right into the title, the question has not changed — only the granularity keeps getting finer.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2017 | Shazeer et al.: One Idea Ignites MoE — "Don't Make All Parameters Work for Every Token"

*** What problem it saw

In 2017 the community was scaling up models, but scaling meant every token had to pass through all parameters — FLOPs rose linearly and training costs exploded.

Shazeer noticed something: *this is actually very wasteful*. A sentence about the weather does not need the "brain circuit" specialized in parsing legal clauses to be activated. A large model does not mean every sample needs to run the entire brain.

*** Its solution

Split the heavy FFN block into N small experts, then add a "gatekeeper" — a lightweight gating network that computes N scores per token, selects only the top K experts to activate, and ignores all others for that step.

The formula is just one line: =y = sum_{i in TopK} w_i * E_i(x)=. But the idea is large: *sparse activation, on-demand invocation*.

*** Why it worked (and what new pitfalls it buried)

Total parameter count can be ten or a hundred times larger than a dense model, yet a single forward pass touches only a small fraction. Capacity goes up; compute does not.

Pitfalls buried: First, choosing K experts via the gating is non-differentiable and requires softmax + noise + various tricks to train stably. Second, K is a hyperparameter — whether to pick 2, 4, or 8 is essentially guesswork. Third, expert load is unbalanced — some experts are called constantly while others are never invoked. Much of the next decade's work fills these three holes.

** 2022 | Switch Transformer: Just Use K=1 — Simplify Whatever You Can

*** What problem it saw

The trouble with Top-2 routing (the direction Shazeer later advocated): each token requires two expert computations, communication cost doubles — on TPU pods, cross-device All-to-All communication is the dominant cost. Training stability with K=2 is also poor.

*** Its solution

K=1. Each token visits exactly one expert. The gate picks only the highest-scoring one.

This looks radical but is actually pragmatic: it cuts MoE's engineering complexity in half and frees room to scale the model to a trillion parameters. Switch Transformer demonstrated that K=1 can work, with a load-balancing loss to distribute traffic evenly.

*** Why it worked (and what new pitfalls it buried)

Simplification opened up the scale ceiling. But the cost of K=1 is limited expressiveness — a token can only borrow one expert's "perspective," and complex samples suffer visibly. The quality ceiling is depressed. That hole is left for the next generation to fill.

** 2024.01 | Mixtral 8x7B: Bring K=2 Back, Push MoE onto the LLM Main Stage

*** What problem it saw

K=1 simplified engineering, but in the open-source LLM world it became clear that quality was insufficient. A dense 7B model still outperformed the Switch K=1 equivalent in active parameters.

*** Its solution

Bring back Top-2: eight 7B experts, K=2, 47B total parameters but only ~13B compute per token. Straightforward, but well-tuned. When Mixtral 8x7B was released, its quality matched GPT-3.5, turning "MoE for LLMs" from a paper idea into a product direction.

*** Why it worked (and what new pitfalls it buried)

K=2 gives the model two parallel "thinking channels"; quality recovers. But the pitfalls are laid out plainly on the table:

- Every token activates exactly two experts — simple tokens, complex tokens, stop words, keywords, all treated identically
- Every layer uses K=2 — shallow layers, deep layers, critical layers, redundant layers, all treated identically

This "one-size-fits-all" waste is the attack surface for all subsequent efficient-MoE work.

** 2024 | XMoE (Top-P Routing): Let Each Token Decide How Many Experts to Activate

*** What problem it saw

Top-K is a fixed count, but the "difficulty" of different tokens varies enormously. Why should "the" and a rare technical term activate the same number of experts?

*** Its solution

Mimic nucleus sampling from language model decoding: sort experts by gating score, accumulate, and stop once the cumulative score exceeds threshold P. A simple token might need just one expert; a hard token might need four. Overall average activation count drops; quality is preserved.

*** Why it worked (and what new pitfalls it buried)

Demand-driven allocation is more precise than a flat cut. Pitfalls:

- Requires joint calibration of P during training — not plug-and-play
- Prone to over-activation — in some layers where routing scores are uniformly flat, every token triggers many experts, and the extra cost can exceed the savings

Subsequent Dynamic-MoE added routing-entropy regularization to mitigate this, but the Top-P family never escaped the requirement of training-time intervention.

** 2024 | NAEE / AdapMoE: Post-Training Can Achieve Savings Too

*** What problem it saw

The XMoE family requires training-time modifications, which is unfriendly to already-released open-source models (Mixtral, DeepSeek). The community wanted "install and run" solutions.

*** Its solution

- *NAEE*: Targets Mixtral-style Top-2 models specifically — if the relative weight of the second expert falls below a threshold, skip it. Essentially "is the second vote weighty enough?"
- *AdapMoE*: Runs offline sensitivity analysis on a calibration set to obtain a per-layer skip threshold; at inference time, dynamically decides whether to activate based on gating scores.

Both are post-training and do not touch model weights.

*** Why it worked (and what new pitfalls it buried)

Cheap and plug-and-play. But the pitfalls are glaring:

- NAEE only works for K=2; completely inapplicable to models like DeepSeek with K=6 or K=8
- AdapMoE's thresholds are decided offline and statically; not robust to distribution shift at inference time
- Neither touches "inter-layer budget allocation" — by default every layer is pruned with the same policy

** 2025 | Yang et al. (2025b) / LExI: Start Allocating Across Layers, but Still Coarse

*** What problem it saw

Token-level squeezing had been pushed fairly far, but *cross-layer differences* had never been seriously addressed. Intuitively, some layers (especially shallow and final ones) are extremely sensitive to expert count — cut one and quality drops; middle layers are redundant and can lose half their experts with no effect. A uniform cut is obviously suboptimal.

*** Its solution

Assign different K per layer:

- *Yang et al. (2025b)*: Use heuristic strategies (linearly increasing, decreasing, rule-based) to give each layer a different K, then pick whichever works best
- *LExI*: A data-free post-training method that computes how many experts each layer should receive based on a proxy metric (no calibration set required)

*** Why it worked (and what new pitfalls it buried)

Acknowledging "layers are not equal" is a conceptual advance. But the remaining pitfalls are large:

- Heuristics carry no optimality guarantee; must be re-tuned for every new model
- Not driven by true end-to-end performance — proxy metrics are used instead
- *Token-level and layer-level methods never meet*: once per-layer K is chosen, how should token-level redistribution happen within that layer? Nobody unified the two threads.

** 2026 | Alloc-MoE: Make the "Budget" Explicit, Jointly Optimize Both Levels

*** What problem it saw

Looking back over the whole timeline: every generation has been saving expert activations, but nobody formalized what "saving" means. Saving a fraction? Saving a total count? How much saving is enough? All vague.

Worse, layer-level and token-level methods *have never been jointly optimized under the same mathematical framework* — the former carves the pie (how much per layer), the latter redistributes within a slice (how tokens share within a layer), and the two worlds never intersect. The result is that both are suboptimal.

*** Its solution

Step one: formalize "total activated experts" as an *activation budget B*. For a model with L layers, the global budget B is the total number of expert activations accumulated across all layers for one token (B = Σ K_i). The optimization objective becomes clear: minimize performance loss subject to fixed B.

Step two, two branches:

- *Alloc-L (inter-layer)*: Proposes an allocation-isolating sensitivity profiling method — when profiling layer i, temporarily compress all downstream layers to K=1, so that downstream "compensation" cannot mask layer i's true sensitivity. This yields an L × K_orig sensitivity matrix, which is then fed into *dynamic programming* to solve exactly for the optimal K_i assignment under the budget constraint B. This is a true global optimum, no longer a heuristic.

- *Alloc-T (intra-layer)*: Given the per-layer budget K_i, the T tokens in a batch share T × K_i activation slots, but each token need not receive exactly K_i. Tokens whose routing scores concentrate on a few experts get fewer activations; those with flat score distributions get more — because "uncertainty about whom to ask" means activating more experts is actually protective. This internal redistribution improves quality without increasing total latency.

*** Why it worked (and what new pitfalls it buried)

Once "budget" is made explicit, everything becomes a constrained optimization problem that can be solved to optimality. The two-level joint optimization operates at different time scales: inter-layer is a one-time offline DP producing a static assignment, while intra-layer is online per-batch dynamic adjustment.

Remaining pitfalls (and the starting point for the next paper):

- The sensitivity matrix is profiled using perplexity; relevance to downstream tasks still needs validation
- The global budget B is fixed in advance; no provision for request-level dynamic adjustment (e.g., small budget for a simple prompt, large budget for a complex one)
- The token-level redistribution relies on routing scores, but routing scores themselves may be distorted by budget-constrained training — whether a self-referential bias exists is left unexamined

* Frontier Extensions

Alloc-MoE is an April 2026 paper. As of this note's writing (2026-05-28), no public follow-up work has directly critiqued or improved it. Predictable next steps:

- *Request-level budget*: Upgrade the budget from a model constant to a per-request dynamic variable; let the model itself decide how many experts a simple prompt deserves
- *Training–inference co-design*: All current expert-saving methods are post-training; the next wave may embed "budget awareness" into the router at pretraining time, so the model learns from the start to perform well under varying budgets
- *Joint optimization of budget with quantization / KV-cache*: Inference budget is not only about expert activation — it also spans KV cache size, quantization precision, and attention head pruning, forming a high-dimensional joint optimization space

* Insights

Reading through this timeline, several things stand out.

*First, over a decade "saving" transformed from a verb into a noun.* Early on, Shazeer used it as a verb — "activate fewer"; later it became an adjective — "adaptive activation"; by Alloc-MoE it became a noun — "budget." Once it becomes a noun, it becomes something that can be allocated, optimized, and constrained. This shift from process to object is the hallmark of an engineering problem reaching maturity.

*Second, granularity always moves finer.* First, saving at the model level (choose a smaller K); then at the layer level (different K per layer); then at the token level (different K per token within the same layer); now both levels at once. The next step is almost certainly *request level* and *phase level* (different budgets for prefill vs. decode). The direction of granularity refinement is monotone, because each additional cut squeezes out a little more performance.

*Third, the discourse on optimality has quietly returned over ten years.* Early MoE papers asked "work or not?"; mid-period papers asked "scale or not?"; now papers ask "optimal or not?" Alloc-MoE's use of dynamic programming is essentially a declaration: *everything you used before was a heuristic — can we please solve for the actual optimum?* This is a signal that the engineering community is beginning to borrow from operations research — LLM inference optimization is increasingly resembling classical resource scheduling.

*Fourth, the target of critique has shifted from "another specific paper" to "another class of methods."* Alloc-MoE's criticism is no longer aimed at particular prior work but at the abstract categories of "token-level methods" and "layer-level methods." This signals that the field has moved from "individual breakthroughs" to "school consolidation" — and the next stage is likely a grand unified framework that packs all dimensions (layer, token, request, cache, quantization) into a single optimization problem.

* Takeaways

For those searching for problems:

- *Look at a problem that has been repeatedly improved and ask yourself: "Has it been formalized?"* If people are still using heuristics, thresholds, and rules — that is usually where a paper waiting to be solved with optimization theory is hiding. Alloc-MoE's core innovation is not a new algorithm; it is writing a clean mathematical definition (activation budget) for an old problem.
- *Look at two technical threads developing in parallel and ask: "Can they be jointly optimized under a single objective function?"* Token-level and layer-level methods ran in parallel for years with nobody unifying them; Alloc-MoE unified them and a result came out. Similar opportunities are everywhere: pruning + quantization, KV-cache + attention, prefill + decode.
- *Granularity refinement is the monotone direction of engineering optimization.* When you see a coarse-grained allocation strategy, ask: can it be cut one level finer? Down to token, to head, to step, to request. Every such cut is the location of a paper.

The takeaway: you can apply this "identify the heuristic → formalize it → jointly optimize → solve to exact optimality" decomposition to scan any sub-field of LLM inference optimization today. There is almost certainly a corner still using thresholds — and that is where the next Alloc-X sits.
