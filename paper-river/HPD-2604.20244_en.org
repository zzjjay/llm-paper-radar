* The Problem River

Take a 7-billion-parameter "teacher" model and compress it into a 1.5-billion-parameter "student" model, teaching the smaller model as much of the larger one's capability as possible — that is knowledge distillation (KD).

It sounds simple: just have the student mimic the teacher's output distribution. But over a decade of work, this line of research has kept circling the same question: *What loss function should the student use to imitate the teacher? Should it look at sentences the teacher actually wrote, or at sentences the student itself wrote? How should the two be mixed?* Get any of these wrong and the student either becomes a "degraded photocopy of the teacher" (says a little of everything, resembles nothing), or falls into "self-absorption" (dares to say only one thing, mode collapse).

Each generation of solutions fixes one specific ailment and leaves behind new pitfalls. HPD is the latest installment in this line as of 2026, arguing that all prior approaches can be placed inside a single mathematical framework — and then, within that framework, picking a combination nobody had tried before.

* Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Hinton et al.: The Starting Point of Knowledge Distillation

*** What problem did it see

At the time nobody was compressing language models — the target was image classifiers. A deep network (teacher) outputs softmax probabilities over 1000 classes, e.g., "cat: 0.85, dog: 0.10, tiger: 0.04, other: 0.01." If you only tell the small model (student) "the label is cat," the information that "dog is closer to cat than tiger is" gets completely discarded.

*** Its solution

Have the student learn not just the correct answer but also the teacher's probability distribution over all wrong answers — that is, use the teacher's softmax as "soft labels." Align the two with KL divergence. Add a temperature parameter to smooth the distribution and amplify the hidden similarity information.

*** Why it worked (and what new pitfall it left behind)

The teacher's full distribution encodes dark knowledge — which classes are similar to which. This dark knowledge cannot be conveyed by a single label, but can be conveyed by the full distribution. This is the mother theme of all KD work in the decade that followed.

But language models perform *autoregressive generation* — each step predicts the next token, and one wrong token cascades through everything after it. Simply "computing KL at every token" is not necessarily optimal at the sequence level, and the sequence search space is exponential, making full KL intractable.

** 2016 | Kim & Rush: SeqKD — Distillation at the Sequence Level

*** What problem did it see

Hinton's method is token-level: at each step the student learns the teacher's full softmax for that step. The problem is that in machine translation and generation tasks, what matters is whether the *whole sentence* resembles the teacher, not whether each token's marginal probability does. Also, with large vocabularies (tens of thousands of tokens), computing the full-vocabulary KL at every step is too expensive.

*** Its solution

Simply have the teacher write out a batch of sentences — e.g., beam-search translations in NMT — and then have the student do supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on those "teacher-written" sentences. This is equivalent to sequence-level Forward-KL but uses one-hot labels, making it ten times cheaper.

*** Why it worked (and what new pitfall it left behind)

Teacher-written sentences are "cleaner" than real data — less noise, less long-tail variation. The student learns quickly.

But there is a hidden pitfall that would not be named clearly for another eight years: during training the student sees "teacher-written sentences," but during inference the student sees "its own previously generated tokens." The student is never trained under its own output distribution, so once it drifts it cannot recover. This is *exposure bias / train-inference mismatch*. This pitfall would not be confronted head-on for eight more years.

** 2023 | Gu et al. MiniLLM: Switching from Forward-KL to Reverse-KL

*** What problem did it see

The LLM era arrived, and when people applied SeqKD to GPT-style large models they found that student outputs were "saying a little of everything and resembling nothing" — classic *mode-averaging*. The cause: Forward-KL `KL(p_teacher || q_student)` heavily penalizes places where the teacher assigns probability but the student does not. To avoid being penalized, the student spreads probability mass widely to cover all modes. A small model with limited capacity ends up blurry across every mode.

*** Its solution

Reverse the KL direction: `KL(q_student || p_teacher)`, called Reverse-KL. This direction heavily penalizes places where the student assigns probability but the teacher does not — so the student concentrates mass only on the modes where the teacher is confident. This is called *mode-seeking*. The result is sharper outputs that closely resemble one of the teacher's modes.

*** Why it worked (and what new pitfall it left behind)

The expectation in Reverse-KL is taken over the *student's* distribution, so online sampling is required — the student writes a sentence, then asks the teacher "how much probability do you assign to this?" But when the student and teacher differ greatly, the sentences the student writes have near-zero probability under the teacher; taking the log causes variance to explode, and training becomes unstable. Gu et al. stabilized training with policy-gradient tricks (single-step decomposition, teacher-mixed sampling, etc.), but the cost and instability remained targets for subsequent work.

** 2024 | Agarwal et al. GKD: On-Policy + JSD as a Unified Framework

*** What problem did it see

MiniLLM solved mode-averaging, but two problems remained: (1) Should we use Forward or Reverse? One is broad but blurry, the other is sharp but narrow — neither is ideal on its own. (2) The train-inference mismatch from SeqKD was still not directly confronted: either use teacher-written sentences (mismatch) or use MiniLLM's on-policy approach (expensive and unstable).

*** Its solution

GKD takes a unified stance: *let the student sample its own outputs* (on-policy), and use *JSD* as the loss — that is, `0.5 * FKL + 0.5 * RKL`, splitting evenly between both directions for a balanced middle ground. The framework also allows mixing in teacher-sampled data at an adjustable ratio.

*** Why it worked (and what new pitfall it left behind)

On-policy sampling solves train-inference mismatch — what the student sees during training matches what it sees during inference. JSD balances coverage and sharpness.

But the cost is that every training step requires the student to sample — for LLMs this means a full generation pass each step, more than ten times more expensive than learning from pre-written data. Moreover, "JSD at a 1:1 ratio" is a globally fixed, arbitrary choice — different tokens at different positions actually have different needs for coverage versus sharpness.

** 2024 | Ko et al. DistiLLM: A Cheaper and More Stable KL Estimator

*** What problem did it see

GKD's on-policy approach is too expensive, and the standard Monte Carlo estimator for KL divergence (called K_3 or k_2 in Schulman 2020) has high variance and noisy gradients on LLM-length sequences.

*** Its solution

Bring in the *K_1 estimator* from Schulman 2020. Written simply: `K_1 = log(q/p)` — it is an *unbiased* estimate of KL with lower variance than K_3 in many settings. Combined with a student/teacher mixed-sampling strategy, on-policy learning becomes both stable and economical.

*** Why it worked (and what new pitfall it left behind)

K_1 is a proven "old friend" from PPO — HPD later uses K_1 directly as a per-token reward signal in the paper, and that path was paved right here by DistiLLM.

However, DistiLLM is still in the mode of "pick a KL direction (usually Reverse), then estimate it with K_1." The question of whether Forward or Reverse is better remains unsettled.

** 2025 | Wang et al. alpha-beta Divergence: Sliding Between Forward and Reverse

*** What problem did it see

GKD's JSD uses a hard-coded 0.5:0.5 split. MiniLLM is 100% Reverse. SeqKD is 100% Forward. But Forward (broad coverage) and Reverse (sharpness) should form a *continuous spectrum* — different tasks and different model-capacity ratios call for different positions on that spectrum.

*** Its solution

Use the *α-β divergence* family — through two hyperparameters (α, β), a single formula slides continuously across Forward-KL, Reverse-KL, JSD, Hellinger distance, and all other common f-divergences. Researchers can tune their way to the divergence that best fits their task.

*** Why it worked (and what new pitfall it left behind)

It turns "which divergence to use" from a discrete choice into a continuous knob that can be searched over a validation set.

But the knob is *globally fixed* — once (α, β) are chosen, every token across the entire dataset is treated with the same weighting. Yet within a single token sequence, some positions have the student underestimating (needs Forward to pull it up) while others have the student overestimating (needs Reverse to push it down). A static weighting cannot be right for both at once. This is exactly the opening that HPD steps through.

** 2026 | HPD: Making "Which KL to Use" a Per-Token Dynamic Decision

*** What problem did it see

Looking back at the whole chain — SeqKD, MiniLLM, GKD, DistiLLM, α-β — HPD first does something clever: *writes them all as the same mathematical expression* — "on some data source, reweight log q(a|s) by a weight w(a|s)." Different methods differ only in the specific form of w (SFT uses constant 1, FKLD uses p(a|s), RKLD uses log(p/q), and so on).

Under this unified view the problem becomes clear: every prior method's w is either globally fixed or committed to a single direction. But for any specific (state, token) pair, the right question is "did the student underestimate or overestimate the teacher here?" — if underestimated, apply Forward to pull it up; if overestimated, apply Reverse to push it down.

*** Its solution

Two things:

1) *Per-token dynamic reweighting (Hybrid Forward-Reverse KL)*: For each token, compute a reverse-K_1 estimate `k_1 = q(a*|s) * (log p(a*|s) - log q(a*|s))`. If `k_1 > 0`, the student underestimates the ground-truth token, triggering Forward-KL weighting `p(a*|s) + k_1`; if `k_1 ≤ 0`, the student already overestimates, so *mask out the Forward component* and keep only `k_1` as Reverse suppression. This *masking* replaces a fixed weighted sum — preventing the two directions' gradients from fighting each other.

2) *Hybrid Off-Policy + Lightweight On-Policy*: Most training uses teacher/offline data (cheap). In addition, the student lightly samples one non-ground-truth token `a_t ≠ a*`; for that student-sampled token its `k_1` is computed, and if `k_1 < 0` (the student has spuriously inflated a token it should not), it is additionally suppressed. Simultaneously, the ground-truth token receives *2× Forward weight* as a reinforce signal.

The entire mechanism uses only one reward signal (K_1), with no new hyperparameters.

*** Why it worked (and what new pitfalls remain)

Three things are visible in the experiments:
- Training entropy no longer collapses (SFT quickly overfits to the teacher; HPD maintains exploration)
- KL loss continues to decrease (other methods plateau)
- On math reasoning, Qwen2.5-3B improves 41% over pure SFT and LLaMA3-3B improves 78%

But open questions remain: (1) K_1 estimation still has high variance under extreme student-teacher divergence; (2) this mechanism has been validated in the "student < teacher" direction only — weak-to-strong has not been explored; (3) most experiments cover small compression ratios (7B → 1B/3B); more aggressive compression (70B → 1B) has not been systematically tested.

* Frontier Extensions

HPD is from April 2026; related work appearing at roughly the same time or after:

- *DistiLLM-2 (Ko et al., 2025)*: Another concurrent direction — contrastive distillation that simultaneously increases the probability of teacher-generated responses and decreases the probability of student-generated responses, also leveraging both off-policy and on-policy data sources. The difference from HPD: DistiLLM-2 uses a contrastive loss framework while HPD uses a reweighted likelihood framework. Both lines independently arrive at the conclusion that "purely off-policy or purely on-policy is insufficient."

- *DPO + HPD in series*: HPD's own §7 reports DPO-stage experiments — using HPD as initialization before DPO preference alignment yields 6 additional points over SFT+DPO. This shows HPD is not an endpoint but a *better starting point*.

As of this note (2026-05), follow-up work along the "per-token dynamic reweighting" direction has not yet solidified; the likely next step is replacing the dynamic weighting signal with a stronger RL-style estimator (advantage, GRPO group baseline, etc.) or extending to multi-teacher (mixture-of-experts as teacher) settings.

* Insights

Reading this 11-year evolutionary arc, three layers of regularity emerge:

*1. "Choose A or B" is a transitional question; "dynamically switch between A and B" is the destination*

The whole line replays this pattern over and over. Forward vs. Reverse? First pick one (MiniLLM), then fixed mixture (GKD), then continuous interpolation (α-β), finally per-token dynamic switching (HPD). Off-policy vs. On-policy? First all-off (SeqKD), then all-on (MiniLLM/GKD), finally off-dominant + on-supplementary (HPD).

Each step looks like progress, but all remain at the level of "use one hyperparameter to choose a position." The true paradigm leap is making the choice itself *data-driven and per-instance*. This is the characteristic trajectory of machine learning moving from "the art of tuning" to "meta-learning the choice."

*2. Mathematical unification is not cosmetic — it is a search space for finding new combinations*

The most methodologically significant move in HPD is rewriting SFT/SeqKD/MiniLLM/GKD/DistiLLM all as a unified "reweighted log-likelihood" form. Once written that way, you can search the design space of w(a|s) — and discover combinations like "dynamic sign-based decision + masking" that nobody had tried before.

This pattern recurs across many fields: first a pile of exotic methods accumulates, then someone writes a unified framework, and the *empty cells* revealed by that framework become the next paper.

*3. Train-inference alignment is an underrated gold mine*

The mismatch SeqKD left behind — "student trains on teacher-written data, infers on its own outputs" — sustained three years of on-policy distillation (OPD) work from 2023 to 2026. HPD pushes this to its logical extreme: not purely off-policy or purely on-policy, but "off-policy for dense supervision + on-policy for calibration," with each playing a distinct role.

This pattern is not limited to KD: RLHF, agent training, world models — any task involving "train on distribution A, deploy on distribution B" contains a SeqKD-style hidden mismatch, and it always takes a few years before someone pulls it apart explicitly.

* Takeaways

This means you can:

- *When doing engineering*: If you are distilling a smaller model, stop arguing about "which KL to use" and directly apply HPD's per-token K_1 sign decision; it adds no new hyperparameters, barely changes compute cost, and produces gains across model families.
- *When doing research*: When you encounter a "F vs. R" binary debate, first ask "can these be written in a unified form?"; if yes, ask "is there a per-instance dimension in the unified form that has not been exploited yet?" HPD's paradigm can be transferred to many domains (reward model training in RLHF, preference learning, agent imitation learning).
- *When writing surveys*: Frame each research line as a three-stage progression — "globally fixed → tunable hyperparameter → data-driven dynamic" — then identify which stage your field is currently at. The next stage is usually your paper.
- *When managing a team*: If you see internal subgroups locked in "Team A vs. Team B" debates, stop and ask "can we switch dynamically between A and B per instance?" Nine times out of ten, the answer is yes.
