* One-liner

The KV cache story spans five steps from 2019 to today — each step answering the same question: "Inside the attention mechanism, what actually needs to be stored, and what is just waste?"

* Where the Story Begins: A Memory-Bandwidth Crisis (2017 → 2019)

** The World in 2017

When Transformer first appeared, attention looked like this: each token has a query, and all tokens have keys and values. Q computes similarity with K ("who should I attend to?"), then uses the attention weights to aggregate V ("what information do I take from them?").

To let the model view information from multiple angles, Vaswani designed multiple heads: Q, K, and V are each split into h pieces, each piece computed independently, then concatenated. This is *MHA (Multi-Head Attention)*. Every head has its own copy of K and V.

During training there is no problem. The entire sequence is processed simultaneously; the GPU computes all K and V together, and matrix multiplications run the card at full tilt.

** The Collapse at Inference

But when using the model to generate text, tokens come out one at a time. For each new token, every previous token's K and V must be retrieved and compared against the new query.

This is where Shazeer noticed something: the GPU computes very fast, but for each generated token, the enormous K and V tensors have to be shuttled from GPU memory to the compute units. The bottleneck is not compute — it is *memory bandwidth*.

Imagine a chef who cooks blazingly fast but must run to the warehouse for ingredients every single time. The cooking isn't slow; the fetch trips are.

** MQA's Blunt Solution (2019)

Shazeer's answer was clean and decisive: *all heads share a single set of K and V*. Queries remain multi-head, but keys and values are reduced to just one group.

This is *MQA (Multi-Query Attention)*. The K and V tensors shrink by a factor of h instantly; transfer cost plummets; inference speeds up several times over.

The price: a drop in model quality. The whole point of multiple heads was to look at information from different angles. Now every head sees the same K and V — like turning a room full of detectives into a single detective. Faster, yes, but something gets missed.

* Exploring the Middle Ground (2019 → 2023)

** What GQA Saw

Four years later, Ainslie's team picked up the problem. MQA is too extreme: it jumps straight from "one copy per head" to "one copy for the whole model." Could there be a compromise in between?

Their proposal is *GQA (Grouped-Query Attention)*: divide the query heads into groups, each group sharing one set of K and V. For example, 32 query heads split into 8 groups gives 8 copies of K and V — four times fewer than MHA's 32, eight times more than MQA's 1.

Tuning a single number (the number of groups) lets you slide between speed and quality. LLaMA-2 and Mistral both use GQA.

** What GQA Left Unsolved

GQA is elegant, but carries a hidden constraint: *the group count must be decided at pretraining time*. If you train with 8 groups, inference uses 8 groups. An already-trained MHA model cannot simply be retrofitted with GQA — the structures don't match.

Either retrain from scratch, or pay a large conversion cost ("uptraining," requiring roughly 5% of the original training budget). This constraint will resurface repeatedly.

* Compressing K and V Together (2023 → 2024)

** MLA's Perspective

The DeepSeek-V2 team approached the problem from a different angle. The previous papers all worked on the axis of "how many heads share one copy." Instead, they asked: can we *jointly compress K and V into a single, smaller latent variable*?

That is *MLA (Multi-Head Latent Attention)*. The mechanism: train a projection matrix to map K and V down into a low-dimensional latent vector c; store only c in the cache. When attention must be computed, a corresponding up-projection matrix recovers K and V from c.

The result was striking: DeepSeek-V2's KV cache is *93.3% smaller* than DeepSeek 67B's.

** Two Pitfalls MLA Left Behind

MLA is powerful, but shares MQA and GQA's core weakness: *it must be designed in at pretraining time*. All those already-trained LLaMA and Mistral models cannot simply be equipped with MLA. Using it means retraining from scratch — or paying enormous conversion costs (as in work like MHA2MLA).

A deeper problem went unaddressed: *MLA treats K and V as the same kind of thing*. They are compressed together and expanded together. But do they actually do the same job?

* The Turning Point: Keys and Values Are Not the Same Thing (2026)

** An Asymmetry Everyone Overlooked

Yao et al. inherited this thread and offered an observation so plain it is surprising no one stated it before.

Q, K, and V in attention all have the same dimension by convention (d_model). But what they do is entirely different:

- *Q and K perform "selection"* — compute similarities, rank them, decide who to attend to. This is fundamentally a sorting problem.
- *V performs "transfer"* — once selection is done, carry the information over. This is a representation problem.

How many dimensions does sorting require? By the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, *to distinguish N tokens from one another you need only O(log N) dimensions*. How many does representation require? At least enough to hold all the information being transferred.

In other words, K occupying the same dimensionality as V means *almost all of those dimensions are wasted*.

** A Second Layer of Asymmetry

K must be stored in the cache — every historical token's K must be remembered. Q does not — it is recomputed fresh at every step.

So if compression cost can be *pushed entirely onto the Q side*, the cache gets off scot-free.

** Factored Keys' Move

Perform SVD on the key projection matrix W_K: W_K ≈ A · B, where A is d×r and B is r×d, with r much smaller than d.

Then:
1. *The new key matrix is simply A*. When computing keys, use W_K' = A; the output dimension shrinks from d to r. Only r-dimensional keys are stored in the cache.
2. *B is absorbed into the query*. The new query matrix becomes W_Q' = W_Q · B^T. Query dimension also shrinks from d to r — but queries never enter the cache, so there is no storage cost.
3. *Attention scores are mathematically unchanged*: q · A · B · x = q · W_K · x, an exact identity.

In short, *the same attention computation, but K in the cache is 4× smaller*.

** Why Nobody Did This Before

Earlier approaches all thought in terms of "how many heads share one copy" or "compress K and V together." The key insight Yao identified is that *K and V are fundamentally asymmetric in the economics of the cache*. Accumulated K storage is expensive; recomputing Q at every step is free.

And SVD is a mathematical identity — no retraining, no calibration data, no architectural changes required. Take any already-trained model, run one SVD pass, and the cache shrinks.

Optionally, a lightweight fine-tune of just the QK portion (3 epochs, <1% of training data) can preserve quality at more aggressive compression ratios.

** Empirical Results

- GPT-2, Mistral-7B: *key cache reduced by 75%, quality drops ~2%*
- 7B trained from scratch: r = d/4 variant achieves perplexity 9.24; full attention achieves 9.25 — *essentially identical, yet 12% fewer parameters and 8% faster training*
- Combined with GQA and quantization: *total compression ratio can reach 16×*

* What Is Really Happening Beneath This Thread

On the surface, KV cache compression keeps getting more aggressive — from no compression to 16×. But the deeper cognitive shift runs like this:

*Phase 1* (MHA → MQA → GQA): multiple heads are viewed as "redundant copies that can be merged." The problem is understood as *redundancy*.

*Phase 2* (GQA → MLA): K and V are viewed as "a joint state" that can be dimensionally reduced as a whole. The problem is understood as *joint compressibility*.

*Phase 3* (MLA → FactoredKeys): K and V are recognized as *two functionally distinct things*. The question is no longer "how do we make it smaller" but *"it was never supposed to be this large."* Key dimensionality is an inherited design coincidence, completely mismatched to the job keys actually do (sorting and selection).

This is a shift from "engineering optimization" to *"rethinking the internal division of labor inside attention."* Once you see clearly that K and V are doing two different jobs, every symmetric design from the past starts to look awkward.

* Where This Could Go Next

If the insight holds — *Q/K perform selection, V performs transfer* — the same logic may apply elsewhere beyond attention:

- *Can V be touched next?* Currently V dimensionality is kept intact, but its own "necessary information content" is a fair question. For instance, do V vectors across different positions have low-rank structure?
- *Query dimensionality?* Q never enters the cache so reducing it saves nothing there, but could different layers be given different Q dimensions?
- *Full decoupling of Selection and Transfer?* Right now they still share an underlying hidden state. Conceptually they could be split into two entirely independent sub-networks.

A more radical step: if selection truly needs only O(log N) dimensions, could *the entire K channel of attention become a learned hash* — using extremely low-dimensional vectors for approximate nearest-neighbor lookup, completely breaking from traditional dense attention?
