* The River of the Problem

You have a massive, articulate language model (say, GPT-5), but it only lets you see the text it produces — no logits, no internal states, no weights. Now you want to compress its capabilities into a model small enough to fit on a laptop. How?

The difficulty fits in one sentence: *the small model's problem isn't that it doesn't know what a good answer looks like — it's that it doesn't know how it itself tends to go wrong.* A teacher hands you ten thousand model essays; the student memorizes them all, then still writes a mess when left alone — because nobody ever told it "that's wrong the way you wrote it."

Everyone working this path over the past decade has been wrestling with exactly one question: *should we let the student see its own output? If yes, how? And can we afford the cost?*

* The Evolutionary Narrative

** 2015 | Hinton's KD: Learn "Soft Probabilities," Not Hard Answers

*** What problem did it see?

Model ensembles win competitions but are far too heavy to deploy in production. If you train a small model directly on the ensemble's final prediction ("dog"), the student sees only a one-hot label — essentially discarding most of the ensemble's information.

*** Its solution

Instead of matching the final prediction, have the small model match the *full probability distribution* the ensemble outputs — "90% dog, 8% wolf, 2% cat." That distribution encodes the large model's sense of similarity (wolf is close to dog; cat is far away), and that is where the ensemble's real intelligence lives.

Technically, a temperature is applied to soften the distribution so the student can learn from it effectively.

*** Why it works (and where the new gap lies)

Soft labels carry far more information than hard labels. But there is a hidden assumption: *you can read the teacher model's probability distribution.* With an open-source teacher this is fine; with GPT-5 or Claude sitting behind an API, you only see string output — this path is closed. That is the seed of the "black-box distillation" problem that motivates everything that follows.

** 2016 | Kim & Rush's SeqKD: The Most Straightforward Fix Under Black-Box — Just Have the Teacher Write Essays

*** What problem did it see?

Hinton's KD is particularly awkward for sequence generation (machine translation, dialogue). Decomposing a full sentence's probability across every token requires the teacher to supply a distribution at each step. The engineering overhead is high and the distributions are so peaked (top-1 probability ≈ 99%) that the soft-label advantage nearly disappears.

*** Its solution

Abandon per-token soft labels entirely. Have the teacher generate a complete response for each prompt, *treat that text as supervised data*, and have the student perform standard supervised fine-tuning.

Put plainly: the teacher writes ten thousand essays; the student copies them ten thousand times and memorizes them.

*** Why it works (and where the new gap lies)

This approach is almost too simple to call AI — it is just SFT. Every black-box teacher works (all you need is text output). Alpaca, Vicuna, WizardLM, LIMA — all are variants of this one trick, which basically bootstrapped the open-source LLM ecosystem in 2023.

But SeqKD has two unavoidable structural flaws that the entire next decade wrestles with:

1. *Only positive examples.* The student sees nothing but "how the teacher writes well." *Nobody tells it "here is how you yourself tend to go wrong."* The student's characteristic failure modes — verbosity, hallucination, style drift — are never corrected.

2. *Exposure bias* (already identified by Bengio 2015). During training the student sees perfect teacher-written prefixes; at inference it sees only its own (possibly already drifted) prefixes — the input distributions simply do not match. One token goes wrong and everything downstream follows.

The remaining gap in one sentence: *the student has never learned on its own distribution.*

** 2024 | Gu et al.'s MiniLLM: Have the Student Learn on Its Own Distribution

*** What problem did it see?

SeqKD's root problem is that it is off-policy — training data comes from the teacher, but inference data comes from the student. To learn stably, the student must train on *what it actually produces.*

*** Its solution

Let the student generate its own responses, then use *reverse KL* to push the student's distribution toward the teacher's. The distinction is subtle but critical:

- Forward KL (implicit in SeqKD): requires the student's distribution to *cover* all of the teacher's outputs. The student tends to "cast a wide net" — hedging even on things the teacher would never say.
- Reverse KL (MiniLLM): requires the student to place probability only *where the teacher has support.* The student tends to "concentrate on modes" — preferring to say less rather than say something wrong.

This brought the concept of "on-policy" distillation into the LLM community. Subsequent work (e.g., Agarwal et al.'s Generalized KD, GKD) extended the idea to broader divergence families.

*** Why it works (and where the new gap lies)

Quality genuinely improves over SeqKD, especially on long-generation tasks. But there is a fatal constraint: *reverse KL requires computing the ratio p_teacher(y|x) / p_student(y|x),* which demands per-token probability access from the teacher — a *white-box teacher.*

GPT-5 does not provide this. Claude does not either. With a commercial API as teacher, this path is immediately closed.

The remaining gap: the benefits of on-policy training are clear, but *how do you do on-policy training with a black-box teacher?*

** 2025 | Ye et al.'s GAD: On-Policy Under a Black-Box Teacher — Enter Adversarial Training

*** What problem did it see?

Gu's on-policy KD requires a white-box teacher. But in real production settings, the teachers worth distilling are exactly the black-box kind — GPT-5, Claude. How do you give the student an on-policy feedback signal without access to the teacher's probability distribution?

*** Its solution

By analogy with GAN (Goodfellow 2014) — since you cannot directly compare probability distributions, *train a discriminator* to judge "does this passage sound like the teacher or the student?"

- Student (generator): write so convincingly that the discriminator thinks it is the teacher.
- Discriminator: distinguish student output from teacher output as accurately as possible.
- Train the student with GRPO (policy gradient).
- At the min-max adversarial equilibrium, the student's distribution converges to the teacher's.

This forces on-policy reasoning into the black-box setting: at every step the student trains on its own current output, with the feedback signal coming from the discriminator rather than the teacher's probabilities.

*** Why it works (and where the new gap lies)

It does work, and it outperforms SeqKD noticeably. But the price is steep:

1. *The discriminator is as large as the student* — memory doubles immediately.
2. *GRPO must sample N rollouts per step* to estimate a baseline reward — generation cost multiplies by N.
3. *All the classic failure modes of adversarial training are inherited:* discriminator too strong → vanishing gradients; discriminator too weak → meaningless signal; min-max balance is fragile and collapses with small hyperparameter changes.
4. Warmup schedules, generator/discriminator learning-rate ratios, rollout counts — the full menagerie of RL pain points.

The remaining gap is pointed: *distillation exists to compress large models cheaply. GAD's training budget exceeds what it costs to train the small model directly — so what is the point?*

And a deeper question surfaces: *does on-policy training actually need to be continuous?* The student's distribution shifts during training, yes — but by how much? Does every step really require fresh samples? Or would *a single look at what the student looks like before training* be enough?

** 2026 | SODA: On-Policy Doesn't Need to Be Continuous — One Static Snapshot Is Enough

*** What problem did it see?

The SODA team seized on GAD's pointed open question and translated it into a concrete observation:

*The capability gap between GPT-5 and a 7B base model is so large that the student's zero-shot outputs are almost "strictly inferior" to the teacher's.*

That means *no complex adversarial setup is needed:* simply pairing "teacher answer vs. student answer" already yields an extremely strong contrastive signal. The student's characteristic errors — verbosity, off-topic rambling, hallucination — are right there in its own zero-shot outputs.

So why does GAD pay the enormous cost of continuous rollout? Because it fears the student's distribution will "drift." SODA argues instead: the base student and the mid-training student *share the same architecture, pre-training, and inductive biases.* The base student's typical failure modes mostly persist throughout training — suppressed somewhat but not erased.

*One static snapshot is therefore sufficient.*

*** Its solution

Three steps:

1. *Before training begins,* have the base student answer all training-set prompts and store the outputs. This is a one-time offline generation pass (batched with vLLM — fast).
2. A brief warmup: run a few SFT steps on the teacher's answers to obtain a stable initial policy.
3. *DPO* (Rafailov 2023): treat (teacher answer, base-student answer) as a preference pair — teacher answer as chosen, base-student answer as rejected. One training pass, done.

No discriminator, no RL, no adversarial objectives, no continuous rollout. Compared to SeqKD, the only additional step is "generate student answers once before training starts."

A finer technical insight: DPO's gradient structure fits naturally here.

- The "suppress the rejected" term in DPO computes a score function *over the region where the student itself assigns probability* — exactly where the student makes mistakes.
- Generic bad answers as rejected samples are useless because the student would never produce them anyway; the gradient is wasted.
- Using the student's own base outputs as rejected samples means *every gradient step repairs a mistake the student genuinely tends to make.*

SODA calls this *semi on-policy*:
- Negative samples come from the student's distribution (the spirit of on-policy)
- But sampled once, fixed forever (off-policy in the temporal sense)

*** Why it works (and where the new gap lies)

The empirical results are solid:

- Across 4 base models (Qwen2.5-3B/7B/14B, Llama-3) and 4 benchmarks, *SODA wins 15 out of 16 combinations and ties 1* (vs. GAD).
- 10× faster training, 27% less memory, no adversarial instability.

On the theory side, the authors use an IRL lens: DPO implicitly recovers a reward function, and *that reward is naturally tailored to this specific student* — it encodes "where the teacher is better than this particular student," not a generic "what counts as a good answer."

New gaps:

1. SODA itself acknowledges that the semi-on-policy signal is *front-loaded* — most useful early in training and increasingly less so afterward. For very long training runs it may eventually stop helping.
2. One snapshot is enough — but what about two? Would periodic refresh help? The paper does not fully explore this spectrum.
3. For settings where the capability gap between teacher and student is small (e.g., 70B distilled to 14B), the "strictly inferior" assumption may not hold, and the method could break down.

* Frontier Extensions

SODA v1 appeared in April 2026; v3 is dated April 23, 2026. Visible trend directions from here:

1. *Periodic refresh / Multi-snapshot*: if one snapshot works well, would a mid-training second snapshot help? This is the most directly explorable opening SODA leaves; someone will likely write it up soon.
2. *Self-evolved negatives*: use the SODA v1 model to generate another round of rejected samples and train v2 — an iterative DPO variant in the distillation setting.
3. *What to do when the capability gap is small*: when teacher and student are close in ability (e.g., GPT-5 → GPT-4-mini), the "strictly inferior" assumption breaks down and SODA may fail. New negative-sample construction methods will be needed.

As of May 2026, this is the latest station on the main line of "making black-box distillation on-policy."

* Insights

On the surface this river is "the evolution of distillation methods"; at a deeper level it is *a changing understanding of what a feedback signal needs to be.*

For ten years, "on-policy" was implicitly understood as "continuously, in real time, using the current student's output." That default assumption brought a cascade of complexity: white-box probabilities (MiniLLM), adversarial training (GAD), RL tuning (GRPO). SODA is the first work to break that assumption apart:

*The essence of on-policy is "the signal must land on the distribution the student actually inhabits" — not "the signal must be updated in real time."*

As long as the capability gap "teacher >> student" is stable, the characteristic error patterns encoded in the base student's outputs will largely persist throughout training — suppressed but not eliminated. A single static snapshot covers them.

This is a classic *cognitive leap that happens by separating coupled dimensions* — previously "on-policy" and "continuous" were assumed inseparable; in fact they are orthogonal: on-policy (signal source is the student's distribution) × recency (frequency of signal updates). SODA drops the latter and keeps the former.

The most likely next directions:

1. *Designing the snapshot as a first-class artifact* — when to take it, how many times, with what prompts.
2. *Treating the capability gap as a first-order variable* — SODA implicitly assumes the teacher is far stronger than the student; the method is elegant under that assumption but may break when the gap narrows. Pushing from the extreme GPT-5 → 3B regime toward moderate gaps will expose the next wave of problems.
3. *Reframing DPO's objective in the distillation setting* — in RLHF, DPO learns "the boundary of human preferences"; in SODA it learns "where the teacher is better than this particular student." That reframing still has unexplored headroom (e.g., can we skip the warmup and go straight to cold-start DPO?).

* Takeaways

Several lessons for how to do research and how to find problems:

1. *When a complex method works, first ask "which part is actually necessary."* GAD bundled "on-policy + adversarial + RL + discriminator" into an inseparable package. SODA unpacked it and found that only the "on-policy signal source" was indispensable; everything else was "engineering tax" paid to achieve that one thing. The ability to decompose like this is more valuable than inventing new methods.

2. *"Obvious facts" are often gold mines.* "A 7B base model writes worse than GPT-5" — everyone knows this, but nobody had treated it as a ready-made contrastive signal. Translating common knowledge into a method is the scarcest skill in research.

3. *Resist the instinct that "dynamic / intelligent = better."* Machine learning has an aesthetic bias: real-time beats static, adaptive beats fixed. SODA reverses this — a static snapshot beats live adversarial training. The next time you see a method propped up by "continuous / adaptive / adversarial," ask first: *would a static version be good enough?*

4. *A method's cost determines its fate.* GAD is an academic winner, but its training cost is high enough that nobody deploys it in production. SODA's 10× speedup is not a nice-to-have — it is what determines whether engineers will actually use it. In research, "engineering feasibility" is a first-order variable, not a bonus point.

This means: the next time you face a problem that seems to demand dynamic / adversarial / complex machinery, run the simplest possible experiment first — *replace the dynamic part with a single static snapshot and measure how much performance drops.* If it drops only a little — you have just found your own SODA.
