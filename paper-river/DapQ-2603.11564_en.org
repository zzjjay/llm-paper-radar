* The River of the Problem

A model reads a million tokens of context, then begins generating one token at a time. The trouble is: the intermediate state (KV cache) corresponding to those million tokens sits in GPU memory, growing by one entry per new token. How do you discard part of it without making the model stupider?

The difficulty is that "which tokens matter" has no fixed answer — it depends on what you're about to generate. But you haven't generated anything yet, so how do you know which tokens you'll look back at? This is the loop every successive generation of this research line has been trying to unwind.

* The Evolutionary Narrative

The protagonist of this line is not any single paper but a question that recurs in every generation: "Before you know what you'll generate, how do you know which tokens are worth keeping?" Each generation answers with a proxy that gets progressively closer to "the future." 

** 2023 | StreamingLLM: First Acknowledgment That "Keep Everything" Is Impossible

*** What Problem It Saw

The most naïve approach is a sliding window — when the context grows too long, keep only the most recent N tokens. But Xiao et al. found that once the very first few tokens scroll out of the window, the model collapses immediately: its output becomes incoherent and perplexity explodes.

Why? Because attention's softmax is normalized — probability mass must go somewhere. Even if they carry no semantic importance, the earliest tokens serve as the model's "probability dump" — they absorb all the attention that has nowhere else to go. These positions are called *attention sinks*.

*** Its Solution

Blunt and effective: always keep the first 4 tokens, plus a sliding window. Whether a token is retained is determined by its *position* (beginning or recent), not its content.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Digs)

It works because it identifies a real physical phenomenon (attention sinks). But its lens is *position*, not *content*. The problem: a needle buried in the middle of a long document is positionally unremarkable, yet the moment your question points to it, it becomes the most important token in the room. The next generation had to be able to look at content.

** 2023 | H2O: Selecting "Heavy Hitters" via Attention Scores

*** What Problem It Saw

StreamingLLM's purely positional selection would wrongly evict critical information from the middle. Zhang et al. reasoned: since the attention matrix itself tells us which tokens receive the most attention, why not use it directly?

*** Its Solution

Heavy Hitter Oracle — sum up the attention every query in the prompt pays to each token, and call the highest-scoring tokens "heavy hitters"; keep them, drop the rest.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Digs)

It works because it finally lets the data speak for itself about importance. But the pit is that "cumulative attention from all queries" is a muddled average — questions, citations, and background passages in the prompt all attend to different things, and their signals cancel out. Worse, because LLMs use causal masking, tokens near the end are seen by fewer queries and thus accumulate lower scores naturally, meaning *the most recent tokens are unjustly penalized*. The next generation had to ask: whose queries should be doing the voting?

** 2024 | SnapKV: Let Only the "Last Few Tokens" Vote

*** What Problem It Saw

H2O lets the whole prompt vote, resulting in different intents averaging out. Li et al. discovered that what each attention head attends to during generation is highly stable — and this pattern is already visible in the last 16–32 tokens of the prompt.

In other words: you don't need the whole room to vote; you only need the small cluster of queries most similar to future generation to cast ballots.

*** Its Solution

Carve out an observation window at the end of the prompt (typically 32 tokens), use the queries in that window to compute attention against all preceding KVs, pool the scores, and keep the top-k. Clean, effective — it immediately pushed performance well above prior work under the same budget.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Digs)

It works because it finds a *proxy*: use prompt-tail queries as a stand-in for future decoding queries. But this proxy is a static snapshot — prompt-tail queries are still describing the tail of the input, whereas actual decoding queries are generating the response. They are structurally different, especially on complex tasks requiring cross-segment recall. The next generation had to ask: can the proxy be made more realistic?

** 2024 | PyramidKV: Different Budgets for Different Layers

*** What Problem It Saw

SnapKV is horizontal — which tokens to keep. Cai et al. switched axes: vertically, attention patterns vary enormously across Transformer layers — shallow layers are broad and diffuse, deep layers are narrow and sharp. Assigning the same budget to every layer wastes it.

*** Its Solution

Pyramid-shaped budget allocation — more tokens preserved in shallow layers, fewer in deep layers, with total budget conserved across layers. The core observation-window logic stays the same; only the budget distribution changes.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Digs)

It works because it acknowledges the heterogeneity inside a Transformer. But it does not address the alignment problem in the observation window itself — it still uses prompt-tail queries, still disconnected from real decoding queries. The question remains open: how do you make the "voters" actually resemble the future "voters"?

** 2025 | LAQ++: Just Generate a Fake Response First

*** What Problem It Saw

Wang et al. aimed directly at SnapKV's fundamental flaw: your observation window lives on the prompt side, but what you're serving is the decoding side. The two sides differ in position and context; there's no reason to believe their queries are aligned.

*** Its Solution

Lookahead — before actually starting generation, let the model *pre-generate a pseudo response*. The queries inside this pseudo response are distributionally much closer to real decoding queries than prompt-tail queries ever were. Use those queries as the observation window, then select which original KVs to keep.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Digs)

It works because it finally moves the proxy from the "input side" to the "output side." But the cost is painfully concrete: to obtain the pseudo response you must do a full prefill first, so the KV cache *balloons to its maximum size before being compressed*. Peak memory is actually higher than without compression, cutting the practical value of the entire scheme in half under tight memory constraints. The next generation had to ask: can you get queries equivalent to real generation without actually generating anything?

** 2026 | DapQ: Pseudo Queries Need Real Position, Not Real Content

*** What Problem It Saw

DapQ picked up an observation from CaliDrop: queries at adjacent positions are very similar to each other. Tian et al. followed that thread and ran a counterintuitive decomposition experiment —

They separated two attributes of a query: *content* and *positional encoding*, then compared all four combinations:

| Condition                          | Cosine similarity to real query |
|------------------------------------+---------------------------------|
| Same content + same position (SC&SP) | 1.0000 (upper bound)          |
| Same content + diff position (SC&DP) | 0.3522                        |
| *Diff content + same position (DC&SP)* | *0.7238*                  |
| Diff content + diff position (DC&DP) | 0.3267                        |

Read this table three times. Content that is total nonsense ("Sorry, I don't know" repeated in a loop), yet with the correct positional encoding, similarity leaps to 0.72. Conversely, perfect content but wrong position yields only 0.35. The conclusion is striking: *in determining what a query looks like, "where it sits in the sequence" matters far more than "what it says."*

This means the entire elaborate machinery of LAQ++ — "pre-generate a pseudo response" — is over-engineered. You don't need to generate real content at all. You only need to *inject the correct future positional encoding into arbitrarily filled tokens*.

*** Its Solution

Append a block of N *synthetic tokens* after the prompt (content arbitrary, e.g. repeated padding), positioned at the future decoding positions [L_p, L_p+N).

Run one prefill pass. The queries of these synthetic tokens are the *position-aware pseudo queries* — content is fake, but positional encoding is genuinely from future positions.

Compute attention between these pseudo queries and the original prompt's keys to obtain importance scores; keep the top-k; discard everything else (including all the synthetic tokens' own KV entries). Then begin real decoding from position L_p.

*** Why It Works (and What Pit It Digs)

It works because it reduces the granularity of the "proxy" from "fabricate a fake text passage" to "fabricate a set of fake positions," entirely avoiding LAQ++'s memory spike — the synthetic tokens' KVs are discarded at the eviction step and never occupy long-term space. Under extreme constraints of 3% KV budget, DapQ achieves 99.5% near-lossless performance on NIAH.

The new gaps it opens are clear. First, pseudo-query length N is a hyperparameter — too short and the signal is weak, too long and it drifts. Second, the entire premise rests on RoPE-style positional encoding genuinely dominating the geometric structure of queries — whether "position > content" still holds under ALiBi variants or learned positional embeddings is an open question.

* Frontier Extensions

DapQ is a March 2026 paper; as of this writing, no published work has directly challenged it. Adjacent directions visible in the same time window:

- *KV merging vs. KV eviction*: Tian et al.'s own 2025 KV merging work replaces "drop" with "merge," complementing rather than competing with DapQ — pseudo queries select what to "keep," while the evicted entries could be merged instead of simply discarded;
- *Decoding-time dynamic eviction*: a family of works (e.g. G-KV, SnapKV-D) lets the observation window slide alongside generation, re-selecting every w tokens. DapQ makes a one-shot decision at prefill time; the two could be fused — pseudo queries for coarse-grained pre-filtering, then a real sliding window for fine-grained adjustment;
- *Orthogonal stacking with quantization and low-rank methods*: DapQ reduces the number of tokens; quantization reduces bytes per token; MLA-style methods compress the dimension. The three mechanisms are independent and expected to compose multiplicatively.

The "spiral" of this line is striking — StreamingLLM uses position, criticized as crude; the middle three generations move progressively toward content; then DapQ delivers a turnabout, discovering that *what determines a query is not what it says but where it is*, and throws the core burden back onto positional encoding. But this time "position" is no longer the crude heuristic "first 4 tokens"; it is "the precise future position where decoding will occur" — a position that has been refined and internalized into the geometric structure of RoPE.

* Insight

Reading the full line, what is really changing is not "finding a better proxy" but rather *a repeated rewriting of what a proxy even is*:

1. Generation one thought the proxy was "position" — intuitive but blind;
2. Generation two thought it was "degree of attention received" — data-driven but noisy;
3. Generation three thought it was "prompt-tail queries" — more focused but misaligned;
4. Generation four thought it was "queries from fake-generated text" — aligned but expensive;
5. *Generation five discovers: the proxy is really just a projection of query geometry onto position; content is the noise term.*

The significance of this reversal extends beyond the KV cache problem. It hints at something larger: many Transformer phenomena we have attributed to "semantics" are in fact governed by *the geometric structure of positional encoding*. RoPE is not merely a rotation matrix — it sculpts a hidden "position manifold" in query/key space, and attention patterns largely flow along that manifold.

Where might things go next? Two directions:

- *Theory*: formalize "position dominates queries." Under what conditions does this equivalence hold? What happens with ALiBi or learned positional embeddings?
- *Engineering*: if position is enough, can the same positional toolkit provide the "proxy query" for KV eviction, speculative decoding, and prefix sharing all at once?

* Takeaway

"Doing research means finding problems" is too vague. This line gives a concrete lesson: *when a field is drowning in engineering complexity, go back and decompose concepts that have been silently bundled together*.

LAQ++ ties "future queries" to "future content," and is therefore forced to actually generate. DapQ pulls those two concepts apart, finds that the content component is redundant, and the engineering immediately collapses into something extremely lightweight.

The practical upshot is that you can:
- *When facing an engineering cost that seems unavoidable*, first ask: "Do I truly need both quantities behind this cost to be accurate, or is one of them actually irrelevant?" — run an ablation and see which dimension is doing the heavy lifting;
- *In LLM inference optimization*, whenever a "simulate the future" proxy is involved (the draft model in speculative decoding, the oracle for predicting importance, the predictor at prefill time), apply the same "content vs. position" four-quadrant decomposition — very often you will find that one dimension can be replaced by a zero-cost synthetic signal.
