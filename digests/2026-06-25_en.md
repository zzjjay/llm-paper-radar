#### Summary
Combining FP4 quantization with N:M semi-structured sparsity for LLM activations is hindered by input-dependent outliers that dominate block scales and by sparsification masks discarding moderate values, coupling two error sources. SharQ addresses this with a training-free, online sparse–dense decomposition: per activation tensor, an input-adaptive N:M mask extracts an outlier-dominated sparse backbone quantized to FP4, while the dense residual is defined relative to the quantized (not unquantized) sparse backbone, correcting both mask-induced loss and sparse-path quantization error. Both paths share a single FP4 weight payload with path-specific scale views, and a fused kernel absorbs mask generation, residual construction, and layer normalization. On Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen2.5-7B, Qwen3-30B-A3B, and Qwen3-VL-8B, SharQ recovers 43–63% of the NVFP4-to-FP16 accuracy gap, delivers 2.2–2.4× latency reduction over FP16 and 1.2–1.4× throughput improvement over FP8 on an RTX 5090, and achieves up to 1.58× speedup on Wan2.2-T2V-A14B video generation when combined with SageAttention.

- 🎯 Online sparse–dense decomposition: input-adaptive N:M mask extracts outlier backbone; residual defined against quantized backbone; dual paths share one FP4 weight
- 📊 Recovers 43–63% of NVFP4-to-FP16 accuracy gap; generalizes across NVFP4, HiF4, and MXFP4 formats
- 📊 RTX 5090: 2.2–2.4× latency reduction vs. FP16, 1.2–1.4× throughput gain vs. FP8; up to 1.58× speedup on video generation
- 💡 Fully training-free and calibration-data-free; fused kernel merges mask generation, residual construction, and layer normalization into one operator

#### Summary
Recursive reasoning models solve complex structured tasks with few million parameters by iteratively updating a latent state, but quantization errors compound across recursive cycles rather than output tokens, invalidating standard compression intuitions. - ⚠️ Collapse is architecture-selective: MLP-mixing recursion is vulnerable; attention-based recursion on the same task is not
