#### Summary
Current FP4 pretraining recipes on NVIDIA Blackwell/Rubin and AMD MI350 systems use the E2M1 data format, which suffers from an inherent Shrinkage Bias: a systematic negative rounding error caused by the geometric asymmetry of its non-uniform representable bins. This bias accumulates multiplicatively across layers and is amplified by the Random Hadamard Transform (RHT), explaining the training instability observed in E2M1-based recipes. Uniform grids (E1M2/INT4) avoid this grid-geometry error and better leverage RHT's improved bucket utilization. The proposed UFP4 recipe applies RHT to all three training GEMMs while restricting stochastic rounding to dY alone, achieving consistently lower BF16-relative loss degradation than strong E2M1 baselines on Dense 1.5B, MoE 7.9B, and MoE 124B long-run pretraining.

- 🎯 E2M1's non-uniform grid causes Shrinkage Bias that accumulates multiplicatively across layers and is amplified by RHT
- 💡 UFP4: uniform 4-bit grid (E1M2/INT4) + RHT on all three training GEMMs + stochastic rounding restricted to dY only
- 📊 UFP4 achieves lower BF16-relative loss degradation than E2M1 baselines across Dense 1.5B, MoE 7.9B, and MoE 124B scales
- ⚠️ Current hardware (Blackwell/MI350) natively supports E2M1 only; E1M2/INT4 requires future accelerator ecosystem support

#### Summary
LLM inference with 16-bit precision and greedy decoding still produces divergent outputs across heterogeneous GPUs. SASS-level profiling identifies the root cause as truncation errors introduced by downcasting at kernel boundaries. HEAL addresses this with two mechanisms: INT16 quantization of Q, K, V tensors that preserves numerical stability without expanding the KV cache footprint, and an algebraic error compensation strategy that synthesizes high-precision matrix multiplications entirely on 16-bit Tensor Cores. A new benchmark, MCR-Bench, is introduced to evaluate reproducibility in mission-critical tasks; HEAL matches FP32-baseline reproducibility on downstream tasks while reducing performance overhead by up to 7.1x.

- 🎯 INT16 quantization of Q/K/V tensors preserves numerical stability without increasing KV cache memory footprint
- 🎯 Algebraic error compensation synthesizes high-precision matmul on 16-bit Tensor Cores, avoiding FP32 hardware paths
- 📊 Reduces performance overhead by up to 7.1x compared to a global FP32 pipeline, matching FP32 reproducibility
- 💡 SASS-level profiling pinpoints downcasting truncation as the root cause; MCR-Bench introduced for mission-critical reproducibility evaluation

#### Summary
The robustness of OpenPangu models under aggressive post-training quantization on Ascend NPUs has not been systematically characterized. This paper evaluates seven representative PTQ methods—RTN, GPTQ, AWQ, SmoothQuant, GPTAQ, BiLLM, and SliM-LLM—on OpenPangu 1B and 7B models running on Huawei Ascend 910B1 NPUs under a unified calibration and evaluation protocol across 18 tasks. 8-bit weight-only quantization is effectively lossless for both models; 4-bit is practical for the 7B model but noticeably harmful for the 1B model on reasoning, math, and code tasks. Ultra-low precision (2-bit and binary) consistently collapses to near-random behavior, and W4A4 SmoothQuant produces non-finite perplexity, establishing an NPU-oriented accuracy map for OpenPangu deployment.
