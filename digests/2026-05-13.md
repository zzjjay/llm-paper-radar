📡 Sources: arxiv
🧪 ptq · NVFP4 microscaling BFP with fine-grained scale search · Llama-3.1-70B · cal: Unknown; data-free scale search implied via mantissa optimization · perf: Near-0 loss for causal LM; 27% quantization error reduction NVFP4

#### Summary
Standard Block Floating Point (BFP) quantization uses the block maximum magnitude to select scales, which is suboptimal for minimizing quantization error. ScaleSearch proposes a fine-grained search over the mantissa bits in microscaling formats (e.g., NVFP4) to find scale factors that minimize quantization error for the actual data distribution. Applied to Post Training Quantization and attention, ScaleSearch reduces NVFP4 quantization error by 27%, improves PTQ accuracy by up to 15 points on MATH500 (Qwen3-8B), and ScaleSearchAttention improves Wikitext-2 perplexity by up to 0.77 points for Llama 3.1 70B.

- 🎯 Method: Fine-grained search over mantissa bits in microscaling BFP formats to minimize block-level quantization error, replacing max-magnitude scale selection.
- 📊 Result: 27% reduction in NVFP4 quantization error; up to 15-point improvement on MATH500 for Qwen3-8B PTQ.
- 📊 Result: ScaleSearchAttention improves Wikitext-2 PPL by up to 0.77 points for Llama 3.1 70B with near-zero performance loss.
- 💡 Innovation: NVFP4-based attention algorithm (ScaleSearchAttention) integrating ScaleSearch with adapted prior low-precision attention techniques.

### 2. Grid Games: The Power of Multiple Grids for Quantizing Large Language Models (9/10)
**arxiv** · `2605.12327` · 2026-05-12
👥 Vage Egiazarian, Erik Schultheis, Andrei Panferov... · 🏷 cs.LG
🔗 [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12327) · [PDF](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.12327v1)
📡 Sources: arxiv
🧪 ptq · Multi-grid FP4 (PO2(NF4), MPO2, PO2(Split87), SFP4) · Llama-like models (size not explicitly stated but contemporary LLM scale) · cal: Post-training quantization; calibration cost not detailed in abstract · perf: TensorCore-implementable SFP4 variant; end-to-end speedup not reported in abstract

#### Summary
Microscaled 4-bit formats (MXFP4, NVFP4) quantize value groups to a fixed floating-point grid; this work extends that by selecting among two or more 4-bit grids per group, encoding the grid choice in the scale bits. The power-of-two-grids (PO2) framework is formalized with theoretical analysis showing significant benefit for small groups (as in MXFP/NVFP) but vanishing advantage for large groups. Four grid families are instantiated—PO2(NF4), MPO2, PO2(Split87), and SFP4 (TensorCore-compatible)—and evaluated on post-training quantization of open LLMs and Llama pre-training, consistently improving accuracy over single-grid FP4 in both W4 and W4A4 settings.

- 🎯 Method: Selects among 2+ 4-bit quantization grids per group using 1+ scale bits, formalizing the PO2 framework with theoretical guarantees for small-group formats.
- 💡 Innovation: SFP4 pairs NVFP4 with two shifted variants into a TensorCore-implementable triple, enabling hardware-friendly multi-grid quantization.
- 📊 Result: Adaptive multi-grid FP4 consistently improves accuracy over single-grid NVFP4/MXFP4 baselines in both weight-only and weight+activation quantization regimes.
- ⚠️ Limitation: Theoretical analysis shows PO2 advantage vanishes for very large quantization groups, limiting applicability to small-group microscaling formats.

🧪 ptq · NVFP4 PTQ with closed-form scale optimization · cal: Unknown; likely post-training only (no gradient updates stated) · perf: Native hardware support; end-to-end speedup unknown

#### Summary
SOAR addresses suboptimal accuracy in NVFP4 quantization of LLMs caused by inflexible scale selection and coupled treatment of quantization/dequantization scales. The framework introduces Closed-form Joint Scale Optimization (CJSO), which jointly optimizes global and block-wise scales via analytical solutions minimizing reconstruction error, and Decoupled Scale Search (DSS), which separates the high-precision quantization scale from its constrained dequantization counterpart and performs discrete search to recover precision lost during scale quantization. SOAR consistently outperforms existing NVFP4 quantization baselines across multiple LLMs with no additional memory or hardware overhead.

- 🎯 Method: CJSO derives closed-form analytical solutions for jointly optimizing global and block-wise NVFP4 scales via reconstruction error minimization.
- 💡 Innovation: DSS decouples quantization scale from dequantization scale, then performs discrete search to mitigate precision loss from scale quantization.
- 📊 Result: Consistently outperforms existing NVFP4 quantization baselines across multiple LLMs under the same memory footprint.
- 🔧 Engineering: No additional hardware overhead; targets native NVFP4 hardware support as a post-training quantization (PTQ) framework.

### 4. OGLS-SD: On-Policy Self-Distillation with Outcome-Guided Logit Steering for LLM Reasoning (7/10)
**arxiv** · `2605.12400` · 2026-05-12
👥 Yuxiao Yang, Xiaoyun Wang, Weitong Zhang · 🏷 cs.LG, cs.AI
🔗 [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12400) · [PDF](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.12400v1)
📡 Sources: arxiv
🧪 distillation · on-policy self-distillation with outcome-guided logit steering · cal: on-policy trajectory collection + reflection + finetune; cost not detailed

#### Summary
On-policy self-distillation (OPSD) for LLM reasoning suffers from miscalibrated token-level supervision caused by reflection-induced bias and template artifacts in teacher responses. OGLS-SD addresses this by using verifiable outcome rewards to contrast successful vs. failed on-policy trajectories, steering teacher logits to correct for this mismatch before distillation. The resulting dense token-level guidance stabilized by outcome-level correctness signals outperforms standard OPSD and its variants across diverse reasoning benchmarks.

- 🎯 Method: Outcome-guided logit steering contrasts correct/incorrect on-policy trajectories to calibrate teacher distributions before self-distillation
- 💡 Innovation: Identifies reflection-induced bias and response template artifacts as a systematic miscalibration problem in OPSD teacher signals
- 📊 Result: Outperforms standard OPSD and multiple variants across diverse reasoning benchmarks
- 🔧 Engineering: Combines verifiable outcome rewards (sparse) with token-level logit steering (dense) without requiring external teacher models

👥 Alireza Nadali, Patrick Cooper, Ashutosh Trivedi... · 🏷 cs.LG, cs.AI, cs.CL
🔗 [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12471) · [PDF](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.12471v1)
📡 Sources: arxiv
🧪 kv_cache · KV cache recurrence, chunk-to-chunk folding · Llama-3.1-8B · cal: training-free, no calibration required · perf: fits 128K context on single 40GB GPU; per-step memory bounded

#### Summary
KV-Fold addresses long-context inference in frozen pretrained transformers without retraining or architectural changes. The method treats the KV cache as an accumulator in a functional left fold (foldl) over sequence chunks: at each step, the model attends to the accumulated KV cache as a prefix, appends newly produced keys and values, and passes the enlarged cache forward. Per-step representation drift saturates into a stable plateau insensitive to 10,000x changes in numerical precision, and on needle-in-a-haystack benchmarks achieves 100% exact-match retrieval across contexts from 16K to 128K tokens with chain depths up to 511 on Llama-3.1-8B within a single 40GB GPU.

- 🎯 Method: Training-free KV cache recurrence via chunk-to-chunk foldl; no model modification or retraining required.
- 📊 Result: 100% exact-match retrieval across 152 trials, 16K–128K token contexts, chain depth up to 511 on Llama-3.1-8B, single 40GB GPU.
- 💡 Innovation: Per-step drift saturates to a stable plateau robust to 10,000x numerical precision variation and varying chunk sizes.
- 🔧 Engineering: Repurposes KV cache concatenation primitive from multi-agent communication as a practical long-context inference protocol.

👥 Jian Tang, Jiawei Fan, Qingbin Liu... · 🏷 cs.CV, cs.LG
🔗 [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.11869) · [PDF](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.11869v1)
📡 Sources: arxiv
🧪 other · Frame Interleaved Sparsity (FIS) · HunyuanVideo 1.5 · cal: Training-free, operator-agnostic execution strategy · perf: 2.11–2.41× speedup on Wan 2.2 and HunyuanVideo 1.5

#### Summary
Per-step inference latency in Video Diffusion Transformers (DiTs) remains a bottleneck even after distillation reduces step count, because existing trajectory-based redundancy methods (feature reuse, predictive caching) fail in few-step regimes due to insufficient temporal states. FIS-DiT addresses this by exploiting the latent frame dimension instead: Frame Interleaved Sparsity (FIS) processes interleaved frame subsets across transformer block layers, ensuring all frame positions are refreshed without full-scale block computation. Evaluated on Wan 2.2 and HunyuanVideo 1.5, the training-free, operator-agnostic method achieves 2.11–2.41× per-step speedup with negligible degradation on VBench-Q and CLIP metrics.

- 🎯 Method: Training-free Frame Interleaved Sparsity executes interleaved frame subsets across DiT block hierarchy, refreshing all latent positions without full block computation.
- 📊 Result: 2.11–2.41× speedup on Wan 2.2 and HunyuanVideo 1.5 with negligible VBench-Q and CLIP metric degradation.
- 💡 Innovation: Shifts acceleration axis from denoising trajectory (temporal steps) to latent frame dimension, overcoming diminishing returns in few-step distilled models.
- ⚠️ Limitation: Effectiveness of step-wise caching methods degrades in few-step regimes due to scarcity of temporal states for reuse or prediction.

🔗 [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.11800) · [PDF](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.11800v1)
📡 Sources: arxiv
🧪 other · MoE expert replacement + router calibration for analog CIM noise · cal: post-training calibration; cost not specified · perf: not reported

#### Summary
MoE LLMs deployed on analog compute-in-memory (CIM) hardware suffer from weight perturbations that disrupt expert load balancing and routing decisions, a problem previously unexplored. ROMER addresses this with a post-training calibration framework: it replaces underactivated experts with duplicates of high-frequency experts to restore load balance, and applies percentile-based normalization to router logits to stabilize routing under noise. Evaluated with noise models calibrated from real chip measurements, ROMER achieves up to 58.6%, 58.8%, and 59.8% perplexity reduction on DeepSeek-MoE, Qwen-MoE, and OLMoE respectively.

- 🎯 Method: Post-training calibration combining expert replacement (underactivated → high-frequency clones) and percentile-based router logit normalization for analog CIM noise robustness.
- 📊 Result: Up to 58.6% / 58.8% / 59.8% perplexity reduction on DeepSeek-MoE / Qwen-MoE / OLMoE under real-chip analog noise conditions.
- 💡 Innovation: First systematic study showing analog CIM hardware noise critically disrupts MoE expert load balance and invalidates clean-trained routing decisions.
- 🔧 Engineering: Noise model calibrated from real chip measurements; no retraining required (post-training framework).
