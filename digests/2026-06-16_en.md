#### Summary
Multi-turn LLM serving accumulates KV cache that grows per turn and per user, making memory—not compute—the throughput bottleneck. Non-uniform KV compression allocates heterogeneous budgets across attention heads to preserve accuracy, but modern serving stacks assume identical KV lengths, trapping freed memory as page fragmentation, spending up to 25% of prefill on reclamation, and inflating decode latency up to 1.7x. Tangram observes that head-wise retention follows a two-level structural regularity—an input-invariant head ranking with narrowly bounded per-head ratios—calibratable offline from as few as 50 samples. It statically resolves heterogeneity via Budget Reservation, Ragged Paging, and Ahead-of-Time Load Balancing. Implemented on vLLM as a drop-in substrate for existing non-uniform compression methods, it matches their accuracy while improving end-to-end throughput by up to 2.6x over the full-KV baseline.

#### Summary
Compressed LLMs have an evaluation blind spot: a pruned model can still score well on multiple-choice tests yet fail to answer the same question in open generation. Tracking identical multilingual QA questions before and after pruning, the authors find a benchmark illusion—under high-sparsity pruning (especially Wanda), models fail in greedy open generation while still selecting the correct multiple-choice answer. In these recognition-only errors the correct answer is usually not erased but demoted, often reappearing with beam search, sampling, or a single in-context example. Multiple-choice benchmarks therefore overstate the usability of compressed LLMs, and such models should be tested on what they can produce, not just recognize.
